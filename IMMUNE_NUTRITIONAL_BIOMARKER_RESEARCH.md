## 1. Evidence Grading System {#evidence-grading}

| Grade | Definition | Description |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **A** | Strong evidence | Multiple RCTs, meta-analyses, or systematic reviews with consistent findings |
| **B** | Moderate evidence | Limited RCTs, consistent cohort studies, or single large meta-analysis |
| **C** | Emerging evidence | Small studies, pilot data, inconsistent findings, mechanistic evidence |
| **D** | Expert opinion / theoretical | Preclinical data, case reports, expert consensus only |

| Clinical-Use Status | Definition |
|-------------------|-----------|
| **Established** | Routinely used in clinical practice with validated protocols |
| **Adjunctive** | Used to supplement clinical assessment; not diagnostic alone |
| **Research** | Primarily research tool; not yet validated for routine clinical use |
| **Experimental** | Early investigation; evidence insufficient for clinical application |

### 1. CRP (C-Reactive Protein)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | C-Reactive Protein |
| **Abbreviation** | CRP |
| **Normal Reference Range** | < 3.0 mg/L (conventional CRP assay); < 10 mg/L excludes acute infection |
| **Optimal/Target** | < 1.0 mg/L (cardiovascular/metabolic health) |
| **Sample Type** | Serum or plasma |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | Major Depressive Disorder (MDD), bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, cognitive decline, vascular dementia, post-stroke depression, PTSD, chronic fatigue |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A** (Multiple large meta-analyses) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Adjunctive (inflammation subtype identification in psychiatry) |

**Key Evidence:**
- Meta-analysis of 37 studies (n=13,541 depressed patients; 155,728 controls): 27% prevalence of low-grade inflammation (CRP >3 mg/L) in depression; OR 1.46 (95% CI 1.22-1.75) vs. controls (Osimo et al., 2019, *Psychological Medicine*)
- 58% of depressed patients show mildly elevated CRP (>1 mg/L)
- Anti-inflammatory treatment shows reduction in anhedonia (Hedges g 0.40) and depressive symptom severity (Hedges g 0.35) in patients with elevated baseline CRP (2024 meta-analysis of 19 RCTs)
- AHA/CDC define CRP >3 mg/L as "high risk" for cardiovascular disease

**Key Confounders:**
- Acute infection, recent trauma, surgery, smoking, obesity (BMI), age, exercise, medications (statins, NSAIDs), hormonal status, chronic physical illness

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "Elevated CRP may indicate the presence of low-grade inflammation, which has been associated with certain depressive phenotypes in research studies. CRP is a non-specific marker and cannot diagnose depression or any specific condition. Results should be interpreted within the full clinical context. Approximately one quarter of patients with depression show CRP levels above 3 mg/L."

**Key References:**
- Osimo EF et al. Prevalence of low-grade inflammation in depression: a systematic review and meta-analysis of CRP levels. *Psychological Medicine*. 2019;49:1958-1970.
- Pearson TA et al. Markers of inflammation and cardiovascular disease. *Circulation*. 2003;107:499-511.
- Nettis MA et al. Augmentation therapy with minocycline in treatment-resistant depression patients with low-grade peripheral inflammation. *Neuropsychopharmacology*. 2021;46:939.

### 2. hs-CRP (High-Sensitivity C-Reactive Protein)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | High-Sensitivity C-Reactive Protein |
| **Abbreviation** | hs-CRP |
| **Normal Reference Range** | < 1.0 mg/L (low risk); 1.0-3.0 mg/L (moderate risk); > 3.0 mg/L (high risk) |
| **Sample Type** | Serum or plasma |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | MDD, treatment-resistant depression, cardiovascular disease risk, stroke risk, cognitive decline, neuroinflammation |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A** |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Established (cardiovascular risk); Adjunctive (psychiatric) |

**Key Evidence:**
- The AHA/CDC consensus defines three cardiovascular risk tiers using hs-CRP: <1 mg/L (low), 1-3 mg/L (moderate), >3 mg/L (high cardiovascular risk)
- In psychiatry, hs-CRP >2 mg/L has been proposed as a cutoff for "inflammatory depression" phenotype
- Anti-inflammatory RCTs in depression have used hs-CRP >=2 mg/L as inclusion criterion
- Baseline CRP predicts anti-inflammatory treatment response in depression

**Key Confounders:**
- Same as conventional CRP; additionally, assay-specific interference from heterophilic antibodies may occur

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "High-sensitivity CRP provides a more precise measurement of low-grade inflammation and is used in cardiovascular risk stratification. In research contexts, levels above 2 mg/L have been associated with an inflammatory subtype of depression that may respond differently to certain interventions. This is an adjunctive marker only."

### 3. ESR (Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate |
| **Abbreviation** | ESR |
| **Normal Reference Range** | Male <50y: < 15 mm/hr; Female <50y: < 20 mm/hr; Male >50y: < 20 mm/hr; Female >50y: < 30 mm/hr; Children: < 10 mm/hr |
| **Sample Type** | Whole blood (EDTA or citrate) |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | Autoimmune conditions, neuropsychiatric SLE (NPSLE), infection, temporal arteritis, polymyalgia rheumatica, depression |
| **Evidence Strength** | **B** (for neurological conditions) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Established (general inflammation); Adjunctive (neurological) |

**Key Evidence:**
- ESR is elevated in RA patients with depression (median 26.5 mm/hr vs. normal <20 mm/hr)
- ESR correlates with disease activity in rheumatoid arthritis (DAS28, CDAI, SDAI)
- ESR >100 mm/hr strongly suggests active disease (malignancy, chronic infection, temporal arteritis, severe autoimmune disease)
- In neuropsychiatric SLE, elevated ESR is a consistent laboratory finding
- Less sensitive than CRP for acute inflammation; more influenced by age, sex, and anemia

**Key Confounders:**
- Age, sex, pregnancy, anemia (especially iron deficiency), obesity, hyperfibrinogenemia, hyperglobulinemia, technical factors (temperature, tube tilt, time to processing), recent fatty meal, menstrual period

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "ESR is a non-specific marker of inflammation that increases with age and is higher in females. An elevated ESR suggests the presence of an inflammatory process but does not identify its cause. Serial measurements may help monitor disease activity in known inflammatory conditions."

### 4. IL-6 (Interleukin-6)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Interleukin-6 |
| **Abbreviation** | IL-6 |
| **Normal Reference Range** | < 5.9 pg/mL (serum); varies by assay and laboratory |
| **Sample Type** | Serum or plasma (EDTA); requires rapid processing |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | MDD, treatment-resistant depression, RA-associated depression, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, post-stroke depression, chronic fatigue, cognitive decline |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A** (depression-neuroinflammation link) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research (psychiatric); Established (RA disease monitoring) |

**Key Evidence:**
- IL-6 is one of the most robustly elevated cytokines in depression (cumulative meta-analysis: Haapakoski et al., 2015, *Brain Behav Immun*)
- IL-6 and TNF-alpha are statistically significant predictors of depression intensity (HAM-D scale) in RA patients (B=0.171, p=0.000 for IL-6)
- IL-6 shows stronger association with depression intensity than TNF-alpha
- IL-6 has a strong positive correlation with disease activity indexes (DAS28, CDAI, SDAI) in RA
- Elevated IL-6 found in depressed patients with suicidal behaviors (SMD 1.26 vs. controls)
- IL-6 is a key therapeutic target: IL-6 inhibitors show promise for depression treatment

**Key Confounders:**
- Acute infection, inflammation, tissue injury, exercise, obesity (adipose tissue produces IL-6), smoking, circadian variation, sample handling (degrades rapidly), medications (IL-6 inhibitors, steroids)

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "IL-6 is a pro-inflammatory cytokine that has been found elevated in some patients with depression in research studies. It is not a diagnostic test for depression or any psychiatric condition. Elevated IL-6 may reflect underlying inflammatory processes. Clinical interpretation requires specialist input."

### 5. TNF-alpha (Tumor Necrosis Factor-alpha)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Tumor Necrosis Factor-alpha |
| **Abbreviation** | TNF-alpha |
| **Normal Reference Range** | < 8.1 pg/mL (serum); varies by assay |
| **Sample Type** | Serum or plasma |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | MDD, RA-associated depression, chronic fatigue, neuroinflammation, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A** |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research (psychiatric); Established (RA, IBD) |

**Key Evidence:**
- Cumulative meta-analysis confirms elevated TNF-alpha in MDD patients (Haapakoski et al., 2015)
- TNF-alpha is a statistically significant predictor of depression severity in RA patients (B=0.039, p=0.012)
- Infliximab (TNF-alpha antagonist) trial in treatment-resistant depression showed benefit only in patients with baseline CRP > 5 mg/L (Raison et al., 2013, *JAMA Psychiatry*)
- TNF-alpha levels in RA patients with depression: median 13.88 pg/mL (IQR 11.00-16.39) vs. ~3.5 pg/mL in primary depression
- TNF-alpha mediates neuroinflammation through microglial activation

**Key Confounders:**
- Acute infection, autoimmune disease, obesity, sleep deprivation, stress, exercise, sample handling, medications (TNF inhibitors, steroids)

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "TNF-alpha is a pro-inflammatory cytokine implicated in neuroinflammatory processes. Research suggests it may be elevated in some patients with depression, particularly those with co-existing inflammatory conditions. It is not a diagnostic marker and should not be used in isolation."

### 6. IL-1beta (Interleukin-1beta)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Interleukin-1 beta |
| **Abbreviation** | IL-1beta |
| **Normal Reference Range** | < 5 pg/mL (varies by assay) |
| **Sample Type** | Serum or plasma; CSF (research) |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | MDD, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, neuroinflammation, Alzheimer's disease |
| **Evidence Strength** | **B** |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research |

**Key Evidence:**
- Cumulative meta-analysis shows elevated IL-1beta in MDD (Haapakoski et al., 2015)
- IL-1beta is a key mediator of neuroinflammation and microglial activation
- Systematic review of cytokines in suicidal depression found no significant difference in IL-1beta between depressed patients with/without suicidal behavior and controls (2025 meta-analysis)
- Preclinical evidence strongly supports IL-1beta role in sickness behavior and depressive-like behaviors
- IL-1 receptor antagonist (anakinra) under investigation for depression treatment

**Key Confounders:**
- Acute infection, tissue injury, sample handling (very labile), circadian variation, medications

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "IL-1beta is a pro-inflammatory cytokine with emerging evidence of association with mood disorders. Current evidence is insufficient for clinical diagnostic use. Research is ongoing."

### 7. IL-10 (Interleukin-10)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Interleukin-10 |
| **Abbreviation** | IL-10 |
| **Normal Reference Range** | < 9.1 pg/mL (varies by assay) |
| **Sample Type** | Serum or plasma |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | MDD, autoimmune conditions; often decreased in depression (reflects reduced anti-inflammatory capacity) |
| **Evidence Strength** | **C** |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research |

**Key Evidence:**
- IL-10 is the primary anti-inflammatory cytokine; reduced levels suggest impaired anti-inflammatory regulation
- Meta-analysis found no significant difference in IL-10 between depressed patients with suicidal behaviors and controls
- The IL-6/IL-10 ratio may be more informative than either marker alone
- Reduced IL-10 may reflect a shift toward pro-inflammatory phenotype in some depressed patients

**Key Confounders:**
- Same as other cytokines; exercise can transiently increase IL-10

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "IL-10 is an anti-inflammatory cytokine. Research into its relationship with mood disorders is ongoing. It is not currently suitable for clinical diagnostic use."

### 8. IL-8 (Interleukin-8 / CXCL8)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Interleukin-8 (CXCL8) |
| **Abbreviation** | IL-8 |
| **Normal Reference Range** | < 20 pg/mL (varies widely by assay) |
| **Sample Type** | Serum or plasma |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | Depression, neuroinflammation, schizophrenia (limited data) |
| **Evidence Strength** | **C** |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research |

**Key Evidence:**
- Limited evidence for IL-8 in psychiatric conditions
- Some studies show elevated IL-8 in depression, but findings are inconsistent
- 2025 systematic review found no significant difference in IL-8 between depressed patients with/without suicidal behavior and controls
- IL-8 is primarily a chemokine for neutrophil recruitment; its role in neuroinflammation is less established than IL-6

**Key Confounders:**
- Acute infection, tissue injury, smoking, obesity

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "IL-8 is a chemokine involved in immune cell recruitment. Evidence for its role in psychiatric conditions is limited and inconsistent. Not suitable for clinical diagnostic use."

### 9. IFN-gamma (Interferon-gamma)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Interferon-gamma |
| **Abbreviation** | IFN-gamma |
| **Normal Reference Range** | < 5 pg/mL (varies by assay) |
| **Sample Type** | Serum or plasma |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | Depression (interferon-alpha therapy-induced), autoimmune conditions, schizophrenia |
| **Evidence Strength** | **B** (interferon-induced depression); **C** (spontaneous depression) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research |

**Key Evidence:**
- Interferon-alpha therapy for hepatitis C is well-documented to induce depression in 20-50% of patients - this is one of the strongest causal links between inflammation and depression
- 2025 systematic review found no significant difference in IFN-gamma between depressed patients with/without suicidal behavior and controls
- IFN-gamma drives Th1 immune responses and may contribute to neuroinflammation via microglial activation
- Evidence for IFN-gamma in spontaneous (non-treatment-induced) depression is less consistent

**Key Confounders:**
- Acute infection, autoimmune disease, medications (especially interferon therapy), sample handling

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "Interferon-gamma has been implicated in interferon-alpha-induced depression, one of the strongest evidence bases for inflammation causing depressive symptoms. Evidence in spontaneous depression is less established. This is a research marker, not a clinical diagnostic tool."

### 10. ANA (Antinuclear Antibodies)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Antinuclear Antibodies |
| **Abbreviation** | ANA |
| **Normal Reference Range** | Negative: < 1:40 titer (IFA method); negative by ELISA |
| **Sample Type** | Serum |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), neuropsychiatric SLE (NPSLE), autoimmune encephalitis, anti-NMDAR encephalitis, drug-induced lupus |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A** (SLE diagnosis); **B** (neuropsychiatric associations) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Established (autoimmune screening) |

**Key Evidence:**
- ANA is positive in virtually 100% of SLE patients at some point during disease course
- In neuropsychiatric SLE, ANA positivity is the most consistent laboratory finding (100% in case series)
- ANA positivity associated with worse clinical outcomes in anti-NMDAR encephalitis (higher NMDAR antibody titers, more severe disease)
- ANA may indicate broader immune dysregulation in patients with autoimmune encephalitis
- Low-titer ANA can be found in ~5-20% of healthy individuals (higher with age)
- ANA positive in ~25-50% of RA patients

**Key Confounders:**
- Age (prevalence increases with age in healthy population), female sex, family history of autoimmune disease, certain medications (hydralazine, procainamide, anti-TNF agents), infections, malignancy

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "ANA is a screening test for autoimmune conditions. A positive result does not confirm any specific diagnosis, as low-titer positivity can occur in healthy individuals and with increasing age. A negative result does not exclude autoimmune disease. Positive results require specialist rheumatology referral and further testing."

### 11. Anti-dsDNA (Anti-Double Stranded DNA)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Anti-Double Stranded DNA Antibodies |
| **Abbreviation** | Anti-dsDNA |
| **Normal Reference Range** | Negative: < 65 IU/mL (ELISA); < 30 IU/mL (varies by assay) |
| **Sample Type** | Serum |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | SLE, neuropsychiatric SLE, lupus nephritis, autoimmune encephalitis |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A** (SLE-specific marker); **B** (neurological associations) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Established (SLE diagnosis and monitoring) |

**Key Evidence:**
- Anti-dsDNA is highly specific for SLE (95-100% specificity)
- Positive in 70-80% of SLE patients; positive in 71% of neuropsychiatric SLE cases
- Rising anti-dsDNA titers may predict lupus flares including neuropsychiatric manifestations
- Anti-dsDNA antibodies can cross-react with NMDA receptors, potentially contributing to neuropsychiatric symptoms
- In anti-NMDAR encephalitis associated with SLE, anti-dsDNA is consistently positive

**Key Confounders:**
- Infection (transient elevation), age, assay method differences, medications

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "Anti-dsDNA is a highly specific marker for systemic lupus erythematosus. Positive results strongly suggest underlying autoimmune disease and require specialist rheumatology assessment. It is not a marker for psychiatric conditions per se but may be relevant when autoimmune encephalopathy is suspected."

### 12. ENA Panel (Extractable Nuclear Antigens)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Extractable Nuclear Antigen Panel |
| **Abbreviation** | ENA |
| **Components** | Anti-Sm, Anti-RNP, Anti-SSA (Ro), Anti-SSB (La), Anti-Scl-70, Anti-Jo-1 |
| **Normal Reference Range** | Negative for all components |
| **Sample Type** | Serum |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | SLE, Sjogren's syndrome, scleroderma, mixed connective tissue disease, NPSLE |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A** (autoimmune diagnosis); **C** (neurological associations) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Established (autoimmune diagnosis) |

**Key Evidence:**
- Anti-Sm is highly specific for SLE (99% specificity, 20-30% sensitivity)
- Anti-SSA/SSB associated with Sjogren's syndrome and neonatal lupus
- ENA panel helps differentiate between autoimmune conditions when ANA is positive
- In NPSLE, specific ENA patterns may correlate with neuropsychiatric manifestations
- Anti-Ro/SSA positivity associated with anti-LGI1 encephalitis in some case reports

**Key Confounders:**
- Age, assay method, low-titre positivity in healthy individuals

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "The ENA panel identifies specific autoantibodies that help classify autoimmune conditions. It is used when ANA is positive to determine the type of autoimmune disease. Individual ENA components have different specificities for different conditions. Results require specialist interpretation."

### 13. RF (Rheumatoid Factor)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Rheumatoid Factor |
| **Abbreviation** | RF |
| **Normal Reference Range** | < 14 IU/mL (most labs); < 20 U/mL (some assays) |
| **Sample Type** | Serum |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | Rheumatoid arthritis, RA-associated depression/cognitive impairment, Sjogren's syndrome, cryoglobulinemia |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A** (RA diagnosis); **B** (neuropsychiatric associations in RA) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Established (RA diagnosis) |

**Key Evidence:**
- RF is an autoantibody directed against Fc portion of IgG
- Positive in 70-80% of established RA (seropositive RA)
- 15-30% of RA patients are seronegative (negative RF but clinically affected)
- RA patients show significantly higher rates of depression and cognitive impairment
- RF-positive RA patients tend to have more severe disease, more extra-articular manifestations (including neuropathies)
- RF can be positive in ~5% of healthy population, and increases with age

**Key Confounders:**
- Age (increases with age in healthy individuals), chronic infections (hepatitis C, endocarditis), other autoimmune diseases, cryoglobulinemia, malignancy

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "Rheumatoid factor is used in the diagnosis of rheumatoid arthritis. A positive result supports but does not confirm RA diagnosis, and a negative result does not exclude it. Low-titer positivity can occur in healthy individuals, with increasing age, and in chronic infections. Results should be interpreted alongside clinical findings and anti-CCP testing."

### 14. Anti-CCP (Anti-Cyclic Citrullinated Peptide)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Anti-Cyclic Citrullinated Peptide Antibody |
| **Abbreviation** | Anti-CCP |
| **Normal Reference Range** | Negative: < 0.95 U/mL (some assays); < 17 U/mL (varies by generation); < 20 U/mL (common cut-off) |
| **Sample Type** | Serum |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | Rheumatoid arthritis (early diagnosis), erosive disease prediction, RA-associated neuropsychiatric complications |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A** |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Established (RA diagnosis) |

**Key Evidence:**
- Anti-CCP is more specific for RA than RF (90-97% specificity vs. 80-90%)
- Sensitivity in established RA: 64-96%; in early RA: 14-84%
- Anti-CCP positivity predicts more aggressive, erosive disease course
- Anti-CCP can be positive years before clinical onset of RA (predictive marker)
- Higher anti-CCP titers associated with more complications (71% of patients with complications have high anti-CCP)
- Found in ~9% of SLE patients, ~5% of Sjogren's patients, ~8% of psoriatic arthritis patients

**Key Confounders:**
- Smoking (strong risk factor for anti-CCP positivity), HLA-DRB1 shared epitope, other autoimmune conditions, assay generation differences

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "Anti-CCP is a highly specific marker for rheumatoid arthritis. It can be positive before clinical symptoms develop and predicts a more severe disease course. A positive result strongly supports RA diagnosis, but results must be interpreted in the clinical context alongside RF and clinical examination findings."

### 15. Homocysteine

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Total Homocysteine |
| **Abbreviation** | tHcy |
| **Normal Reference Range** | 5-15 micromol/L (adults); optimal: < 10-11 micromol/L |
| **Cardiovascular Risk** | Moderate: 11-15 micromol/L; High: > 15 micromol/L |
| **Sample Type** | Serum or plasma (EDTA); fasting preferred |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | Cognitive decline, dementia, Alzheimer's disease, vascular dementia, depression, stroke, cardiovascular disease |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A** (cognitive decline/dementia); **B** (depression) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Established (cardiovascular risk); Adjunctive (cognitive/neurological assessment) |

**Key Evidence:**
- **International Consensus Statement** (2018): Elevated plasma total homocysteine is a modifiable risk factor for cognitive decline, dementia, and Alzheimer's disease
- Relative risk of dementia for moderately elevated homocysteine: 1.15-2.5 across meta-analyses
- Population Attributable Risk: 4.3-31%
- Threshold for concern: > 11 micromol/L (OPTIMA study: increased rate of medial temporal lobe atrophy above this level)
- B vitamin treatment (folate, B12, B6) slows rate of whole and regional brain atrophy and cognitive decline in elderly with cognitive impairment
- Intervention threshold: > 13 micromol/L used in modeling studies; cognitive benefits demonstrated at 11-13 micromol/L range
- Meta-analysis: 5 micromol/L increase in tHcy associated with adjusted OR 1.50 (1.13-2.0) for dementia
- Depression at baseline associated with higher homocysteine (OR 1.19-1.33 per quintile increase)

**Key Confounders:**
- Fasting status, B12/folate/B6 status, renal function (homocysteine cleared by kidneys), age, sex (men higher), methylation genetics (MTHFR C677T), medications (methotrexate, phenytoin, carbamazepine), hypothyroidism, smoking, coffee consumption

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "Elevated homocysteine is a modifiable risk factor associated with increased risk of cognitive decline and dementia in research studies. It reflects functional status of B vitamins (folate, B12, B6) and can be lowered with B vitamin supplementation. A level above 11 micromol/L may warrant further assessment of B vitamin status. This is not a diagnostic test for dementia or depression."

**Key References:**
- Smith AD et al. Homocysteine and Dementia: An International Consensus Statement. *J Alzheimers Dis*. 2018;62:561-570.
- Seshadri S et al. Plasma homocysteine as a risk factor for dementia and Alzheimer's disease. *N Engl J Med*. 2002;346:476-483.
- Hoogendijk WJG et al. Predictive value of folate, vitamin B12 and homocysteine levels in late-life depression. *Br J Psychiatry*.

### 16. Omega-3 Index (EPA+DHA %)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Omega-3 Index (percentage of EPA+DHA in total erythrocyte fatty acids) |
| **Abbreviation** | O3I |
| **Normal Reference Range** | < 4%: low; 4-8%: intermediate; > 8%: optimal/target |
| **Sample Type** | Erythrocytes (red blood cell membrane fatty acids) |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | MDD, ADHD, autism spectrum disorder (ASD), bipolar disorder, cognitive decline, cardiovascular disease |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A** (cardiovascular); **B** (depression/ADHD) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Adjunctive (cardiovascular); Research (psychiatric) |

**Key Evidence:**
- Omega-3 PUFAs reported lower in youth with ADHD, ASD, and MDD
- Higher fish intake associated with 17% lower risk of depression (2016 meta-analysis of 26 studies)
- EPA+DHA >= 750 mg/d for 16-24 weeks recommended for ADHD; 1,000-2,000 mg/d (EPA:DHA 2:1) for 12-16 weeks for MDD
- Omega-3 index and inflammation may be treatment response markers for omega-3 supplementation
- Youth with high inflammation or low baseline omega-3 index show better response to supplementation
- 2015 Cochrane Review found insufficient evidence for omega-3s in MDD, though small beneficial effect noted

**Key Confounders:**
- Dietary intake of fatty fish, supplementation, age, BMI, genetic variation (FADS1/2 enzymes), alcohol intake, medications (statins may increase omega-3 index), sample handling

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "The Omega-3 Index reflects long-term omega-3 fatty acid status. Levels below 4% suggest low omega-3 status. Research suggests omega-3 supplementation may benefit some individuals with depression and ADHD, particularly those with low baseline levels. This is a nutritional status marker, not a diagnostic test. Dietary modification and supplementation should be discussed with a healthcare provider."

### 17. Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxal 5-Phosphate / PLP)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Pyridoxal 5-Phosphate (active form of Vitamin B6) |
| **Abbreviation** | PLP / B6 |
| **Normal Reference Range** | 5-50 mcg/L (20-202 nmol/L); adequacy threshold: >= 20 nmol/L |
| **Toxicity Range** | > 80 mcg/L (associated with neuropathy risk) |
| **Sample Type** | Plasma (fasting 8-12 hours; no supplements for 24 hours) |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | Depression, peripheral neuropathy, cognitive impairment, homocysteine metabolism, neurotransmitter synthesis |
| **Evidence Strength** | **B** (neurological function); **A** (deficiency-neuropathy link) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Established (deficiency detection); Adjunctive (neurological assessment) |

**Key Evidence:**
- PLP is the biologically active coenzyme form of vitamin B6
- Essential cofactor for neurotransmitter synthesis (serotonin, dopamine, GABA, norepinephrine)
- B6 deficiency associated with depression, irritability, confusion, peripheral neuropathy
- Both deficiency AND excess can cause neuropathy ("B6 paradox": very high pyridoxine can inhibit PLP-dependent enzymes)
- EFSA (2023) revised tolerable upper intake to 12 mg/day (reduced from 25 mg/day) due to neuropathy evidence
- NIH/FNB (USA): upper limit remains 100 mg/day
- Peripheral neuropathy documented at plasma PLP 259.9 nmol/L from a multivitamin containing only 6 mg B6
- Mean serum pyridoxine in patients with neuropathy: 907 nmol/L (reference 51-183 nmol/L)
- PLP reference: 5-50 mcg/L; pyridoxic acid: 3-30 mcg/L

**Key Confounders:**
- Recent supplementation (fasting 12h + 24h supplement-free required), hypophosphatasia (low alkaline phosphatase + high B6), liver disease, kidney disease, medications (isoniazid, hydralazine, L-DOPA, antiepileptics), alcohol

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "Vitamin B6 (PLP) is essential for neurotransmitter synthesis and nerve function. Both deficiency and excess can cause neurological symptoms, particularly peripheral neuropathy. Testing should be done fasting and after discontinuing supplements for at least 24 hours. Results should be interpreted with caution as reference ranges vary between laboratories."

### 18. Vitamin B1 (Thiamine)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Thiamine (Thiamine Pyrophosphate / TPP) |
| **Abbreviation** | B1 / TPP |
| **Normal Reference Range** | Whole blood thiamine pyrophosphate: 66.5-200 nmol/L; Erythrocyte transketolase activity coefficient: < 1.15 |
| **Sample Type** | Whole blood (EDTA); serum less reliable |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | Wernicke encephalopathy, Korsakoff syndrome, peripheral neuropathy (dry beriberi), depression, cognitive impairment, alcohol-related brain damage |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A** (Wernicke-Korsakoff); **B** (general neurological) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Established (deficiency/Wernicke assessment) |

**Key Evidence:**
- Thiamine is a critical cofactor for energy metabolism (pyruvate dehydrogenase, alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase, transketolase)
- Thiamine deficiency leads to lactic acidosis and cellular energy failure
- Wernicke encephalopathy classic triad: ocular abnormalities, altered mental status, gait ataxia (complete triad in only 10-16.5%)
- Autopsy prevalence of Wernicke lesions: 0.4-2.8% in general population; 12.5% in alcohol use disorder; 29-59% in alcohol-related deaths
- Thiamine stores deplete within ~4 weeks of intake cessation
- EFNS recommendation: IV thiamine 200 mg three times daily for suspected/confirmed Wernicke
- Must give thiamine BEFORE glucose to prevent exacerbation of deficiency
- Untreated mortality: up to 50%
- Korsakoff syndrome (chronic amnestic state) often irreversible despite treatment

**Key Confounders:**
- Alcohol use disorder, malnutrition, bariatric surgery, hyperemesis gravidarum, diuretic use, dialysis, chronic illness, increased metabolic demand (pregnancy, hyperthyroidism), refeeding syndrome

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "Thiamine (vitamin B1) is essential for brain energy metabolism. Deficiency can cause serious neurological complications including Wernicke encephalopathy, which is a medical emergency. At-risk patients should receive empiric thiamine supplementation before laboratory confirmation. Thiamine must always be administered before intravenous glucose."

### 20. Vitamin E

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Alpha-Tocopherol |
| **Abbreviation** | Vitamin E |
| **Normal Reference Range** | 6-10 mg/dL (5.7-20 mg/L varies by lab); some labs: 5.5-17 mg/L |
| **Deficiency Threshold** | < 5 mg/dL |
| **Sample Type** | Serum (fasting; lipid panel ideally performed simultaneously) |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | Ataxia with vitamin E deficiency (AVED), peripheral neuropathy, retinitis pigmentosa, neurodegeneration, fat malabsorption |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A** (AVED); **C** (general neuroprotection) |

> "Vitamin E deficiency can cause neurological symptoms including ataxia and peripheral neuropathy, particularly in individuals with fat malabsorption. ### 32. Lactate/Pyruvate Ratio

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Lactate to Pyruvate Molar Ratio |
| **Abbreviation** | L:P ratio |
| **Normal Reference Range** | 10-20:1 (or < 23:1); normal NADH/NAD+ ratio |
| **Elevated Threshold** | > 23-30:1 suggests mitochondrial dysfunction |
| **Sample Type** | Blood; CSF (specialist) |
| **Conditions with Evidence** | Mitochondrial disorders, pyruvate dehydrogenase deficiency, thiamine deficiency, respiratory chain defects |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A** (mitochondrial disease) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Established (mitochondrial assessment) |

**Key Evidence:**
- L:P ratio reflects the cytoplasmic NADH/NAD+ redox state; a key indicator of mitochondrial function
- Elevated L:P ratio suggests: (a) respiratory chain defect (lactate high, pyruvate relatively normal) OR (b) thiamine deficiency/pyruvate dehydrogenase impairment
- Critical distinction: In pyruvate dehydrogenase deficiency, BOTH lactate and pyruvate are proportionally elevated, so L:P ratio is NORMAL
- In mitochondrial respiratory chain defects, lactate is disproportionately elevated, so L:P ratio is ELEVATED
- Diagnostic performance: AUC 0.83-0.90 depending on study; sensitivity 31-82%; specificity 69-100%
- CSF L:P ratio: sensitivity 76.5%, specificity 90.6% for mitochondrial disease
- Threshold: > 23.3 (one study); > 30 (another study)
- Blood L:P ratio evaluated in 8 studies (195 blood samples, 17 CSF samples); all 3 statistically significant studies confirmed utility

**Key Confounders:**
- Same as lactate and pyruvate individually; critical sample handling (both must be measured simultaneously under identical conditions); must be fasting, at rest, collected on ice

**Safe Clinical Wording:**
> "The lactate/pyruvate ratio is the most useful single biomarker for mitochondrial dysfunction when collected and processed correctly. A normal ratio with elevated lactate and pyruvate suggests pyruvate dehydrogenase deficiency, while an elevated ratio suggests mitochondrial respiratory chain dysfunction. This test requires specialist collection and handling procedures. Interpretation should be by a metabolic specialist."

## 5. Key Confounders & Clinical Wording {#confounders}

### Universal Confounders for All Biomarkers

| Category | Confounders |
|----------|-------------|
| **Demographic** | Age, sex, ethnicity, BMI, menopausal status |
| **Acute factors** | Recent infection, fever, trauma, surgery, exercise, stress |
| **Medications** | Statins, NSAIDs, steroids, immunosuppressants, antibiotics, anticonvulsants, supplements |
| **Lifestyle** | Smoking, alcohol, diet, sleep quality, caffeine intake |
| **Sample handling** | Collection time, fasting status, processing delay, storage temperature, hemolysis |
| **Comorbidities** | Chronic disease, autoimmune conditions, liver/kidney disease, thyroid dysfunction |

### Safe Clinical Wording Template

For ALL biomarkers in this document, the following principles apply:

1. **No biomarker in this document is diagnostic of any psychiatric or neurological condition**
2. **Results must always be interpreted in the full clinical context**
3. **Biomarkers are adjunctive tools that may support clinical assessment**
4. **Reference ranges vary between laboratories and assays**
5. **Single measurements have limited value; trends over time are more informative**
6. **Inflammatory markers are non-specific and elevated in many physical conditions**
7. **Nutritional markers reflect status but do not establish causation**
8. **Always consider confounders before attributing results to psychiatric/neurological conditions**

### Risk Stratification Summary

| Risk Level | hs-CRP | Homocysteine | Action |
|-----------|--------|-------------|--------|
| Low | < 1.0 mg/L | < 10 micromol/L | Routine monitoring |
| Moderate | 1.0-3.0 mg/L | 10-15 micromol/L | Lifestyle modification; review B vitamins |
| High | > 3.0 mg/L | > 15 micromol/L | Clinical assessment; consider intervention |
