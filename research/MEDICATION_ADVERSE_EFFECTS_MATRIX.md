## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This matrix compiles adverse effect profiles for 16 major drug classes used in psychiatric and neuromodulation practice. Data sources include FDA labels, FAERS pharmacovigilance data, meta-analyses, randomized controlled trials (RCTs), and systematic reviews published through June 2025.

### Key Cross-Cutting Findings

- **Seizure Risk Hierarchy (highest to lowest):** Bupropion (0.4%) > TCAs (~1%) > SNRIs (venlafaxine 0.3%) > SSRIs (~0.1%) > Antipsychotics (variable)
- **QT Prolongation Hierarchy (highest to lowest):** Thioridazine > Ziprasidone > Haloperidol IV > TCAs (amitriptyline) > Citalopram > Escitalopram > Aripiprazole/Brexpiprazole (minimal)
- **Metabolic Risk Hierarchy (weight gain):** Clozapine/Olanzapine (worst) > Quetiapine/Risperidone (intermediate) > Aripiprazole/Ziprasidone/Lurasidone (least)
- **Cognitive Risk Hierarchy (most impairing):** Benzodiazepines > Topiramate ("Dopamax") > TCAs (anticholinergic) > Antipsychotics (variable)
- **Overall Mortality Risk in Overdose:** TCAs > MAOIs > Lithium > Other antidepressants

## 2. METHODOLOGY & EVIDENCE GRADING

### Evidence Grades Used

| Grade | Definition |
|-------|------------|
| **A** | Strong evidence: Multiple RCTs or large meta-analyses |
| **B** | Moderate evidence: Limited RCTs or well-designed observational studies |
| **C** | Emerging evidence: Case series, small trials, or conflicting data |
| **FDA** | Derived from FDA labeling data |
| **FAERS** | Derived from FDA Adverse Event Reporting System signals |

### Data Sources
- FDA-approved prescribing information (product labels)
- FAERS quarterly reports (2019-2025)
- Published meta-analyses (PubMed/MEDLINE)
- APA Resource Documents
- Cochrane systematic reviews

### 3.1 SELECTIVE SEROTONIN REUPTAKE INHIBITORS (SSRIs)

**Representative Agents:** Fluoxetine, Sertraline, Paroxetine, Citalopram, Escitalopram, Fluvoxamine

| Domain | Profile | Evidence Grade |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **Common AEs (>5%)** | Nausea (15-30%), sexual dysfunction (30-60%), insomnia (10-20%), headache (15-25%), diarrhea (10-20%), agitation/anxiety (5-15%), dry mouth, somnolence | A |
| **Serious AEs (<5%)** | Serotonin syndrome (rare, <0.1%), SIADH/hyponatremia (elderly, 2-5%), GI bleeding (2-4%), QT prolongation (citalopram >40mg), extrapyramidal symptoms (rare, 0.1-0.5%), discontinuation syndrome (paroxetine/fluvoxamine most severe) | A |
| **Seizure Threshold** | **Mild decrease** - SSRIs lower seizure threshold via 5-HT2/5-HT1A activation. Risk ~0.1% at therapeutic doses. Short-term use associated with greater risk (OR 1.48, 95% CI 1.32-1.66) | A |
| **EEG Effects** | **Beta increase, alpha decrease** - SSRIs increase beta power (especially 18-30 Hz), decrease alpha power. May potentiate latent epileptiform activity at high doses. Minimal slowing at therapeutic doses | B |
| **Cognitive Effects** | Minimal negative effects at therapeutic doses. Possible mild improvement in psychomotor speed. No significant impairment in reaction time, memory, or attention in most patients | A |
| **Cardiac Effects** | Citalopram: QT prolongation dose-dependent (>40mg), FDA warning. Escitalopram: mild QT effect (not clinically significant at standard doses). Sertraline: best-studied, appears safe even with risk factors. Overall: low arrhythmia risk at therapeutic doses | A |
| **Metabolic Effects** | Weight: generally neutral; paroxetine may cause modest weight gain. Glucose: neutral or slightly beneficial. Lipids: neutral. Prolactin: minimal effect | A |
| **Washout (Neuromodulation)** | 5 half-lives (~5 days for most SSRIs; fluoxetine requires 5-6 weeks due to long half-life/norfluoxetine metabolite) | A |
| **FDA Black Box Warnings** | **Suicidality in children, adolescents, and young adults (18-24 years)** - Increased risk of suicidal ideation during initial treatment phase. Extended to age 25 in 2007 | FDA |

**Key Notes:**
- Paroxetine has the highest discontinuation syndrome risk due to shortest half-life and lack of active metabolite
- Citalopram and escitalopram have minimal CYP450 interactions compared to other SSRIs
- Fluvoxamine has highest GI side effect profile
- Sertraline is the best-studied SSRI in cardiac patients

### 3.2 SEROTONIN-NOREPINEPHRINE REUPTAKE INHIBITORS (SNRIs)

**Representative Agents:** Venlafaxine, Desvenlafaxine, Duloxetine, Levomilnacipran

| Domain | Profile | Evidence Grade |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **Common AEs (>5%)** | Nausea (20-40%), headache (15-25%), insomnia (10-20%), dizziness (10-20%), sweating (5-20%), constipation (10-15%), dry mouth (10-20%), hypertension (dose-dependent) | A |
| **Serious AEs (<5%)** | Sustained hypertension (duloxetine, venlafaxine; dose-dependent), serotonin syndrome (rare), discontinuation syndrome (venlafaxine most severe), SIADH, abnormal bleeding, hepatotoxicity (duloxetine; rare) | A |
| **Seizure Threshold** | **Mild decrease** - Venlafaxine: seizure risk ~0.3% (clinical trials); Duloxetine: ~0.2%. FAERS data shows venlafaxine has strongest SNRI seizure signal (ROR 2.98). Meta-analysis: SNRI seizure OR 1.65 (95% CI 1.24-2.19) | A |
| **EEG Effects** | **Beta increase** - Similar to SSRIs, SNRIs increase beta activity. Cortical arousal effects more pronounced than SSRIs due to norepinephrine component. May see mild activation patterns | B |
| **Cognitive Effects** | Minimal cognitive impairment. Some studies suggest improved processing speed. Duloxetine associated with mild improvement in attention | A |
| **Cardiac Effects** | Venlafaxine: rare QT prolongation, especially in elderly; dose-dependent BP elevation (diastolic >5-10 mmHg at 300mg). Duloxetine: mild BP increase. Desvenlafaxine: minimal QT effect. Overall low-moderate cardiac risk | A |
| **Metabolic Effects** | Weight: generally neutral to slight loss. Venlafaxine may cause anorexia/weight loss at higher doses. Glucose: neutral. Lipids: neutral. Prolactin: minimal effect | B |
| **Washout (Neuromodulation)** | 7 days (venlafaxine t1/2=5h, but active metabolite extends effect; duloxetine t1/2=12h) | A |
| **FDA Black Box Warnings** | **Suicidality in children, adolescents, and young adults (18-24 years)** (class warning for all antidepressants) | FDA |

**Key Notes:**
- Venlafaxine has the most severe discontinuation syndrome of all antidepressants
- SNRIs have greater activating properties than SSRIs; may cause more anxiety early in treatment
- Norepinephrine component provides additional benefit for pain syndromes

### 3.3 TRICYCLIC ANTIDEPRESSANTS (TCAs)

**Representative Agents:** Amitriptyline, Nortriptyline, Imipramine, Clomipramine, Doxepin, Desipramine

| Domain | Profile | Evidence Grade |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **Common AEs (>5%)** | Anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, blurred vision, tachycardia), sedation (H1 blockade), orthostatic hypotension (alpha-1 blockade), weight gain, cognitive impairment, tremor | A |
| **Serious AEs (<5%)** | Cardiac conduction abnormalities, arrhythmias, seizures (~1% incidence; clomipramine highest risk), agranulocytosis (rare), hepatotoxicity, delirium (elderly), falls, narrow therapeutic index - overdose fatal | A |
| **Seizure Threshold** | **Moderate decrease** - Seizure incidence ~1% (highest among antidepressants). Clomipramine has highest TCA seizure risk. Maprotiline (tetracyclic): 0.4% at therapeutic doses but higher post-marketing. TCAs reduce seizure threshold via sodium channel blockade and anticholinergic effects | A |
| **EEG Effects** | **Slowing (delta/theta increase, alpha decrease)** - Sedating TCAs (amitriptyline, doxepin) markedly increase slow-wave activity and decrease alpha power. Less sedation with desipramine/nortriptyline. EEG changes dose-dependent | B |
| **Cognitive Effects** | **Significant impairment** - Anticholinergic burden causes dose-dependent deficits in: immediate memory (RBANS: beta -4.98, p<0.001), attention (RBANS: beta -6.81, p<0.001), reaction time, and processing speed. Effects most pronounced in elderly. Cognitive impairment correlates with anticholinergic load | A |
| **Cardiac Effects** | **HIGH RISK** - TCA overdose: QRS widening (>100ms predictive of seizures; >160ms predictive of arrhythmias), QT prolongation, heart block, ventricular arrhythmias. Therapeutic doses: orthostatic hypotension, sinus tachycardia, conduction delay. Nortriptyline and amitriptyline showed significant QTc changes at 4 weeks. Avoid in cardiac patients | A |
| **Metabolic Effects** | Weight: significant gain (amitriptyline > nortriptyline). Glucose: may increase. Lipids: neutral to mild increase. Prolactin: minimal effect | B |
| **Washout (Neuromodulation)** | 14 days (long half-lives: amitriptyline 10-28h, nortriptyline 18-44h, but active metabolites extend duration) | A |
| **FDA Black Box Warnings** | None specific to TCAs (general antidepressant suicidality warning applies) | FDA |

**Key Notes:**
- TCAs have the highest fatality rate in overdose of any antidepressant class
- Lofepramine has the lowest overdose toxicity; desipramine the highest
- TCAs remain first-line for neuropathic pain and migraine prophylaxis despite safety concerns
- Anticholinergic burden is dose-limiting, especially in elderly (>65 years)

### 3.4 MONOAMINE OXIDASE INHIBITORS (MAOIs)

**Representative Agents:** Phenelzine, Tranylcypromine, Isocarboxazid, Selegiline (transdermal)

| Domain | Profile | Evidence Grade |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **Common AEs (>5%)** | Orthostatic hypotension (most common, ~10-30%), insomnia (tranylcypromine), weight gain (phenelzine), sexual dysfunction (20-30%), dry mouth, constipation, edema (phenelzine), hypertensive episodes (with tyramine) | A |
| **Serious AEs (<5%)** | Hypertensive crisis (with tyramine-containing foods or serotonergic drugs), serotonin syndrome (combination with SSRIs/SNRIs), hepatotoxicity (phenelzine), mania induction, subarachnoid hemorrhage (hypertensive crisis) | A |
| **Seizure Threshold** | **Neutral to mild decrease** - No significant seizure risk at therapeutic doses. Risk primarily from hypertensive crisis or serotonin syndrome interactions | B |
| **EEG Effects** | **Minimal data** - Limited systematic pharmaco-EEG studies. Some reports of mild generalized slowing with phenelzine due to sedative effects | C |
| **Cognitive Effects** | Cognitive effects largely understudied. Orthostatic hypotension may contribute to fall risk and cognitive complaints in elderly. No significant direct cognitive toxicity reported | C |
| **Cardiac Effects** | Orthostatic hypotension is most common cardiovascular effect. Hypertensive crisis is a pharmacologic emergency (can cause intracranial hemorrhage, aortic dissection). Requires dietary tyramine restriction | A |
| **Metabolic Effects** | Weight: phenelzine causes significant weight gain. Glucose: neutral. Lipids: neutral. Prolactin: minimal effect | B |
| **Washout (Neuromodulation)** | **14 days minimum** before starting any serotonergic agent; irreversible inhibition requires new MAO synthesis. 14 days washout when switching to SSRIs/SNRIs/TCAs; 21 days for clomipramine or imipramine | A |
| **FDA Black Box Warnings** | **Hypertensive crisis with tyramine-containing foods** (oral agents). Risk of severe, sometimes fatal, hypertension when consumed with tyramine-rich foods | FDA |

**Key Notes:**
- MAOIs remain most effective treatment for atypical depression
- Transdermal selegiline bypasses first-pass metabolism, allowing lower doses with reduced dietary restrictions at 6mg/24h
- Moclobemide (reversible MAO-A inhibitor) has much shorter washout (24 hours)

### 3.5 ATYPICAL ANTIPSYCHOTICS

**Representative Agents:** Clozapine, Olanzapine, Risperidone, Quetiapine, Aripiprazole, Ziprasidone, Lurasidone, Brexpiprazole, Cariprazine

| Domain | Profile | Evidence Grade |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **Common AEs (>5%)** | Sedation (clozapine, quetiapine, olanzapine), weight gain (olanzapine, clozapine), akathisia (aripiprazole), extrapyramidal symptoms (risperidone > others), hyperprolactinemia (risperidone, paliperidone), orthostatic hypotension, constipation, dry mouth | A |
| **Serious AEs (<5%)** | Agranulocytosis (clozapine: ~1%), NMS (rare, <0.1%), metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, dyslipidemia, QT prolongation (ziprasidone > others), myocarditis (clozapine: rare), tardive dyskinesia (1-5% annually), seizures (clozapine: ~3-5%), diabetic ketoacidosis | A |
| **Seizure Threshold** | **Variable** - Clozapine: **decreases** (3-5% seizure risk, dose-dependent). Olanzapine/quetiapine: mild decrease. Aripiprazole/risperidone: minimal effect. Chlorpromazine: decreases. Seizure risk increases with rapid dose escalation | A |
| **EEG Effects** | **Variable by agent** - Clozapine: marked increase in slow waves (delta/theta), decrease alpha (occipital). Other antipsychotics: increase alpha activity. Low-potency agents (chlorpromazine): increase slow waves. High-potency agents (haloperidol): increase alpha/beta with less slowing | B |
| **Cognitive Effects** | Variable. Clozapine may impair attention/working memory. Olanzapine: mild sedation/cognitive slowing. Aripiprazole: least cognitive impact. Long-term antipsychotic use associated with cognitive deficits in processing speed and working memory | B |
| **Cardiac Effects** | **QT prolongation hierarchy:** Thioridazine (30.1ms) > Ziprasidone (15.9ms) > Haloperidol IV > Quetiapine (5.7ms) > Risperidone (3.6-3.9ms) > Olanzapine (1.7ms). Ziprasidone carries highest modern atypical risk. TdP risk greatest with thioridazine and IV haloperidol | A |
| **Metabolic Effects** | **Weight gain hierarchy:** Clozapine (3.01kg) > Olanzapine (2.5-4kg) > Quetiapine (intermediate) > Risperidone (intermediate) > Aripiprazole/Ziprasidone/Lurasidone (minimal). **Prolactin:** Risperidone/Paliperidone (worst, 65% of females) > Amisulpride > Haloperidol > Olanzapine > Quetiapine/Aripiprazole (minimal/decrease) | A |
| **Washout (Neuromodulation)** | Aripiprazole: 14 days (t1/2=75h, active metabolite). Olanzapine: 7 days (t1/2=30h). Risperidone: 7 days. Quetiapine: 3 days (t1/2=6h). Clozapine: 14-28 days (t1/2=12h but tissue accumulation) | A |
| **FDA Black Box Warnings** | **Increased mortality in elderly patients with dementia-related psychosis** (class warning for all antipsychotics). **Clozapine:** Severe neutropenia (agranulocytosis), myocarditis. **Aripiprazole/Brexpiprazole:** Elderly dementia-related mortality | FDA |

**Key Notes:**
- Metabolic syndrome risk with clozapine/olanzapine is highest among all psychiatric medications
- Clozapine requires REMS program monitoring (ANC every week x6 months, then biweekly)
- Prolactin elevation with risperidone/paliperidone can cause sexual dysfunction, amenorrhea, galactorrhea, and bone density loss
- Aripiprazole has the most favorable metabolic profile of all antipsychotics

### 3.6 LITHIUM

**Representative Agent:** Lithium carbonate/citrate

| Domain | Profile | Evidence Grade |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **Common AEs (>5%)** | Tremor (dose-related, 30-50%), polyuria/polydipsia (nephrogenic DI, 20-40%), diarrhea (10-30%), nausea (10-30%), weight gain (10-20%), cognitive slowing/brain fog (15-25%), acne, psoriasis exacerbation | A |
| **Serious AEs (<5%)** | Lithium toxicity (serum >1.5 mEq/L): tremor, ataxia, seizures, coma, death. Nephrotoxicity (chronic interstitial nephritis, 1-5% long-term), hypothyroidism (5-30%; women 40-59 highest risk), hyperparathyroidism, ECG changes (T-wave flattening, sinus node dysfunction), benign intracranial hypertension, teratogenicity (Ebstein anomaly) | A |
| **Seizure Threshold** | **Decreases at toxic levels** - Therapeutic levels: minimal effect. Toxic levels (>1.5 mEq/L): seizures, encephalopathy. Risk increases with dehydration, renal impairment, NSAID co-administration | A |
| **EEG Effects** | **Slowing (delta/theta increase)** - Lithium causes generalized asynchronous slowing, reduces peak alpha frequency. High doses: bisynchronous spikes/polyspikes, increased delta/theta. Toxicity: dramatic slowing, triphasic discharges resembling metabolic encephalopathy. Can potentiate latent epileptiform activity | B |
| **Cognitive Effects** | **Moderate impairment** - "Lithium fog": slowed processing speed, impaired memory encoding, decreased creativity. Effects often improve with dose reduction. Long-term use associated with mild cognitive impairment. Some reports of reversible cerebral atrophy | A |
| **Cardiac Effects** | T-wave flattening/inversion (common, benign), sinus node dysfunction, sinoatrial block (rare), bradycardia. Avoid in sick sinus syndrome or AV block. Generally low arrhythmia risk at therapeutic levels | A |
| **Metabolic Effects** | Weight: modest gain (2-10kg). Glucose: may impair glucose tolerance. Lipids: neutral. Prolactin: minimal. Thyroid: hypothyroidism risk increases 5-fold. Calcium: hyperparathyroidism with hypercalcemia | A |
| **Washout (Neuromodulation)** | **5-7 days** (t1/2=18-24h in young adults; 36h in elderly; renally eliminated) | A |
| **FDA Black Box Warnings** | **Fetal harm/teratogenicity** (pregnancy category D - Ebstein anomaly). **Lithium toxicity** - narrow therapeutic index (0.6-1.0 mEq/L maintenance; >1.5 = toxic) | FDA |

**Key Notes:**
- Lithium is the only agent proven to reduce suicide risk in bipolar disorder
- Monitoring required: serum lithium every 3-6 months, TSH, creatinine, calcium every 6-12 months
- Dehydration, low sodium intake, and thiazide diuretics increase toxicity risk

### 3.7 VALPROATE

**Representative Agents:** Valproic acid, Divalproex sodium, Sodium valproate

| Domain | Profile | Evidence Grade |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **Common AEs (>5%)** | Nausea (10-30%), tremor (dose-related, 10-30%), somnolence (10-25%), weight gain (10-30%, mean 2-5kg), hair loss/thinning (5-15%), dizziness, headache, diarrhea | A |
| **Serious AEs (<5%)** | Hepatotoxicity (fatal in infants <2 years; rare in adults), hyperammonemic encephalopathy (confusion, seizures, lethargy, coma), pancreatitis (rare, potentially fatal), thrombocytopenia (dose-dependent), polycystic ovary syndrome (young women), teratogenicity (neural tube defects - spina bifida risk 1-2%) | A |
| **Seizure Threshold** | **Increases** (antiepileptic mechanism) - Sodium channel blockade, GABA enhancement, T-type calcium channel blockade. Used as mood stabilizer and anticonvulsant | A |
| **EEG Effects** | **Beta increase** - Increases beta activity, especially with long-term use. Some increase in delta activity reported. Normalizes epileptiform discharges | B |
| **Cognitive Effects** | Moderate cognitive effects: sedation, slowed processing speed. Some reports of reversible cerebral atrophy and pseudo-dementia with long-term use. Hyperammonemia causes acute cognitive decline | B |
| **Cardiac Effects** | Generally minimal direct cardiac effects. Rare reports of atrial fibrillation. Avoid in patients with urea cycle disorders | B |
| **Metabolic Effects** | Weight: significant gain (2-10kg; among highest of mood stabilizers). Glucose: may induce insulin resistance. Lipids: neutral to mild increase. Prolactin: minimal. Hair loss: common | A |
| **Washout (Neuromodulation)** | **5-7 days** (t1/2=9-16h) | A |
| **FDA Black Box Warnings** | **Hepatotoxicity** (fatal liver failure; children <2 years at highest risk). **Fetal risk** (pregnancy category X for migraine; neural tube defects). **Pancreatitis** (potentially fatal) | FDA |

**Key Notes:**
- Contraindicated in pregnancy for migraine; use in bipolar disorder only if no alternatives
- Monitor LFTs, ammonia levels (if encephalopathic symptoms), platelet count
- Drug interactions extensive (highly protein-bound, CYP enzyme interactions)

### 3.8 CARBAMAZEPINE

**Representative Agent:** Carbamazepine (Tegretol, Equetro)

| Domain | Profile | Evidence Grade |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **Common AEs (>5%)** | Dizziness (15-30%), drowsiness (15-30%), nausea (10-25%), ataxia, diplopia/blurred vision (10-20%), headache, dry mouth, constipation, rash (benign, 5-15%) | A |
| **Serious AEs (<5%)** | Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS) / Toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN): 1-6 per 10,000 in Caucasians; 10x higher in Asian patients (HLA-B*1502 screening required), agranulocytosis (~6 per million/year), aplastic anemia, hepatotoxicity, hyponatremia/SIADH (10-30%, elderly at risk), AV block, cardiac arrest | A |
| **Seizure Threshold** | **Increases** (antiepileptic mechanism) - Sodium channel blockade. Also used as mood stabilizer | A |
| **EEG Effects** | **Slowing** - Decreases alpha power, increases delta/theta power, increases beta power. Slows background EEG activity. Effects more pronounced than with other mood stabilizers | B |
| **Cognitive Effects** | Mild cognitive effects: slowed processing speed, attention deficits. Double vision and dizziness contribute to functional impairment. Effects generally milder than other AEDs | B |
| **Cardiac Effects** | AV conduction abnormalities, sinus node dysfunction, cardiorespiratory arrest (rare). Contraindicated in AV block or significant cardiac conduction disease. Auto-induction of metabolism within 2-4 weeks | A |
| **Metabolic Effects** | Weight: neutral to slight gain. Glucose: may decrease. Lipids: neutral. Hyponatremia: 10-30% (especially elderly). Prolactin: minimal. Auto-induces hepatic metabolism (CYP3A4) | A |
| **Washout (Neuromodulation)** | **7-14 days** (t1/2=12-17h initially, auto-induction reduces to 5-10h with chronic use) | A |
| **FDA Black Box Warnings** | **Serious dermatologic reactions (SJS/TEN)** - HLA-B*1502 screening required in Asian ancestry. **Aplastic anemia/agranulocytosis** - 5-8x general population risk. **Hepatotoxicity** (fatal cases reported). **AV block** | FDA |

**Key Notes:**
- Auto-induces hepatic metabolism: dose must be increased after 2-4 weeks
- Hyponatremia common in elderly; monitor sodium levels
- Extensive drug interactions via CYP3A4 induction
- HLA-B*1502 screening mandatory in patients of Asian ancestry

### 3.9 LAMOTRIGINE

**Representative Agent:** Lamotrigine (Lamictal)

| Domain | Profile | Evidence Grade |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **Common AEs (>5%)** | Dizziness (20-40%), headache (20-30%), diplopia/blurred vision (10-25%), nausea (10-20%), ataxia, tremor, somnolence, insomnia, rash (benign, 5-10%) | A |
| **Serious AEs (<5%)** | Stevens-Johnson syndrome / TEN (risk ~0.1-0.3% in adults; higher in children; dose-dependent, decreased with slow titration), DRESS syndrome, hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH), aseptic meningitis (rare), blood dyscrasias (neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, aplastic anemia) | A |
| **Seizure Threshold** | **Increases** (antiepileptic mechanism) - Sodium channel blockade, glutamate inhibition. Also used as mood stabilizer for bipolar depression | A |
| **EEG Effects** | **Normalizing** - Decreases delta and theta power, decreases alpha and beta power. Sodium-channel blocker effects on gamma in 50-60 Hz range. Generally "cleaner" EEG profile compared to other AEDs | B |
| **Cognitive Effects** | **Favorable profile** - Among best-tolerated AEDs cognitively. Minimal cognitive effects. Some patients report improved cognitive function due to seizure control. Mild word-finding difficulty reported in some patients | A |
| **Cardiac Effects** | Generally minimal direct cardiac effects. No significant QT prolongation. Well-tolerated in cardiac patients | B |
| **Metabolic Effects** | Weight: neutral (weight-neutral mood stabilizer). Glucose: neutral. Lipids: neutral. Prolactin: minimal. Hair loss: rare | A |
| **Washout (Neuromodulation)** | **7-14 days** (t1/2=25-30h; 60h with valproate co-administration; 12h with carbamazepine co-administration) | A |
| **FDA Black Box Warnings** | **Serious skin rashes (SJS/TEN)** - Risk increased with rapid dose titration, valproate co-administration (inhibits metabolism), and in children <16 years. Risk ~0.3-0.8% in adults; 0.8% in pediatrics | FDA |

**Key Notes:**
- Slow titration essential to minimize SJS/TEN risk (START LOW, GO SLOW)
- Valproate inhibits lamotrigine metabolism (dose must be halved); carbamazepine induces metabolism (dose must be doubled)
- Only mood stabilizer with proven efficacy for acute bipolar depression (monotherapy)
- Weight-neutral profile makes it preferred in patients concerned about weight gain

### 3.10 BENZODIAZEPINES

**Representative Agents:** Alprazolam, Lorazepam, Clonazepam, Diazepam, Temazepam, Midazolam

| Domain | Profile | Evidence Grade |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **Common AEs (>5%)** | Sedation/drowsiness (20-50%), dizziness (10-20%), cognitive impairment (10-30%), ataxia (5-15%), confusion (elderly, 5-20%), memory impairment/amnesia (anterograde, 5-20%), dysarthria, headache, fatigue | A |
| **Serious AEs (<5%)** | Respiratory depression (especially with opioids/alcohol), dependence/tolerance, withdrawal seizures, delirium (elderly), falls/hip fractures (elderly), paradoxical disinhibition/aggression, sleep-driving/sleep-eating (z-drugs) | A |
| **Seizure Threshold** | **Increases** at therapeutic doses (anticonvulsant mechanism); **decreases** with withdrawal/tolerance. Abrupt discontinuation can precipitate status epilepticus. Paradoxical seizures rare | A |
| **EEG Effects** | **Beta increase, alpha decrease, potential epileptiform activation** - Increases fast beta activity (12-30 Hz), decreases alpha. Can produce epileptiform discharges in some patients. Chronic use associated with diffuse beta enhancement that may obscure underlying EEG abnormalities | B |
| **Cognitive Effects** | **Significant impairment** - Dose-dependent deficits in: immediate memory (RBANS: beta -4.98, p<0.001), attention (RBANS: beta -6.81, p<0.001), reaction time, psychomotor speed, executive function. Impairment greatest with long-acting agents. Recovery after discontinuation may take weeks to months. Elderly: delirium risk | A |
| **Cardiac Effects** | Minimal direct cardiac effects. Lorazepam/diazepam: mild BP lowering. No QT prolongation. IV midazolam: respiratory depression > cardiac effects | A |
| **Metabolic Effects** | Weight: generally neutral. Glucose: neutral. Lipids: neutral. Prolactin: minimal | B |

- Withdrawal can be life-threatening (seizures, delirium tremens-like syndrome)

## 5. TOP 10 HIGHEST-RISK ADVERSE EFFECT PROFILES

### Profile #1: CLOZAPINE - The "Metabolic-Neuro-Hematologic" Risk
**Overall Risk Score: CRITICAL**

Clozapine carries the most complex and dangerous adverse effect profile of any psychiatric medication:

- **Hematologic:** Agranulocytosis (1% incidence) requiring mandatory REMS monitoring with weekly-to-biweekly ANC checks
- **Metabolic:** Greatest weight gain (+3.0 kg), dyslipidemia, and glucose elevation of all antipsychotics; diabetes and metabolic syndrome common
- **Neurologic:** Highest seizure risk of any antipsychotic (3-5%, dose-dependent); marked EEG slowing with increased delta/theta
- **Cardiac:** Myocarditis (rare but fatal); dose-independent eosinophilic myocarditis
- **Other:** Sialorrhea (drooling), constipation (can lead to ileus), sedation, orthostatic hypotension
- **FDA Black Box Warnings:** 4 separate warnings (agranulocytosis, myocarditis, dementia-related mortality, seizures/GI hypomotility)
- **Evidence Grade: A**

### Profile #2: BUPROPION - The "Seizure Threshold" Risk
**Overall Risk Score: HIGH**

Bupropion's seizure risk is the highest of all antidepressants and increases dramatically with dose:

- **Seizures:** 0.1% (<300mg), 0.4% (300-450mg), 2.3% (600mg), 2.8% (600-900mg) - exponential dose-response
- **Contraindications:** Seizure disorders, eating disorders (bulimia/anorexia prevalence 1-3%), abrupt alcohol/benzodiazepine withdrawal, CNS tumors, head trauma
- **Cardiac (overdose):** QRS widening (gap junction inhibition), QT prolongation, cardiogenic shock
- **Psychiatric:** Agitation, anxiety, insomnia (30%); psychosis at high doses
- **FDA Black Box Warnings:** Suicidality, neuropsychiatric symptoms (smoking cessation)
- **Evidence Grade: A**

### Profile #3: TCAs (ESPECIALLY AMITRIPTYLINE/DESIPRAMINE) - The "Cardiac-Overdose-Anticholinergic" Risk
**Overall Risk Score: HIGH**

TCAs represent the greatest lethality in overdose and significant anticholinergic burden:

- **Overdose mortality:** Highest fatality index of any antidepressant class. QRS >100ms predicts seizures; >160ms predicts fatal arrhythmias. 10-20 mg/kg potentially fatal
- **Cardiac:** Therapeutic doses cause orthostatic hypotension, tachycardia, conduction delays. Overdose: complete heart block, ventricular arrhythmias, TdP
- **Cognitive:** Severe anticholinergic impairment (memory, attention, reaction time); delirium risk in elderly
- **Seizures:** ~1% incidence at therapeutic doses; higher in overdose
- **Other:** Urinary retention, narrow-angle glaucoma, falls, constipation
- **Evidence Grade: A**

### Profile #4: LITHIUM - The "Narrow Therapeutic Index" Risk
**Overall Risk Score: HIGH**

Lithium's narrow therapeutic window creates pervasive organ toxicity:

- **Therapeutic index:** 0.6-1.0 mEq/L (therapeutic); >1.5 (toxic); >2.0 (potentially fatal)
- **Renal:** Chronic interstitial nephritis (1-5% long-term), nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (20-40%), progressive renal failure requiring dialysis
- **Endocrine:** Hypothyroidism (5-30%; 5-fold increased risk), hyperparathyroidism with hypercalcemia
- **Cognitive:** "Lithium fog" - memory impairment, processing speed reduction, decreased creativity
- **Teratogenicity:** Ebstein anomaly (cardiac malformation); contraindicated in pregnancy
- **Other:** Tremor (30-50%), polyuria, weight gain, acne
- **FDA Black Box Warnings:** Teratogenicity, lithium toxicity
- **Evidence Grade: A**

### Profile #5: VALPROATE - The "Teratogenic-Hepatotoxic" Risk
**Overall Risk Score: HIGH**

Valproate combines severe teratogenicity with multi-organ toxicity:

- **Teratogenicity:** Neural tube defects (spina bifida risk 1-2%); craniofacial, cardiovascular, genitourinary malformations. **FDA Pregnancy Category X for migraine.** Avoid in women of childbearing potential
- **Hepatotoxicity:** Fatal liver failure, especially in children <2 years (risk 1:500); rare in adults but potentially fatal
- **Hyperammonemia:** Encephalopathy (confusion, seizures, coma, death) without transaminase elevation; more common with polytherapy
- **Pancreatitis:** Rare (1:50,000) but potentially fatal; can occur without warning
- **Other:** Weight gain (significant), hair loss, tremor, thrombocytopenia
- **FDA Black Box Warnings:** Hepatotoxicity, teratogenicity, pancreatitis
- **Evidence Grade: A**

### Profile #6: CARBAMAZEPINE - The "Dermatologic-Hematologic-Genetic" Risk
**Overall Risk Score: HIGH**

Carbamazepine's life-threatening skin reactions and hematologic toxicity require vigilant monitoring:

- **SJS/TEN:** 1-6 per 10,000 in Caucasians; 10x higher in Asian patients. **HLA-B*1502 screening mandatory** in patients of Asian ancestry
- **Hematologic:** Agranulocytosis (~6 per million/year); aplastic anemia; pancytopenia. WBC monitoring required
- **Hepatotoxicity:** Hepatitis, hepatic failure (fatal cases reported)
- **Cardiac:** AV block, sinus node dysfunction, cardiorespiratory arrest. Contraindicated in cardiac conduction disease
- **Hyponatremia:** SIADH in 10-30%, especially elderly; sodium monitoring required
- **FDA Black Box Warnings:** SJS/TEN, agranulocytosis/aplastic anemia, hepatotoxicity, AV block
- **Evidence Grade: A**

### Profile #7: OLANZAPINE/CLOZAPINE - The "Metabolic Syndrome" Risk
**Overall Risk Score: HIGH**

Second-generation antipsychotics with the worst metabolic profiles cause accelerated cardiovascular disease:

- **Weight gain:** Olanzapine (+2.5-4.0 kg), Clozapine (+3.0 kg) over 6 weeks; rapid onset in first 4-9 weeks
- **Glucose:** Clozapine increases fasting glucose by 1.05 mmol/L; risk of diabetic ketoacidosis even without weight gain
- **Lipids:** Olanzapine increases LDL cholesterol, triglycerides, total cholesterol. Clozapine: worst lipid profile of all antipsychotics
- **Cardiovascular:** For every 1 kg weight gain, CVD risk increases 3.1%. Combined metabolic effects create substantial CVD burden
- **Prolactin:** Olanzapine has moderate effect; less than risperidone
- **FDA Black Box Warnings:** Dementia-related mortality (class warning)
- **Evidence Grade: A**

### Profile #8: BENZODIAZEPINES - The "Dependence-Cognitive-Respiratory" Risk
**Overall Risk Score: MODERATE-HIGH**

Benzodiazepines cause profound cognitive impairment and physical dependence:

- **Cognitive impairment:** Most cognitively impairing drug class. Immediate memory (RBANS beta -4.98, p<0.001), attention (beta -6.81, p<0.001), reaction time, executive function all impaired. Recovery may take months after discontinuation
- **Dependence:** Tolerance develops within 2-4 weeks; withdrawal can cause seizures, delirium, death
- **Respiratory depression:** Profound when combined with opioids/alcohol (FDA black box warning). Risk in COPD, sleep apnea, elderly
- **Falls/Fractures:** 2-3x increased fall risk in elderly; hip fractures. Major contributor to geriatric morbidity/mortality
- **Paradoxical reactions:** Disinhibition, agitation, aggression (especially in elderly and children)
- **FDA Black Box Warnings:** Opioid coadministration (respiratory depression, coma, death)
- **Evidence Grade: A**

### Profile #9: STIMULANTS - The "Cardiovascular-Psychiatric-Dependence" Risk
**Overall Risk Score: MODERATE-HIGH**

CNS stimulants carry risks of cardiac events, psychiatric destabilization, and abuse:

- **Cardiovascular:** BP elevation (2-5 mmHg systolic), HR increase (3-10 bpm); rare sudden death with structural heart disease. Screen for cardiac history before prescribing
- **Psychiatric:** Psychosis, mania, aggression (rare but serious); anxiety, irritability (common). Can exacerbate underlying bipolar disorder
- **Dependence/misuse:** All Schedule II controlled substances; diversion common. Misuse: up to 30% of college students report non-medical use
- **Seizures:** Rare at therapeutic doses; overdose causes seizures
- **Growth suppression:** Controversial evidence for height/weight suppression in children
- **FDA Black Box Warnings:** Abuse and dependence, sudden death/cardiovascular events
- **Evidence Grade: A**

### Profile #10: TOPiramate - The "Cognitive-Metabolic Acidosis-Nephrolithiasis" Risk
**Overall Risk Score: MODERATE-HIGH**

Topiramate's cognitive toxicity is uniquely severe among mood stabilizers:

- **Cognitive impairment:** Nicknamed "Dopamax" for cognitive "dumbing down." 41% of patients experience CNS side effects: mental slowing, word-finding difficulty, concentration deficits, confusion. Most common reason for discontinuation
- **Metabolic acidosis:** Dose-dependent reduction in serum bicarbonate; can require supplementation. Risk of osteopenia with long-term use
- **Nephrolithiasis:** 1.5% incidence (calcium phosphate stones); 2-4x general population risk. Requires adequate hydration
- **Ocular:** Acute myopia, secondary angle-closure glaucoma (rare, <1%); requires immediate discontinuation
- **Weight loss:** Paradoxically beneficial for weight but can be excessive
- **FDA Black Box Warnings:** Fetal toxicity (oral clefts), hyperammonemia with valproate
- **Evidence Grade: A**

## 6. NEUROMODULATION WASHOUT REFERENCE

### 6.1 BEFORE TRANSCRANIAL MAGNETIC STIMULATION (TMS)

| Drug Class | Recommended Washout | Rationale |
|------------|-------------------|-----------|
| SSRIs (except fluoxetine) | 5-7 days | Serotonergic effects may modulate cortical excitability |
| Fluoxetine | 5-6 weeks | Long half-life (t1/2=1-3 days) and active metabolite norfluoxetine (t1/2=4-16 days) |
| SNRIs | 7 days | Norepinephrine effects on cortical excitability |
| TCAs | 7-14 days | Anticholinergic sedation; sodium channel effects |
| MAOIs | 14 days minimum | Irreversible enzyme inhibition; risk of serotonin syndrome |
| Atypical Antipsychotics | 3-14 days (agent-specific) | Variable receptor profiles; dopamine blockade affects plasticity |
| Lithium | 5-7 days | Narrow therapeutic index; affects neuronal excitability |
| Valproate | 5-7 days | GABA enhancement; affects cortical excitability |
| Carbamazepine | 7-14 days | Sodium channel blockade; enzyme induction |
| Lamotrigine | 7-14 days | Sodium channel blockade |
| Benzodiazepines | 3-14 days (agent-specific) | GABA-A enhancement; sedation |
| Stimulants | 3-5 days | Catecholamine effects on cortical excitability |
| Bupropion | 5-7 days | Seizure risk; dopamine/norepinephrine effects |
| Mirtazapine | 5-7 days | H1 sedation; serotonergic effects |
| Pregabalin/Gabapentin | 3-5 days | Calcium channel effects; sedation |
| Topiramate | 5-7 days | Carbonic anhydrase inhibition; cognitive effects |

### 6.2 BEFORE ELECTROCONVULSIVE THERAPY (ECT)

| Drug Class | Recommended Management | Rationale |
|------------|----------------------|-----------|
| All antidepressants | Generally continue | Protects against cognitive side effects; no need for washout |
| Benzodiazepines | Reduce/withdraw if possible | Prolongs seizure threshold; may shorten seizure duration |
| Anticonvulsants | Reduce/withdraw if clinically appropriate | Raise seizure threshold; may prevent adequate seizure |
| Lithium | Discontinue 48-72h before ECT | Risk of prolonged seizures, delirium, NMS-like syndrome |
| Antipsychotics | Generally continue | May provide additional therapeutic benefit |
| Stimulants | Hold day of treatment | Cardiovascular stress |

### 7.2 KEY FAERS PHARMACOVIGILANCE FINDINGS

**Antidepressant-Seizure Association (2025 FAERS Analysis):**
- 7,393 seizure events reported with antidepressants
- Female predominance (54.5% vs 30.6% male)
- Peak age: 18-65 years (49.5%)
- Top seizure RORs: Bupropion 8.63 > Amitriptyline 4.65 > Imipramine 3.38 > Venlafaxine 2.98 > Mirtazapine 2.96 > Escitalopram 2.72

**Antipsychotic-Metabolic Signal (Network Meta-Analysis, 100 RCTs):**
- 25,952 patients analyzed
- Clozapine and olanzapine ranked worst across all metabolic parameters
- Aripiprazole, brexpiprazole, cariprazine, lurasidone ranked best
- Clinical improvement correlated with metabolic worsening (r=0.36-0.84)
