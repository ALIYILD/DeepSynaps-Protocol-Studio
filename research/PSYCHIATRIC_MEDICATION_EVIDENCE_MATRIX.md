## 1. Executive Summary & Clinical Takeaways

### Overview

Psychiatric medications are frequently co-prescribed with neuromodulation interventions. Understanding drug-neuromodulation interactions is essential for patient safety, protocol optimization, and clinical efficacy. This matrix synthesizes evidence across 12 major psychiatric drug classes and their interactions with tDCS, tACS, tRNS, rTMS, ECT, VNS, tFUS, DBS, and neurofeedback.

### Top 10 Clinical Takeaways

1. **SSRIs are the safest antidepressant class for combination with neuromodulation.** They synergize with rTMS (meta-analysis: A-grade evidence) and have minimal seizure risk. Escitalopram and sertraline show the strongest augmentation data.

2. **Bupropion is NOT a contraindication to rTMS.** Despite historical caution, systematic review of 25 rTMS-induced seizures found zero cases in bupropion-only patients. Doses <400mg XR are generally safe; remeasure motor threshold if dose changes.

3. **Benzodiazepines impair ECT efficacy.** They raise seizure threshold and shorten seizure duration. Hold lorazepam >10 hours before ECT; use flumazenil reversal if necessary.

4. **Anticonvulsants counteract ECT.** Valproate, carbamazepine, and lamotrigine significantly raise seizure threshold. Withhold evening dose before ECT; delay morning dose until after recovery.

5. **Lithium + ECT = delirium risk.** Combined lithium and ECT increases risk of prolonged confusion, delirium, and prolonged seizures. Hold lithium the evening before and morning of ECT; maintain lowest effective level.

6. **Clozapine has the highest seizure risk of any psychiatric medication.** Risk reaches 3.5-5% at doses >600mg/day. In rTMS schizophrenia populations, seizure prevalence was 3.49/1000 (not significantly different from baseline). Valproate co-prescription is recommended for seizure prophylaxis.

7. **TCAs lower seizure threshold and have anticholinergic/cardiac effects.** They are Category 1 (strong potential hazard) for rTMS. In ECT, TCAs may paradoxically improve outcomes but require cardiac monitoring.

8. **tDCS + benzodiazepines = worse outcomes.** Naturalistic study (n=82) found benzodiazepine use independently associated with worse tDCS response (beta=5.8, p<0.01). Dual-reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs/TCAs) showed enhanced tDCS effects.

9. **Antidepressants as a class lower resting motor threshold.** A neurosurgical study (n=642) found antidepressants decreased RMT (estimate=-3.24, p=0.019), while anticonvulsants and benzodiazepines increased RMT. This has practical implications for rTMS intensity calibration.

10. **Stimulants (methylphenidate, amphetamines) show efficacy without significant seizure risk increase.** Atomoxetine has the lowest seizure risk among ADHD medications. Category 1 (caution) for rTMS per safety guidelines.

## 2. SSRIs (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors)

### 2.1 Key Medications & Brand Names

| Generic | Brand Names | FDA Indications | Typical Dose Range |
|---------|------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Fluoxetine | Prozac, Sarafem | MDD, OCD, panic, bulimia, PMDD | 20-80 mg/day |
| Sertraline | Zoloft | MDD, OCD, panic, PTSD, social anxiety, PMDD | 50-200 mg/day |
| Escitalopram | Lexapro, Cipralex | MDD, GAD | 10-20 mg/day |
| Citalopram | Celexa | MDD | 20-40 mg/day |
| Paroxetine | Paxil, Pexeva, Brisdelle | MDD, OCD, panic, social anxiety, PTSD, GAD, PMDD | 20-60 mg/day |
| Fluvoxamine | Luvox | OCD | 100-300 mg/day |
| Vilazodone | Viibryd | MDD | 10-40 mg/day |
| Vortioxetine | Trintellix | MDD | 5-20 mg/day |

### 2.2 Mechanism of Action

**Primary Target:** Serotonin transporter (SERT/SLC6A4) inhibition at the presynaptic axon terminal
- Blocks reuptake of serotonin (5-HT) into presynaptic neurons
- Increases synaptic 5-HT concentration and prolongs postsynaptic receptor stimulation
- **Receptor targets:** 5-HT1A, 5-HT2A, 5-HT2C (downstream), minimal effect on NE/DA transporters
- **Adaptive changes:** Chronic administration desensitizes 5-HT1A/B autoreceptors, increases BDNF expression, enhances neuroplasticity
- **Key downstream cascade:** cAMP-PKA-CREB-BDNF signaling

### 2.3 Common Indications in Neuromodulation Context

- **rTMS adjunct:** MDD (first-line combination), OCD, anxiety disorders
- **tDCS adjunct:** MDD augmentation, bipolar depression
- **ECT continuation:** Relapse prevention post-acute ECT course
- **DBS adjunct:** Depression (VCNS/SCC targets), OCD

### 2.4 Seizure Threshold Effects

| Parameter | Data |
|-----------|------|
| Seizure risk (therapeutic dose) | 0.1-0.25% (general population) |
| Seizure risk relative to placebo | SIR = 0.48 (95% CI 0.36-0.61) — LOWER than placebo (Alper et al.) |
| Proconvulsant at overdose | Rare; less than TCAs |
| Evidence grade | A (meta-analysis) |

**Key Finding:** SSRIs may have anticonvulsant properties. Among people with epilepsy on SSRIs/SNRIs, 48% showed >50% reduction in seizure frequency.

### 2.5 rTMS Interaction Evidence

| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Synergy with rTMS | **Yes — additive benefit confirmed** |
| Meta-analysis (2024) | SSRIs + rTMS showed significant augmentation vs sham (SMD -0.65 to -0.79 for sertraline/escitalopram) |
| Mechanism | SSRIs enhance cortical excitability; rTMS potentiates this via greater neuroplastic effects than SNRIs |
| Motor threshold effect | Minimal effect on RMT (fluoxetine, sertraline); citalopram may slightly increase RMT |
| Recommendation | **Continue SSRI during rTMS — synergistic** |
| Evidence grade | A (meta-analysis, n=71 studies) |

### 2.6 tDCS Interaction Evidence

| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Synergy with tDCS | Combined tDCS + medication associated with improvement in depression (systematic review) |
| Specific interaction | Dual-reuptake inhibitors (venlafaxine, duloxetine) may positively modulate tDCS effects |
| Safety | No specific contraindications |
| Recommendation | **Continue SSRI during tDCS** |
| Evidence grade | B (RCTs) |

### 2.7 ECT Interaction Evidence

| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Seizure duration effect | Minimal effect on ECT seizure duration |
| Safety | Generally safe; rare serotonin syndrome reported with paroxetine/fluoxetine |
| Efficacy | Continuation SSRIs post-ECT reduce relapse rates |
| Discontinuation | Gradual taper to avoid discontinuation syndrome (fluoxetine lowest risk due to long half-life) |
| Specific caution | Venlafaxine >300mg/day: possible increased asystole risk |
| Recommendation | **Continue SSRIs during ECT; use for relapse prevention** |
| Evidence grade | B (RCTs) |

### 2.8 Key References

- PMID: 38526472 (Antidepressant class + concurrent rTMS: systematic review & meta-analysis, 2024)
- PMID: 23182847 (Brunoni et al., tDCS-medication interactions: naturalistic study, 2013)
- Alper et al. (Standardized Incidence Ratio analysis, antidepressants vs placebo)
- Rossi S et al., Clin Neurophysiol 2021;132:269-306 (IFCN safety guidelines)

## 3. SNRIs (Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors)

### 3.1 Key Medications

| Generic | Brand | FDA Indications | Dose Range |
|---------|-------|----------------|------------|
| Venlafaxine | Effexor XR | MDD, GAD, panic, social anxiety | 75-225 mg/day |
| Desvenlafaxine | Pristiq, Khedezla | MDD | 50 mg/day |
| Duloxetine | Cymbalta | MDD, GAD, fibromyalgia, chronic musculoskeletal pain, diabetic neuropathy | 30-120 mg/day |
| Levomilnacipran | Fetzima | MDD | 20-120 mg/day |
| Milnacipran | Savella | Fibromyalgia | 100-200 mg/day |

### 3.2 Mechanism of Action

- **Primary:** Dual inhibition of serotonin (SERT) and norepinephrine (NET) reuptake
- **Dose-dependent effects:** Venlafaxine — 30x higher affinity for 5-HT reuptake at low doses; NE reuptake inhibition emerges at higher doses (>​150mg); weak DA reuptake inhibition at very high doses
- **Receptor targets:** 5-HT2A, 5-HT2C, alpha-2 adrenergic (variable), minimal anticholinergic/antihistaminic effects

### 3.3 Common Indications

- MDD with comorbid chronic pain (duloxetine)
- Anxiety disorders with depression
- rTMS/tDCS augmentation (dual mechanism may enhance neuroplasticity)

### 3.4 Seizure Threshold Effects

| Parameter | Data |
|-----------|------|
| Seizure risk | 0.2-0.3% (duloxetine, venlafaxine) |
| Venlafaxine >300mg/day | Increased risk; possible asystole with ECT |
| Relative risk | Low to moderate |
| Evidence grade | B (RCT data) |

### 3.5 rTMS Interaction Evidence

- **Meta-analysis finding:** SNRIs did NOT show additive clinical benefits to rTMS beyond medication alone (unlike SSRIs)
- **Mechanistic hypothesis:** SNRIs lack the GABAergic initial effect and neuroplastic enhancement seen with SSRIs
- **RMT effect:** Minimal to no effect on resting motor threshold
- **Recommendation:** May continue; consider switching to SSRI if rTMS response is suboptimal
- **Evidence grade:** A (meta-analysis)

### 3.6 tDCS Interaction Evidence

- Naturalistic study: dual-reuptake inhibitors positively changed tDCS effects (beta=-4.7, p=0.02)
- **Recommendation:** Continue; may enhance tDCS response
- **Evidence grade:** C (observational)

### 3.7 ECT Interaction Evidence

- Venlafaxine XR: evidence-based for relapse prevention post-ECT (Sackeim et al., 2009)
- Venlafaxine >300mg/day: possible increased asystole risk during ECT
- Generally safe with ECT; minimal effect on seizure duration
- **Recommendation:** Continue; preferred for continuation therapy post-ECT
- **Evidence grade:** B (RCT)

### 3.8 Key References

- PMID: 38526472 (rTMS + antidepressant class meta-analysis, 2024)
- PMID: 23182847 (tDCS medication interactions, Brunoni 2013)
- Sackeim et al., JAMA 2009 (ECT continuation pharmacotherapy)

## 4. TCAs (Tricyclic Antidepressants)

### 4.1 Key Medications

| Generic | Brand | Classification | Dose Range |
|---------|-------|---------------|------------|
| Amitriptyline | Elavil | Tertiary amine | 25-150 mg/day |
| Clomipramine | Anafranil | Tertiary amine | 25-250 mg/day |
| Imipramine | Tofranil | Tertiary amine | 25-200 mg/day |
| Doxepin | Silenor, Sinequan | Tertiary amine | 25-150 mg/day |
| Nortriptyline | Pamelor, Aventyl | Secondary amine | 25-150 mg/day |
| Desipramine | Norpramin | Secondary amine | 25-200 mg/day |
| Trimipramine | Surmontil | Tertiary amine | 50-200 mg/day |

### 4.2 Mechanism of Action

- **Primary:** Inhibition of serotonin AND norepinephrine reuptake (tertiary amines > serotonin; secondary amines > norepinephrine)
- **Secondary (off-target):**
  - Muscarinic cholinergic antagonism (anticholinergic effects: dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, blurred vision, cognitive impairment)
  - Histamine H1 antagonism (sedation, weight gain)
  - Alpha-1 adrenergic antagonism (orthostatic hypotension)
  - Cardiac Na+ channel blockade (QT prolongation, conduction delays)

### 4.3 Common Indications

- Treatment-resistant depression (second-line)
- Neuropathic pain (amitriptyline, nortriptyline)
- Migraine prophylaxis
- OCD (clomipramine)
- ECT augmentation

### 4.4 Seizure Threshold Effects

| Parameter | Data |
|-----------|------|
| Seizure risk (therapeutic) | 0.1-0.4% |
| Clomipramine >300mg/day | 2.1% seizure risk |
| Clomipramine <250mg/day | 0.48% seizure risk |
| Clomipramine SIR | 4.08 (95% CI 2.64-6.02) vs placebo — HIGHEST of all antidepressants |
| Overdose | HIGH seizure risk; cardiotoxic (arrhythmias) |
| Evidence grade | A (meta-analysis) |

**TCAs are the most proconvulsant antidepressant class at therapeutic doses.**

### 4.5 rTMS Interaction Evidence

| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Safety classification | Category 1 (STRONG potential hazard per safety guidelines) |
| Mechanism concern | Na+ channel blockade + anticholinergic effects increase seizure risk |
| Clinical data | TCAs associated with decreased RMT in some studies |
| Recommendation | **Relative contraindication for high-frequency rTMS**; if used, exercise extreme caution with HF protocols; consider LF rTMS or tDCS instead |
| Evidence grade | C (observational, expert consensus) |

### 4.6 tDCS Interaction Evidence

- Naturalistic study: trend toward positive tDCS effects (beta=-4.0, p=0.06)
- **Recommendation:** Generally compatible; monitor for side effects
- **Evidence grade:** C (observational)

### 4.7 ECT Interaction Evidence

- **Paradoxical finding:** TCAs + ECT associated with BETTER outcomes and SHORTER total seizure time than ECT alone
- Nortriptyline: effective for continuation therapy post-ECT (Navarro et al. RCT)
- Cardiac monitoring essential due to sympathomimetic surge during ECT
- **Recommendation:** Continue with cardiac monitoring; reduce dose to minimize hypertensive response; avoid in elderly/cardiac patients
- **Evidence grade:** B (RCT data)

### 4.8 Key References

- PMID: 32491723 (TCAs: StatPearls review, 2023)
- PMID: 23182847 (tDCS medication interactions)
- PMID: 3178101 (Continuation ECT review)

## 5. MAOIs (Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors)

### 5.1 Key Medications

| Generic | Brand | Type | Selectivity | Dose Range |
|---------|-------|------|-------------|------------|
| Phenelzine | Nardil | Irreversible | Nonselective (MAO-A>B) | 15-90 mg/day |
| Tranylcypromine | Parnate | Irreversible | Nonselective | 10-60 mg/day |
| Isocarboxazid | Marplan | Irreversible | Nonselective | 10-60 mg/day |
| Selegiline (transdermal) | Emsam | Irreversible at high doses | MAO-B at low; MAO-A+B at high | 6-12 mg/24hr patch |
| Moclobemide | Aurorix, Manerix | Reversible | MAO-A selective | 300-600 mg/day |

### 5.2 Mechanism of Action

- **Primary:** Inhibition of monoamine oxidase (MAO), the mitochondrial enzyme that degrades serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine
- **MAO-A:** Degrades 5-HT, NE, DA (primary target for antidepressant effect)
- **MAO-B:** Degrades DA, phenylethylamine (relevant for Parkinson's)
- **Result:** Increased presynaptic monoamine stores; enhanced synaptic availability
- **Dietary restriction required:** Tyramine-containing foods (hypertensive crisis risk)

### 5.3 Common Indications

- Treatment-resistant depression (TRD)
- Atypical depression (reversed vegetative signs)
- Parkinson's disease (selegiline, rasagiline)
- Social anxiety disorder

### 5.4 Seizure Threshold Effects

- Seizure risk: Low to moderate (0.2-0.4% range)
- No significant seizure threshold lowering at therapeutic doses
- Hypertensive crisis is the primary acute risk (not seizure)

### 5.5 rTMS Interaction Evidence

- Minimal direct interaction data available
- Theoretical concern: enhanced monoaminergic tone may interact with rTMS-induced neuroplasticity
- **Recommendation:** No specific contraindication; standard rTMS protocols appropriate
- **Evidence grade:** D (expert opinion)

### 5.6 tDCS Interaction Evidence

- No specific interaction data available
- **Recommendation:** No known contraindication
- **Evidence grade:** D (expert opinion)

### 5.7 ECT Interaction Evidence

| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Safety | No evidence of adverse interaction |
| Prospective study | No differences between MAOI + ECT vs controls |
| Anesthetic interaction | Avoid ketamine (sympathetic stimulation); propofol and etomidate safe |
| Hypertensive crisis | Avoid indirect sympathomimetics |
| Washout | No washout period needed before ECT |
| Recommendation | **Continue MAOI during ECT if therapeutically beneficial** |
| Evidence grade | B (prospective studies) |

### 5.8 Key References

- PMID: 34384943 (Carbamazepine/valproate ECT interaction)
- PMID: 4844444 (Medication management during ECT)

## 7. Atypical Antipsychotics (Second-Generation Antipsychotics — SGA)

### 7.1 Key Medications

| Generic | Brand | Primary Receptor Targets | Dose Range |
|---------|-------|-------------------------|------------|
| Aripiprazole | Abilify | D2 partial agonist, 5-HT1A partial agonist, 5-HT2A antagonist | 5-30 mg/day |
| Olanzapine | Zyprexa | 5-HT2A/D2 antagonist, H1, M1, alpha-1 | 5-20 mg/day |
| Quetiapine | Seroquel | 5-HT2A/D2 antagonist, H1, alpha-1 | 50-800 mg/day |
| Risperidone | Risperdal | D2/5-HT2A antagonist, alpha-1, alpha-2 | 2-8 mg/day |
| Clozapine | Clozaril | 5-HT2A/D4/M1/alpha-1 antagonist; weak D2 (Ki=75nM) | 25-900 mg/day |
| Ziprasidone | Geodon | 5-HT2A/D2 antagonist, 5-HT1A agonist | 40-160 mg/day |
| Lurasidone | Latuda | D2/5-HT2A antagonist, 5-HT7 antagonist | 20-120 mg/day |
| Asenapine | Saphris | 5-HT2A/D2 antagonist | 5-20 mg/day |

### 7.2 Mechanism of Action

- **Core mechanism:** Dopamine D2 receptor blockade (varying affinity) + serotonin 5-HT2A receptor antagonism
- **Atypical profile:** Lower D2 occupancy at therapeutic doses; faster dissociation from D2 receptors ("fast-off D2")
- **Additional targets:** 5-HT1A partial agonism (aripiprazole), 5-HT7 antagonism (lurasidone), H1 antagonism (olanzapine, quetiapine — sedation/weight gain), M1 antagonism (clozapine, olanzapine)
- **Antidepressant mechanism:** Enhanced PFC monoamine release via 5-HT2C antagonism; direct NE/5-HT reuptake inhibition (some agents)

### 7.3 Common Indications in Neuromodulation Context

- Schizophrenia (rTMS adjunct for negative symptoms, auditory hallucinations)
- Bipolar disorder (mania, maintenance; quetiapine/lurasidone for bipolar depression)
- rTMS-resistant depression (augmentation strategy)
- Clozapine-resistant schizophrenia (ECT augmentation)

### 7.4 Seizure Threshold Effects

| Drug | Seizure Risk | EEG Abnormalities |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Clozapine** | **3.5%** (premarketing); 1.3% (postmarketing, >5000 patients) | Dose-dependent: 1% (<300mg), 2.7% (300-600mg), 4.4% (600-900mg) |
| Olanzapine | 0.9% | Moderate |
| Quetiapine | 0.8% | Low to moderate |
| Risperidone | 0.3% | Low |
| Aripiprazole | 0.1-0.4% | None; may have anticonvulsant properties |
| Ziprasidone | 0.4% | Low |
| FGAs (chlorpromazine) | 0.5-0.9% | Moderate |

**Clozapine:** Seizure risk increases 0.7% per each 100mg above 300mg/day. Between 600-900mg/day, risk reaches 5%.

### 7.5 rTMS Interaction Evidence

| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Clozapine + rTMS | 285 patients on clozapine: 1 seizure (prevalence 3.49/1000, not significantly different from overall schizophrenia rTMS population of 1.39/1000) |
| Safety | rTMS appears safe in clozapine-treated patients; no additional seizure risk beyond clozapine's inherent risk |
| Motor threshold | Antipsychotics as a class may alter cortical excitability |
| Category | Category 2 (relative hazard) for clozapine, olanzapine, quetiapine |
| Recommendation | **Continue antipsychotic during rTMS**; clozapine NOT a contraindication |
| Evidence grade | A (systematic review + meta-analysis, schizophrenia rTMS safety) |

### 7.6 tDCS Interaction Evidence

- No specific drug-tDCS interaction data for antipsychotics
- tDCS studies in schizophrenia generally maintain baseline antipsychotic regimens
- **Recommendation:** Continue; no dose adjustment needed for tDCS
- **Evidence grade:** D (expert opinion)

### 7.7 ECT Interaction Evidence

| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Antipsychotic + ECT | Common and generally safe combination |
| Clozapine + ECT | SAFE — contrary to early concerns. Confusion reported but no absolute contraindication. Seizure duration may be increased in ~16.6%. Valproate prophylaxis recommended. |
| FGA + ECT | Phenothiazines (chlorpromazine) may cause prolonged seizures |
| Efficacy | Combination may enhance outcomes in psychotic depression |
| Recommendation | **Continue antipsychotics during ECT; essential for schizophrenia indications** |
| Evidence grade | B (RCTs, case series) |

## 9. Lithium

### 9.1 Key Information

| Parameter | Data |
|-----------|------|
| **Brand** | Eskalith, Lithobid, Priadel, Camcolit |
| **Class** | Mood stabilizer (first-line for bipolar disorder) |
| **FDA indications** | Bipolar I disorder (acute mania, maintenance), MDD augmentation |
| **Therapeutic range** | 0.6-1.2 mEq/L (acute); 0.6-0.8 mEq/L (maintenance) |
| **Dose range** | 600-2400 mg/day (divided doses) |

### 9.2 Mechanism of Action

- **Primary:** Inhibition of inositol monophosphatase (IMPase) and inositol polyphosphate 1-phosphatase
- **Inositol depletion hypothesis:** Reduces intracellular inositol; dampens phosphoinositide signaling cascade
- **GSK-3 beta inhibition:** Both direct (competitive with Mg2+) and indirect (via Akt phosphorylation)
- **Neuroprotective effects:** Increases BDNF, bcl-2 expression; promotes neurogenesis (dentate gyrus); inhibits apoptosis
- **Neurotransmitter effects:** Reduces glutamate excitotoxicity; increases GABA/glutamate ratio; modulates dopamine and serotonin signaling
- **Anti-inflammatory:** Reduces IL-6, TNF-alpha; inhibits IDO1/kynurenine pathway

### 9.3 Seizure Threshold Effects

| Parameter | Data |
|-----------|------|
| At therapeutic levels | May be PROTECTIVE against seizures |
| At toxic levels (>1.5 mEq/L) | Can CAUSE seizures (dose-dependent) |
| Seizure risk | Generally low at therapeutic doses |
| Evidence grade | B (clinical studies) |

### 9.4 rTMS Interaction Evidence

- No direct lithium-rTMS interaction studies identified
- Lithium's enhancement of BDNF and neuroplasticity may theoretically synergize with rTMS
- **Recommendation:** Continue; monitor lithium levels; no dose adjustment for rTMS
- **Evidence grade:** D (expert opinion)

### 9.5 tDCS Interaction Evidence

- tDCS trials in bipolar depression typically include patients on lithium
- No specific interaction reported

### 9.6 ECT Interaction Evidence (CRITICAL)

## 10. Anticonvulsant Mood Stabilizers

### 10.1 Key Medications

| Generic | Brand | Primary Mechanism | Mood Indication | Dose Range |
|---------|-------|------------------|----------------|------------|
| **Valproate/Valproic Acid** | Depakote, Depakene | Na+ channel blockade, T-type Ca2+ channel blockade, GABA-T inhibition, HDAC inhibition | Acute mania, mixed episodes, maintenance | 750-2000 mg/day |
| **Carbamazepine** | Tegretol, Carbatrol | Na+ channel blockade, L-type Ca2+ channel antagonism | Acute mania, maintenance (bipolar) | 400-1200 mg/day |
| **Oxcarbazepine** | Trileptal | Na+ channel blockade (K+ channel effect) | Bipolar maintenance (off-label) | 600-2400 mg/day |
| **Lamotrigine** | Lamictal | Na+ channel blockade, inhibition of glutamate release, Ca2+ channel modulation | Bipolar maintenance, bipolar depression | 25-400 mg/day |
| **Topiramate** | Topamax | Na+ channel blockade, AMPA/kainate antagonism, GABA-A enhancement | Bipolar maintenance (off-label) | 50-400 mg/day |

### 10.2 Mechanism Summary

- **Valproate:** Broad-spectrum; increases GABA synthesis (blocks GABA transaminase); blocks voltage-gated Na+ and T-type Ca2+ channels; inhibits histone deacetylase (HDAC) — epigenetic effects; NMDA receptor antagonism
- **Carbamazepine:** Voltage-gated Na+ channel blockade (stabilizes inactive state); L-type Ca2+ channel blockade; NMDA receptor antagonism
- **Lamotrigine:** Na+ channel blockade; inhibits glutamate release; voltage-gated Ca2+ channel antagonism (R, N, P/Q, T type); neuroprotective properties
- **All agents:** Generally increase GABAergic tone and/or decrease glutamatergic excitation

### 10.3 Common Indications

- Bipolar disorder (mania: valproate, carbamazepine; depression prevention: lamotrigine)
- Epilepsy comorbidity
- Augmentation in treatment-resistant depression
- Migraine prophylaxis (topiramate, valproate)
- PTSD, BPD (off-label)

### 10.4 Seizure Threshold Effects

| Parameter | Data |
|-----------|------|
| Effect | ANTI-convulsant — these drugs RAISE seizure threshold |
| Seizure risk | Protective (not proconvulsant) |
| RMT effect | INCREASES resting motor threshold (carbamazepine: +3.82%, p=0.014) |
| ECT effect | INCREASES seizure threshold (valproate: 177.7 mC; carbamazepine: 243.9 mC vs control 138.7 mC) |
| Evidence grade | A (established anticonvulsant efficacy) |

### 10.5 rTMS Interaction Evidence

| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Motor threshold | Anticonvulsants INCREASE RMT (raise cortical excitability threshold) |
| rTMS intensity | May need higher stimulation intensity to achieve equivalent effect |
| Paradoxical effect | Valproate + 1Hz LF-rTMS may convert inhibitory to excitatory effect (unique interaction) |
| Recommendation | Continue; remeasure RMT if dose changes; may need intensity adjustment |
| Evidence grade | B (clinical studies) |

### 10.6 tDCS Interaction Evidence

- No specific anticonvulsant-tDCS interaction data
- Theoretical consideration: increased GABA tone may reduce tDCS modulatory effects
- **Recommendation:** Continue; monitor response
- **Evidence grade:** D (expert opinion)

### 10.7 ECT Interaction Evidence (CRITICAL)

| Parameter | Valproate | Carbamazepine |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| Seizure threshold effect | Increases (moderate) | Increases (HIGHEST) |
| Mean initial threshold | 177.7 mC | 243.9 mC |
| vs Control | 138.7 mC | 138.7 mC |
| Seizure inhibition | Moderate | Strongest |

| Anesthetic interaction | Decreases propofol dose needed; may elevate VPA levels | Enzyme inducer — may reduce levels of other drugs |
| Course progression | ECT has anticonvulsant effect; may reduce VPA dose as course proceeds | Similar |

**General Management for ECT:**
1. Withdraw anticonvulsant before ECT course if used solely as antidepressant augmentation
2. In bipolar: balance manic relapse risk vs seizure adequacy
3. Withhold evening dose before each ECT; delay morning dose until recovery
4. Consider using etomidate or ketamine instead of propofol (less anticonvulsant)
5. Monitor seizure adequacy; adjust stimulus intensity upward

**Evidence grade:** B (prospective studies, clinical data)

## 11. Benzodiazepines

### 11.1 Key Medications

| Generic | Brand | Half-life | Dose Range | Primary Use |
|---------|-------|-----------|------------|-------------|
| Lorazepam | Ativan | 10-20 hr | 0.5-6 mg/day | Anxiety, acute agitation, seizure rescue |
| Clonazepam | Klonopin | 18-50 hr | 0.25-4 mg/day | Anxiety, panic, seizure disorder |
| Diazepam | Valium | 20-70 hr | 2-40 mg/day | Anxiety, muscle spasm, seizures, ECT premed |
| Alprazolam | Xanax | 11-16 hr | 0.25-4 mg/day | Panic disorder, GAD |
| Temazepam | Restoril | 8-15 hr | 7.5-30 mg | Insomnia |
| Clobazam | Onfi | 36-42 hr | 5-40 mg/day | Lennox-Gastaut, anxiety |
| Midazolam | Versed | 1.5-3 hr | IV: 1-5 mg | Procedural sedation, status epilepticus |

### 11.2 Mechanism of Action

- **Primary:** Positive allosteric modulation of GABA-A receptors
- **Binding site:** Benzodiazepine recognition site on GABA-A receptor (alpha + gamma subunits)
- **Effect:** Increases frequency of chloride channel opening in response to GABA binding
- **Result:** Enhanced GABAergic inhibition; hyperpolarization of postsynaptic neurons; reduced neuronal excitability
- **Differentiation:** Barbiturates increase duration of channel opening; BZDs increase frequency

### 11.3 Common Indications

- Anxiety disorders (GAD, panic, social anxiety)
- Insomnia (short-term)
- Seizure disorders (adjunctive; status epilepticus — IV lorazepam/midazolam/diazepam)
- Alcohol withdrawal
- Acute agitation
- Muscle spasticity
- ECT premedication (historical)

### 11.4 Seizure Threshold Effects

| Parameter | Data |
|-----------|------|
| Effect | STRONGLY ANTI-convulsant — raises seizure threshold significantly |
| Seizure risk | Protective (used to terminate seizures) |
| RMT effect | INCREASES resting motor threshold (estimate=+3.45, p=0.004) |
| Chronic use | More pronounced RMT elevation than acute use |
| Evidence grade | A (established anticonvulsant properties) |

### 11.5 rTMS Interaction Evidence

| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Motor threshold | Increases RMT; may reduce cortical excitability |
| rTMS efficacy | May theoretically reduce plasticity-related effects |
| Safety | No specific contraindication |
| Recommendation | Continue if chronic use; abrupt discontinuation may cause withdrawal seizures |
| Evidence grade | B (clinical studies on RMT) |

### 11.6 tDCS Interaction Evidence (IMPORTANT)

| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Brunoni naturalistic study (n=82) | Benzodiazepine use independently associated with **WORSE tDCS outcome** |
| Statistical finding | beta=4.92 (p<0.01) univariate; beta=5.8 (p<0.01) multivariate |
| Mechanism hypothesis | GABAergic enhancement may counteract tDCS-induced LTP-like effects |
| **Clinical implication** | Benzodiazepines may blunt tDCS antidepressant effects |
| Recommendation | **Consider tapering benzodiazepines before tDCS course** if clinically feasible |
| Evidence grade | C (observational) |

### 11.7 ECT Interaction Evidence (CRITICAL)

| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Effect on ECT | INCREASES seizure threshold; DECREASES seizure duration |
| Efficacy impact | May impair ECT efficacy, especially with unilateral ECT |
| Cognitive effects | May increase cognitive side effects when combined with ECT |
| Long-acting BZDs | Should be avoided; may cause treatment failure |
| Lorazepam | 0-3 mg/day withheld >10 hours before ECT has no relation to seizure threshold |
| Flumazenil | Can be used to temporarily reverse BZD effects before ECT |
| Z-drugs (zolpidem, zopiclone) | May also inhibit seizure activity |
| Management | 1. Stop BZD before ECT if possible (may need days for long-acting); 2. Use lorazepam at lowest dose, withhold >10hr before; 3. Consider flumazenil reversal; 4. Taper anesthesia dose |
| Evidence grade | B (clinical studies, RCTs) |

## 12. CNS Stimulants (ADHD Medications)

### 12.1 Key Medications

| Generic | Brand | Mechanism | Dose Range | Seizure Risk |
|---------|-------|-----------|------------|--------------|
| Methylphenidate | Ritalin, Concerta, Daytrana | DA/NE reuptake inhibition | 5-60 mg/day | Low-moderate |
| Dextroamphetamine | Dexedrine, Zenzedi | DA/NE release + reuptake inhibition | 5-40 mg/day | Low-moderate |
| Mixed amphetamine salts | Adderall, Adderall XR | DA/NE release + reuptake inhibition | 5-40 mg/day | Low-moderate |

| Clonidine XR | Kapvay | Alpha-2A agonist | 0.1-0.4 mg/day | Negligible |

### 12.2 Mechanism of Action

- **Methylphenidate:** Blocks DA (SLC6A3) and NE (SLC6A2) transporters; increases extracellular DA and NE in PFC and striatum; 2200x greater affinity for DAT vs SERT; 1300x greater affinity for NET vs SERT
- **Amphetamines:** Reverse transport of DA/NE via DAT/NET; increase release + block reuptake; more potent than methylphenidate
- **Atomoxetine:** Selective NE reuptake inhibitor (minimal DA effect in PFC)
- **Alpha-2A agonists:** Enhance prefrontal cortical function via postsynaptic alpha-2A receptor stimulation

### 12.3 Common Indications

- ADHD (primary)
- Narcolepsy
- Treatment-resistant depression (augmentation)
- Cognitive enhancement (controversial)
- Binge eating disorder (lisdexamfetamine)

### 12.4 Seizure Threshold Effects
