## 1. Evidence Grading Legend

| Grade | Definition | Criteria |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **A** | Strong Evidence | Multiple RCTs, meta-analyses, or systematic reviews with consistent findings; clinical utility established |
| **B** | Moderate Evidence | Some RCTs, cohort studies, or meta-analyses with generally supportive but heterogeneous findings |
| **C** | Limited Evidence | Small studies, case series, or single-center trials; preliminary findings requiring replication |
| **D** | Insufficient / Controversial | Inconsistent findings, methodological limitations, or active scientific debate; not ready for clinical use |

### Clinical-Use Status Definitions

| Status | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **FDA-Cleared** | FDA 510(k) clearance or PMA approval for specific diagnostic indication |
| **Clinical Adjunct** | Supported by professional guidelines as adjunct to clinical evaluation (not standalone) |
| **Research Tool** | Evidence supports research use only; not yet validated for clinical decision-making |
| **Experimental** | Early-stage investigation; insufficient evidence for clinical or research standardization |

## 2. ADHD

### 2.1 Theta/Beta Ratio (TBR)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Theta (4-8 Hz) / Beta (13-21 Hz) power ratio at Cz electrode |
| **Description** | Elevated TBR reflects cortical hypoarousal; most extensively studied qEEG biomarker for ADHD |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** (Moderate) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | FDA-Cleared (NEBA device, 2013) - adjunctive use only; AAN Level R recommendation |
| **Research-Only** | No - FDA cleared, but with significant restrictions |
| **Sensitivity** | 86-90% (Monastra et al. multicenter study) |
| **Specificity** | 94-98% vs. normal controls |

**Key Findings:**
- Meta-analyses support elevated TBR in ADHD vs. controls with medium-to-large effect sizes
- 2024 meta-analysis (Boxum et al., N=417) found TBR has limited diagnostic value due to ADHD neurophysiological heterogeneity; identified subgroups (non-slow-alpha TBR, slow alpha peak frequency) with no association between EEG subtype and behavioral traits
- TBR is non-specific: elevated TBR also found in anxiety disorders, depression, learning disabilities, and head injury
- AAN Practice Advisory (2016): Level B recommendation - "should not replace standard clinical evaluation"; "unacceptably high false-positive diagnostic rate" for standalone use
- FDA approved NEBA device as adjunct to clinical evaluation for ages 6-17

**Safe Wording:**
> "The theta/beta ratio is a validated physiological measure that may provide supplementary information in ADHD assessment when interpreted as part of a comprehensive clinical evaluation. It is not diagnostic on its own and does not replace clinical judgment or standardized behavioral assessment."

### 2.2 Slow-Wave Excess (Delta/Theta)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Increased absolute/relative delta (0.5-4 Hz) and theta (4-8 Hz) power, especially frontocentral |
| **Description** | Reflects cortical immaturity or hypoarousal; 'slow-wave disease' conceptualization |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** (Moderate) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- Consistent finding across 50+ years of EEG literature
- Children with ADHD show high amplitudes of low-frequency waves (delta, theta, alpha) and lower amplitudes of fast waves (beta)
- Clarke et al. identified "Maturational Lag Type" subtype with excess theta and deficit beta
- Effect modified by comorbid conditions (IGD, anxiety, depression)
- Limited predictive validity for treatment response

**Safe Wording:**
> "Increased slow-wave activity on qEEG is a commonly observed physiological pattern in individuals with ADHD, consistent with models of cortical hypoarousal. This pattern is not specific to ADHD and should not be used independently for diagnosis."

## 3. ASD

### 3.1 Mu Rhythm Abnormality

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Mu rhythm (8-13 Hz over sensorimotor cortex, C3/C4/Cz) desynchronization during action observation |
| **Description** | Mu rhythm suppression during action observation reflects mirror neuron system (MNS) activation; hypothesized biomarker for social-communication deficits |
| **Evidence Grade** | **C** (Limited) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Experimental |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- Mu rhythm studies in ASD show mixed and inconsistent results
- Some studies report reduced mu suppression during action observation in ASD; others find no difference
- Methodological variability (frequency bands, electrode sites, tasks) contributes to inconsistency
- Correlation between mu attenuation and social skills subscale of AQ found in some studies (R2=0.55)
- Test-retest reliability of EEG power spectral density lower in ASD than typically developing controls
- Higher autism spectrum traits in neurotypical samples associated with mu rhythm slowing
- **Critical limitation:** No standardized protocol; results highly dependent on experimental parameters

**Safe Wording:**
> "Research into mu rhythm patterns in ASD is ongoing. While some studies suggest differences in mu rhythm desynchronization during action observation, findings are not consistent enough for clinical use. Mu rhythm should be considered an experimental research measure only."

### 3.2 Connectivity Abnormalities

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Reduced long-range coherence; altered short-range connectivity |
| **Description** | Reduced fronto-occipital, fronto-temporal coherence; enhanced lateral-frontal local coherence |
| **Evidence Grade** | **C** (Limited) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- Reduced alpha coherence between frontal cortex and temporal, parietal, occipital cortices
- Weaker left frontal-temporal connectivity within beta band
- Reduced interhemispheric coherence in frontal, temporal, central/parietal/occipital regions
- Some studies report enhanced local coherence over lateral-frontal regions (delta band)
- Findings parallel fMRI studies showing reduced fronto-temporal connectivity
- Duffy & Als (using surface Laplacian) found overall reduction in short-range connectivity
- High heterogeneity across studies; confounded by data blurring and reference electrode effects

**Safe Wording:**
> "Connectivity patterns on qEEG show preliminary differences in some individuals with ASD, but findings are heterogeneous across studies. qEEG connectivity measures are not validated for ASD diagnosis or clinical assessment."

## 4. Depression (MDD)

### 4.1 Frontal Alpha Asymmetry (FAA)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | (F4-F3) alpha power asymmetry; also F8-F7, Fp2-Fp1 |
| **Description** | Relative left frontal hypoactivation (decreased left alpha = increased left activity) or relative right frontal hyperactivation associated with depression, withdrawal behavior, and emotion dysregulation |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** (Moderate) - Limited diagnostic utility despite decades of research |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- 2025 meta-analysis (Luo et al., npj Mental Health Research): 23 studies, 1928 MDD patients, 2604 controls
- Found small but significant grand mean effect size (ES = -0.03) for FAA (F4-F3) with high heterogeneity (I2 = 75.1%)
- F8-F7 and Fp2-Fp1 showed non-significant results
- Post-hoc: significant three-way interaction of gender x age x depression severity
- Previous meta-analyses (Van der Vinne et al., 2017; N=1883 MDD, 2161 controls) found non-significant effect sizes
- High heterogeneity across studies remains unresolved
- FAA may have value as treatment predictor (iSPOT-D study: responders showed more right-sided alpha asymmetry)

**Safe Wording:**
> "Frontal alpha asymmetry has been studied extensively in depression research. While some meta-analyses show a small statistical association, the effect is modest and highly heterogeneous across studies. qEEG alpha asymmetry is not a diagnostic test for depression and should not replace clinical evaluation."

### 4.2 Frontal Theta Activity

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Increased frontal theta (4-8 Hz) power, especially at Fz, FCz |
| **Description** | Elevated frontal theta associated with rumination, cognitive control deficits, and prefrontal dysfunction |
| **Evidence Grade** | **C** (Limited) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- Increased frontal theta reported in multiple depression studies
- Associated with psychomotor retardation (Cantisani et al., 2015)
- Linked to anterior cingulate cortex dysfunction
- Frontal theta asymmetry also investigated as potential marker
- Limited by small sample sizes and medication confounds

**Safe Wording:**
> "Elevated frontal theta activity has been observed in some depression studies and may reflect aspects of prefrontal dysfunction. This measure is not validated for clinical diagnosis of depression."

## 5. Anxiety Disorders

### 5.1 High Beta Activity (Temporal)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Increased absolute beta power at T3 and T4; elevated temporal high-beta |
| **Description** | Increased beta (especially 18-25 Hz) over temporal regions associated with amygdala hyperactivity and cortical hyperarousal |
| **Evidence Grade** | **C** (Limited) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- Higher beta at T3 and T4 in treatment-resistant anxiety patients (Byeon et al., 2023)
- Beta waves in T3 and T4 associated with excessive amygdala activity and fear/panic responses
- Elevated TBR also reported in anxiety disorders
- 2024 study: patients with anxiety disorders showed attenuated low-beta power in temporal regions; deficit in right temporal beta
- No significant qEEG differences found between GAD and panic disorder subtypes
- Findings suggest beta abnormalities may be common across anxiety subtypes rather than specific to any one disorder

**Safe Wording:**
> "Some research has identified beta power alterations in anxiety disorders, particularly over temporal regions. These findings are preliminary and not specific enough to distinguish anxiety disorders from other conditions or to guide clinical diagnosis."

### 5.2 Theta/Beta Ratio (Anxiety)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Elevated TBR, particularly frontal |
| **Description** | Suggests thalamocortical dysrhythmia and disturbance in thalamus-cortex interaction |
| **Evidence Grade** | **C** (Limited) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- TBR elevation negatively correlated with attentional control and trait anxiety
- TBR elevation shows disturbance in thalamus-cortex interaction
- Not specific to anxiety; overlaps with ADHD, PTSD, and other conditions

**Safe Wording:**
> "Theta/beta ratio elevations have been reported in some anxiety disorder studies, but this pattern is non-specific and overlaps with multiple other conditions. TBR is not validated as a diagnostic marker for anxiety."

## 6. PTSD

### 6.1 Frontal Alpha Asymmetry

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Frontal alpha asymmetry (F4-F3) at rest and during tasks |
| **Description** | PTSD associated with relative right frontal asymmetry (withdrawal pattern); some studies show left frontal asymmetry in trauma-exposed individuals |
| **Evidence Grade** | **C** (Limited) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- PTSD patients show lower alpha power overall compared to non-PTSD controls
- Mixed findings on direction of asymmetry: some studies show relative right frontal asymmetry (withdrawal); others show left frontal asymmetry, especially in trauma-exposed individuals without PTSD (resilience marker?)
- 2022 study found trauma-exposed individuals (especially PTSD) exhibited greater relative left FAA during inhibitory control tasks with emotional stimuli
- Three-way interaction of Group x Emotion x Task suggests context-dependent FAA patterns
- Comorbid depression confounds FAA findings (depression also associated with left FAA)
- Kemp et al. (2010) found disorder-specific patterns despite PTSD-MDD comorbidity

**Safe Wording:**
> "Frontal alpha asymmetry patterns in PTSD are inconsistent across studies and may differ depending on comorbid conditions, trauma type, and task demands. qEEG asymmetry is not a validated diagnostic tool for PTSD."

### 6.2 Low Alpha Power / High Beta

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Decreased alpha power; increased beta activity (eyes-open conditions) |
| **Description** | Cortical hyperarousal model of PTSD; decreased alpha suppression and increased beta reflect hypervigilance |
| **Evidence Grade** | **C** (Limited) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- Eyes-open: PTSD patients show significant decrease in delta, theta, alpha bands; beta band less consistently affected
- Eyes-closed: fewer significant differences across bands
- Magnitude results 21-22% higher with eyes open vs. eyes closed in PTSD
- Higher alpha peak frequency reported in some PTSD studies
- Consistent with hyperarousal model but non-specific

**Safe Wording:**
> "Some studies report altered alpha and beta power in PTSD, consistent with models of cortical hyperarousal. However, these patterns are not specific to PTSD and overlap with anxiety disorders and depression. qEEG is not approved for PTSD diagnosis."

## 7. Sleep Disorders

### 7.1 Beta Hyperarousal (Insomnia)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Increased beta power (>13 Hz) during wakefulness and all sleep stages |
| **Description** | 24-hour cortical hyperarousal model; increased sensorimotor and cognitive process activity during sleep |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** (Moderate) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool |
| **Research-Only** | Yes (for diagnosis) |

**Key Findings:**
- 2021 meta-analysis (Zhao et al.): 24 studies, 977 participants, 148 effect sizes
- Patients with insomnia showed increased beta band power throughout wakefulness and sleep
- Increased theta and gamma during wakefulness; increased alpha and sigma during REM
- Decreased delta power during NREM; increased theta, alpha, sigma during NREM
- Relative power more sensitive biomarker during NREM than absolute power
- Overnight beta positively correlated with short TST, low SE, long WASO, high arousal index
- Pattern consistent with 24-hour cortical hyperarousal hypothesis

**Safe Wording:**
> "Increased beta activity during sleep and wakefulness is a well-documented physiological pattern in insomnia disorder, consistent with the cortical hyperarousal model. While this pattern has research value, it is not used as a standalone diagnostic tool for insomnia."

### 7.2 Delta/Slow-Wave Deficit (Sleep Disorders)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Decreased delta (0.5-4 Hz) and slow-wave activity during NREM sleep |
| **Description** | Reduced slow-wave sleep depth and sleep homeostasis impairment |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** (Moderate) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Clinical Adjunct (polysomnography) |
| **Research-Only** | No (part of standard PSG) |

**Key Findings:**
- Decreased delta power during NREM consistently found in insomnia
- Slow-wave activity reduction correlates with subjective sleep quality complaints
- Delta power deficits also found in OSA, comorbid insomnia-OSA (COMISA)
- Standard polysomnographic measure; not a qEEG-specific biomarker

**Safe Wording:**
> "Reduced slow-wave activity during NREM sleep is a standard polysomnographic finding in multiple sleep disorders. This measure is routinely assessed during clinical sleep studies."

## 8. Epilepsy

### 8.1 Interictal Epileptiform Discharges (IEDs)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Spikes, sharp waves, spike-and-wave complexes; frequency, morphology, distribution |
| **Description** | Pathological electrophysiological events between seizures; hallmark of epileptogenic cortex |
| **Evidence Grade** | **A** (Strong) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Clinical Standard (established diagnostic tool) |
| **Research-Only** | No |

**Key Findings:**
- IEDs defined as di- or tri-phasic waves with sharp/spiky morphology distinct from background
- Duration: spikes 20-70 ms, sharp waves 70-200 ms
- Meta-analysis: sensitivity 17.3%, specificity 94.7% for seizure recurrence after single unprovoked seizure
- IEDs maximal during NREM sleep; minimal during REM sleep and wakefulness
- Prevalence associated with long-term cognitive impairment (language, memory)
- Precise localization aids in identifying seizure onset zone for surgical candidates
- IED prevalence associated with seizure recurrence risk (HR = 3.82, p < 0.0001)

**Safe Wording:**
> "Interictal epileptiform discharges are a well-established electrophysiological hallmark of epilepsy. Their presence, morphology, and distribution provide clinically valuable information for diagnosis, syndrome classification, and pre-surgical evaluation."

### 8.2 Spike-and-Wave Complexes / Periodic Discharges

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Generalized spike-wave (3 Hz), periodic discharges (PDs), rhythmic delta activity (RDA) |
| **Description** | Specific patterns associated with particular epilepsy syndromes and acute seizure risk |
| **Evidence Grade** | **A** (Strong) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Clinical Standard |
| **Research-Only** | No |

**Key Findings:**
- 3-Hz spike-and-wave: absence epilepsy hallmark
- Periodic discharges (PDs): associated with status epilepticus risk (ACNS standardized terminology)
- Lateralized periodic discharges (LPDs): seizure recurrence risk factor
- Breach rhythm, rhythmic delta activity also clinically significant
- Standardized critical care EEG terminology (ACNS) enables inter-rater reliability

**Safe Wording:**
> "Specific epileptiform patterns including spike-and-wave complexes and periodic discharges are established clinical markers used in epilepsy diagnosis, syndrome classification, and acute seizure risk assessment."

## 9. TBI / Concussion

### 9.1 EEG Slowing (Delta/Theta Increase)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Increased delta and theta power; increased slow-wave counts during wakefulness; theta:beta ratio elevation |
| **Description** | Intrusive sleep-like EEG frequencies during wakefulness reflecting neural injury and local sleep dysregulation |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** (Moderate) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | FDA-Cleared (BrainScope - adjunctive) |
| **Research-Only** | No |

**Key Findings:**
- mTBI subjects show significantly more EEG slow waves while awake vs. controls
- Increased theta:beta amplitude ratios in human mTBI
- Decreased global coherence of slow waves while awake correlated with symptom severity (r = 0.84, R2 = 0.71)
- Slow wave quantity and global coherence may represent sensitive markers for diagnosis and prognosis
- BrainScope device FDA-cleared (2019) for structural injury classification and brain function assessment
- Device uses QEEG measures: absolute/relative power, asymmetry, coherence, fractal dimension

**Safe Wording:**
> "Increased slow-wave activity on qEEG has been associated with traumatic brain injury and may provide objective information about brain function changes post-injury. FDA-cleared devices incorporating qEEG are available as adjuncts to standard clinical assessment of head injury."

### 9.2 Coherence / Connectivity Disruption

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Reduced global coherence index; altered functional connectivity |
| **Description** | Decreased synchronization of slow waves across cortical regions; reflects network disruption |
| **Evidence Grade** | **C** (Limited) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | FDA-Cleared (as part of BrainScope composite) |
| **Research-Only** | No (part of cleared device) |

**Key Findings:**
- mTBI showed significantly less EEG global coherence while awake (p = 0.000008)
- Global coherence strongly correlated with Neurobehavioral Symptom Inventory scores
- Cognitive component of NSI most strongly correlated with synchrony index
- No significant coherence differences during N2 sleep
- Method: novel global coherence index across multiple channels (not traditional pairwise coherence)

**Safe Wording:**
> "EEG coherence measures may reflect changes in brain network synchronization following traumatic brain injury. These measures are incorporated into some FDA-cleared adjunctive assessment devices but are not standalone diagnostic tools."

## 10. Dementia / MCI

### 10.1 Alpha Slowing

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Decreased alpha peak frequency; decreased alpha power; shift from posterior to more diffuse alpha |
| **Description** | Slowing of dominant posterior rhythm below 8 Hz; reflects cholinergic dysfunction and cortical degeneration |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** (Moderate) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Clinical Adjunct (supportive biomarker per research criteria) |
| **Research-Only** | No (used in research and specialty clinical settings) |

**Key Findings:**
- Decreased alpha power correlates with enhanced AD severity
- Posterior alpha power reduction is a consistent finding across AD studies
- Alpha peak frequency slowing below 8 Hz is a hallmark of neurodegeneration
- Alpha power changes can monitor treatment response (e.g., rivastigmine: increased posterior alpha power correlated with cognitive improvement)
- Machine learning models using alpha features show 65-80% classification accuracy for AD vs. controls
- Alpha3/alpha2 ratio reliably associated with hippocampal atrophy in MCI

**Safe Wording:**
> "Alpha rhythm slowing and reduced posterior alpha power are well-documented physiological changes associated with Alzheimer's disease and related dementias. These qEEG measures may serve as supportive biomarkers when interpreted in the context of a comprehensive neuropsychological and clinical evaluation."

### 10.2 Theta Increase / Theta-Alpha Ratio

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Increased theta (4-8 Hz) power; elevated theta/alpha ratio; theta/gamma ratio |
| **Description** | Theta power increase reflects compensatory mechanisms or disinhibition due to cholinergic deficit |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** (Moderate) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Clinical Adjunct |
| **Research-Only** | No (research and specialty clinical settings) |

**Key Findings:**
- Increased theta power and theta/alpha ratio consistently found in AD and MCI
- Theta/gamma ratio associated with amygdalo-hippocampal atrophy
- Theta/alpha1 ratio reliable index for cerebrovascular damage estimation
- Reduction in theta power during treatment predicts therapeutic response
- PQ912 (glutaminyl cyclase inhibitor): reduced theta band relative power after 12 weeks correlated with drug effect
- Theta increase + alpha decrease pattern distinguishes vascular from degenerative MCI

**Safe Wording:**
> "Increased theta activity relative to alpha is a commonly observed qEEG pattern in mild cognitive impairment and dementia. While this pattern has been associated with disease severity and may track treatment response, it is not specific to any single dementia subtype and should not be used as a standalone diagnostic test."

### 10.3 Delta Power Increase

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Increased delta (0.5-4 Hz) power, especially frontal regions |
| **Description** | Severe slowing indicating advanced neurodegeneration and cortical dysfunction |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** (Moderate) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Clinical Adjunct |
| **Research-Only** | No |

**Key Findings:**
- Delta power increase proportional to cerebrovascular damage severity
- Significant delta increase in severe CV damage vs. no-CV-damage MCI groups
- Donepezil: temporal delta power decrease associated with treatment response
- Delta activity increase correlates with AD progression

**Safe Wording:**
> "Increased delta activity is associated with more advanced stages of cognitive impairment and cerebrovascular damage. Delta power changes may be monitored in conjunction with other clinical measures."

## 11. Parkinson's Disease

### 11.1 Beta Oscillation Abnormality

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Abnormal beta (13-30 Hz) oscillations; exaggerated beta synchrony in sensorimotor cortex; decreased cortical beta coherence post-medication |
| **Description** | Paradoxical beta hypersynchrony associated with bradykinesia and rigidity; beta band 'freezing' prevents movement initiation |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** (Moderate) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- Exaggerated beta oscillations (15-30 Hz) in STN, GPe, and cortex are pathophysiological hallmark
- Beta oscillations suppressed by voluntary movement and dopaminergic medication
- Elevated beta synchrony across sensorimotor cortex correlates with motor symptom severity
- Decreased cortical beta coherence following dopaminergic medication (paradoxical but consistent with improved symptoms)
- Beta oscillations present even in non-tremor PD patients
- Low beta (8-15 Hz) increased in MPTP-treated monkeys; high beta (20-30 Hz) increased in 6-OHDA-lesioned rats
- Beta band 'freezing' hypothesis: excessive beta prevents normal movement initiation

**Safe Wording:**
> "Abnormal beta band oscillations are a well-documented electrophysiological feature of Parkinson's disease in research settings, particularly in subcortical recordings. Cortical beta measures via scalp EEG are an active area of research but are not currently validated for clinical diagnosis or monitoring of PD."

### 11.2 Cortical Slowing (Theta/Delta Increase)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Increased spectral power in slower bands; decreased faster band power; lower dominant frequency |
| **Description** | General cortical slowing reflecting diffuse neurodegeneration beyond basal ganglia |
| **Evidence Grade** | **C** (Limited) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- Systematic review found increase in slower band power and decrease in faster band power at rest
- Lower dominant frequency or increased theta power in PD patients
- Cortical slowing related to motor impairment (bradykinesia at OFF state, freezing)
- Decrease of beta frequency band associated with general motor function impairment
- During movement: abnormal pattern mainly in contralateral cortex with complex tasks
- Simple motor tasks fail to show abnormality (task complexity matters)

**Safe Wording:**
> "Cortical slowing on qEEG has been observed in some Parkinson's disease research studies, but findings are variable and not specific to PD. qEEG is not established as a clinical diagnostic tool for Parkinson's disease."

### 11.3 Tremor Frequency Activity

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | 4-6 Hz oscillation artifact; tremor-related EEG components |
| **Description** | Parkinsonian tremor produces movement artifacts that overlap with neural oscillations; challenging to separate |
| **Evidence Grade** | **C** (Limited) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- PD tremor at 4-6 Hz produces electrical signals overlapping with neural delta/theta oscillations
- Standard preprocessing (ICA) struggles to separate tremor artifacts from genuine brain activity
- Some studies report near-perfect classification using tremor artifacts (98.68%), but this detects movement not neural activity
- Rigorous artifact removal reduces classification accuracy but isolates genuine neurophysiological signals
- Maximum classification ~76% after tremor artifact removal

**Safe Wording:**
> "Tremor-related electrical activity can be detected on EEG recordings in Parkinson's disease, but separating genuine brain activity from movement artifact remains a significant technical challenge. Tremor frequency analysis on scalp EEG is not a validated clinical diagnostic method for PD."

## 12. Learning Disorders

### 12.1 Elevated Slow-Wave Activity (Theta/Delta)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Increased delta and theta power, especially left frontotemporal; theta/alpha ratio elevation |
| **Description** | Increased slow-wave activity reflects cortical immaturity or delayed maturation in reading/language networks |
| **Evidence Grade** | **C** (Limited) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- LD children show increased delta/theta power in 95% of task conditions (discriminant accuracy 98%)
- Poor readers: theta increase and alpha decrease over left frontotemporal regions
- EEG predicts literacy performance (up to 78% correct) independent of IQ/SES
- Severe LD: divergent maturation pattern - theta coherence increases with age (opposite of typical development)
- Longitudinal studies support "maturational lag" model: some children show partial normalization over time
- Global theta absolute power increase with large effect size (d > 0.8)

**Safe Wording:**
> "Research has identified increased slow-wave activity in some children with learning disorders, particularly over language-related brain regions. While these patterns may reflect cortical maturation differences, qEEG is not validated for diagnosing specific learning disorders."

### 12.2 Coherence Deficits

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Reduced alpha coherence in frontotemporal and temporo-parietal connections; atypical coherence trajectories |
| **Description** | Impaired long-range synchronization between brain regions critical for reading and language processing |
| **Evidence Grade** | **C** (Limited) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- Good readers: alpha coherence increases with age; severe LD: theta coherence increases and alpha coherence decreases (divergent)
- LD children lack normal posterior Cz-centered coherence maturation
- Frontal alpha/beta coherence increased in LD (atypical trajectory)
- Upper-alpha low-resolution cortical power (LPC) decreased in frontal-parietal and temporal-occipital networks
- Impaired network integration in language and reading circuits

**Safe Wording:**
> "EEG coherence differences have been observed in some research studies of children with learning disorders, suggesting altered functional connectivity in language-related networks. These findings are preliminary and not suitable for clinical diagnosis."

### 12.3 Theta/Alpha Ratio (TAR)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Marker** | Theta/alpha ratio, especially frontal-temporal and parietal regions |
| **Description** | Reflects balance between immature (theta) and mature (alpha) cortical rhythms |
| **Evidence Grade** | **C** (Limited) |
| **Clinical-Use Status** | Research Tool (neurofeedback target) |
| **Research-Only** | Yes |

**Key Findings:**
- TAR reduction via neurofeedback may encourage transition toward age-appropriate cortical rhythms
- TAR training targets theta decrease and/or alpha enhancement over frontal-temporal or parietal areas
- Effectiveness for reading/spelling improvement not conclusively demonstrated
- Part of broader multi-phase neurofeedback protocols for LD

**Safe Wording:**
> "The theta/alpha ratio has been investigated as a potential physiological marker in learning disorders and as a target for neurofeedback training. Evidence for clinical utility in diagnosis or treatment remains preliminary."

## 15. Safe Wording Guidelines

### General Principles

1. **Never state qEEG is diagnostic on its own** - always frame as "adjunct," "supportive," or "one component of"
2. **Acknowledge heterogeneity** - biomarkers show group-level differences, not individual diagnostic certainty
3. **Note specificity limitations** - most qEEG markers are not specific to a single condition
4. **Distinguish research vs. clinical use** - clearly label which markers are FDA-cleared vs. investigational
5. **Include age/medication/confound context** - biomarker performance varies with demographics and comorbidities

### Condition-Specific Recommended Wording

**For ADHD:**
> "qEEG theta/beta ratio is an FDA-cleared measure that may provide additional physiological information when used alongside a comprehensive clinical evaluation. It is not a standalone diagnostic test and should not replace clinical judgment or behavioral assessment. The American Academy of Neurology recommends against using qEEG to confirm or rule out ADHD outside of research settings due to concerns about false-positive rates."

**For Depression:**
> "Frontal alpha asymmetry has been studied in depression research for several decades. While meta-analyses show a small statistical association, the clinical utility for individual diagnosis remains limited. qEEG is not an FDA-approved diagnostic tool for depression."

**For Anxiety:**
> "Some research has identified beta power alterations in anxiety disorders, but these findings are preliminary, show significant overlap with other conditions, and are not validated for clinical diagnosis."

**For PTSD:**
> "qEEG findings in PTSD are inconsistent across studies and may be confounded by comorbid conditions such as depression and anxiety. qEEG is not a validated diagnostic tool for PTSD."

**For Sleep Disorders:**
> "Beta hyperarousal during sleep is a well-documented research finding in insomnia. While it supports the cortical hyperarousal model, it is not used as a standalone clinical diagnostic marker. Standard polysomnography remains the clinical gold standard."

**For Epilepsy:**
> "Interictal epileptiform discharges are a well-established electrophysiological feature of epilepsy and are a standard component of clinical EEG interpretation. Their presence, morphology, and distribution provide valuable diagnostic and prognostic information."

**For TBI/Concussion:**
> "qEEG measures are incorporated into FDA-cleared adjunctive devices for head injury assessment. These measures should be used as part of a multi-modal assessment and do not replace CT scanning or clinical evaluation when indicated."

**For Dementia/MCI:**
> "qEEG slowing patterns (decreased alpha, increased theta/delta) are recognized supportive biomarkers in research criteria for neurodegenerative diseases. They may complement neuropsychological testing and neuroimaging but are not specific to any single dementia subtype."

**For Parkinson's:**
> "Beta oscillation abnormalities are a well-established research finding in Parkinson's disease, particularly in invasive recordings. Scalp EEG measures are not validated for PD diagnosis or clinical monitoring."

**For Learning Disorders:**
> "Increased slow-wave activity and coherence differences have been reported in research on learning disorders. These patterns may reflect cortical maturation differences but are not validated for clinical diagnosis of specific learning disorders."

## Appendix B: Non-Specificity Warning

Many qEEG markers show **significant cross-condition overlap**:

- **Elevated TBR**: ADHD, anxiety, PTSD, learning disorders, head injury
- **Alpha asymmetry changes**: Depression, anxiety, PTSD (direction varies)
- **Beta alterations**: Anxiety, PTSD, ADHD (hyperarousal subtype), insomnia
- **Slow-wave increase**: ADHD, learning disorders, TBI, dementia, PD
- **Coherence deficits**: ASD, TBI, learning disorders, dementia

> **Clinical implication:** No single qEEG biomarker is pathognomonic for any specific neuropsychiatric condition. Interpretation must always occur within the full clinical context.
