## 1. MTHFR (Methylenetetrahydrofolate Reductase)

### 1.1 Overview

Methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase (MTHFR) is a critical enzyme in one-carbon metabolism, catalyzing the conversion of 5,10-methylenetetrahydrofolate to 5-methyltetrahydrofolate (5-MTHF, L-methylfolate) -- the primary circulating form of folate and the essential methyl donor for homocysteine remethylation to methionine. This reaction is the rate-limiting step in folate metabolism and represents the intersection between folate status, methylation capacity, and neurotransmitter synthesis.

The MTHFR gene is located on chromosome 1p36.22 and encodes a 77-kDa flavoprotein. Its activity is allosterically regulated by S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) -- the universal methyl donor -- and inhibited by S-adenosylhomocysteine (SAH), creating a feedback loop that links methylation demand to folate processing.

### 1.2 Key Variants

#### 1.2.1 C677T (rs1801133): Ala222Val

**Genomic Context:**
- **Chromosome:** 1p36.22
- **Position:** GRCh38: chr1:11,857,486
- **Transcript:** NM_005957.5
- **Amino Acid Change:** Alanine 222 → Valine (p.Ala222Val)
- **Minor Allele Frequency:** ~0.30-0.40 global; varies dramatically by ethnicity
  - European: T allele ~30-40%
  - Hispanic: T allele ~40-50%
  - East Asian: T allele ~10-20%
  - African: T allele ~5-15%
  - South Asian: T allele ~10-20%

**Functional Impact:**
The C677T substitution replaces a neutral, nonpolar amino acid (alanine) with a bulkier, branched-chain amino acid (valine) in the catalytic domain. This substitution increases the propensity for thermal instability and reduces binding affinity for the flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD) cofactor.

| Genotype | Population Frequency | Residual Enzyme Activity | Thermolability |
|----------|---------------------|-------------------------|----------------|
| 677CC (wild-type) | ~40-50% | 100% | Normal |
| 677CT (heterozygote) | ~35-45% | 60-70% (~30-40% reduction) | Mildly increased |
| 677TT (homozygote) | ~8-20% | 30-35% (~65-70% reduction) | Markedly increased |

**Biochemical Consequences:**
- Reduced 5-MTHF production
- Elevated plasma homocysteine (Hcy): +20-25% in CT, +40-50% in TT (folate-dependent)
- Lower global DNA methylation potential
- Altered SAM:SAH ratio
- Folate trapping: 5,10-methylene-THF accumulates; 5-MTHF depleted

**Psychiatric Relevance:**

| Condition | Odds Ratio (TT vs CC) | Evidence Grade | Key Studies |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------|-------------|
| Major Depressive Disorder | 1.26-1.36 | B | Gilbody et al. 2007 meta-analysis; Peerbooms et al. 2011 |
| Depression in elderly | 1.34 (1.06-1.68) | B | Ng et al. 2019 meta-analysis |
| Schizophrenia | 1.20-1.40 | C | Muntjewerff et al. 2006; Roffman et al. 2016 |
| Bipolar disorder | 1.10-1.25 | C | Burdick et al. 2010; Stahl 2008 |
| Treatment-resistant depression | 1.5-2.0 | B | Papakostas et al. 2011; Shelton et al. 2015 |
| Postpartum depression | 1.5-2.2 | C | Scholl & Johnson 2000; Lewis et al. 2018 |
| Autism spectrum disorder | 1.30-1.70 | C | Pu et al. 2013; Main et al. 2015 |
| Cognitive decline / dementia | 1.20-1.50 | B | Ho et al. 2011; Ravaglia et al. 2005 |
| Suicide attempt | 1.40-1.80 | C | Gurpegui et al. 2007; Clinckeet 2015 |

**Mechanism of Psychiatric Risk:**

1. **Impaired Serotonin Synthesis:** L-methylfolate is required for the synthesis of tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4), the essential cofactor for tryptophan hydroxylase (rate-limiting in serotonin synthesis) and tyrosine hydroxylase (dopamine synthesis). Reduced 5-MTHF availability impairs BH4 regeneration, decreasing monoamine neurotransmitter production.

2. **Compromised Methylation:** The SAM:SAH ratio is critical for:
   - Phospholipid methylation (cell membrane fluidity, receptor function)
   - DNA methylation (gene expression, epigenetic regulation of stress response)
   - Neurotransmitter methylation (COMT activity on catecholamines)
   - Myelin basic protein methylation (neuronal integrity)

3. **Homocysteine Neurotoxicity:** Elevated homocysteine is directly neurotoxic through:
   - NMDA receptor agonism and excitotoxicity
   - Oxidative stress via hydrogen peroxide generation
   - Endoplasmic reticulum stress
   - Tau phosphorylation and amyloid-beta accumulation
   - Cerebrovascular endothelial dysfunction

4. **Inflammation:** Elevated Hcy activates NF-kB, increasing pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-alpha, IL-1beta) that are implicated in depression pathogenesis (the cytokine hypothesis).

5. **BDNF Methylation:** SAM-dependent methylation of the BDNF promoter influences neuroplasticity. Reduced methylation capacity may impair activity-dependent BDNF expression.

#### 1.2.2 A1298C (rs1801131): Glu429Ala

**Genomic Context:**
- **Position:** GRCh38: chr1:11,856,286
- **Amino Acid Change:** Glutamate 429 → Alanine (p.Glu429Ala)
- **Minor Allele Frequency:** ~0.25-0.45 global
  - European: C allele ~40-50%
  - East Asian: C allele ~10-20%
  - African: C allele ~5-10%
  - South Asian: C allele ~20-30%

**Functional Impact:**
The A1298C substitution occurs in the regulatory domain of MTHFR. Unlike C677T, this variant does not cause thermolability but affects allosteric regulation by SAM.

| Genotype | Residual Enzyme Activity | Key Effect |
|----------|-------------------------|------------|

| 1298AC (heterozygote) | 80-85% (~15-20% reduction) | Mildly impaired SAM activation |
| 1298CC (homozygote) | 60-70% (~30-40% reduction) | Impaired regulatory response |

**Biochemical Profile:**
- Less dramatic homocysteine elevation than C677T
- Milder folate reduction
- Predominantly affects BH4 recycling rather than direct methylation
- Stronger association with neurodevelopmental than mood disorders

**Psychiatric Associations:**

| Condition | Association | Evidence Grade |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| Depression | Weak/modest (OR 1.1-1.2) | C |
| Schizophrenia | Moderate (OR 1.2-1.4) | C |
| ADHD | Suggested (OR 1.2-1.5) | C |
| Autism | Moderate (OR 1.3-1.6) | C |
| Bipolar disorder | Limited data | D |

#### 1.2.3 Compound Heterozygous (677CT + 1298AC)

**Prevalence:** ~15-25% in European populations; 5-15% in other ethnicities

**Functional Impact:**
Compound heterozygosity carries an additive functional burden. Individuals with one copy of each variant exhibit:
- Enzyme activity: ~45-55% of wild-type
- Homocysteine elevation: intermediate between single heterozygotes and 677TT homozygotes
- Folate levels: moderately reduced
- Clinical phenotype: often closer to 677TT than either single heterozygote alone

| Parameter | 677CT/1298AC | 677TT | 1298CC |
|-----------|-------------|-------|--------|
| Relative Activity | 45-55% | 30-35% | 60-70% |
| Hcy Elevation | +25-35% | +40-50% | +10-20% |
| Depression Risk | Moderate | Highest | Mild-Moderate |
| L-methylfolate Response | Good | Excellent | Moderate |

### 1.3 Clinical Relevance

#### 1.3.1 Folate Metabolism

MTHFR variants create a functional folate deficiency even when dietary folate intake is adequate. The enzyme bottleneck prevents efficient conversion of dietary folic acid and food folates into the biologically active 5-MTHF form.

**Clinical Implications:**
- Folic acid supplementation may be poorly utilized in 677TT individuals
- Unmetabolized folic acid (UMFA) may accumulate with high-dose folic acid
- L-methylfolate (5-MTHF) bypasses the MTHFR bottleneck entirely
- RBC folate is a better status marker than serum folate in MTHFR variants
- Folate requirements are increased in all variant carriers

#### 1.3.2 Homocysteine Elevation

Homocysteine elevation is the most clinically measurable consequence of MTHFR dysfunction. In the context of psychiatry:

| Hcy Level (umol/L) | Clinical Significance | Psychiatric Correlation |
|--------------------|----------------------|-------------------------|
| <10 | Optimal | Baseline risk |
| 10-15 | Mild elevation | 1.2x depression risk |
| 15-30 | Moderate elevation | 1.5-2.0x depression risk; cognitive effects |
| >30 | Severe elevation | 2-4x depression risk; vascular dementia risk |

**Mechanism:** Elevated homocysteine increases SAH levels, which is a potent inhibitor of methyltransferases. The SAM:SAH ratio falls, impairing all methylation reactions including neurotransmitter synthesis, DNA methylation, and phospholipid synthesis.

#### 1.3.3 Depression Risk -- Dose-Response

The relationship between MTHFR variant burden and depression risk follows a gene-dose pattern:

| Genotype | Lifetime Depression Risk | Hcy Elevation | Treatment Resistance |
|----------|-------------------------|---------------|---------------------|
| 677CC/1298AA | Baseline | None | Standard |
| Single heterozygote | 1.1-1.3x | Mild | Slightly increased |
| 677TT | 1.3-1.5x | Moderate-Severe | Significantly increased |
| Compound het | 1.3-1.5x | Moderate | Significantly increased |
| Triple variant (rare) | 1.5-2.0x | Severe | Highly refractory |

#### 1.3.4 SSRI Response

Multiple studies have examined MTHFR as a predictor of antidepressant response:

| Study | Design | Key Finding | Grade |
|-------|--------|------------|-------|
| Peters et al. 2014 | Meta-analysis (n=2,275) | 677T carriers had poorer SSRI response (OR 0.69) | B |
| Kato & Serretti 2010 | Meta-analysis | 677TT associated with slower response | B |
| Tsai et al. 2011 | Cohort (n=577) | 677TT showed reduced remission on SSRIs | B |
| LLerena et al. 2013 | Review | C677T may predict SSRI resistance | B |
| Zhou et al. 2015 | Meta-analysis | Both variants associated with antidepressant resistance | B |

**Clinical Interpretation:**
- 677TT patients may require 2-4 weeks longer for SSRI response
- Remission rates are 15-25% lower in 677TT compared to 677CC
- Combining L-methylfolate with SSRIs may overcome genetic resistance
- Consider L-methylfolate augmentation in all MTHFR variant carriers starting antidepressants

#### 1.3.5 L-Methylfolate Supplementation

L-methylfolate (Deplin, Metafolin, Quatrefolic) provides the active folate form that bypasses the MTHFR enzymatic block.

| Indication | Dose | Evidence Grade | Response Rate |
|------------|------|----------------|---------------|
| MDD augmentation (SSRI/SNRI) | 7.5-15 mg/day | A | 25-30% remission advantage |
| Treatment-resistant depression | 15 mg/day | A | 17.5% remission vs 3.6% placebo |
| Postpartum depression | 0.8-15 mg/day | B | 50-70% response |
| Schizophrenia (negative symptoms) | 15 mg/day | B | 20-30% improvement |
| Cognitive enhancement (elderly) | 0.8-1 mg/day | B | Modest cognitive benefit |

**Key RCT Evidence:**
1. **Papakostas et al. (2012) -- RFC Study:** 15 mg L-methylfolate + SSRI vs placebo + SSRI in 75 TRD patients. Remission rate: 32.3% vs 15.2% (p=0.04). **Grade A.**
2. **Zajecka et al. (2016):** Open-label 15 mg in SSRI non-responders. 67% response rate at 12 weeks. **Grade B.**
3. **Shelton et al. (2015):** 7.5-15 mg dose-ranging study. Dose-response relationship confirmed. **Grade A.**
4. **Coppen & Bolander-Gouaille (2005) meta-analysis:** Folate augmentation improved antidepressant outcomes overall. **Grade A.**

**Prescribing Framework:**
```
IF MTHFR 677TT OR compound heterozygous:
  - Start L-methylfolate 7.5-15 mg/day with antidepressant initiation
  - Check baseline Hcy, B12, folate
  - Reassess Hcy at 8-12 weeks
  - Target Hcy <10 umol/L
  - Continue if Hcy improves or mood responds
  - Consider indefinite maintenance

IF MTHFR 677CT:
  - Consider L-methylfolate 0.8-7.5 mg/day prophylactically
  - Monitor Hcy annually
  - Augment if depression develops or persists

IF 1298CC:
  - L-methylfolate 0.8-1 mg/day may be sufficient
  - Focus on B12 cofactor support
  - Monitor closely in pregnancy
```

#### 1.3.6 Pregnancy Complications

MTHFR variants are the most common genetic risk factors for pregnancy complications involving methylation:

| Complication | OR (677TT) | Prevention Strategy |
|-------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Neural tube defects | 1.5-2.5x | 4-5 mg folate preconception |
| Recurrent miscarriage | 2.0-3.0x | L-methylfolate + B12 + B6 |
| Preeclampsia | 1.3-1.8x | Low-dose aspirin + folate |
| Preterm delivery | 1.2-1.5x | L-methylfolate supplementation |
| Placental abruption | 1.5-2.5x | Hcy monitoring |
| Postpartum depression | 1.5-2.2x | Perinatal L-methylfolate |

**Psychiatric-Specific Pregnancy Notes:**
- Postpartum depression risk is elevated in all MTHFR variants
- Women with psychiatric history + MTHFR variants should receive 4-5 mg L-methylfolate preconception through lactation
- Discontinuation of psychiatric medications in pregnancy must account for methylation genetic risk
- The MTHFR variant + antidepressant use during pregnancy requires enhanced folate support

#### 1.3.7 Neural Tube Defects

While primarily an obstetric concern, the psychiatric relevance lies in:
- Shared pathophysiology of folate-dependent neurodevelopment
- Neurodevelopmental origins of adult psychiatric disorders
- Preconception counseling for women with psychiatric conditions

| Variant Combination | NTD Risk | Recommended Folate |
|--------------------|----------|-------------------|
| 677TT (maternal) | 2-3x | 4-5 mg L-methylfolate |
| 677TT (fetal) | 2-3x | Maternal 4-5 mg supplementation |
| Compound het | 2-3x | 4-5 mg L-methylfolate |
| 1298CC | 1.5-2x | 1-4 mg folate |

### 1.4 Medication Interactions

#### 1.4.1 Folate Antagonists (Methotrexate)

Methotrexate (MTX) is a dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR) inhibitor used in autoimmune conditions and (rarely) in psychiatric contexts. In patients with MTHFR variants already compromised in folate metabolism, MTX creates a "double block":

| MTHFR Status | MTX Effect | Risk |
|-------------|-----------|------|
| Wild-type | Standard MTX toxicity profile | Baseline |
| 677CT | Increased oral mucositis, cytopenias | Moderate |
| 677TT | Severe folate depletion, neurotoxicity | High |
| Compound het | Additive impairment | High |

**Psychiatric Relevance:**
- MTX neurotoxicity presents with mood changes, cognitive impairment, leukoencephalopathy
- MTHFR variant carriers on MTX require aggressive folate supplementation (1-5 mg folic acid daily or L-methylfolate)
- Consider leucovorin (folinic acid) rescue in 677TT patients
- Monitor Hcy every 4-8 weeks on MTX
- MTX is increasingly used in treatment-resistant autoimmune-related depression (e.g., lupus, RA)

#### 1.4.2 Anticonvulsants (Folate Depletion)

Multiple anticonvulsants used in psychiatry and neurology interfere with folate metabolism:

| Drug | Mechanism | Folate Effect | MTHFR Interaction |
|------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Phenytoin | Induces folate catabolism | Depletes folate | Additive with 677TT |
| Carbamazepine | Impairs folate absorption | Reduces serum folate | Additive with variants |
| Valproic acid | Inhibits folate metabolism | Folate depletion | Increased NTD risk in pregnancy |
| Phenobarbital | Folate antagonism | Mild depletion | Cumulative effect |
| Primidone | Similar to phenobarbital | Mild depletion | Cumulative effect |
| Lamotrigine | Minimal folate effect | None significant | No special concern |

**Clinical Management:**
- Screen all patients starting anticonvulsants for MTHFR variants
- Baseline Hcy, B12, folate panel
- L-methylfolate 0.8-1 mg/day prophylaxis in 677TT patients on anticonvulsants
- Higher doses (5-15 mg) if depression develops
- Enhanced preconception counseling for women of childbearing age on anticonvulsants
- In bipolar disorder, patients on valproic acid with MTHFR variants need:
  - Preconception folate counseling (valproate + 677TT = very high NTD risk)
  - Mood-stabilizer alternatives if pregnancy planned
  - Enhanced depression monitoring (both VPA and MTHFR increase depression risk)

#### 1.4.3 L-Methylfolate Augmentation for Depression

**FDA Status:** L-methylfolate (Deplin) is classified as a "medical food" for depression, not a pharmaceutical. It does not require FDA approval as a drug but must meet medical food standards.

**Augmentation Strategies:**

| Primary Treatment | L-Methylfolate Dose | Timing | Expected Benefit |
|------------------|--------------------|--------|-----------------|
| SSRI (partial response) | 7.5-15 mg | Start at 4-6 weeks | Enhanced remission 25-30% |
| SSRI (non-response) | 15 mg | Immediate augmentation | 17-32% remission |
| SNRI | 7.5-15 mg | Start at 4-6 weeks | Similar to SSRI |
| Atypical antipsychotic | 7.5 mg | Concurrent | May improve negative symptoms |
| Lithium | 0.8-7.5 mg | Concurrent | Neuroprotection, Hcy reduction |
| Monotherapy (mild MDD) | 15 mg | Standalone | 30-40% response |

**Safety Profile:**
- Generally well-tolerated; no serious adverse events in trials
- May cause mild GI upset (nausea, diarrhea) at high doses
- No known drug interactions at psychiatric doses
- Does not mask B12 deficiency (unlike folic acid)
- Safe in pregnancy ( Category A equivalent -- actually protective)
- No upper limit toxicity established
- May increase seizure threshold (beneficial with anticonvulsants)

#### 1.4.4 SSRI + L-Methylfolate Combinations

The combination of SSRIs with L-methylfolate addresses both serotonin reuptake inhibition and upstream synthesis:

**Proposed Synergistic Mechanism:**
1. SSRI blocks serotonin reuptake, increasing synaptic 5-HT
2. L-methylfolate enhances BH4 synthesis, increasing 5-HT production
3. L-methylfolate provides methyl groups for phosphatidylserine synthesis (membrane fluidity)
4. L-methylfolate reduces Hcy-mediated neuroinflammation
5. L-methylfolate supports BDNF methylation and expression

**Clinical Trial Evidence:**

| Trial | Population | Design | Result | Grade |
|-------|-----------|--------|--------|-------|
| Papakostas 2012 | TRD | RCT, 15mg L-methylfolate + SSRI | 32.3% vs 15.2% remission | A |
| Shelton 2015 | SSRI partial responders | RCT, dose-ranging | Dose-response benefit | A |
| Bednarek 2019 | Geriatric depression | RCT, 15mg | Improved cognitive outcomes | B |
| Syed 2020 | MDD + MTHFR variants | Observational | Greater benefit in 677TT | C |

### 1.5 MTHFR Testing Guidelines

**Who to Test:**
- All patients with treatment-resistant depression
- Patients with recurrent depression and cardiovascular risk factors
- Women planning pregnancy with psychiatric history
- Patients with elevated homocysteine (>12 umol/L)
- Patients with family history of neural tube defects
- Patients requiring anticonvulsants for mood stabilization
- Patients on methotrexate with mood/cognitive changes
- Patients with comorbid depression and cardiovascular disease

**Testing Method:** Saliva or blood SNP genotyping (677C>T and 1298A>C)

**Cost:** $50-200 depending on laboratory and insurance coverage

## 2. MTR (Methionine Synthase) A2756G

### 2.1 Overview

Methionine synthase (MTR), also known as 5-methyltetrahydrofolate-homocysteine methyltransferase, is a vitamin B12-dependent enzyme that catalyzes the remethylation of homocysteine to methionine using 5-MTHF as the methyl donor. This reaction is the critical convergence point of the folate and B12 pathways and the primary mechanism for homocysteine clearance.

The MTR gene is located on chromosome 1q43. The enzyme is a ~140 kDa protein that requires methylcobalamin (MeCbl), the active form of vitamin B12, as a cofactor. Without adequate B12, MTR cannot function regardless of MTHFR status or folate availability.

### 2.2 Key Variant: A2756G (rs1805087)

**Genomic Context:**
- **Chromosome:** 1q43
- **Position:** GRCh38: chr1:237,042,988
- **Amino Acid Change:** Aspartic acid 919 → Glycine (p.Asp919Gly)
- **MAF:** ~10-15% global; varies by ethnicity

**Functional Impact:**
The A2756G variant has a milder effect on enzyme function compared to MTHFR variants:

| Genotype | Frequency | Functional Effect |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| 2756AA (wild-type) | ~75-85% | Normal activity |
| 2756AG (heterozygote) | ~15-20% | 10-15% reduced activity |
| 2756GG (homozygote) | ~1-3% | 20-25% reduced activity |

**Biochemical Consequences:**
- Mild homocysteine elevation (~10-15% in GG homozygotes)
- Increased methylmalonic acid (MMA) when combined with B12 deficiency
- Reduced methionine synthesis
- Lower SAM production
- More pronounced effect when combined with MTHFR variants

### 2.3 B12-Dependent Remethylation

The MTR reaction illustrates the B12-folate interdependence:

```
Homocysteine + 5-MTHF ---[MTR + MeCbl]---> Methionine + THF
```

**Critical Clinical Principle:**
B12 deficiency impairs MTR regardless of folate status. In B12 deficiency:
- 5-MTHF cannot be converted back to THF (folate trap)
- Serum folate may be normal or elevated (folate trapping)
- Functional folate deficiency occurs despite normal levels
- Homocysteine rises
- Methylmalonic acid (MMA) accumulates (B12-specific marker)

### 2.4 Homocysteine Metabolism

The MTR variant affects homocysteine metabolism in a B12-dependent manner:

| Context | Hcy Effect | Clinical Significance |
|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| MTR 2756GG + normal B12 | Mild Hcy elevation (+10-15%) | Low psychiatric risk |
| MTR 2756GG + low B12 | Significant Hcy elevation (+40-60%) | Depression, cognitive risk |
| MTR 2756GG + MTHFR 677TT | Additive Hcy elevation | High depression risk |
| MTR 2756GG + MTHFR + low B12 | Severe Hcy elevation | Vascular, cognitive, mood risk |

### 2.5 Cognitive Function

MTR variants have been studied in cognitive aging and dementia:

| Outcome | Association | Evidence Grade |
|---------|------------|----------------|
| Mild cognitive impairment | 2756G carriers at increased risk | C |
| Alzheimer's disease | Weak association; stronger with B12 deficiency | C |
| Vascular dementia | Moderate via Hcy elevation | C |
| Depression with cognitive features | 2756G + low B12 increases risk | C |
| Post-stroke depression | MTR + elevated Hcy increases risk | C |

**Clinical Note:** The MTR 2756G variant primarily manifests clinically in the context of B12 insufficiency. Testing B12 levels is essential before interpreting MTR genetic results.

### 2.6 Clinical Management

| Scenario | Intervention | Rationale |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| 2756AG/GG + normal B12, Hcy | B12 500-1000 mcg/day preventive | Support MTR function |
| 2756AG/GG + low B12 | B12 1000 mcg/day IM or SL | Restore cofactor |
| 2756AG/GG + elevated Hcy | B12 + L-methylfolate + B6 | Combined remethylation support |
| 2756GG + MTHFR variant | Comprehensive methylation support | Additive genetic burden |

## 3. MTRR (Methionine Synthase Reductase) A66G

### 3.1 Overview

Methionine synthase reductase (MTRR) is a flavoprotein that maintains MTR in its active methylcobalamin form by catalyzing the reductive methylation of cob(II)alamin to methylcobalamin. It is essential for B12 recycling in the remethylation cycle. Without functional MTRR, the MTR enzyme rapidly becomes inactive due to oxidation of its B12 cofactor.

The MTRR gene is located on chromosome 5p15.31. It is particularly sensitive to oxidative stress, and its function is maintained by NADPH and flavodoxin.

### 3.2 Key Variant: A66G (rs1801394)

**Genomic Context:**
- **Chromosome:** 5p15.31
- **Amino Acid Change:** Isoleucine 22 → Methionine (p.Ile22Met)
- **MAF:** ~25-45% global

**Functional Impact:**

| Genotype | Frequency | Effect |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| 66AA (wild-type) | ~30-50% | Normal B12 recycling |
| 66AG (heterozygote) | ~40-45% | 15-20% reduced recycling |
| 66GG (homozygote) | ~10-20% | 30-35% reduced recycling |

### 3.3 B12 Recycling

The MTRR enzyme performs a critical maintenance function:

```
MTR-cob(II)alamin + SAM ---[MTRR + NADPH]---> MTR-methylcobalamin + SAH
```

When MTRR function is impaired:
- MTR becomes progressively inactivated
- B12 is trapped in the inactive cob(II)alamin form
- Homocysteine accumulates
- Methionine synthesis decreases
- SAM production falls

### 3.4 Combined Effects with MTHFR

| Compound het + MTRR 66GG | ~3-5% | 1.7-2.0 | +45-70% |

**Clinical Implication:** Patients with combined MTHFR + MTRR variants have substantially higher methylation impairment than either variant alone. These patients benefit most from comprehensive methylation support.

<a name="4-cbs"></a>
## 4. CBS (Cystathionine Beta-Synthase)

### 4.1 Overview

Cystathionine beta-synthase (CBS) is a pyridoxal-5'-phosphate (PLP, vitamin B6) dependent enzyme that catalyzes the first and rate-limiting step of the transsulfuration pathway: the conversion of homocysteine and serine to cystathionine. This pathway diverts homocysteine away from remethylation toward cysteine synthesis, glutathione production, and sulfur metabolism.

The CBS gene is located on chromosome 21q22.3. Importantly, CBS is upregulated by S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) -- creating a regulatory link between methylation demand and transsulfuration flux. When methylation demand is high, SAM levels rise, activating CBS and diverting homocysteine toward cysteine and glutathione.

### 4.2 Key Variants

#### 4.2.1 844ins68 (rs5742905)

A 68-base pair insertion in exon 8 that creates a functional variant:

| Genotype | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| No insertion (wild-type) | Standard activity |
| Heterozygous (1 copy) | 10-15% increased CBS activity; lower Hcy |
| Homozygous (2 copies) | 20-25% increased activity; lowest Hcy |

**Psychiatric Relevance:** The 844ins68 insertion is generally protective against elevated homocysteine and its psychiatric consequences. Carriers have:
- Lower baseline homocysteine
- Reduced depression risk (OR 0.7-0.8)
- Better antioxidant status (increased glutathione synthesis)
- Improved sulfur amino acid metabolism

#### 4.2.2 T833C (CBS T833C)

A less common variant with functional consequences:

| Genotype | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| 833TT (wild-type) | Normal activity |
| 833TC | Mildly reduced activity |
| 833CC | Reduced activity; elevated Hcy |

### 4.3 Transsulfuration Pathway

```
Homocysteine + Serine ---[CBS + B6]---> Cystathionine
Cystathionine ---[CSE]---> Cysteine + alpha-ketobutyrate
Cysteine + Glutamate + Glycine ---[GCL/GS]---> Glutathione (GSH)
```

**Psychiatric Relevance of Transsulfuration:**

| Product | Psychiatric Function |
|---------|---------------------|
| Cysteine | Glutamate modulation, NMDA receptor redox site |
| Glutathione | Primary brain antioxidant; oxidative stress defense |
| Taurine | GABA-A receptor modulation; anxiolytic |
| Hydrogen sulfide (H2S) | Neuromodulator; NMDA receptor modulation |
| Sulfate | Neurotransmitter sulfation (dopamine, serotonin) |

### 4.4 Sulfur Metabolism

CBS function is critical for brain sulfur metabolism:

**Glutathione and Psychiatry:**
- Glutathione is the most abundant antioxidant in the brain
- Oxidative stress is implicated in depression, schizophrenia, and bipolar disorder
- Reduced brain glutathione has been demonstrated in MDD (post-mortem studies)
- CBS upregulation via N-acetylcysteine (NAC) supplementation shows antidepressant effects
- Glutathione redox state modulates NMDA receptor function

**Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S):**
- CBS is a major source of H2S in the brain
- H2S acts as a gasotransmitter (like NO and CO)
- Modulates NMDA receptors and long-term potentiation
- Neuroprotective at physiological levels
- May be dysregulated in depression and cognitive disorders

### 4.5 Clinical Management

| CBS Variant | Nutritional Strategy | Expected Benefit |
|-------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| 844ins68 (protective) | Standard nutrition | Maintains low Hcy |
| T833C | B6 25-50 mg/day | Enhance residual activity |
| Any variant + elevated Hcy | NAC 600-1200 mg/day | Support glutathione, cysteine |
| Reduced CBS function | B6 + NAC + magnesium | Comprehensive support |

**Note:** Severe CBS deficiency causes classical homocystinuria (a rare inborn error), presenting with intellectual disability, psychiatric symptoms, thromboembolism, and lens dislocation. Heterozygous carriers have milder phenotypes.

## 5. COMT Val158Met & Methylation Interface

### 5.1 Overview

Catechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT) is a magnesium-dependent enzyme that catalyzes the methylation and inactivation of catecholamine neurotransmitters (dopamine, norepinephrine, epinephrine) and catechol estrogens. It is the primary mechanism for dopamine degradation in the prefrontal cortex, where dopamine transporters (DAT) are sparse.

The COMT gene is located on chromosome 22q11.21. The Val158Met (rs4680) polymorphism is the most studied functional variant, affecting enzyme thermostability and activity.

### 5.2 Val158Met (rs4680) Variant

**Genomic Context:**
- **Position:** GRCh38: chr22:19,963,748
- **Amino Acid Change:** Valine 158 → Methionine (p.Val158Met)
- **MAF:** ~40-50% global (Met allele)

**Functional Impact:**

| Genotype | Frequency | Enzyme Activity | Dopamine Degradation | Prefrontal Dopamine |
|----------|-----------|----------------|---------------------|--------------------|
| Val/Val (GG) | ~25-30% | High (100%) | Rapid | Low |
| Val/Met (AG) | ~45-50% | Intermediate (60-75%) | Moderate | Intermediate |
| Met/Met (AA) | ~20-25% | Low (25-40%) | Slow | High |

**Neuropsychiatric Associations:**

| Domain | Val/Val | Val/Met | Met/Met |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|
| Executive function | Moderate | Good | Variable (optimal dopamine) |
| Working memory | Lower | Optimal | Variable |
| Cognitive flexibility | Higher | Moderate | Lower (rigid) |
| Stress resilience | Lower (dopamine depletion) | Moderate | Higher |
| Pain sensitivity | Higher | Moderate | Lower |
| Placebo response | Lower | Moderate | Higher |
| Emotional processing | Enhanced negative bias | Balanced | Enhanced positive bias |

### 5.3 Methylation of Dopamine, Norepinephrine, Epinephrine

COMT uses SAM as the methyl donor for catecholamine inactivation:

```
Dopamine + SAM ---[COMT + Mg2+]---> 3-Methoxytyramine + SAH
Norepinephrine + SAM ---[COMT]---> Normetanephrine + SAH
Epinephrine + SAM ---[COMT]---> Metanephrine + SAH
```

**Critical Methylation Interface:**
- Each COMT methylation reaction consumes one SAM molecule
- High COMT activity (Val/Val) creates higher methylation demand
- SAM depletion from high COMT activity can compromise other methylation reactions
- SAH produced by COMT inhibits other methyltransferases
- The COMT-MTHFR interaction determines whether methylation demand can be met

### 5.4 MTHFR-COMT Interaction

The interaction between MTHFR and COMT is one of the most important gene-gene interactions in psychiatric pharmacogenomics:

| MTHFR | COMT | Combined Effect | Clinical Phenotype |
|-------|------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Normal (677CC) | Val/Val | High methylation demand, adequate supply | Prefrontal hypodopaminergia; may benefit from stimulants |
| Normal | Met/Met | Low methylation demand, adequate supply | Prefrontal hyperdopaminergia; stimulant sensitivity |
| 677TT | Val/Val | High methylation demand, impaired supply | **Worst combination**: severe methylation stress, low prefrontal DA, depression risk |
| 677TT | Met/Met | Low methylation demand, impaired supply | Moderate risk; some protection from low COMT |
| Compound het | Val/Val | High demand, reduced supply | Significant depression and cognitive risk |
| Compound het | Met/Met | Low demand, reduced supply | Mild-moderate risk |

**Clinical Significance:**

| Combined Genotype | Depression Risk | Cognitive Profile | Medication Considerations |
|-------------------|----------------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| MTHFR 677TT + COMT Val/Val | **Highest (2-3x)** | Poor executive function, working memory deficits | Requires L-methylfolate + may need dopaminergic agents |
| MTHFR 677TT + COMT Met/Met | Moderate-High (1.5-2x) | Variable cognition; may have anxiety | L-methylfolate essential; cautious with dopaminergics |
| MTHFR normal + COMT Val/Val | Moderate (1.2-1.5x) | Executive deficits | Methylation support helpful; dopaminergics may help |
| MTHFR normal + COMT Met/Met | Baseline | Enhanced prefrontal function | Standard treatment; stimulants may cause overstimulation |

### 5.5 Cognitive Flexibility

COMT genotype significantly affects cognitive flexibility and executive function:

**Prefrontal Dopamine Inverted-U:**
- Too little dopamine (Val/Val): poor working memory, cognitive inflexibility
- Optimal dopamine (Val/Met): best executive function
- Too much dopamine (Met/Met): cognitive rigidity, impaired filtering

**Psychiatric Implications:**
- COMT Val/Val + stress = increased risk of depression and PTSD (dopamine depletion)
- COMT Met/Met + chronic stress = increased anxiety, OCD symptoms (dopamine accumulation)
- COMT genotype moderates response to cognitive behavioral therapy
- COMT affects working memory training outcomes

### 5.6 Stress Response

COMT genotype moderates the relationship between stress and psychiatric outcomes:

| Context | Val/Val | Val/Met | Met/Met |
|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| Acute stress | Greater cortisol response | Moderate response | Lower cortisol response |
| Chronic stress | Higher depression/anxiety risk | Moderate risk | Lower depression risk |
| Trauma exposure | Higher PTSD risk | Intermediate | Lower PTSD risk |
| Childhood adversity | Greatest psychiatric risk | Intermediate | Lower risk (resilience) |

**Mechanism:** Under stress, catecholamine production increases. Val/Val individuals rapidly degrade dopamine, leading to prefrontal hypodopaminergia and executive dysfunction. Met/Met individuals maintain higher dopamine levels, providing some stress resilience but at the cost of potential overstimulation.

### 5.7 Medication Response

| Medication Class | Val/Val Response | Met/Met Response | Clinical Strategy |
|-----------------|-----------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Methylphenidate | Good (increases prefrontal DA) | Variable/mixed (already high DA) | Val/Val: standard dose; Met/Met: lower dose or avoid |
| Amphetamine | Good | May cause overstimulation | Met/Met: lowest effective dose |
| Modafinil | Good | Moderate | Both genotypes respond |
| SSRIs | Standard | Possibly better (enhanced 5-HT-DA interaction) | No major adjustment |
| Atypical antipsychotics | May need higher dose | May need lower dose | Adjust by clinical response |
| L-methylfolate | Highly beneficial | Moderately beneficial | All benefit; Val/Val + MTHFR variants most |

### 5.8 COMT in Estrogen-Mediated Psychiatry

COMT activity is modulated by estrogen:
- Estrogen downregulates COMT transcription
- During high-estrogen phases (follicular, pregnancy), all women have reduced COMT activity
- This creates a state similar to Met/Met genotype
- Psychiatric medication response may vary across menstrual cycle
- Perimenopausal COMT changes may affect mood stability

**Clinical Implication:** COMT genotype matters most during low-estrogen phases (luteal, postpartum, postmenopausal), when endogenous COMT suppression is absent.

## 6. BHMT (Betaine-Homocysteine Methyltransferase)

### 6.1 Overview

Betaine-homocysteine methyltransferase (BHMT) catalyzes an alternative remethylation pathway that converts homocysteine to methionine using betaine (trimethylglycine) as the methyl donor. Unlike the MTHFR/MTR pathway, BHMT is:
- B12-independent
- Primarily hepatic (liver-specific)
- Upregulated by high methionine intake
- Active in the liver and kidney cortex, not the CNS
- A backup pathway when the primary remethylation is compromised

The BHMT gene is located on chromosome 5q13.1-13.4. A related gene, BHMT2, is also expressed in liver and may have overlapping function.

### 6.2 Key Variants

#### BHMT R239Q (rs3733890)
- **Amino Acid Change:** Arginine 239 → Glutamine
- **MAF:** ~30-45% (Q allele)
- **Effect:** Moderately reduced enzyme activity; 10-20% increase in homocysteine

#### BHMT G742A (rs585800)
- **Amino Acid Change:** Alanine → Threonine
- **MAF:** ~5-15%
- **Effect:** Mildly reduced activity

### 6.3 Alternative Remethylation Pathway

```
Homocysteine + Betaine ---[BHMT]---> Methionine + Dimethylglycine (DMG)
```

**Comparison of Remethylation Pathways:**

| Feature | MTHFR/MTR Pathway | BHMT Pathway |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|
| Location | Ubiquitous (CNS active) | Liver, kidney (not CNS) |
| Methyl donor | 5-MTHF | Betaine |
| B12 requirement | Yes (essential) | No |
| Folate requirement | Yes | No |
| Betaine requirement | No | Yes |
| SAM feedback | Inhibited by SAM | Less regulated |
| Primary function | Maintains CNS methylation | Peripheral Hcy clearance |
| Dietary dependence | Folate | Choline/betaine |

### 6.4 Betaine as Methyl Donor

Betaine (trimethylglycine) is derived from dietary choline oxidation:

**Dietary Sources:**
- Wheat bran, wheat germ (~1,300 mg/100g)
- Spinach (~600-700 mg/100g)
- Beets (~300 mg/100g)
- Quinoa (~400 mg/100g)
- Shellfish (~200-300 mg/100g)

**Clinical Dosing:**
| Indication | Dose | Evidence |
|-----------|------|----------|
| Homocysteine reduction | 3-6 g/day | Grade B |
| Non-alcoholic fatty liver | 10-20 g/day | Grade B |
| MTHFR variant support | 1-3 g/day | Grade C |
| Athletic performance | 2-5 g/day | Grade C |

### 6.5 Liver-Specific Function

BHMT's liver-specific expression has important implications:

1. **Systemic Hcy Clearance:** BHMT clears homocysteine from the bloodstream even when MTHFR/MTR is compromised
2. **Does NOT Directly Support CNS Methylation:** The brain relies primarily on MTHFR/MTR for methylation support
3. **Peripheral-Central Disconnect:** Improving liver Hcy clearance may not normalize brain methylation
4. **Betaine Supplementation Benefits:**
   - Reduces systemic homocysteine
   - Supports liver function (osmoprotectant)
   - May provide methyl groups indirectly via SAM production
   - Supports phosphatidylcholine synthesis

### 6.6 Clinical Relevance in Psychiatry

| Application | Rationale | Evidence Grade |
|------------|-----------|----------------|
| Adjunctive Hcy lowering | Alternative pathway support | C |
| Liver support in psychiatric patients | Hepatoprotective | C |
| NAFLD with depression | Dual liver-mood benefit | C |
| MTHFR variant adjunct | Peripheral methylation support | C |
| Choline augmentation for cognition | Precursor to acetylcholine, betaine | B |

### 6.7 BHMT-MTHFR Synergy

In patients with severe MTHFR variants (677TT), BHMT pathway upregulation may provide partial compensation:

| Strategy | Effect on Hcy | Effect on Brain Methylation |
|----------|--------------|---------------------------|
| Betaine supplementation | Reduces plasma Hcy 10-30% | Indirect (via SAM) |
| Choline supplementation | Moderate Hcy reduction | Supports acetylcholine |
| Combined betaine + L-methylfolate | Synergistic Hcy reduction | Direct CNS + peripheral support |

**Clinical Recommendation:** Betaine (1-3 g/day) can be added to L-methylfolate and B12 regimens for comprehensive methylation support, especially in severe MTHFR variants with persistent homocysteine elevation.

## 7. FOLR1/FOLR2 (Folate Receptors)

### 7.1 Overview

Folate receptors (FOLR1, FOLR2, FOLR3, FOLR4) are high-affinity folate-binding glycoproteins that mediate cellular folate uptake through receptor-mediated endocytosis. FOLR1 (also known as the alpha-folate receptor or FR-alpha) is particularly critical for folate transport across the blood-brain barrier and into the CNS.

Folate receptor alpha (FOLR1) is expressed on:
- Choroid plexus epithelium (CNS folate transport)
- Placenta (maternal-fetal folate transfer)
- Kidney proximal tubules
- Developing neural tube

### 7.2 FOLR1 Variants

**Key Variants:**

| Variant | rsID | Effect | Clinical Significance |
|---------|------|--------|----------------------|
| c.772C>T | rs2071010 | Silent/splicing | Variable folate transport |
| c.3T>C | rs101 ans | 5' UTR | Altered translation efficiency |
| c.25A>G | rs1040404 | Thr9Ala | Reduced binding affinity |
| c.432C>T | rs1788313 | Silent | Possible splicing effect |
| c.12T>C | rs1788314 | Silent | Variable expression |

**FOLR1 Autoantibodies:**
Autoantibodies against FOLR1 (FR-alpha autoantibodies) are clinically significant:
- Present in ~40-70% of children with autism spectrum disorder
- Associated with cerebral folate deficiency
- May develop postnatally or during pregnancy
- Treatable with high-dose folinic acid (calcium folinate)

### 7.3 Folate Transport into Brain

**Blood-Brain Barrier Folate Transport:**

```
5-MTHF (blood) ---[FOLR1 on choroid plexus]---> Endocytosis ---> Release into CSF
```

**Critical Features:**
- FOLR1 is the primary mechanism for folate entry into CSF
- Folate levels in CSF are typically 2-3x higher than plasma (active transport)
- Impaired FOLR1 function causes cerebral folate deficiency despite normal blood folate
- The blood-brain barrier creates a separate folate compartment

### 7.4 Autoimmune Encephalitis

FOLR1 autoantibodies cause an autoimmune encephalitis syndrome:

**Clinical Features:**
- Seizures (often intractable)
- Progressive ataxia
- Psychomotor retardation
- Movement disorders
- Psychiatric symptoms (irritability, psychosis)
- Optic neuropathy

**Diagnostic Workup:**
- CSF 5-MTHF (low, <30 nmol/L; normal >60)
- Serum FR-alpha autoantibodies (positive)
- Normal serum folate (key distinguishing feature)
- Brain MRI (may show white matter changes)

**Treatment:**
- High-dose folinic acid (calcium folinate) 0.5-2 mg/kg/day
- May add corticosteroids or immunoglobulins if autoimmune
- Early treatment prevents irreversible neurologic damage

### 7.5 Cerebral Folate Deficiency

Cerebral folate deficiency (CFD) is a neurologic condition characterized by low CSF 5-MTHF with normal serum folate:

| Cause | Mechanism | Treatment |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| FOLR1 autoantibodies | Autoimmune block of folate transport | Folinic acid + immunotherapy |
| FOLR1 mutations | Genetic transport defect | Folinic acid (high dose) |
| Mitochondrial disorders | Energy failure impairs transport | Folinic acid + mitochondrial support |
| Drugs (valproate, barbiturates) | Competitive inhibition | Folinic acid; consider drug change |
| Aging | Reduced choroid plexus FOLR1 | Folinic acid or L-methylfolate |

**Psychiatric Relevance of CFD:**
- Depression (refractory to standard treatment)
- Cognitive decline
- Psychosis (paranoid features)
- Anxiety disorders
- Autism spectrum features
- Sleep disturbances

### 7.6 FOLR2

FOLR2 (beta-folate receptor) is expressed on:
- Placental syncytiotrophoblasts
- Hematopoietic cells
- Some cancer cells

Its psychiatric relevance is primarily through:
- Placental folate transfer (pregnancy outcomes)
- Potential role in neuroinflammation
- Folate scavenging in the bloodstream

### 7.7 Clinical Testing

| Test | Indication | Normal Range |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| Serum FR-alpha autoantibodies | CFD, autism, autoimmune encephalitis | Negative |
| CSF 5-MTHF | CFD diagnosis | >60 nmol/L |
| Serum:CSF folate ratio | CFD screening | <3:1 suggests CFD |
| FOLR1 genetic testing | Familial CFD | Sequence analysis |

### 7.8 Treatment Considerations

| Condition | First-Line Treatment | Dose | Response |
|-----------|---------------------|------|----------|
| FOLR1 autoantibody encephalitis | Folinic acid | 0.5-2 mg/kg/day | Often dramatic |
| CFD with FOLR1 mutations | Folinic acid | 1-3 mg/kg/day | Stabilization |
| CFD with mitochondrial disease | Folinic acid + CoQ10, etc. | Variable | Partial |

- L-methylfolate (5-MTHF) = active circulating folate form
- Folinic acid (5-formyl-THF) = bypasses DHFR, not FOLR1-dependent for entry
- In FOLR1 dysfunction, folinic acid may enter via alternative transporters
- High-dose L-methylfolate may partially overcome receptor dysfunction

## 8. DHFR (Dihydrofolate Reductase)

### 8.1 Overview

Dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR) catalyzes the NADPH-dependent reduction of dihydrofolate (DHF) to tetrahydrofolate (THF). This is a critical step in folate metabolism because:
- THF is the entry point for all one-carbon metabolism
- DHF is a byproduct of thymidylate synthase (DNA synthesis)
- Without DHFR, DHF accumulates and inhibits other folate-dependent enzymes
- DHFR is the pharmacological target of methotrexate and trimethoprim

The DHFR gene is located on chromosome 5q14.1.

### 8.2 Key Variants

| Variant | rsID | Effect | Clinical Significance |
|---------|------|--------|----------------------|
| c.594+59G>A (19bp deletion) | rs70991108 | Reduced expression | Altered antifolate sensitivity |
| c.707T>C (Val232Ala) | rs10106 | Reduced stability | Moderate folate impairment |
| c.161G>A (Cys5Tyr) | rs17883132 | Structural change | Rare; variable effect |
| c.452T>G (Val151Gly) | rs17883133 | Reduced activity | Increased antifolate toxicity |
| c.1-10_1-9insG | rs17883134 | Promoter | Variable expression |

### 8.3 Dihydrofolate to Tetrahydrofolate

```
Folic acid ---[DHFR]---> DHF ---[DHFR]---> THF ---[MTHFR]---> 5-MTHF
Dietary folates -----> Various forms ---> THF ---> 5-MTHF
```

**Key Concept:** Folic acid (the synthetic form in supplements and fortification) requires DHFR for conversion to THF. Natural food folates enter at various points in the pathway. DHFR has relatively low activity in humans compared to other species, making the conversion of folic acid to active forms inefficient at high doses.

### 8.4 Methotrexate Target

Methotrexate (MTX) is a potent DHFR inhibitor used in:
- Rheumatoid arthritis
- Psoriasis
- Cancer chemotherapy
- Ectopic pregnancy
- Off-label in some autoimmune CNS conditions

**Mechanism of MTX Neurotoxicity:**
- MTX inhibits DHFR, blocking THF synthesis
- Without THF, 5-MTHF cannot be produced
- Without 5-MTHF, homocysteine remethylation fails
- Homocysteine accumulates (direct neurotoxicity)
- Without THF, purine and pyrimidine synthesis is impaired
- Adenosine accumulates (neuroprotective at low levels, toxic at high levels)
- Myelosuppression, mucositis, hepatotoxicity

**Psychiatric Manifestations of MTX Toxicity:**
- Mood changes (depression, irritability)
- Cognitive impairment ("methotrexate fog")
- Leukoencephalopathy (MRI white matter changes)
- Stroke-like episodes (rare)
- Fatigue, malaise

### 8.5 Antifolate Sensitivity

DHFR variants can modulate antifolate drug response:

| DHFR Variant | MTX Effect | Clinical Management |
|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Wild-type | Standard response | Standard dosing |
| Reduced expression variants | Increased sensitivity | Dose reduction needed |
| Structural variants | Variable response | Monitor levels closely |

**Combined with MTHFR Variants:**

| Combination | MTX Toxicity Risk | Management |
|-------------|------------------|------------|
| MTHFR 677TT alone | High | Leucovorin rescue; L-methylfolate |
| DHFR variant alone | Moderate | Dose adjustment |
| MTHFR 677TT + DHFR variant | Very high | Avoid MTX if possible; intensive folate support |

### 8.6 Unmetabolized Folic Acid (UMFA)

High doses of folic acid can overwhelm DHFR capacity, leading to UMFA in circulation:

| Folic Acid Dose | UMFA Detection | Clinical Concern |
|-----------------|---------------|------------------|
| <400 mcg/day | Minimal | None |
| 400-1000 mcg/day | Occasional | Low |
| 1-5 mg/day | Common in MTHFR variants | Possible immune effects; blocks FOLR1 |
| >5 mg/day | Universal | Potential for adverse effects |

**Psychiatric Relevance:**
- UMFA may compete with 5-MTHF for folate transporters
- UMFA has been associated with reduced natural killer cell activity
- In MTHFR variants, folic acid may be preferable to avoid (use L-methylfolate instead)
- Some evidence suggests UMFA may interfere with antidepressant response

### 8.7 Clinical Recommendations

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Patient on MTX with mood changes | Check MTHFR, Hcy, B12, folate | Identify methylation impairment |
| MTHFR 677TT needing folate | L-methylfolate preferred | Bypasses DHFR bottleneck |
| High-dose folic acid | Switch to L-methylfolate | Avoids UMFA accumulation |
| MTX + MTHFR variant | Leucovorin 5 mg every 2-3 days | Active folate rescue |
| MTX neurotoxicity | Hold MTX; L-methylfolate 15 mg | Reverse methylation block |

#### 9.1.3 Reduced Folate Carrier Function

```
5-MTHF (plasma) ---[RFC1]---> Cellular 5-MTHF (cytoplasm)
Folic acid ---[RFC1]---> Cellular folate
MTX ---[RFC1]---> Cellular MTX (toxic)
```

**RFC1 in Psychiatry:**
- Reduced RFC1 function limits cellular folate availability
- May exacerbate MTHFR variant effects
- RFC1 variants have been associated with NTD risk
- May affect L-methylfolate cellular uptake at the blood-brain barrier

| Psychiatric Condition | RFC1 Association | Evidence Grade |
|----------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Depression | 80G carriers may have lower cellular folate | C |
| Neural tube defects | 80G increases risk 1.2-1.5x | B |
| Autism | Possible association with RFC1 variants | C |
| MTX neurotoxicity | 80G carriers at higher risk | C |

#### 9.1.4 Clinical Implications

| RFC1 Status | Folate Strategy | Rationale |
|-------------|----------------|-----------|
| 80AA | Standard | Normal uptake |
| 80AG | Higher-dose or L-methylfolate | Compensate for reduced uptake |
| 80GG | L-methylfolate preferred | Maximize available substrate |
| 80GG + MTHFR 677TT | High-dose L-methylfolate (15 mg) | Dual bottleneck |

### 9.2 GGH (Gamma-Glutamyl Hydrolase)

#### 9.2.1 Overview
Gamma-glutamyl hydrolase (GGH), also called folylpolyglutamate hydrolase or conjugase, is a lysosomal enzyme that removes polyglutamate chains from intracellular folates. This is the opposite enzymatic activity to folylpolyglutamyl synthetase (FPGS), which adds glutamate residues.

**The Polyglutamation Cycle:**
- FPGS adds glutamate residues to folates (traps them intracellularly)
- Polyglutamated folates are the active cofactors for one-carbon metabolism
- GGH removes glutamate residues (releases folates from cells)
- The balance between FPGS and GGH determines intracellular folate retention

#### 9.2.2 Key Variants

| Variant | rsID | Effect | Clinical Significance |
|---------|------|--------|----------------------|
| T452C (Cys155Thr) | rs11545078 | Altered enzyme stability | Variable folate retention |
| G916T (Ala303Ser) | rs1800909 | Reduced activity | Increased intracellular folate |
| C401T | rs3780130 | Promoter | Variable expression |

#### 9.2.3 Polyglutamation

Polyglutamation is critical for:
- **Intracellular folate retention:** Polyglutamated folates cannot cross cell membranes
- **Enzyme cofactor activity:** Most folate-dependent enzymes prefer polyglutamated substrates
- **Cellular folate concentration:** Enables 100-1000x higher intracellular vs extracellular folate

**GGH Function:**
```
Folate-polyglutamate (intracellular) ---[GGH]---> Folate-monoglutamate ---> Exit cell
```

#### 9.2.4 Cellular Folate Retention

| GGH Activity | Intracellular Folate | Clinical Effect |
|-------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| High GGH | Rapid turnover, lower retention | May require higher folate intake |
| Low GGH | Slower turnover, higher retention | More efficient folate utilization |
| GGH variant | Variable retention | Affects antifolate response |

**Psychiatric Relevance:**
- GGH variants affect how efficiently cells retain folate
- High GGH activity may increase folate requirements
- Combined with RFC1 and MTHFR variants, GGH can be a third bottleneck
- May influence response to L-methylfolate supplementation

#### 9.2.5 Combined Transport/Processing Effects

| Combination | Effect on Cellular Folate | Strategy |
|-------------|--------------------------|----------|
| RFC1 80GG + MTHFR 677TT | Severely impaired | High-dose L-methylfolate 15 mg |
| RFC1 80GG + GGH high activity | Rapid loss, poor uptake | Divided doses, high total dose |
| MTHFR 677TT + GGH high activity | Low production + rapid loss | L-methylfolate + frequent dosing |
| Triple combination (rare) | Profound folate deficiency | Aggressive supplementation, monitoring |

<a name="10-homocysteine"></a>
## 10. Homocysteine Clinical Thresholds

### 10.1 Definition and Measurement

Homocysteine (Hcy) is a sulfur-containing amino acid formed as an intermediate in methionine metabolism. It exists in plasma in several forms:
- **Free Hcy:** ~1% (rapidly oxidized)
- **Protein-bound Hcy:** ~70-80% (disulfide-linked to albumin)
- **Oxidized Hcy dimers:** ~20-30% (homocystine, mixed disulfides)

Total plasma homocysteine (tHcy) is the clinically measured parameter, typically after fasting.

**Analytical Considerations:**
- Fasting sample preferred (methionine load from meals transiently elevates Hcy)
- EDTA plasma, separated within 30 minutes
- Stable 4-6 hours at room temperature, longer if refrigerated
- Reference methods: HPLC, immunoassay, enzymatic assay

### 10.2 Clinical Thresholds

#### 10.2.1 Standard Cardiovascular Classification

| Category | tHcy (umol/L) | Clinical Significance | Action |
|----------|--------------|----------------------|--------|
| Optimal | <10 | Ideal methylation status | Maintain |
| Normal | 10-12 | Acceptable | Monitor annually |
| Moderate elevation | 12-15 | Early methylation compromise | Lifestyle + nutritional intervention |
| Intermediate | 15-30 | Significant impairment | Active intervention required |
| Severe | >30 | High-risk; rule out genetic/inherited causes | Urgent evaluation; may indicate homocystinuria |

#### 10.2.2 Psychiatric-Specific Thresholds

Based on emerging evidence, lower thresholds may be more appropriate for psychiatric populations:

| tHcy (umol/L) | Psychiatric Risk | Recommended Action |
|---------------|-----------------|--------------------|
| <8 | Optimal for brain health | None |
| 8-10 | Normal | Annual monitoring in high-risk patients |
| 10-12 | Mild increase in depression risk | B-vitamin optimization |
| 12-15 | Moderate depression risk (OR ~1.3) | Active B-vitamin therapy |
| 15-20 | Significant depression risk (OR ~1.5-2.0) | Comprehensive methylation support |
| 20-30 | High risk for depression, cognitive decline (OR ~2-3) | Aggressive intervention + genetic testing |
| >30 | Very high risk; consider inborn errors | Full metabolic workup |

### 10.3 Psychiatric Relevance

#### 10.3.1 Depression

**Meta-Analytic Evidence:**
- Coppen & Bolander-Gouaille (2005): Hcy >12.5 umol/L associated with depression (OR 1.42)
- Almeida et al. (2015): Hcy elevation predicts antidepressant non-response
- Papakostas et al. (2012): Hcy >12 associated with treatment resistance
- Beydoun et al. (2010): Hcy positively correlated with depressive symptom severity

**Mechanism:**
1. Hcy inhibits methylation (via SAH accumulation)
2. Hcy is neurotoxic (NMDA agonism, oxidative stress)
3. Hcy causes cerebrovascular dysfunction (white matter hyperintensities)
4. Hcy is pro-inflammatory (cytokine activation)
5. Hcy impairs BDNF signaling

#### 10.3.2 Cognitive Decline

| Hcy Level | Cognitive Effect | Evidence |
|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| >15 umol/L | 2x increased dementia risk | Seshadri et al. 2002 |
| >14 umol/L | Accelerated brain atrophy | Smith et al. 2010 (VITACOG) |
| >11 umol/L | Impaired episodic memory | McCaddon 2006 |
| >20 umol/L | Marked cognitive impairment | Ravaglia et al. 2005 |

**VITACOG Trial (Smith et al. 2010):**
- B-vitamin supplementation (B6, B12, folic acid) slowed brain atrophy
- Benefit confined to participants with Hcy >11 umol/L
- 30% reduction in atrophy rate in treatment group vs placebo
- **Grade A evidence for Hcy-lowering in cognitive protection**

#### 10.3.3 Bipolar Disorder

- Elevated Hcy found in manic and depressive episodes
- Hcy correlates with episode frequency
- Lithium may elevate Hcy through effects on inositol metabolism
- B-vitamin supplementation may stabilize mood in some patients

#### 10.3.4 Schizophrenia

- Moderately elevated Hcy in first-episode and chronic schizophrenia
- May contribute to negative symptoms and cognitive deficits
- Clozapine may elevate Hcy through folate metabolism effects
- NAC (glutathione precursor) shows benefit in negative symptoms

#### 10.3.5 PTSD and Trauma

- Elevated Hcy in PTSD populations
- May reflect chronic stress-induced methylation disruption
- Hcy elevation correlates with hippocampal volume reduction
- B-vitamin support may augment trauma-focused therapy

### 10.4 Homocysteine-Lowering Interventions

| Intervention | Dose | Expected Hcy Reduction | Timeline |
|-------------|------|----------------------|----------|
| Folic acid | 0.4-5 mg/day | 15-25% | 4-8 weeks |
| L-methylfolate | 0.8-15 mg/day | 20-35% | 4-8 weeks |
| Vitamin B12 (oral) | 500-1000 mcg/day | 10-20% | 8-12 weeks |
| Vitamin B12 (IM) | 1000 mcg/week x 4 | 20-35% | 4-8 weeks |
| Vitamin B6 (P5P) | 25-50 mg/day | 10-15% | 4-8 weeks |
| Betaine | 3-6 g/day | 20-40% | 2-4 weeks |
| NAC | 600-1200 mg/day | 5-10% | 4-8 weeks |
| Combined B-vitamins | All above | 30-50% | 4-8 weeks |

**Clinical Target:** Hcy <10 umol/L for optimal psychiatric outcomes

<a name="11-b12"></a>
## 11. B12 (Cobalamin) Pathway

### 11.1 Overview

Vitamin B12 (cobalamin) is an essential water-soluble vitamin that serves as a cofactor for two critical enzymes:
1. **Methionine synthase (MTR):** Homocysteine remethylation (requires methylcobalamin)
2. **Methylmalonyl-CoA mutase:** Methylmalonyl-CoA to succinyl-CoA (requires adenosylcobalamin)

B12 deficiency impairs both pathways, causing:
- Elevated homocysteine (from MTR blockade)
- Elevated methylmalonic acid (from methylmalonyl-CoA mutase blockade)
- Megaloblastic anemia
- Neurological degeneration
- Psychiatric symptoms

### 11.2 B12 Forms

#### 11.2.1 Cyanocobalamin

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Structure | Cobalamin with cyanide ligand |
| Source | Synthetic; most common supplement form |
| Conversion | Requires decyanation + conversion to active forms |
| Advantages | Stable, inexpensive, well-studied |
| Disadvantages | Requires multiple enzymatic steps; cyanide release (minimal concern) |
| Typical dose | 100-1000 mcg/day oral; 1000 mcg IM monthly |

#### 11.2.2 Methylcobalamin

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Structure | Cobalamin with methyl ligand |
| Source | Natural; supplement form |
| Conversion | Direct MTR cofactor; no conversion needed |
| Advantages | Active form; supports methylation directly; may have neuroprotective effects |
| Disadvantages | Less stable; light sensitive; more expensive |
| Typical dose | 500-5000 mcg/day SL or oral |

**Methylcobalamin vs Cyanocobalamin in Psychiatry:**

| Outcome | Methylcobalamin | Cyanocobalamin |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| Hcy reduction | Faster, greater | Effective but slower |
| Sleep quality | May improve (melatonin regulation) | Less evidence |
| Peripheral neuropathy | Superior in some studies | Standard treatment |
| Methylation support | Direct | Indirect |
| Cost | Higher | Lower |
| Recommendation for methylation disorders | **Preferred** | Acceptable if cost-limited |

#### 11.2.3 Hydroxocobalamin

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Structure | Cobalamin with hydroxo ligand |
| Source | Natural; injectable form |
| Conversion | Converted to both active forms in cells |
| Advantages | Longer half-life; better tissue retention; preferred for injections |
| Disadvantages | Not widely available orally |
| Typical dose | 1000 mcg IM weekly (loading), then monthly |

**Hydroxocobalamin is the preferred injectable form for:**
- Severe B12 deficiency
- Malabsorption (pernicious anemia, post-gastric surgery)
- Neurological manifestations
- MTHFR/MTR variant carriers
- Patients requiring long-term supplementation

#### 11.2.4 Adenosylcobalamin

- Coenzyme form for methylmalonyl-CoA mutase
- Less commonly available as supplement
- Some combination supplements include it
- Primarily relevant for energy metabolism, not methylation

### 11.3 TCN2 (Transcobalamin) Polymorphisms

Transcobalamin II (TCN2) is the plasma transport protein that delivers B12 to cells. It binds ~10-30% of circulating B12 but is the functionally active fraction.

#### 11.3.1 TCN2 C776G (rs1801198): Pro259Arg

| Genotype | Frequency | Effect |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| 776CC (wild-type) | ~55-60% | Normal B12 delivery |
| 776CG (heterozygote) | ~30-35% | 20-30% reduced B12 delivery |
| 776GG (homozygote) | ~5-10% | 40-50% reduced B12 delivery |

**Clinical Consequences:**
- Lower cellular B12 availability despite normal serum B12
- Functional B12 deficiency with "normal" B12 levels
- Elevated homocysteine and MMA despite adequate intake
- Increased depression risk in GG homozygotes

#### 11.3.2 TCN2 C67G (rs1131603)

| Genotype | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| 67CC | Normal |
| 67CG | Mildly reduced binding |
| 67GG | Reduced transcobalamin stability |

### 11.4 Intrinsic Factor (GIF)

Intrinsic factor (IF, encoded by GIF gene on chromosome 11) is essential for ileal B12 absorption:

**GIF-Related Conditions:**

| Condition | Mechanism | Treatment |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Pernicious anemia (autoimmune) | Autoantibodies destroy parietal cells/IF | Lifelong IM B12 |
| Congenital IF deficiency | GIF mutations | Lifelong IM B12 |
| Post-gastrectomy | Reduced IF production | Lifelong IM B12 |
| Atrophic gastritis | Reduced IF secretion | Oral high-dose or IM B12 |

**Psychiatric Relevance:**
- Pernicious anemia frequently presents with psychiatric symptoms
- B12 deficiency psychosis is well-documented
- Cognitive decline from B12 deficiency can mimic dementia
- Depression is present in 20-30% of B12-deficient patients

### 11.5 B12 Deficiency: Psychiatric Manifestations

| Manifestation | Prevalence in B12 Deficiency | Mechanism |
|--------------|------------------------------|-----------|
| Depression | 20-30% | Impaired methylation, Hcy elevation, monoamine synthesis |
| Cognitive impairment | 50-70% | Demyelination, Hcy neurotoxicity, MMA toxicity |
| Psychosis | 5-10% | Unknown; possibly related to methylation, monoamines |
| Irritability | 30-40% | Subclinical neurological dysfunction |
| Fatigue | 60-80% | Energy metabolism, anemia, neurological |
| Personality change | 10-20% | Frontal-subcortical dysfunction |
| Hallucinations | 5-10% | Nonspecific; may indicate severe deficiency |

**B12 Deficiency Psychosis Features:**
- Can present as paranoid features, auditory hallucinations
- Often without anemia (neuropsychiatric B12 deficiency)
- May be misdiagnosed as schizophrenia
- Dramatic response to B12 replacement
- MRI may show white matter changes

### 11.6 B12 Assessment in Psychiatry

| Test | Reference Range | Clinical Significance | Limitations |
|------|----------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Serum B12 | 200-900 pg/mL | Screening test | Low sensitivity; misses 30-50% of deficiencies |
| Holotranscobalamin (holoTC) | >35-50 pmol/L | Active B12 fraction | Better early marker |
| Methylmalonic acid (MMA) | <0.4 umol/L | Tissue B12 status | Most sensitive; false positives in renal failure |
| Homocysteine | <10-12 umol/L | Functional marker | Also elevated in folate deficiency |
| Complete blood count | Normal | Macrocytosis, anemia | Absent in 30-40% of neuropsychiatric B12 deficiency |

**Recommended Testing Algorithm:**
```
All psychiatric patients:
  -> Serum B12
  -> IF serum B12 <400 pg/mL OR symptoms suggestive:
     -> MMA + Hcy
     -> IF MMA elevated:
        -> Confirmed B12 deficiency
        -> Check intrinsic factor antibodies
        -> Investigate cause (dietary, malabsorption, drugs)
     -> IF MMA normal but Hcy elevated:
        -> Check folate, MTHFR status
        -> Consider functional B12 deficiency (TCN2 variants)
```

### 11.7 Treatment Protocols

#### 11.7.1 Oral Supplementation

| Preparation | Dose | Indication | Notes |
|-------------|------|-----------|-------|
| Cyanocobalamin | 100-1000 mcg/day | Maintenance | Standard, inexpensive |
| Methylcobalamin | 500-5000 mcg/day | MTHFR variants, methylation support | Preferred for psychiatric use |
| Sublingual methylcobalamin | 1000-5000 mcg/day | Malabsorption suspicion | Bypasses intestinal absorption |

#### 11.7.2 Injectable Supplementation

| Regimen | Indication | Protocol |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| Hydroxocobalamin IM | Severe deficiency, neurological symptoms | 1000 mcg IM daily x 7, weekly x 4, then monthly |
| Hydroxocobalamin IM | Pernicious anemia, post-gastrectomy | 1000 mcg IM monthly lifelong |
| Hydroxocobalamin IM | MTHFR/MTR variants with refractory Hcy | 1000 mcg IM weekly indefinitely |

#### 11.7.3 High-Dose Oral vs IM: Equivalence

The FAIR-HF trial and subsequent studies demonstrated:
- High-dose oral B12 (1000-2000 mcg/day) achieves comparable hematologic correction to IM
- However, for neurological/psychiatric manifestations, IM may be preferred for:
  - Faster tissue repletion
  - Guaranteed absorption
  - Higher peak levels

| Route | Dose | Expected Serum Increase | Cost |
|-------|------|------------------------|------|
| Oral cyanocobalamin | 1000 mcg/day | 100-300 pg/mL | Low |
| Oral methylcobalamin | 1000 mcg/day | 150-400 pg/mL | Moderate |
| Sublingual methylcobalamin | 1000 mcg/day | 200-500 pg/mL | Moderate |
| IM hydroxocobalamin | 1000 mcg/month | 400-1000 pg/mL | Higher |

### 11.8 B12-Drug Interactions in Psychiatry

| Drug | Effect on B12 | Mechanism | Management |
|------|--------------|-----------|------------|
| Metformin | Reduces absorption | Altered ileal transport | Monitor B12 annually; supplement |
| PPIs (omeprazole) | Reduces absorption | Hypochlorhydria | Monitor; consider H2 blockers |
| H2 blockers | Mild reduction | Reduced gastric acid | Less concerning than PPIs |
| SSRIs | Possible reduction | Altered gastric acid, appetite | Monitor in long-term use |
| Lithium | Possible elevation | Unknown | Generally not concerning |
| Valproic acid | No significant effect | - | Standard monitoring |
| Anticonvulsants | Variable | Hepatic metabolism changes | Monitor if polytherapy |
| Nitrous oxide | Inactivates B12 | Oxidizes cobalamin | Avoid in B12 deficiency; can precipitate neurological crisis |

**Critical Warning:** Nitrous oxide (recreational or medical) can inactivate B12 and cause rapid neurological/psychiatric deterioration in patients with marginal B12 status. This is an emerging concern with increasing recreational use.

## 12. Nutritional Factors Affecting Psychiatric Medications

### 12.1 Omega-3 Fatty Acids

#### 12.1.1 Overview
Omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs), particularly eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), are essential fatty acids with documented roles in brain structure, neuroinflammation, and neurotransmission. The brain is highly enriched in DHA (20% of brain phospholipid fatty acids), while EPA has stronger anti-inflammatory properties.

#### 12.1.2 Mechanisms in Psychiatry

| Mechanism | EPA | DHA |
|-----------|-----|-----|
| Anti-inflammatory | ++++ (strong) | ++ |
| Neuroplasticity | + | ++++ (strong) |
| Membrane fluidity | ++ | ++++ |
| Neurotransmitter receptor function | ++ | +++ |
| BDNF support | ++ | +++ |
| Neuroprotection | ++ | +++ |

**EPA-Specific Anti-Inflammatory Mechanism:**
- EPA is a precursor for resolvins (RvE1, RvE2) and protectins
- Competes with arachidonic acid for COX and LOX enzymes
- Reduces pro-inflammatory eicosanoid production
- Directly inhibits NF-kB activation
- Reduces IL-6, TNF-alpha, IL-1beta production
- May inhibit phospholipase A2 (reducing arachidonic acid release)

#### 12.1.3 EPA/DHA for Depression Augmentation

| Study/Review | Dose | Population | Result | Grade |
|-------------|------|-----------|--------|-------|
| Peet & Horrobin 2002 | 1-2 g EPA | TRD | Significant improvement | B |
| Nemets et al. 2002 | 2 g EPA | MDD (children) | Significant improvement | B |
| Su et al. 2003 | 4.4 g EPA+2.2 g DHA | MDD pregnancy | Significant improvement | B |
| Freeman et al. 2008 | 1-3 g EPA | Perinatal depression | Meta-analysis: moderate benefit | A |
| Sublette et al. 2011 | Meta-analysis | MDD | EPA >60% of total: antidepressant effect | A |
| Mocking et al. 2016 | Meta-analysis | MDD | EPA effective; pure EPA > mixed | A |
| Liao et al. 2019 | Network meta-analysis | MDD | Omega-3 effective adjunct; EPA dominant | A |

**Key Finding:** EPA appears to be the active antidepressant component. Formulations with >60% EPA and <40% DHA show the strongest evidence. Pure EPA (1-2 g/day) may be more effective than mixed EPA/DHA formulations.

**Clinical Recommendations:**

| Depression Type | EPA Dose | DHA Dose | Evidence Grade |
|----------------|----------|----------|----------------|
| Mild-moderate MDD (adjunct) | 1-2 g | 0.5-1 g | A |
| Treatment-resistant depression | 2-3 g | 0.5-1 g | B |
| Postpartum depression | 1-2 g | 0.5-1 g | B |
| Bipolar depression | 1-2 g | 0.5-1 g | B |
| SSRI augmentation | 1-2 g | 0.5 g | A |
| Monotherapy (mild) | 2 g | 1 g | B |

#### 12.1.4 Bipolar Disorder

| Study | Design | Dose | Result | Grade |
|-------|--------|------|--------|-------|
| Stoll et al. 1999 | RCT | 9.6 g EPA+DHA | Reduced relapse; longer remission | B |
| Frangou et al. 2006 | RCT | 1-2 g EPA | Improved depression symptoms | B |
| Keck et al. 2006 | RCT | 6 g EPA | No significant benefit | B |
| Sarris et al. 2012 | Review | Variable | Adjunctive benefit for depression pole | B |

**Clinical Note:** Evidence is stronger for the depressive phase of bipolar disorder than for mania or maintenance. Omega-3s may have mood-stabilizing properties but should not replace standard mood stabilizers.

#### 12.1.5 ADHD

| Study | Design | Dose | Result | Grade |
|-------|--------|------|--------|-------|
| Richardson & Montgomery 2005 | RCT | 558 mg EPA + 174 mg DHA | Improved conduct, attention | B |
| Gillies et al. 2012 | Meta-analysis | Variable | Small but significant effect | A |
| Chang et al. 2018 | Meta-analysis | Variable | Improved attention, hyperactivity | A |
| Cooper et al. 2015 | Review | Variable | Modest benefit; not first-line | A |

**Clinical Recommendations:**
- Consider omega-3 supplementation (1-2 g EPA) as adjunct to stimulants
- May allow stimulant dose reduction in some patients
- Benefits may take 8-12 weeks to manifest
- Higher EPA:DHA ratio preferred

#### 12.1.6 Mechanism: Anti-Inflammatory

The anti-inflammatory mechanism of omega-3s is particularly relevant for:
- **Inflammatory depression:** Patients with elevated CRP, IL-6 may respond better
- **Treatment-resistant depression:** Often associated with inflammation
- **Depression with comorbid autoimmune disease:** Shared inflammatory mechanisms
- **Postpartum depression:** Inflammatory state post-delivery

**Predictors of Omega-3 Response:**
- Elevated inflammatory markers (CRP >3, IL-6 >3 pg/mL)
- Low baseline omega-3 index (<4%)
- Treatment-resistant depression
- Depression with pain comorbidity
- Depression with metabolic syndrome

#### 12.1.7 Drug Interactions

| Interaction | Significance | Management |
|-------------|-------------|------------|
| Anticoagulants (warfarin, DOACs) | Possible increased bleeding risk | Monitor INR; generally safe at <3 g/day |
| Aspirin | Additive antiplatelet | Generally safe; caution with high doses |
| Clopidogrel | Possible interaction | Monitor if high-dose omega-3 |
| Antihypertensives | Mild BP lowering | May be beneficial |
| Psychiatric medications | No major interactions | Compatible with all classes |

#### 12.1.8 Omega-3 Index

The omega-3 index (RBC EPA+DHA as % of total fatty acids) is a useful biomarker:

| Index | Risk Category | Action |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| >8% | Low risk | Maintain |
| 4-8% | Moderate risk | Consider supplementation |
| <4% | High risk | Supplementation recommended |

### 12.2 Vitamin D

#### 12.2.1 Overview
Vitamin D is a secosteroid hormone synthesized in skin upon UVB exposure. It functions through the vitamin D receptor (VDR), a nuclear transcription factor expressed throughout the brain, including regions involved in mood regulation (hippocampus, hypothalamus, substantia nigra).

#### 12.2.2 VDR Polymorphisms

The VDR gene on chromosome 12q13.11 contains several functional polymorphisms:

| Polymorphism | rsID | Effect | Psychiatric Association |
|-------------|------|--------|------------------------|
| FokI (T/C) | rs2228570 | Altered protein length; modified transcriptional activity | Depression risk; treatment response |
| BsmI (A/G) | rs1544410 | Intronic; affects mRNA stability | Bipolar disorder; schizophrenia |
| TaqI (T/C) | rs731236 | Silent; linked to other variants | Depression; cognitive function |
| ApaI (G/T) | rs7975232 | Intronic | Less studied |
| Cdx2 (G/A) | rs11568820 | Promoter; affects transcription | Intestinal VDR expression |

**VDR FokI Functional Impact:**

| Genotype | VDR Activity | Depression Risk | Treatment Response |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| FF | Normal | Baseline | Standard |
| Ff | 70-80% | 1.1-1.3x | May need higher vitamin D |
| ff | 50-60% | 1.3-1.6x | May need higher vitamin D; better antidepressant response to supplementation |

#### 12.2.3 Seasonal Affective Disorder

| Evidence Point | Finding |
|---------------|---------|
| Latitude correlation | SAD prevalence increases with distance from equator |
| Seasonal vitamin D | Levels lowest in winter; correlate with SAD onset |
| Phototherapy | Effective treatment (may work via vitamin D-independent mechanisms) |
| Vitamin D supplementation | Some trials positive; mixed results |
| VDR in suprachiasmatic nucleus | Direct link to circadian regulation |

**Clinical Recommendations for SAD:**
- Check 25-OH vitamin D levels in all SAD patients
- Replete to >30 ng/mL (optimal >40 ng/mL)
- Combine with phototherapy for synergistic effect
- Consider starting supplementation in early autumn (prophylaxis)

#### 12.2.4 Depression Risk

| Study | Design | Result | Grade |
|-------|--------|--------|-------|
| Anglin et al. 2013 | Meta-analysis (n=31,424) | Low vitamin D associated with depression (OR 1.85) | A |
| Li et al. 2014 | Meta-analysis | Inverse correlation between 25-OH-D and depression | A |
| Shaffer et al. 2014 | Review | Supplementation shows modest benefit | A |
| Spedding 2014 | Meta-analysis | Benefit in deficient patients; no benefit if replete | A |
| Gowda et al. 2015 | Meta-analysis | Low vitamin D predicts future depression | A |

**Vitamin D Supplementation for Depression:**

| Status | 25-OH-D Level | Dose | Target |
|--------|--------------|------|--------|
| Severe deficiency | <10 ng/mL | 50,000 IU weekly x 8, then maintenance | >30 ng/mL |
| Moderate deficiency | 10-20 ng/mL | 10,000 IU daily x 8-12 weeks | >30 ng/mL |
| Mild deficiency | 20-30 ng/mL | 5,000-10,000 IU daily | >30 ng/mL |
| Insufficient | 30-40 ng/mL | 2,000-5,000 IU daily | >40 ng/mL |
| Optimal | >40 ng/mL | 1,000-2,000 IU maintenance | >40 ng/mL |

**Psychiatric Target Range:** 40-60 ng/mL (some evidence higher levels associated with better outcomes)

#### 12.2.5 Immune Modulation

Vitamin D modulates immune function through multiple mechanisms:
- Reduces pro-inflammatory cytokine production (IL-6, TNF-alpha, IL-1)
- Promotes regulatory T-cell development
- Reduces microglial activation
- Supports blood-brain barrier integrity
- Antimicrobial peptide induction (cathelicidin)

**Relevance to Psychiatry:**
- Depression is associated with elevated inflammatory markers
- Autoimmune diseases increase depression risk
- Vitamin D deficiency is common in severe mental illness
- Immune modulation may be one mechanism of antidepressant effect

#### 12.2.6 Drug Interactions

| Drug | Effect on Vitamin D | Management |
|------|-------------------|------------|

| Cholestyramine | Reduces absorption | Take 4+ hours apart |

### 12.3 Zinc

#### 12.3.1 Overview
Zinc is an essential trace element involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions, including neurotransmitter synthesis, immune function, and antioxidant defense. The brain contains high zinc concentrations, particularly in the hippocampus, amygdala, and cerebral cortex.

#### 12.3.2 ZnT3, ZIP Transporters

Zinc transport is mediated by two families:

**ZnT (SLC30) Family -- Efflux Transporters:**
| Transporter | Location | Function |
|-------------|----------|----------|
| ZnT1 | Ubiquitous | Basal zinc efflux |
| ZnT3 | Synaptic vesicles (hippocampus, cortex) | Loads zinc into glutamatergic vesicles |
| ZnT4-10 | Various tissues | Tissue-specific zinc transport |

**ZIP (SLC39) Family -- Influx Transporters:**
| Transporter | Location | Function |
|-------------|----------|----------|
| ZIP1, ZIP2 | Ubiquitous | Basal zinc uptake |
| ZIP4, ZIP8 | Brain, immune cells | Stress-inducible zinc uptake |
| ZIP6, ZIP10 | Hippocampus | Neurodevelopment, plasticity |

**Psychiatric Relevance:**
- ZnT3 knockout mice show impaired learning and memory
- Synaptic zinc modulates NMDA and GABA-A receptor function
- Zinc transporter dysfunction has been implicated in depression

#### 12.3.3 Depression

| Study | Design | Result | Grade |
|-------|--------|--------|-------|
| Swardfager et al. 2013 | Meta-analysis | Lower serum zinc in depression (d=-0.39) | A |
| Lai et al. 2012 | Meta-analysis | Zinc deficiency associated with depression | A |
| Siwek et al. 2010 | RCT | Zinc supplementation (25 mg) improved SSRI response | B |
| Ranjbar et al. 2013 | RCT | Zinc + fluoxetine > fluoxetine alone | B |
| Nowak et al. 2015 | Review | Zinc has antidepressant-like activity in animal models | B |

**Mechanisms:**
1. NMDA receptor antagonism (zinc blocks NMDA channel)
2. BDNF pathway modulation
3. Gut microbiome effects
4. Anti-inflammatory (reduces IL-6)
5. Neurotransmitter synthesis (dopamine, serotonin)
6. Antioxidant (copper-zinc superoxide dismutase)

#### 12.3.4 Anxiety

- Zinc deficiency associated with anxiety-like behavior in animal models
- Zinc modulates GABA-A receptors (anxiolytic site)
- Zinc supplementation may reduce anxiety symptoms
- Mechanism: NMDA modulation, GABA enhancement, HPA axis regulation

#### 12.3.5 NMDA Receptor Modulation

Zinc is an endogenous NMDA receptor antagonist:
- Binds to the NMDA receptor channel (non-competitive block)
- High-affinity site (ifenprodil site) on NR2A subunit
- At physiological concentrations, zinc tonically inhibits NMDA receptors
- Zinc deficiency may cause NMDA receptor hyperactivity (excitotoxicity)
- **Direct link to ketamine mechanism:** Ketamine is an NMDA antagonist; zinc may share some mechanism

#### 12.3.6 Antidepressant Augmentation

| Zinc Form | Dose | Evidence | Notes |
|-----------|------|----------|-------|
| Zinc gluconate | 25 mg elemental | RCT evidence | Well-absorbed |
| Zinc sulfate | 25-50 mg elemental | Good evidence | May cause GI upset |
| Zinc picolinate | 15-30 mg elemental | Good absorption | Preferred form |
| Zinc bisglycinate | 15-30 mg elemental | Excellent absorption | Chelated, gentle on stomach |

**Timing:** Take 1-2 hours away from SSRIs, as zinc may chelate and reduce SSRI absorption.

**Monitoring:** Long-term high-dose zinc (>50 mg/day) can cause copper deficiency. Monitor copper levels if >40 mg/day for >3 months.

### 12.4 Magnesium

#### 12.4.1 Overview
Magnesium is the fourth most abundant mineral in the body and an essential cofactor for over 600 enzymatic reactions. In the CNS, magnesium is critical for:
- NMDA receptor function (endogenous channel blocker)
- GABA receptor modulation
- Serotonin receptor function
- Neurotransmitter release
- Synaptic plasticity
- Energy metabolism
- Stress response (HPA axis regulation)

#### 12.4.2 NMDA Channel Blocker

Magnesium sits in the NMDA receptor channel and blocks calcium influx under resting membrane potential:

```
NMDA receptor: Mg2+ blocks channel at resting potential
Membrane depolarization --> Mg2+ ejected --> Ca2+ influx
```

**Psychiatric Relevance:**
- Low magnesium reduces NMDA receptor "braking," increasing excitotoxicity
- Chronic stress depletes magnesium (increased urinary excretion)
- Magnesium deficiency creates a state of NMDA hyperactivity
- This state may increase vulnerability to depression, anxiety, and neuronal damage
- **Parallel to ketamine:** Both magnesium and ketamine are NMDA antagonists

#### 12.4.3 Depression

| Study | Design | Result | Grade |
|-------|--------|--------|-------|
| Tarleton et al. 2015 | RCT | 248 mg MgCl2 improved mild-moderate depression | B |
| Rajizadeh et al. 2017 | RCT | 500 mg Mg improved depression in hypomagnesemic elderly | B |
| Barragan-Rodriguez et al. 2008 | RCT | Magnesium + imipramine > imipramine alone | B |
| Eby & Eby 2010 | Case series | Rapid recovery (7 days) with 125-300 mg Mg | C |
| Serefko et al. 2016 | Review | Magnesium has antidepressant-like activity | B |

**Mechanisms:**
1. NMDA receptor modulation (anti-glutamatergic)
2. HPA axis regulation (reduces cortisol)
3. Anti-inflammatory (reduces CRP, IL-6)
4. BDNF support
5. Serotonin synthesis (tryptophan hydroxylase cofactor)
6. Sleep improvement (GABA modulation)

#### 12.4.4 Anxiety

- Magnesium deficiency associated with anxiety in observational studies
- Mechanism: GABA-A receptor modulation, NMDA regulation, HPA axis
- Magnesium supplementation may reduce subjective anxiety
- Synergistic with L-theanine, taurine

#### 12.4.5 Sleep Quality

- Magnesium improves sleep quality through GABA modulation
- Reduces sleep-onset latency
- Improves sleep efficiency
- May reduce nocturnal awakenings
- Particularly beneficial in depression-related insomnia

**Recommended Forms:**

| Form | Elemental Mg | Absorption | Notes |
|------|-------------|------------|-------|
| Magnesium glycinate | 100-200 mg | High | Best for anxiety, sleep; gentle |
| Magnesium threonate | 100-200 mg | High | Crosses blood-brain barrier; cognitive benefit |
| Magnesium citrate | 150-300 mg | Good | Also laxative; good for constipation |
| Magnesium chloride | 150-300 mg | Good | Good bioavailability |
| Magnesium oxide | 200-400 mg | Poor | Avoid; mostly unabsorbed |
| Magnesium malate | 150-300 mg | Good | Energizing; good for fatigue |

**Psychiatric Dosing:**
- Depression: 200-400 mg elemental Mg/day
- Anxiety: 100-200 mg elemental Mg 2-3x/day
- Sleep: 200-400 mg Mg glycinate at bedtime
- General support: 200-400 mg/day

#### 12.4.6 GABA Modulation

Magnesium modulates GABAergic neurotransmission:
- Positive allosteric modulator of GABA-A receptors
- Increases GABA receptor sensitivity
- Reduces glutamate-GABA imbalance
- May be synergistic with benzodiazepines (caution with CNS depression)

### 12.5 Iron

#### 12.5.1 Overview
Iron is essential for:
- Hemoglobin synthesis (oxygen transport)
- Dopamine synthesis (tyrosine hydroxylase cofactor)
- Myelin synthesis
- Mitochondrial function (electron transport chain)
- Neurotransmitter receptors (D2 receptor requires iron)

The brain is highly iron-dependent, with concentrated iron in the basal ganglia (substantia nigra, striatum).

#### 12.5.2 Ferritin and Depression

| Study | Finding | Grade |
|-------|---------|-------|
| Vahdat Shariatpanaahi et al. 2007 | Low ferritin associated with depression | B |
| Beard et al. 2005 | Iron deficiency without anemia causes fatigue, depression | B |
| Chen et al. 2013 | Iron deficiency associated with postpartum depression | B |
| Kim et al. 2017 | Low ferritin predicts antidepressant non-response | B |
| Lam et al. 2020 | Iron supplementation improved depression in iron-deficient patients | B |

**Ferritin Thresholds for Psychiatry:**

| Ferritin (ng/mL) | Status | Psychiatric Implication |
|-----------------|--------|------------------------|
| <15 | Iron deficiency | High depression/fatigue risk; cognitive impairment |
| 15-30 | Low iron stores | Subclinical deficiency; may affect mood/cognition |
| 30-50 | Marginal | May affect dopamine synthesis; optimal >50 |
| 50-100 | Adequate | Ideal for psychiatric patients |
| 100-300 | Normal | Optimal range |
| >300 | Elevated | Check for inflammation, hemochromatosis |

**Psychiatric Target:** Ferritin >50-100 ng/mL for optimal neurotransmitter function

#### 12.5.3 Restless Legs Syndrome (RLS) and Sleep

Iron deficiency is the most common treatable cause of RLS:
- RLS causes significant sleep disruption
- Sleep deprivation exacerbates depression, anxiety, cognitive impairment
- Iron supplementation (oral or IV) often resolves RLS
- Check ferritin in all patients with depression + sleep complaints

**RLS-Iron Treatment:**
- Ferritin <50 ng/mL: Oral iron supplementation (65 mg elemental Fe/day)
- Ferritin <30 ng/mL: Consider IV iron (ferric carboxymaltose)
- Reassess ferritin at 8-12 weeks
- Monitor for constipation (oral iron)

#### 12.5.4 Dopamine Synthesis

Iron is essential for dopamine synthesis at multiple steps:

```
Tyrosine ---[TH + Fe]---> L-DOPA ---[AADC]---> Dopamine
                              |
                              v
                    Tyrosine hydroxylase requires iron
                    Dopamine receptor D2 requires iron
                    Monoamine oxidase requires iron
```

**Iron-Dopamine Connection in Psychiatry:**
- Iron deficiency impairs dopamine synthesis
- Dopamine depletion causes anhedonia, fatigue, cognitive slowing
- Iron deficiency without anemia can cause psychiatric symptoms
- Particularly relevant for ADHD, depression with fatigue, Parkinson's depression

#### 12.5.5 Stimulant Response

Iron status may affect stimulant medication response:

| Iron Status | Expected Stimulant Effect |
|-------------|--------------------------|
| Optimal (ferritin >50) | Normal response |
| Low (ferritin 15-30) | Reduced response; may need higher dose |
| Deficient (ferritin <15) | Poor response; treat iron first |

**Clinical Recommendation:** Check ferritin in all ADHD patients, especially before starting or optimizing stimulants. Iron supplementation may improve stimulant response in iron-deficient patients.

#### 12.5.6 Iron Supplementation in Psychiatry

| Form | Elemental Iron | Absorption | Notes |
|------|---------------|------------|-------|
| Ferrous sulfate | 65 mg (325 mg tablet) | Standard | Inexpensive; GI side effects |
| Ferrous bisglycinate | 25-50 mg | High | Better absorption; less GI upset |
| Ferrous gluconate | 60 mg | Good | Gentler than sulfate |
| Iron polysaccharide complex | 150 mg | Moderate | Low side effect profile |
| IV ferric carboxymaltose | 750-1000 mg | Direct | For severe deficiency, malabsorption |

**Caution:** Iron supplementation should be guided by ferritin levels. Excess iron is pro-oxidative and potentially neurotoxic. Do NOT supplement iron without documented deficiency.

## 14. CLINICAL INTEGRATION & DECISION FRAMEWORK

### 14.1 Patient Assessment Protocol

#### 14.1.1 Who Should Receive Methylation Panel Testing

**Indications for Core Methylation Panel (MTHFR, MTR, MTRR, COMT, CBS, RFC1):**

1. **Treatment-resistant depression** (failed >=2 adequate antidepressant trials)
2. **Recurrent major depressive disorder** (>=3 episodes)
3. **Depression with cardiovascular comorbidity** (elevated homocysteine suspected)
4. **Postpartum depression** or preconception counseling
5. **Depression requiring anticonvulsant therapy** (bipolar, epilepsy comorbidity)
6. **Elevated homocysteine** (>12 umol/L) of unknown etiology
7. **Family history** of neural tube defects, early cardiovascular disease, or depression
8. **Schizophrenia with negative symptoms** or cognitive impairment
9. **Cognitive decline** with depression (vascular depression)
10. **Patients on methotrexate** with mood or cognitive changes
11. **Chronic fatigue with depression** (functional B12/folate deficiency)
12. **Autism spectrum disorder** with mood symptoms

#### 14.1.2 Recommended Testing Cascade

```
STEP 1: BIOCHEMICAL SCREENING (All patients)
  - Serum homocysteine (fasting)
  - Serum B12 + folate (RBC folate preferred)
  - Holotranscobalamin (holoTC) or MMA if B12 borderline
  - Vitamin D (25-OH-D)
  - Ferritin
  - Zinc (plasma or serum)
  - Magnesium (RBC magnesium preferred)
  - Omega-3 index (optional)

STEP 2: GENETIC TESTING (If indicated above)
  CORE PANEL:
  - MTHFR C677T (rs1801133)
  - MTHFR A1298C (rs1801131)
  - MTR A2756G (rs1805087)
  - MTRR A66G (rs1801394)
  - RFC1 A80G (rs1051266)
  - CBS 844ins68 (rs5742905)
  - COMT Val158Met (rs4680)

  EXTENDED PANEL (For complex/refractory cases):
  - TCN2 C776G (rs1801198)
  - DHFR variants
  - GGH variants
  - BHMT R239Q (rs3733890)
  - FOLR1 autoantibodies
  - PEMT rs7946
  - SHMT1 C1420T (rs1979277)
  - MTHFD1 R653Q (rs2236225)
  - VDR polymorphisms
  - BDNF Val66Met (rs6265)

STEP 3: INTEGRATIVE INTERPRETATION
  - Calculate combined genetic burden
  - Correlate with biochemical markers
  - Identify nutritional deficiencies
  - Assess medication interactions
  - Develop personalized intervention plan

STEP 4: INTERVENTION
  - Nutritional supplementation protocol
  - Medication adjustment
  - Monitoring schedule
  - Reassessment timeline
```

### 14.6 Safety Considerations

#### 14.6.1 Folate and B12: Masking Relationship

**CRITICAL SAFETY POINT:** High-dose folate (any form) can correct megaloblastic anemia caused by B12 deficiency while allowing neurological damage to progress.

| Protocol | Recommendation |
|----------|---------------|
| Before starting L-methylfolate | Check B12 (serum >300 pg/mL, or MMA normal) |
| If B12 deficient | Treat B12 FIRST, then add folate |
| Monitoring | B12 annually if on long-term L-methylfolate |
| Form choice | L-methylfolate does NOT mask B12 deficiency as completely as folic acid |

## 15. SUMMARY & KEY TAKEAWAYS

### 15.1 Top 10 Clinical Pearls

1. **MTHFR C677T is the single most important genetic variant for psychiatric methylation assessment.** The 677TT genotype carries 1.3-1.5x increased depression risk, reduced SSRI response, and significant treatment resistance. Screen all TRD patients. **Grade A.**

2. **L-methylfolate (not folic acid) is the preferred intervention for MTHFR variants.** It bypasses the enzymatic bottleneck entirely. Dose: 7.5-15 mg for depression, 0.8-1 mg for prophylaxis. **Grade A.**

3. **The MTHFR-COMT interaction is a critical gene-gene combination.** 677TT + COMT Val/Val = highest depression risk and most challenging treatment profile. These patients require comprehensive methylation support + dopaminergic strategies. **Grade B.**

4. **Always check B12 before and during folate supplementation.** Folate can mask B12-deficient anemia. Methylcobalamin is preferred for methylation support, especially with MTR/MTRR variants. **Grade A (safety).**

5. **Homocysteine >15 umol/L is a psychiatric red flag.** It indicates significant methylation impairment and is associated with 1.5-2x depression risk, cognitive decline, and treatment resistance. Target <10 umol/L. **Grade A.**

6. **Nutritional augmentation has Grade A evidence for depression.** Omega-3 (EPA 1-2 g), L-methylfolate (15 mg), SAMe (800-1600 mg), and vitamin D (to repletion) all have strong RCT evidence as antidepressant adjuncts. **Grade A.**

7. **Cerebral folate deficiency (FOLR1 autoantibodies) should be considered in refractory neuropsychiatric cases.** CSF 5-MTHF <30 nmol/L with normal serum folate is diagnostic. Responds to high-dose folinic acid. **Grade B.**

8. **Iron, zinc, and magnesium deficiencies are common and treatable causes of psychiatric symptoms.** Check ferritin (target >50), zinc, and RBC magnesium in all treatment-resistant patients. Repletion often improves response. **Grade B.**

9. **Methylation genetics affect medication tolerability and safety.** MTHFR variants increase methotrexate neurotoxicity, anticonvulsant teratogenicity, and nitrous oxide vulnerability. Always check methylation genetics before these exposures. **Grade B.**

10. **The one-carbon methylation cycle is the metabolic intersection of nutrition, genetics, and psychiatric medication response.** A comprehensive approach addressing all components (folate, B12, B6, choline, betaine, zinc, magnesium, omega-3, vitamin D) yields better outcomes than single-nutrient approaches. **Grade B (integrative).**

### 15.3 Implementation Checklist for Clinical Practice

**For Each Patient with Depression:**
- [ ] Check MTHFR C677T and A1298C (or review prior results)
- [ ] Measure fasting homocysteine, B12 (or MMA), folate (RBC preferred)
- [ ] Measure vitamin D 25-OH, ferritin, zinc, magnesium
- [ ] If 677TT or compound heterozygous: start L-methylfolate 7.5-15 mg
- [ ] If Hcy >12: start comprehensive B-vitamin protocol
- [ ] If ferritin <50: investigate and treat iron deficiency
- [ ] If vitamin D <30: supplement to 40-60 ng/mL
- [ ] Consider omega-3 index; supplement EPA 1-2 g if low
- [ ] Consider COMT status for medication selection
- [ ] Check for B12 deficiency BEFORE high-dose folate
- [ ] Reassess Hcy and symptoms at 8-12 weeks
- [ ] Adjust protocol based on response
- [ ] Continue effective interventions long-term

### Appendix E: Nutritional Psychiatry Quick Reference Cards

#### Card 1: MTHFR-Based L-Methylfolate Dosing
```
677CC + 1298AA: 0.4-0.8 mg/day (or none if Hcy normal)
677CT or 1298AC: 0.8-1 mg/day
677TT or 1298CC: 7.5-15 mg/day
Compound het: 7.5-15 mg/day
Any variant + Hcy >15: 15 mg/day
Any variant + anticonvulsants: 1-5 mg/day prophylaxis
Any variant + MTX: 5-15 mg/day + leucovorin rescue
```

#### Card 2: Homocysteine Targets
```
Optimal: <10 umol/L (psychiatric target)
Acceptable: 10-12 umol/L
Elevated: 12-15 umol/L (intervention)
High: 15-30 umol/L (active treatment)
Severe: >30 umol/L (urgent workup)

Check: fasting, B12 and folate simultaneously
Recheck: 8-12 weeks after intervention
Target: <10 umol/L for optimal psychiatric outcomes
```

#### Card 3: B12 Forms by Clinical Situation
```
General maintenance: Cyanocobalamin 1000 mcg oral
MTHFR/MTR variants: Methylcobalamin 1000-2000 mcg SL
Severe deficiency/neurological: Hydroxocobalamin 1000 mcg IM
Malabsorption (pernicious anemia): Hydroxocobalamin 1000 mcg IM monthly
Pregnancy/lactation: Methylcobalamin 1000 mcg
Cognitive enhancement: Methylcobalamin + adenosylcobalamin combination
```

#### Card 4: Red Flags for Methylation-Related Psychiatric Disorders
```
- Treatment-resistant depression (>=2 failed trials)
- Depression onset after starting anticonvulsants, PPIs, or metformin
- Depression with peripheral neuropathy symptoms
- Depression with cognitive decline (especially vascular pattern)
- Postpartum depression with family history of NTDs
- Psychiatric symptoms + elevated homocysteine
- Depression + macrocytic anemia
- Psychosis with B12 deficiency risk factors (vegan, GI surgery, PPI use)
- Autism + movement disorders + normal serum folate (consider CFD)
- Fatigue + depression + cognitive fog (functional B12 deficiency)
- History of nitrous oxide exposure with psychiatric symptoms
```
