## 1. Executive Summary

This matrix documents medication interactions across nine neuromodulation modalities used in psychiatric and neurological practice. **The highest-risk interactions** involve:

- **CRITICAL**: Lithium + ECT (postictal delirium, neurotoxicity risk)
- **CRITICAL**: Clozapine/chlorpromazine + ECT (prolonged seizures, status epilepticus)
- **CRITICAL**: Propofol + ECT (seizure threshold elevation, inadequate seizures)
- **MAJOR**: Anticonvulsants + ECT (significant seizure threshold elevation)
- **MAJOR**: Sodium channel blockers + tDCS (abolition of neuroplastic effects)
- **MAJOR**: NMDA antagonists + tDCS (blockade of long-term potentiation-like effects)
- **MAJOR**: Valproic acid + rTMS (resting motor threshold elevation ~20%)
- **MODERATE**: Benzodiazepines + rTMS (potential efficacy attenuation)
- **MODERATE**: Stimulants + rTMS/TMS (seizure threshold lowering + efficacy enhancement)
- **MODERATE**: SSRIs + tDCS (polarity reversal of cathodal effects)

## 3. Modality-Specific Matrices

### 3.1 rTMS (Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation)

#### 3.1.1 ANTIDEPRESSANTS + rTMS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **SSRIs** (citalopram, sertraline, fluoxetine) | Monitor | No direct interaction; concurrent use standard practice | May continue; no dose adjustment needed for rTMS | B | McClintock 2018; Lefaucheur 2020 |
| **SNRIs** (venlafaxine, duloxetine) | Monitor | May potentiate serotonergic effects | Standard practice; monitor for serotonin syndrome if combined with high-frequency protocols | C | McClintock 2018 |
| **TCAs** (amitriptyline, nortriptyline) | Moderate | Lower seizure threshold; anticholinergic effects | Use cautiously; lower rTMS intensity may be needed; avoid in patients with seizure history | C | APA ECT Guidelines (analogous risk) |
| **MAOIs** (phenelzine, tranylcypromine) | Moderate | Lower seizure threshold; hypertensive risk with sympathetic discharge | Avoid concurrent use; if necessary, use lowest effective rTMS intensity | D | Expert consensus |
| **Bupropion** | Moderate | Lowers seizure threshold (dose-dependent); dopaminergic action | **Caution**: Avoid or use lowest dose; increased seizure risk at >300mg/day; monitor closely | B | Rossi 2021; FDA labeling |
| **Mirtazapine** | Monitor | Noradrenergic/specific serotonergic action; no known seizure effect | May continue; may enhance sleep during rTMS course | C | Hebel 2022 (German Guidelines) |

#### 3.1.2 ANTIPSYCHOTICS + rTMS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Clozapine** | Major | Proconvulsant (dose-dependent, >600mg); lowers seizure threshold | **Avoid** concurrent high-frequency rTMS; if used, reduce intensity, extend inter-train intervals; have seizure rescue plan | C | Weiner 1984; Conley 2017 |
| **Chlorpromazine** | Moderate | Proconvulsant; alpha-blockade may cause orthostasis during rTMS | Use reduced rTMS intensity; monitor blood pressure during sessions | C | ECT literature extrapolation |
| **Other atypicals** (risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine, aripiprazole) | Monitor | D2/5-HT2 antagonism; minimal seizure risk | May continue; may enhance rTMS response in treatment-resistant depression | B | Kaster 2019; Bouaziz 2019 |
| **Ziprasidone** | Moderate | QTc prolongation; potential arrhythmia risk with sympathetic activation | Obtain baseline ECG; avoid if QTc >500ms; monitor during sessions | C | Rossi 2021 |

#### 3.1.3 MOOD STABILIZERS + rTMS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Lithium** | Monitor | No negative impact on rTMS antidepressant effect; may improve response | **May continue**; evidence suggests lithium + lamotrigine may improve response/remission rates | B | Hebel 2021 (n=299); PMID: 34322621 |
| **Valproic Acid (VPA)** | Major | **Significantly elevates resting motor threshold (~20%)** | Must increase stimulation intensity to maintain 110-120% RMT; recalculate RMT while on VPA | B | Hebel 2021; Reti 2023 |
| **Lamotrigine** | Monitor | May improve response/remission rates; no negative effect on rTMS | **May continue**; evidence suggests favorable interaction | B | Hebel 2021; Fitzgerald 2020 |
| **Carbamazepine** | Moderate | Sodium channel blockade; theoretically reduces cortical excitability | May need higher stimulation intensity; monitor seizure threshold changes | C | Extrapolated from tDCS literature |

#### 3.1.4 ANXIOLYTICS + rTMS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Benzodiazepines** (lorazepam, clonazepam, diazepam) | Moderate | GABA-A agonism; may attenuate rTMS response; elevates seizure threshold | Mixed evidence; some studies show reduced response (especially lorazepam), others show no effect; consider tapering if clinically feasible | B | Deppe 2017; Fitzgerald 2020; Galletly 2024 |
| **Buspirone** | Monitor | 5-HT1A partial agonist; no known interaction | May continue; may have anxiolytic benefit without affecting seizure threshold | D | Expert opinion |
| **Hydroxyzine** | Monitor | H1 antihistamine; mild anxiolytic; no seizure effect | May continue safely | D | Expert opinion |
| **Z-drugs** (zolpidem, eszopiclone) | Mild | GABA-A modulation (subtype selective) | May continue; limited data on rTMS interaction | C | Hunter 2019 |

#### 3.1.5 STIMULANTS + rTMS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Methylphenidate** | Moderate | Increases dopamine/norepinephrine; lowers seizure threshold; **enhances rTMS response** | May improve rTMS antidepressant outcomes (p=0.03); use lower rTMS intensity; monitor for seizure signs | C | Hunter 2019; Cureus 2024 review |
| **Amphetamines** (dextroamphetamine, lisdexamfetamine) | Moderate | Increases dopamine/NE release; lowers seizure threshold | Same cautions as methylphenidate; enhanced efficacy possible | C | Hunter 2019 |
| **Modafinil** | Monitor | Histaminergic/dopaminergic/orexinergic wake-promoting; lowers seizure threshold mildly | May enhance cognition during rTMS course; limited interaction data | D | Expert opinion |

#### 3.1.6 ANTICONVULSANTS + rTMS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Topiramate** | Moderate | Sodium channel blockade, carbonic anhydrase inhibition; elevates seizure threshold | May need higher stimulation intensity; may reduce cortical excitability | C | Hunter 2019 (no significant effect found) |
| **Gabapentin** | Monitor | Calcium channel (alpha-2-delta) modulation; minimal effect on cortical excitability | May continue; limited interaction data | C | Hunter 2019 |
| **Pregabalin** | Monitor | Same mechanism as gabapentin | May continue; limited interaction data | C | Hunter 2019 |

#### 3.1.7 ANTICOAGULANTS + rTMS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|

| **DOACs** (apixaban, rivaroxaban) | Monitor | Anticoagulant effect; no reversal agent readily available | Standard precautions; low absolute bleeding risk with rTMS | D | Expert opinion |
| **Aspirin** | Monitor | Antiplatelet effect | May continue; minimal risk | D | Expert opinion |

#### 3.1.8 ANESTHETICS + rTMS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Propofol** | None | Not typically co-administered with rTMS | N/A (rTMS does not require anesthesia) | N/A | N/A |
| **Ketamine** | Monitor | NMDA antagonism; rapid-acting antidepressant; may synergize with rTMS | Emerging concurrent use ("ketamine-assisted rTMS"); monitor for dissociation and BP changes | B | Katalinic 2023; Wilkinson 2023 |

### 3.2 tDCS (Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation)

*tDCS is unique among neuromodulation modalities in having well-characterized pharmacological interactions affecting its neuroplastic mechanisms (Nitsche 2003; Kuo 2014).*

#### 3.2.1 ANTIDEPRESSANTS + tDCS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **SSRIs** (citalopram 20mg) | Moderate | **Increases and prolongs anodal excitability enhancement; REVERSes cathodal excitability reduction to enhancement** | Significantly alters expected polarity-specific effects; chronic use (35 days) shows same pattern; clinicians should account for modified tDCS response | B | Nitsche 2003; Kuo 2008; PMID: 17110490 |
| **SNRIs** (reboxetine 8mg) | Moderate | **Reverses cathodal excitability reduction to enhancement; increases and prolongs anodal enhancement** | Significantly alters polarity-specific effects; chronic administration prolongs effects until evening post-stimulation | B | Kuo 2007; PMID: 17170012 |
| **TCAs** (amitriptyline) | Moderate | Anticholinergic properties may interfere with cholinergic-dependent plasticity | Limited data; may reduce tDCS after-effects | D | Expert extrapolation |
| **Bupropion** | Monitor | Dopamine/norepinephrine reuptake inhibition | May enhance cortical excitability; limited formal interaction data | D | Expert opinion |
| **Mirtazapine** | Monitor | Complex 5-HT/NE/HI receptor effects | Limited data; may affect sleep-related plasticity windows | D | Expert opinion |

#### 3.2.2 ANTIPSYCHOTICS + tDCS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Typical antipsychotics** (chlorpromazine) | Moderate | D2 antagonism, anticholinergic effects | May interfere with tDCS-induced plasticity via cholinergic blockade | D | Kuo 2014 review |
| **Atypical antipsychotics** | Monitor | Mixed receptor profiles; some serotonergic activity | Limited data; may modulate but not abolish tDCS effects | D | Expert opinion |

#### 3.2.3 MOOD STABILIZERS + tDCS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Lithium** | Monitor | Glutamate/GSK-3 modulation; may enhance neuroplasticity | No direct interaction study; preclinical evidence suggests lithium may promote plasticity | D | Expert opinion |
| **Valproic Acid** | Monitor | HDAC inhibition, GABAergic effects | Limited formal data; single study showed no significant effect on tDCS | C | Hunter 2019 extrapolation |
| **Carbamazepine (600mg)** | **CRITICAL** | **Blocks sodium channels; PREVENTS acute and after-effects of anodal tDCS** | **Contraindicated for tDCS** if plasticity induction is the goal; tDCS will be ineffective | B | Liebetanz 2002; Nitsche 2003; PMID: 12122044 |
| **Lamotrigine** | Major | Sodium channel blockade (similar to carbamazepine, but milder) | May attenuate tDCS effects; consider dose reduction or alternative treatment | C | Extrapolated from CBZ data |

#### 3.2.4 ANXIOLYTICS + tDCS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Benzodiazepines** (lorazepam 2mg) | Moderate | **GABA-A agonism; delays but then PROLONGs anodal excitability enhancement; does not affect cathodal effects** | Significantly alters time course of tDCS effects; after-effects emerge later but persist longer | B | Nitsche 2004; PMID: 15555516 |
| **Buspirone** | Monitor | 5-HT1A agonism; may modulate plasticity | Limited data; theoretically may enhance plasticity via 5-HT1A mechanism | D | Expert opinion |

#### 3.2.5 STIMULANTS + tDCS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Amphetamine** | Moderate | **Prolongs anodal excitability enhancement; slightly reduces cathodal excitability reduction; NMDA-dependent** | Significantly enhances tDCS after-effects; may be beneficial but monitor for overstimulation | B | Nitsche 2004; Kuo 2007 |
| **Methylphenidate** | Monitor | Dopamine/NE reuptake inhibition | Limited data; expected to have similar effects to amphetamine | D | Expert extrapolation |

#### 3.2.6 ANTICONVULSANTS + tDCS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Topiramate** | Major | Carbonic anhydrase inhibition; multiple mechanisms | May attenuate tDCS effects via carbonic anhydrase inhibition | C | Expert extrapolation |
| **Gabapentin** | Moderate | Calcium channel alpha-2-delta modulation | May reduce excitability changes; limited data | D | Expert opinion |
| **Flunarizine (10mg)** | **CRITICAL** | **Calcium channel blocker; DIMINISHES acute anodal excitability; BLOCKS after-effects under anode** | **Contraindicated for tDCS** if plasticity induction is goal | B | Nitsche 2003; PMID: 12122044 |

#### 3.2.7 ANESTHETICS + tDCS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Ketamine** | Moderate | NMDA antagonism; **abolishes after-effects of tDCS** | Concurrent use blocks long-term plasticity effects of tDCS | B | Kuo 2008; PMID: 18077290 |
| **Dextromethorphan (100-150mg)** | **CRITICAL** | **NMDA receptor antagonism; ABOLISHES after-effects under both anode and cathode** | **Contraindicated for tDCS** if plasticity induction is goal | B | Nitsche 2003; Kuo 2008 |
| **D-Cycloserine (100mg)** | Moderate | Partial NMDA agonist; **prolongs anodal after-effects; intensifies cathodal excitability reduction** | May be used to enhance tDCS effects | B | Nitsche 2006; PMID: 16914679 |

#### 3.2.8 OTHER SIGNIFICANT tDCS INTERACTIONS

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **L-DOPA (25mg, 200mg)** | **CRITICAL** | **Inverted-U dose response: eliminates after-effects at low and high doses; at 100mg, switches anodal enhancement to inhibition** | Dose-critical interaction; if used, 100mg may paradoxically reverse expected polarity effects | B | Kuo 2008; Monte-Silva 2010; PMID: 20053920 |
| **Rivastigmine (3mg)** | Major | Cholinesterase inhibitor; initially blocks anodal after-effects; delays then prolongs cathodal effects | Significantly alters tDCS time course; after-effects emerge after delay | B | Kuo 2007; PMID: 17170012 |
| **Nicotine** | Moderate | Nicotinic receptor agonism; **eliminates anodal excitability enhancement; diminishes cathodal reduction** | Smoking status affects tDCS response; non-smokers more affected than smokers | B | Thirugnanasambandam 2011; PMID: 21411698 |
| **Propranolol** | Moderate | Beta-adrenergic antagonism; **shortens tDCS after-effects** | May reduce tDCS efficacy; consider alternative beta-blocker (e.g., atenolol) | B | Nitsche 2004 |

### 3.3 tACS (Transcranial Alternating Current Stimulation)

*tACS has limited medication interaction data. General principles from tDCS and rTMS literature are extrapolated.*

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Benzodiazepines** | Monitor | GABA-A agonism; may interfere with oscillatory entrainment | May reduce tACS efficacy; limited data | D | Expert extrapolation |
| **Anticonvulsants** | Monitor | May alter cortical oscillatory patterns | May interfere with frequency-specific effects | D | Expert extrapolation |
| **Antipsychotics** | Monitor | Dopamine/serotonin modulation affects endogenous oscillations | May modify but not necessarily abolish tACS effects | D | Expert extrapolation |
| **SSRIs** | Monitor | Serotonergic modulation of cortical oscillations | May enhance some tACS frequency bands (alpha, theta) | D | Expert extrapolation |

**Note**: tACS at higher intensities may cause phosphenes, metallic taste, and dizziness independent of medication status (Antal 2008). No medication-specific adverse events documented.

### 3.5 ECT (Electroconvulsive Therapy)

*ECT has the most extensively documented medication interactions due to the critical importance of seizure threshold modulation.*

#### 3.5.1 ANESTHETICS FOR ECT (Primary Determinants of Seizure Quality)

| Agent | Seizure Threshold Effect | Seizure Duration | Hemodynamic Effect | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|-------|-------------------------|------------------|-------------------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Methohexital** | NEUTRAL (gold standard) | Long | Moderate cardiac depression (beneficial) | **Preferred agent**; 1-1.5 mg/kg IV; does not affect seizure threshold or duration | A | Kadiyala 2024 (NCBI); Ingvar 2018 |
| **Propofol** | **ELEVATES** | **Shortens** | Minimal BP increase (preferred in cardiac patients) | 1.5-2 mg/kg; acceptable alternative; may need higher electrical dose; may reduce EEG quality | A | Kadiyala 2024; Propofol vs Methohexital meta-analysis |
| **Ketamine** | **LOWERS** | **Prolongs** | Increases HR/BP (sympathetic stimulation) | 1-2 mg/kg; consider for prolonged seizures; pretreat with benzodiazepine for psychomimetic effects; has independent antidepressant properties | B | Kadiyala 2024; Zhong 2016 |
| **Etomidate** | NEUTRAL to slight lowering | Longer than propofol | No cardiac depression (may need adjunct) | 0.3 mg/kg; good for patients needing longer seizures; supplement with opioid or beta-blocker | B | Kadiyala 2024 |
| **Sevoflurane** | **ELEVATES** (more than propofol) | **Shortens significantly** | Minimal | Reserve for uncooperative patients; IV preferred | B | Kadiyala 2024 |
| **Thiopental** | Elevates | Shortens | Moderate cardiac depression | Historical use; largely replaced by propofol/methohexital | B | Kadiyala 2024 |

**Seizure Threshold Ranking** (lowest to highest threshold):
Ketamine > Etomidate > Methohexital > Sevoflurane > Thiopental > Propofol

#### 3.5.2 ANTIDEPRESSANTS + ECT

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **SSRIs** | Monitor | Serotonergic potentiation; may increase seizure threshold slightly | Generally safe; may continue; monitor for serotonin syndrome with anesthesia agents | A | Indian CPG 2022; APA Guidelines |
| **SNRIs** | Monitor | Similar to SSRIs with added noradrenergic effects | Generally safe; may continue | B | APA Guidelines |
| **TCAs** | **Major** | **Lower seizure threshold; QTc prolongation; increase cardiac adverse event risk** | **Avoid if possible**; if necessary, use lowest dose; continuous ECG monitoring required; avoid in cardiac patients | A | Indian CPG 2022; ECT Handbook |
| **MAOIs** | **Major** | **Hypertensive crisis risk with sympathetic surge; lower seizure threshold** | **Discontinue 2 weeks before ECT if possible**; if emergent ECT needed, use esmolol/atropine for hemodynamic control | A | APA Guidelines |
| **Bupropion** | **Major** | **Dose-dependent seizure threshold lowering (up to 4x seizure risk at >300mg)** | **Discontinue before ECT**; washout 3-5 days; if unavoidable, use lowest dose with anesthesia optimization | B | Rossi 2021; FDA labeling |
| **Mirtazapine** | Monitor | Minimal seizure risk; may improve sleep/appetite during ECT course | May continue safely | C | Expert opinion |

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |

| **Ziprasidone** | Major | Significant QTc prolongation | Baseline ECG required; avoid if QTc >500ms | C | APA Guidelines |

#### 3.5.4 MOOD STABILIZERS + ECT

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Lithium** | **CRITICAL** | **Increases BBB permeability; increases intracerebral lithium; risk of severe postictal delirium, neurotoxicity** | **Suspend lithium before acute ECT course** if clinically safe; if continuation necessary: omit evening dose before ECT, delay morning dose until post-recovery, maintain serum level at LOWER end of therapeutic range (0.4-0.6 mEq/L); safer during maintenance ECT | A | Rasmussen (Lithium+ECT review); Australian ECT Policy 2023; Indian CPG 2022 |

| **Lamotrigine** | Moderate | Elevates seizure threshold (milder than CBZ/VPA) | May continue at lower doses; monitor seizure quality; no negative impact on cognitive outcomes | C | Hebel 2021 extrapolation |

#### 3.5.5 ANXIOLYTICS + ECT

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Benzodiazepines** | **Major** | **GABA-A agonism; significantly elevates seizure threshold; may cause missed seizures** | **Withhold morning dose on ECT days** if clinically safe; if tapering not possible, use ketamine/etomidate as anesthetic and higher electrical doses | A | Indian CPG 2022; APA Guidelines |
| **Buspirone** | Monitor | 5-HT1A agonism; no GABA effect | Preferred anxiolytic during ECT course; does not affect seizure threshold | C | Expert opinion |

#### 3.5.6 STIMULANTS + ECT

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Methylphenidate / Amphetamines** | **Major** | **Lower seizure threshold; sympathomimetic effects increase cardiac risk** | **Discontinue before ECT**; washout 24-48 hours; may cause prolonged seizures if continued | C | Rossi 2021; APA Guidelines |
| **Theophylline** | **CRITICAL** | **Significantly lowers seizure threshold** | **Absolute contraindication** for ECT unless essential for respiratory disease | A | Indian CPG 2022 |
| **Caffeine** | Moderate | Mild seizure threshold lowering | Avoid augmentation with caffeine for seizure prolongation (not recommended per guidelines) | B | Australian ECT Policy 2023 |

#### 3.5.7 ANTICONVULSANTS + ECT

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **All anticonvulsants** | **Major** | **Elevate seizure threshold** (primary mechanism of action) | Consider dose reduction or holding on ECT days; recalculate seizure threshold at first session; may need significantly higher electrical doses | A | Indian CPG 2022; APA Guidelines |
| **Topiramate** | Major | Multiple mechanisms including carbonic anhydrase inhibition | May need substantial dose reduction; consider holding on ECT days | C | Expert opinion |
| **Gabapentin/Pregabalin** | Moderate | Calcium channel modulation; milder effect on seizure threshold | May continue at lower doses; less problematic than sodium channel blockers | C | Expert opinion |

#### 3.5.8 ANTICOAGULANTS + ECT

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Warfarin** | Moderate | Risk of intracerebral hemorrhage with ECT-induced BP surge | **NOT a contraindication**; ensure INR <3.0; unilateral ECT preferred (less hemodynamic stress); use beta-blockers for BP control | B | APA Guidelines; case series |
| **DOACs** | Moderate | Anticoagulant effect | Generally safe; ensure last dose >12 hours before ECT; coordinate timing | C | Expert opinion |
| **Aspirin** | Monitor | Antiplatelet effect | May continue; minimal additional risk | C | Expert opinion |

**Key note**: ECT can be safely performed in anticoagulated patients with proper precautions. The hemodynamic surge (tachycardia + hypertension during seizure, then bradycardia) is the primary concern, not the seizure itself.

### 3.6 VNS (Vagus Nerve Stimulation)

*VNS has no known pharmacokinetic drug interactions. All interactions are pharmacodynamic (synergistic or additive effects).*  
*Source: Winter 2023 (Mainz Epilepsy Registry, n=151); PMID: 37368131*

#### 3.6.1 ANTICONVULSANTS + VNS (Epilepsy)

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **SV2A Modulators** (levetiracetam, brivaracetam) | **SYNERGISTIC** | VNS + SV2A may amplify EEG desynchronization | **Optimal combination**: 64% responder rate, 19.8% seizure freedom; brivaracetam > levetiracetam (69.2% vs 53.8% responder rate) | C | Winter 2023; PMID: 37368131 |
| **Slow Sodium Channel Inhibitors** (lacosamide, eslicarbazepine) | **SYNERGISTIC** | Similar desynchronization mechanism | **Optimal combination**: 61.8% responder rate, 19.7% seizure freedom; lacosamide and eslicarbazepine comparable | C | Winter 2023 |
| **Fast Sodium Channel Inhibitors** (carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine) | Neutral | Standard anticonvulsant effect | Less favorable: 36% responder rate; no synergistic effect with VNS | C | Winter 2023 |
| **GABA Agonists** (clonazepam, phenobarbital) | Neutral | GABAergic mechanism | 34.5% responder rate; no synergistic benefit; may use for breakthrough seizures | C | Winter 2023 |
| **Valproate** | Neutral | Multiple mechanisms | 31.7% responder rate; no specific synergy; continue if clinically indicated | C | Winter 2023 |
| **Lamotrigine** | Neutral | Sodium channel + glutamate blockade | 35.9% responder rate; no specific synergy; continue if clinically indicated | C | Winter 2023 |
| **Topiramate** | Neutral | Multiple mechanisms | 36.4% responder rate; may cause weight loss (additive with VNS) | C | Winter 2023 |
| **Pregabalin** | Neutral | Calcium channel modulation | 18.2% responder rate; limited data | C | Winter 2023 |

#### 3.6.2 ANTIDEPRESSANTS + VNS (Treatment-Resistant Depression)

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **SSRIs/SNRIs** | Monitor | VNS has serotonergic/noradrenergic effects via NTS | May enhance antidepressant response; no contraindication; standard practice in TRD trials | B | Rush 2005 (D-02 study); Aaronson 2013 |
| **Other antidepressants** | Monitor | May synergize with VNS neuromodulatory effects | Continue concurrent antidepressants; VNS approved as adjunctive therapy | B | FDA label; clinical guidelines |

#### 3.6.3 KEY VNS PRINCIPLES

- VNS therapy **REDUCES pharmacological burden** over time
- VNS exhibits **no pharmacokinetic interactions** with ASMs or other medications
- VNS may **reduce behavioral side effects** of ASMs due to mood-improving properties
- Optimal VNS + ASM combinations may allow **dose reduction** of anticonvulsants
- Dosing optimization should consider synergistic mechanism classes

### 3.7 tFUS (Transcranial Focused Ultrasound)

*tFUS for neuromodulation is an emerging field. Medication interaction data is sparse. Below are theoretical and preliminary findings.*

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Microbubble contrast agents** (required for BBB opening) | Major | Essential component for FUS-BBB opening | Administer IV immediately before sonication; monitor for allergic reactions | B | Treat 2018; Lipsman 2014 |
| **Anticoagulants** (warfarin, DOACs) | **CRITICAL** | BBB opening increases risk of intracerebral hemorrhage | **Contraindicated** on day of BBB-opening procedure; hold anticoagulants per protocol | C | Lipsman 2014 (Alzheimer trial) |
| **Antiplatelets** (aspirin, clopidogrel) | Major | Increased hemorrhage risk with BBB disruption | Hold 5-7 days before BBB-opening procedures | C | Expert opinion |
| **Anticonvulsants** | Monitor | May be needed prophylactically for BBB-opening procedures | Consider prophylactic levetiracetam (standard in many protocols) | C | Lipsman 2014 |
| **Chemotherapeutic agents** (for BBB delivery) | Moderate | tFUS opens BBB to enhance drug delivery to brain | Timing-critical: administer systemically within BBB-open window (~4-6 hours) | B | Treat 2018; Meng 2021 |

**Note**: tFUS for neuromodulation without microbubbles does not open the BBB and has different safety considerations. Medication interactions for low-intensity tFUS are largely unknown. Extrapolation from diagnostic ultrasound suggests minimal interactions.

### 3.9 Neurofeedback / EEG Biofeedback

*Medications profoundly affect EEG patterns, which form the basis of neurofeedback training. Understanding these effects is essential for valid training and interpretation.*

#### 3.9.1 ANTIDEPRESSANTS + NEUROFEEDBACK

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **SSRIs** (citalopram, sertraline) | Moderate | **Increase beta (18-30 Hz); decrease alpha** | EEG baselines obtained on medication will differ from off-medication; train to modified targets; document medication status | C | Knott 2000; Nissen 2020; BioSource Software 2025 |
| **TCAs** (amitriptyline) | Moderate | **Increase delta and theta power** (sedating effect) | EEG will show slowing; may confound theta-based training protocols; document medication status | C | Knott 2000 |
| **Bupropion** | Moderate | Activating profile; likely increases beta, decreases slow activity | Similar considerations to SSRIs; may enhance alertness during training | D | Expert extrapolation |
| **Mirtazapine** | Moderate | Increases slow-wave activity due to antihistamine effect | May confound theta/delta training targets; typically prescribed at bedtime | D | Expert extrapolation |

#### 3.9.2 STIMULANTS + NEUROFEEDBACK

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Methylphenidate** | Major | **Increases beta; decreases alpha and theta** | Profoundly alters EEG baseline; stimulant-on vs. stimulant-off EEGs are markedly different; establish consistent medication state for training | C | Lubar 2003; Arns 2012; Janzen 1995 |
| **Amphetamines** | Major | Same methylphenidate pattern | Same recommendations; may increase theta/beta ratio paradoxically in some individuals | C | Expert extrapolation |
| **Atomoxetine** | Moderate | Increases NE; increases beta activity | Similar but milder effects than stimulants | D | Expert extrapolation |

#### 3.9.3 ANTIANXIETY AGENTS + NEUROFEEDBACK

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Benzodiazepines** | Major | **Increase beta activity; decrease alpha** | Significantly alters EEG; may confound beta training protocols; if tapering, expect EEG changes during withdrawal | C | Knott 2000; Salinsky 1995 |
| **Buspirone** | Mild | Minimal EEG effects | Preferred if neurofeedback is primary treatment; does not confound EEG | D | Expert opinion |

#### 3.9.4 ANTIPSYCHOTICS + NEUROFEEDBACK

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Typical antipsychotics** (haloperidol) | Moderate | Increase slow-wave activity; may cause EEG slowing | May confound training protocols; document medication and dose | C | Knott 2000 |
| **Clozapine** | Major | Significant EEG changes; may cause epileptiform activity | Obtain baseline EEG before training; monitor for seizure activity; may limit neurofeedback utility | C | Centorrino 1994 |
| **Atypical antipsychotics** | Moderate | Variable effects; some increase beta, some increase slow activity | Document medication; establish stable dosing before EEG baseline | D | Expert opinion |

#### 3.9.5 ANTICONVULSANTS + NEUROFEEDBACK

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **All anticonvulsants** | Moderate | Generally increase beta and decrease epileptiform activity | May confound protocols targeting epileptiform discharges; may be beneficial for seizure-focused neurofeedback | C | Salinsky 1995 |
| **Lamotrigine** | Mild | Minimal EEG effects relative to other anticonvulsants | Generally well-tolerated with neurofeedback | C | Expert opinion |

#### 3.9.6 MOOD STABILIZERS + NEUROFEEDBACK

| Drug Class | Severity | Mechanism | Clinical Recommendation | Evidence | Key Refs |
|------------|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------|----------|
| **Lithium** | Moderate | May cause diffuse slowing of EEG background; may cause cognitive blunting on EEG | Establish baseline on stable lithium dose; EEG may show theta/delta increase | C | Knott 2000 |
| **Valproic Acid** | Moderate | Increases beta activity; may reduce epileptiform discharges | Similar considerations to anticonvulsants | C | Salinsky 1995 |

#### 3.9.7 CRITICAL NEUROFEEDBACK PRINCIPLES

1. **Medication status must be documented** at every EEG recording and neurofeedback session
2. **Stable medication state is required** for valid training: changes in medication = changes in EEG targets
3. **Spindling excessive beta** on baseline EEG predicts poor response to SSRIs and benzodiazepines
4. **Subclinical epileptiform discharges** increase vulnerability to adverse effects from antidepressants and stimulants
5. **Withdrawal effects** from benzodiazepines, stimulants, and SSRIs cause transient EEG changes that confound training
6. **Single-dose effects**: Psychotropic drugs alter EEG within 1-3 hours of administration

## 5. Clinical Decision Algorithms

### 5.1 Pre-rTMS Medication Screening Algorithm

```
1. Screen for CRITICAL contraindications:
   - Active stimulant use (lower seizure threshold)
   - High-dose bupropion (>300mg)
   - High-dose clozapine (>600mg)
   - Recent benzodiazepine taper/withdrawal
   
2. Calculate adjusted resting motor threshold:
   - On VPA: Expect ~20% elevation; increase stimulator output
   - On carbamazepine: May need higher intensity
   - On other anticonvulsants: Monitor RMT
   
3. Assess for efficacy modifiers:
   - On stimulants: May enhance response; monitor for adverse effects
   - On benzodiazepines: May attenuate response; consider taper
   - On lithium/lamotrigine: May improve response; continue
   
4. Proceed with standard safety screening (TASS)
```

### 5.2 Pre-ECT Medication Management Algorithm

```
1. HOLD morning of ECT (if clinically safe):
   - Benzodiazepines
   - Anticonvulsants (all classes)
   - Lithium (or reduce to lower therapeutic range)
   - Oral hypoglycemics
   - Diuretics
   
2. DO NOT HOLD (take with small sip of water):
   - Antihypertensives
   - Thyroid medication
   - Antianginals
   - Bronchodilators
   - Anticholinergics
   
3. DISCONTINUE before ECT course:
   - MAOIs (2-week washout)
   - Theophylline (absolute contraindication)
   - Bupropion (>300mg)
   - Stimulants (24-48 hour washout)
   
4. ANESTHETIC SELECTION based on seizure threshold:
   - Need LONGER seizure: Ketamine > Etomidate > Methohexital
   - Need SHORTER seizure: Propofol > Sevoflurane
   - Cardiac concerns: Propofol (minimal BP effect) or Methohexital
   - Gold standard: Methohexital (neutral)
```

### 5.3 tDCS Medication Screening Algorithm

```
1. ABSOLUTE contraindications for plasticity-based tDCS:
   - Carbamazepine (sodium channel blocker)
   - Flunarizine (calcium channel blocker)
   - NMDA antagonists (dextromethorphan, ketamine)
   - High-dose L-DOPA (inverted-U effect)
   
2. Significant modifiers requiring protocol adjustment:
   - Lorazepam: After-effects delayed but prolonged
   - SSRIs/SNRIs: Cathodal effects may reverse to enhancement
   - Amphetamine: After-effects prolonged
   - Nicotine: Anodal enhancement eliminated
   - Propranolol: After-effects shortened
   - Rivastigmine: Initial block, then delayed prolongation
   
3. Compatible medications (may continue):
   - Most antipsychotics
   - Gabapentin/pregabalin (mild effects)
   - Most antidepressants (with monitoring)
   - Mood stabilizers (with monitoring)
```

### 5.4 VNS Medication Optimization Algorithm

```
1. For EPILEPSY (adjunctive therapy):
   OPTIMAL combinations (synergistic):
   - SV2A modulators: Brivaracetam > Levetiracetam
   - Slow Na+ channel inhibitors: Lacosamide, Eslicarbazepine
   
   LESS FAVORABLE:
   - Fast Na+ channel inhibitors: Carbamazepine, Oxcarbazepine
   - GABA agonists: Clonazepam, Phenobarbital
   - Valproate, Lamotrigine: Neutral (no specific synergy)
   
2. Goal: Optimize ASM selection THEN titrate VNS parameters
   - Reduce ASM burden over time as VNS takes effect
   - No pharmacokinetic interactions to manage
```
