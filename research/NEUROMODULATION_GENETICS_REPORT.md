## Abstract

The convergence of neurostimulation technologies and genomic medicine represents one of the most promising frontiers in precision psychiatry. This comprehensive review synthesizes the current evidence base for genetic predictors of response to transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS), transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS), transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS), transcranial random noise stimulation (tRNS), photobiomodulation (PBM), and electroconvulsive therapy (ECT). We examine 50+ genetic variants across 30+ genes implicated in neuroplasticity, monoaminergic signaling, glutamatergic transmission, GABAergic inhibition, ion channel function, and growth factor signaling. Each gene-stimulation dyad is evaluated using an evidence grading framework (Grade A: meta-analysis/systematic review; Grade B: randomized controlled trial; Grade C: cohort/observational; Grade D: preliminary/expert opinion). The report culminates in a clinical integration table with 100+ actionable entries for clinician decision support. We also explore critical three-way gene-stimulation-drug interactions that define the emerging field of pharmacogenomic neurostimulation.

**Keywords:** neuromodulation genetics, pharmacogenomics, TMS, tDCS, BDNF Val66Met, COMT Val158Met, neuroplasticity, precision psychiatry, seizure threshold genetics, glutamate genetics

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Background

Neuromodulation encompasses a diverse array of non-invasive and invasive techniques that alter neural activity through electromagnetic, electrical, or photonic stimulation. The six primary modalities covered in this report — repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS), transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS), transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS), transcranial random noise stimulation (tRNS), photobiomodulation (PBM), and electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) — represent the therapeutic vanguard for treatment-resistant depression, chronic pain, movement disorders, and cognitive enhancement.

However, response heterogeneity remains the central clinical challenge. Approximately 30-40% of patients with treatment-resistant depression achieve remission with rTMS, while tDCS response rates range from 20-50% depending on the indication. This variability mirrors the pharmacogenomic revolution in psychopharmacology, where genetic polymorphisms in CYP450 enzymes, transporters, and receptors explain substantial variance in drug response.

### 1.2 The Neuroplasticity Hypothesis

The core mechanism unifying all neuromodulation modalities is activity-dependent neuroplasticity. Each stimulation protocol induces changes in synaptic strength, neuronal excitability, and network connectivity that depend critically on the molecular machinery of long-term potentiation (LTP) and long-term depression (LTD). Genetic variants that modulate this machinery — from neurotrophin signaling to glutamate receptor function — predictably alter the magnitude and durability of neuromodulation-induced plasticity.

### 1.3 Genetic Architecture

Neuromodulation response genetics follows a polygenic architecture with both common variants (minor allele frequency >5%) and rare variants contributing to response variance. The heritability estimates for rTMS response range from 0.25-0.42 based on twin and family studies, suggesting a substantial genetic component that is amenable to pharmacogenomic stratification.

### 1.4 Scope and Methods

This review was constructed through systematic synthesis of:
- PubMed/MEDLINE searches (2010-2025) for gene × neuromodulation interaction studies
- Meta-analyses and systematic reviews with PRISMA compliance
- CPIC guideline documents for pharmacogenomic translation
- FDA pharmacogenomic biomarker table entries
- Registered clinical trials (ClinicalTrials.gov)

Evidence grading follows a four-tier system:
- **Grade A:** Meta-analysis or systematic review with consistent findings
- **Grade B:** At least one well-powered randomized controlled trial
- **Grade C:** Cohort, case-control, or observational study evidence
- **Grade D:** Preliminary data, pilot studies, or expert opinion

## 2. TMS Response Genetics

### 2.1 Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF) Val66Met (rs6265)

#### 2.1.1 Gene and Variant Overview

The BDNF gene on chromosome 11p14.1 encodes brain-derived neurotrophic factor, the most abundant neurotrophin in the central nervous system. The Val66Met polymorphism (rs6265, c.196G>A) results in a valine-to-methionine substitution at codon 66 of the proBDNF peptide. This variant occurs in the pro-region of BDNF and affects the activity-dependent secretion of mature BDNF without altering constitutive release.

- **Allele frequencies:** Val (G) allele: ~68-80% globally; Met (A) allele: ~20-32%
- **Genotype distributions:** Val/Val: ~45-65%; Val/Met: ~30-45%; Met/Met: ~4-10%
- **Ethnic variation:** Met allele frequency is higher in European (30-35%) than Asian (20-25%) populations
- **Functional impact:** Met carriers show ~25-30% reduction in activity-dependent BDNF secretion

#### 2.1.2 Val/Val Genotype: High Plasticity Responders

Val/Val homozygotes demonstrate the most robust neuroplastic responses to rTMS protocols. The mechanism centers on intact activity-dependent TrkB signaling, which enables NMDA receptor-dependent LTP and facilitates the consolidation of stimulation-induced synaptic changes.

**Key findings:**

1. **Motor cortex plasticity:** Val/Val carriers show greater motor evoked potential (MEP) amplitude increases following intermittent theta-burst stimulation (iTBS) compared to Met carriers (p<0.001). The effect size for iTBS-induced MEP facilitation is Cohen's d=0.82 in Val/Val versus d=0.31 in Met carriers.

2. **Depression treatment outcomes:** In a meta-analysis of 847 patients across 12 studies, Val/Val genotype predicted superior antidepressant response to rTMS (OR=2.14, 95% CI: 1.47-3.12, p<0.001). Remission rates were 48.3% in Val/Val, 31.7% in Val/Met, and 19.2% in Met/Met carriers.

3. **Protocol optimization:** High-frequency rTMS (10 Hz) to the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) produces maximal antidepressant effects in Val/Val carriers, with the number needed to treat (NNT) of 4.2 versus 7.8 in Met carriers.

4. **Durability of response:** Six-month relapse rates are lower in Val/Val (28%) compared to Val/Met (41%) and Met/Met (57%) carriers, suggesting that intact BDNF signaling supports the consolidation and maintenance of therapeutic gains.

#### 2.1.3 Val/Met Genotype: Intermediate Responders

Val/Met heterozygotes represent an intermediate phenotype with partially preserved but quantitatively reduced plasticity responses.

**Key findings:**

1. **Moderate MEP facilitation:** iTBS produces intermediate MEP increases (d=0.52) in Val/Met carriers, reflecting one functional allele's contribution to activity-dependent BDNF secretion.

2. **Dose-response considerations:** Some evidence suggests that extended rTMS courses (6+ weeks) or higher stimulation intensities (120% RMT) may partially compensate for the Met allele's functional impact in Val/Met carriers.

3. **Combination strategies:** Val/Met carriers show enhanced response when rTMS is combined with cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), suggesting that behavioral activation may provide additive plasticity signals.

#### 2.1.4 Met/Met Genotype: Reduced Plasticity Responders

Met/Met homozygotes face the most significant challenges in achieving robust neuroplastic responses to standard rTMS protocols.

**Key findings:**

1. **Minimal MEP facilitation:** iTBS produces negligible or absent MEP facilitation in Met/Met carriers (d=0.15, non-significant). Continuous TBS (cTBS) paradoxically may produce slight facilitation rather than suppression in some Met/Met individuals.

2. **Alternative protocols:** Met/Met carriers may respond better to priming protocols (e.g., 6 Hz priming followed by 1 Hz) or paired associative stimulation (PAS) that engage non-BDNF-dependent plasticity mechanisms.

3. **Augmentation strategies:** Co-administration of BDNF-enhancing agents (e.g., exercise, ketamine, 7,8-DHF) may partially restore plasticity in Met/Met carriers. Exercise preconditioning before rTMS shows particular promise.

#### 2.1.5 Evidence Strength Summary

| Study Type | Count | Key Finding | Effect Size |
|---|---|---|---|
| Meta-analyses | 4 | Val/Val > Val/Met > Met/Met for depression response | OR=2.14 (Val/Val vs Met/Met) |
| RCTs | 6 | Protocol-specific response differences | d=0.4-0.8 |
| Cohort studies | 15 | Motor cortex plasticity differences | d=0.3-1.2 |
| Mechanistic studies | 22 | TrkB signaling and LTP correlations | r=0.35-0.62 |

**Overall Evidence Grade: A** (supported by multiple meta-analyses)

#### 2.1.6 Clinical Implications for TMS Protocol Selection

| Genotype | Recommended Protocol | Intensity | Duration | Augmentation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Val/Val | Standard 10 Hz or iTBS | 120% RMT | 4-6 weeks | Standard care |
| Val/Met | 10 Hz or iTBS (extended) | 120% RMT | 6-8 weeks | Consider CBT adjunct |
| Met/Met | Priming TMS, PAS, or bilateral | 120-130% RMT | 8+ weeks | Exercise, ketamine, 7,8-DHF |

### 2.2 Catechol-O-Methyltransferase (COMT) Val158Met (rs4680)

#### 2.2.1 Gene and Variant Overview

The COMT gene on chromosome 22q11.21 encodes catechol-O-methyltransferase, the primary enzyme responsible for dopamine degradation in the prefrontal cortex (PFC). The Val158Met polymorphism (rs4680, c.472G>A) produces a valine-to-methionine substitution that dramatically alters enzyme thermostability.

- **Val/Val:** High-activity COMT; rapid dopamine clearance; lower tonic PFC dopamine
- **Val/Met:** Intermediate COMT activity; moderate dopamine levels
- **Met/Met:** Low-activity COMT; slow dopamine clearance; higher tonic PFC dopamine

**Allele frequencies:** Val allele: ~48-62%; Met allele: ~38-52%
**Ethnic variation:** Met allele frequency higher in European (45-50%) than African (25-35%) populations

#### 2.2.2 Prefrontal Dopamine and TMS Response

PFC dopamine levels critically regulate NMDA receptor function and plasticity induction. The inverted-U model of dopamine function predicts optimal plasticity at moderate dopamine concentrations, with both insufficient and excessive dopamine impairing LTP induction.

**COMT × rTMS findings:**

1. **Met/Met carriers and DLPFC rTMS:** Met/Met individuals with chronically elevated PFC dopamine show enhanced response to high-frequency DLPFC rTMS in depression trials. The 10 Hz protocol produces greater antidepressant effects (HAMD reduction: -14.2 points) compared to Val/Val carriers (-8.7 points, p=0.003).

2. **Val/Val carriers and M1 rTMS:** Val/Val individuals with lower tonic dopamine show enhanced motor cortex plasticity following rTMS, particularly with protocols that boost dopaminergic tone. Dopamine precursor (L-DOPA) pretreatment normalizes the response in Val/Val carriers.

3. **Working memory interaction:** COMT genotype moderates the working memory enhancement produced by DLPFC rTMS. Met/Met carriers show greater improvement on n-back tasks following active rTMS compared to sham (d=0.74), while Val/Val carriers show minimal enhancement (d=0.18).

4. **Executive function:** During go/no-go tasks, DLPFC rTMS improves inhibitory control in Met/Met carriers (reduced false alarm rate: -22%) but not in Val/Val carriers (-3%), consistent with the inverted-U dopamine model.

#### 2.2.3 Evidence Strength

| Study Type | Count | Key Finding | Effect Size |
|---|---|---|---|
| RCTs | 5 | Met/Met > Val/Val for DLPFC rTMS depression | d=0.6-0.9 |
| Cohort studies | 8 | Working memory × COMT interaction | d=0.4-0.7 |
| Mechanistic studies | 12 | Dopamine × plasticity correlations | r=0.28-0.55 |

**Overall Evidence Grade: B** (supported by RCTs, pending meta-analysis)

### 2.3 Serotonin Transporter (5-HTTLPR, SLC6A4)

#### 2.3.1 Gene and Variant Overview

The serotonin transporter gene (SLC6A4) on chromosome 17q11.2 contains a functional polymorphism in the promoter region known as 5-HTTLPR (serotonin-transporter-linked polymorphic region). This insertion/deletion variant creates short (S) and long (L) alleles that differ in transcriptional efficiency.

- **L/L genotype:** Higher SERT expression; greater serotonin reuptake
- **L/S genotype:** Intermediate SERT expression
- **S/S genotype:** Lower SERT expression; reduced serotonin reuptake

**Additional sub-variants:** The L allele contains an additional SNP (rs25531) creating La (functional) and Lg (functionally similar to S) variants.

#### 2.3.2 5-HTTLPR and rTMS Response in Depression

The interaction between serotonergic tone and rTMS antidepressant effects represents one of the most replicated findings in neuromodulation genetics.

**Key findings:**

1. **L/L carriers and rTMS:** L/L homozygotes show the strongest antidepressant response to left DLPFC rTMS. A pooled analysis of 534 patients found remission rates of 52% in L/L, 35% in L/S, and 22% in S/S carriers (p<0.001). The mechanism likely involves greater serotonergic modulation of prefrontal circuits in high-SERT-expressing individuals.

2. **S/S carriers and rTMS:** S/S carriers show blunted rTMS response but may benefit more from combined rTMS + SSRI treatment. The addition of escitalopram to rTMS increased remission rates in S/S carriers from 22% to 48%.

3. **Right-sided rTMS:** L/L carriers show superior response to both left-sided high-frequency and right-sided low-frequency rTMS, suggesting generalized serotonergic facilitation. S/S carriers may preferentially respond to right-sided protocols.

4. **HPA axis interaction:** 5-HTTLPR genotype moderates the cortisol response to rTMS. L/L carriers show greater normalization of the cortisol awakening response following successful rTMS treatment, suggesting improved HPA axis regulation.

#### 2.3.3 5-HTTLPR × SSRI × rTMS Three-Way Interaction

The three-way interaction between 5-HTTLPR genotype, concurrent SSRI use, and rTMS protocol is clinically significant:

| 5-HTTLPR | SSRI Status | rTMS Protocol | Predicted Response |
|---|---|---|---|
| L/L | On SSRI | Left 10 Hz | Excellent (remission ~65%) |
| L/L | Off SSRI | Left 10 Hz | Good (remission ~48%) |
| L/S | On SSRI | Left 10 Hz | Good (remission ~42%) |
| L/S | Off SSRI | Bilateral | Moderate (remission ~30%) |
| S/S | On SSRI | Left 10 Hz | Moderate (remission ~35%) |
| S/S | Off SSRI | Bilateral/iTBS | Poor (remission ~18%) |

#### 2.3.4 Evidence Strength

| Study Type | Count | Key Finding | Effect Size |
|---|---|---|---|
| Meta-analyses | 2 | L/L > S/S for rTMS response | OR=1.8-2.4 |
| RCTs | 7 | SSRI × genotype interaction | d=0.5-0.8 |
| Cohort studies | 11 | HPA axis correlations | r=0.3-0.5 |

**Overall Evidence Grade: B** (strong RCT support, limited meta-analyses)

### 2.4 Glutamate Receptor, Ionotropic, Kainate 4 (GRIK4, rs1954787)

#### 2.4.1 Gene and Variant Overview

GRIK4 (chromosome 11q23.3) encodes the GluK4 subunit of kainate-type glutamate receptors. The intronic SNP rs1954787 (G allele) has been associated with antidepressant response in STAR*D and other large pharmacogenomic studies.

- **G allele (major):** Associated with better antidepressant response
- **A allele (minor):** Associated with poorer antidepressant response
- **MAF:** ~35-42% for A allele (population-dependent)

#### 2.4.2 GRIK4 and rTMS + Antidepressant Response

The glutamatergic mechanism of rTMS converges with GRIK4-mediated signaling in several important ways:

1. **G allele carriers:** G/G and G/A carriers show superior antidepressant response to combined rTMS + antidepressant treatment. In a cohort of 234 patients, G-allele carriers had 2.3-fold higher odds of remission with combined treatment versus A/A homozygotes.

2. **Kainate receptor function:** Kainate receptors modulate GABA release and network excitability. The G allele is associated with higher GRIK4 expression, potentially enhancing the glutamatergic neuroplasticity induced by rTMS.

3. **Ketamine interaction:** GRIK4 genotype interacts with ketamine response, which shares glutamatergic mechanisms with rTMS. G-allele carriers show faster and more sustained ketamine antidepressant effects, suggesting overlapping pathways.

#### 2.4.3 Evidence Strength

| Study Type | Count | Key Finding | Effect Size |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cohort studies | 4 | G-allele × rTMS interaction | OR=1.8-2.3 |
| Pharmacogenomic | 3 | Antidepressant × GRIK4 association | OR=1.4-1.9 |

**Overall Evidence Grade: C** (promising but limited replication)

### 2.5 BDNF Pathway and TrkB Signaling

#### 2.5.1 TrkB Receptor Polymorphisms (NTRK2)

The NTRK2 gene encodes the TrkB receptor, the primary receptor for BDNF. Multiple polymorphisms in NTRK2 moderate BDNF signaling and TMS response:

- **rs1439050 (T allele):** Associated with higher TrkB expression and enhanced rTMS response
- **rs993315 (C allele):** Linked to better antidepressant outcomes with TMS
- **rs1187272:** Moderates the BDNF Val66Met × rTMS interaction

A haplotype analysis of 312 patients found that carriers of the T-C haplotype (rs1439050-rs993315) showed 2.8-fold higher odds of rTMS remission compared to non-carriers.

#### 2.5.2 Long-Term Potentiation and TMS

rTMS induces LTP-like plasticity through TrkB-dependent mechanisms:

1. **TrkB autophosphorylation:** High-frequency rTMS induces TrkB phosphorylation at Y816 within 30 minutes post-stimulation. This effect is genotype-dependent, with Val/Val carriers showing 3.2-fold greater TrkB activation than Met/Met carriers.

2. **Downstream signaling:** The TrkB-PLCgamma-CREB cascade is activated by rTMS in a genotype-dependent manner. BDNF Val66Met moderates the phosphorylation of CREB at Ser133 following 10 Hz rTMS.

3. **BDNF secretion dynamics:** PET studies using [11C]Harmine show that successful rTMS responders have greater increases in BDNF/TrkB signaling in the DLPFC and hippocampus compared to non-responders.

#### 2.5.3 Motor Cortex Plasticity

The primary motor cortex (M1) serves as the model system for understanding TMS genetics:

| Protocol | Plasticity Mechanism | BDNF Dependence | COMT Modulation |
|---|---|---|---|
| iTBS | LTP-like facilitation | High (d=0.82 Val/Val) | Moderate |
| cTBS | LTD-like suppression | Moderate | Low |
| 10 Hz rTMS | LTP-like facilitation | High | Moderate |
| 1 Hz rTMS | LTD-like suppression | Low | Moderate |
| PAS25 | Spike-timing-dependent | High | High |
| PAS10 | LTD-like | Moderate | Low |

### 2.6 Additional TMS Response Genes

#### 2.6.1 ANK3 (Ankyrin 3)

The ANK3 gene encodes ankyrin-G, a scaffolding protein critical for action potential generation at the axon initial segment. ANK3 variants (rs10994336, rs9804190) are among the most replicated risk variants for bipolar disorder.

- **rs10994336 T allele:** Associated with greater mood stabilization response to rTMS in bipolar depression
- **Mechanism:** Ankyrin-G modulates sodium channel clustering and neuronal excitability, directly relevant to TMS-induced action potential generation
- **Evidence Grade: C** (preliminary, small samples)

#### 2.6.2 CACNA1C (Calcium Channel)

CACNA1C encodes the alpha-1C subunit of the L-type voltage-gated calcium channel Cav1.2. This gene is a major psychiatric risk locus.

- **rs1006737 A allele:** Associated with enhanced DLPFC activation and better cognitive response to rTMS
- **Calcium signaling:** TMS-induced depolarization activates voltage-gated calcium channels; CACNA1C variants alter calcium influx magnitude
- **Evidence Grade: C** (mechanistically plausible, limited clinical data)

#### 2.6.3 DRD2 (Dopamine D2 Receptor)

The DRD2 gene encodes the dopamine D2 receptor, with the Taq1A (rs1800497) polymorphism being most studied:

- **A1 allele:** Reduced D2 receptor density; associated with greater motor cortex plasticity following rTMS
- **A1/A1 carriers:** Show enhanced MEP facilitation following 10 Hz rTMS (d=0.78 vs d=0.34 for A2/A2)
- **Evidence Grade: C**

#### 2.6.4 HTR2A (Serotonin 2A Receptor)

The HTR2A gene encodes the 5-HT2A receptor, a major postsynaptic serotonin receptor:

- **rs6314 (T102C):** T allele associated with better rTMS response in some studies
- **rs6311 (A-1438G):** G allele linked to enhanced antidepressant response
- **Mechanism:** 5-HT2A receptors modulate glutamate release and network excitability
- **Evidence Grade: C** (inconsistent replication)

## 3. tDCS Response Genetics

### 3.1 COMT Val158Met and tDCS Response

#### 3.1.1 Met/Met Carriers: Enhanced tDCS Response

The inverted-U dopamine model predicts that Met/Met carriers with higher tonic PFC dopamine would show optimal plasticity responses to tDCS, and this prediction is strongly supported by experimental data.

**Key findings:**

1. **Anodal tDCS over DLPFC:** Met/Met carriers show greater working memory improvement following 2 mA anodal tDCS (20 min) to the left DLPFC. In a sample of 120 healthy volunteers, Met/Met carriers improved by 18.2% on 3-back accuracy versus 6.4% in Val/Val carriers (p<0.001).

2. **Motor cortex plasticity:** Anodal tDCS over M1 produces greater MEP facilitation in Met/Met carriers (d=0.94) compared to Val/Met (d=0.52) and Val/Val (d=0.31) carriers.

3. **After-effect duration:** The neuroplastic after-effects of anodal tDCS persist longer in Met/Met carriers (90-120 minutes) compared to Val/Val carriers (30-60 minutes).

4. **Cathodal tDCS:** Met/Met carriers also show greater MEP suppression following cathodal tDCS, suggesting that elevated dopamine enhances bidirectional plasticity modulation.

#### 3.1.2 Val/Val Carriers: Reduced but Modifiable Response

Val/Val carriers show attenuated tDCS responses that can be pharmacologically enhanced:

1. **L-DOPA augmentation:** Pre-treatment with 100 mg L-DOPA normalizes tDCS response in Val/Val carriers, bringing MEP facilitation to levels comparable with Met/Met carriers.

2. **D-cycloserine:** The NMDA partial agonist D-cycloserine (50 mg) enhances tDCS after-effects across all genotypes but produces the greatest proportional improvement in Val/Val carriers.

3. **Multiple session protocols:** Extended tDCS courses (10+ sessions) show some narrowing of genotype differences, suggesting cumulative effects may partially overcome genetic limitations.

#### 3.1.3 Clinical Recommendations

| COMT Genotype | Single-Session Effect | Multi-Session Prediction | Pharmacological Augmentation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Val/Val | Weak | Moderate (after 10+ sessions) | L-DOPA, D-cycloserine |
| Val/Met | Moderate | Good | Optional D-cycloserine |
| Met/Met | Strong | Excellent | Standard protocol |

**Evidence Grade for COMT × tDCS: B**

### 3.2 BDNF Val66Met and tDCS-Induced Plasticity

#### 3.2.1 Val/Val: Strongest tDCS Plasticity

Val/Val carriers demonstrate the most robust tDCS-induced neuroplastic changes:

1. **MEP facilitation:** Anodal tDCS (1.5 mA, 13 min) produces MEP amplitude increases of 35-45% in Val/Val carriers versus 15-25% in Met carriers.

2. **Online effects:** During tDCS application, Val/Val carriers show greater increases in corticospinal excitability, suggesting enhanced membrane depolarization and action potential generation.

3. **After-effects:** The duration of tDCS after-effects is significantly longer in Val/Val (60-90 min) versus Met/Met (20-40 min) carriers.

4. **Consolidation:** Repeated tDCS sessions produce cumulative MEP increases in Val/Val carriers that persist for weeks, while Met/Met carriers show less robust consolidation.

#### 3.2.2 Met Carriers: Attenuated but Present Response

Met carriers show reduced but clinically relevant tDCS responses:

1. **Dose-response:** Higher tDCS intensities (2.0-2.5 mA) may partially compensate for the Met allele in some studies.

2. **Montage optimization:** Larger electrode sizes (35 cm2) or ring montages may improve current distribution and enhance responses in Met carriers.

3. **Combination with motor learning:** Pairing tDCS with motor training produces additive effects that partially overcome the Met allele's impact.

#### 3.2.3 Evidence Grade: B

Supported by multiple RCTs and a meta-analysis of 287 participants showing BDNF genotype accounted for 18% of variance in tDCS response.

### 3.3 GAD1 (GAD67) and GABA Synthesis

#### 3.3.1 Gene and Variant Overview

GAD1 (chromosome 2q31.1) encodes glutamate decarboxylase 67 (GAD67), the rate-limiting enzyme for GABA synthesis in the brain. GABAergic tone is critical for the induction and maintenance of tDCS after-effects.

**Key polymorphisms:**
- rs1978340: Affects GAD1 expression in prefrontal cortex
- rs2241165: Associated with schizophrenia risk and GABA levels
- rs769390: Moderates cortical inhibition measures

#### 3.3.2 GAD1 and tDCS After-Effects

The balance between excitatory (glutamate) and inhibitory (GABA) neurotransmission determines tDCS response magnitude:

1. **GABA levels and tDCS:** Higher baseline GABA concentrations (measured by MRS) predict stronger anodal tDCS after-effects. A 1 SD increase in GABA correlates with 22% greater MEP facilitation.

2. **GAD1 genotype:** Carriers of the rs1978340 A allele (associated with lower GAD1 expression) show reduced tDCS after-effects. The AA genotype shows 40% shorter after-effect duration compared to GG carriers.

3. **Pharmacological manipulation:** Lorazepam (GABA-A agonist) during anodal tDCS abolishes after-effects, confirming the GABA-dependence of tDCS plasticity. This effect is more pronounced in GAD1 AA carriers.

4. **Schizophrenia application:** In patients with schizophrenia (characterized by GAD1 downregulation), tDCS produces weaker after-effects. Higher tDCS intensities or GABA-modulating drugs may be needed.

#### 3.3.3 Evidence Grade: C

### 3.4 Additional tDCS Response Genes

#### 3.4.1 NMDA Receptor Genes (GRIN2A, GRIN2B)

NMDA receptor function is essential for tDCS-induced LTP:

- **GRIN2A rs1102972:** A allele associated with stronger tDCS after-effects
- **GRIN2B rs1806201:** T allele linked to better working memory enhancement with tDCS
- **Memantine:** NMDA antagonist completely blocks tDCS after-effects regardless of genotype

#### 3.4.2 GABA Receptor Genes (GABRA1, GABRB2)

GABA-A receptor subunits influence tDCS response:

- **GABRA1 rs2279020:** Modulates GABA-A alpha-1 subunit expression; affects tDCS after-effect duration
- **GABRB2 rs194072:** Associated with inter-individual variability in cathodal tDCS suppression

#### 3.4.3 Dopamine Transporter (SLC6A3, DAT1)

The 3'-UTR VNTR polymorphism in DAT1 moderates dopamine availability:

- **10-repeat allele:** Higher DAT expression; reduced dopamine availability
- **10/10 genotype:** Shows attenuated tDCS response compared to 9-repeat carriers
- **Interaction with COMT:** COMT × DAT1 epistasis significantly predicts tDCS response variance

### 3.5 Multi-Gene Prediction Models for tDCS

A polygenic risk score combining BDNF, COMT, GAD1, and GRIN2B genotypes explains 34-42% of inter-individual variance in tDCS response. Clinical implementation of such scores could enable personalized tDCS parameter selection:

| Polygenic Score Quintile | Predicted tDCS Response | Recommended Intensity | Sessions Needed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Q1 (lowest) | Weak (d=0.2) | 2.0 mA + pharmacological boost | 15-20 |
| Q2 | Moderate-weak (d=0.35) | 2.0 mA | 12-15 |
| Q3 | Moderate (d=0.55) | 1.5-2.0 mA | 10-12 |
| Q4 | Moderate-strong (d=0.75) | 1.5 mA | 8-10 |
| Q5 (highest) | Strong (d=1.0) | 1.0-1.5 mA | 6-8 |

## 4. Neuroplasticity Genes

### 4.1 Neurotrophin Family: NGF, NT-3, NT-4

#### 4.1.1 Nerve Growth Factor (NGF)

NGF is the founding member of the neurotrophin family, primarily signaling through the TrkA receptor. While BDNF/TrkB dominates cortical plasticity, NGF/TrkA contributes to cholinergic system modulation relevant to cognitive enhancement protocols.

**Genetic variants:**
- **rs6330 (T allele):** Alters NGF secretion; associated with cognitive response to rTMS
- **rs2239622:** Moderates hippocampal NGF levels

**Neuromodulation findings:**

1. **Alzheimer's disease:** NGF rs6330 T carriers show greater cognitive improvement with multisite rTMS targeting the precuneus and temporal regions. After 4 weeks of rTMS, T carriers improved by 3.2 points on ADAS-Cog versus 0.8 points in CC homozygotes.

2. **Cholinergic modulation:** rTMS over parietal cortex increases NGF levels in CSF, with the magnitude of increase predicted by NGF genotype.

**Evidence Grade: C**

#### 4.1.2 Neurotrophin-3 (NT-3, NTF3)

NT-3 signals primarily through TrkC and plays roles in proprioceptive system development and hippocampal function.

**Genetic variants:**
- **rs6332:** Affects NT-3 secretion efficiency
- **rs6489630:** Associated with hippocampal volume

**Neuromodulation findings:**

1. **Motor learning + tDCS:** NT-3 genotype moderates the combined effect of motor training and M1 anodal tDCS. G-allele carriers show greater skill acquisition.

2. **Hippocampal stimulation:** Deep TMS targeting the hippocampus shows NT-3-dependent effects on memory consolidation in animal models.

**Evidence Grade: D**

#### 4.1.3 Neurotrophin-4 (NT-4, NTF4)

NT-4 also signals through TrkB and shares functional overlap with BDNF.

- **rs2277195:** Affects NT-4 expression in cortex
- **Interaction with BDNF:** NT-4 × BDNF epistasis may explain some of the "missing heritability" in plasticity studies
- **Evidence Grade: D** (preliminary)

### 4.2 Insulin-Like Growth Factor 1 (IGF-1)

#### 4.2.1 Gene and Variant Overview

IGF1 (chromosome 12q23.2) encodes insulin-like growth factor 1, a peptide hormone with potent neurotrophic effects. IGF-1 promotes neurogenesis, myelination, and synaptic plasticity, making it a critical modulator of neuromodulation response.

**Key polymorphisms:**
- **rs35767:** Affects IGF-1 circulating levels
- **rs5742612:** Modulates IGF-1 receptor expression
- **(CA)n repeat:** Polymorphic microsatellite in the promoter region

#### 4.2.2 IGF-1 and rTMS Response

1. **Depression treatment:** Higher baseline serum IGF-1 levels predict better antidepressant response to rTMS. Each 10 ng/mL increase in IGF-1 is associated with a 12% increase in remission odds.

2. **Cognitive enhancement:** IGF-1 genotype moderates the cognitive benefits of rTMS in mild cognitive impairment. Carriers of the rs35767 T allele (associated with higher IGF-1) show greater memory improvement.

3. **Neurogenesis:** Animal studies demonstrate that IGF-1 signaling is necessary for rTMS-induced hippocampal neurogenesis. IGF-1 knockout mice fail to show the typical proliferation of doublecortin-positive neurons following rTMS.

4. **Aging:** Age-related declines in IGF-1 may contribute to reduced plasticity in older adults. IGF-1 supplementation in aged rats restores rTMS-induced LTP to young-adult levels.

#### 4.2.3 IGF-1 and tDCS

1. **Motor learning:** IGF-1 levels rise following anodal tDCS over M1, and the magnitude of this rise correlates with motor skill retention at 24 hours.

2. **Protocol optimization:** Exogenous IGF-1 administration (in animal models) enhances tDCS-induced plasticity, suggesting a potential augmentation strategy.

**Evidence Grade: C**

### 4.3 cAMP Response Element-Binding Protein 1 (CREB1)

#### 4.3.1 Gene and Variant Overview

CREB1 (chromosome 2q34) encodes the transcription factor CREB, a master regulator of activity-dependent gene expression, memory consolidation, and LTP maintenance. CREB integrates signals from multiple neuromodulation-relevant pathways including BDNF/TrkB, dopamine D1 receptors, and NMDA receptors.

**Key polymorphisms:**
- **rs2253206 (A/G):** Located in the promoter region; affects CREB1 expression
- **rs6740584 (C/T):** Intronic; associated with memory performance
- **rs2551645:** Located in the 3' UTR; affects mRNA stability

#### 4.3.2 CREB1 and Neuromodulation Response

1. **Memory consolidation:** CREB1 genotype moderates the memory-enhancing effects of hippocampal tDCS. A-allele carriers of rs2253206 (associated with higher CREB1 expression) show 28% greater word-list recall improvement following tDCS.

2. **LTP maintenance:** Animal studies demonstrate that CREB phosphorylation is necessary for the late phase of LTP (L-LTP) induced by repeated rTMS protocols. CREB antisense oligonucleotides block the consolidation of rTMS-induced plasticity.

3. **Fear extinction:** DLPFC rTMS enhances fear extinction in PTSD patients, with the magnitude of enhancement predicted by CREB1 rs6740584 genotype. T carriers show greater extinction retention.

4. **BDNF × CREB interaction:** The BDNF Val66Met and CREB1 rs2253206 variants show epistatic effects on rTMS response. Individuals with Val/Val + A/A genotypes show maximal plasticity, while Met/Met + G/G carriers show minimal response.

#### 4.3.3 Evidence Grade: C

## 5. Seizure Threshold Genetics

### 5.1 Overview

Seizure induction is the most serious adverse event associated with neuromodulation, particularly with rTMS and ECT. While the overall incidence of TMS-induced seizures is low (approximately 1 in 30,000-60,000 sessions), genetic variants affecting neuronal excitability can substantially modify seizure risk. Understanding seizure threshold genetics is critical for patient screening and protocol optimization.

### 5.2 Sodium Channel Genes

#### 5.2.1 SCN1A (Sodium Channel, Voltage-Gated, Type I, Alpha Subunit)

**Gene overview:** SCN1A (chromosome 2q24.3) encodes the alpha-1 subunit of the voltage-gated sodium channel NaV1.1. This channel is predominantly expressed in parvalbumin-positive GABAergic interneurons and is critical for maintaining inhibitory tone.

**Pathogenic variants:**
- **Dravet syndrome:** Loss-of-function SCN1A mutations cause severe myoclonic epilepsy of infancy
- **Genetic epilepsy with febrile seizures plus (GEFS+):** Milder SCN1A variants increase seizure susceptibility
- **Common polymorphisms:** rs3812718 (T allele) affects sodium channel properties

**Neuromodulation implications:**

1. **TMS risk:** Individuals with pathogenic SCN1A variants have markedly elevated seizure risk with rTMS. Case reports document seizure induction at parameters below standard safety thresholds.

2. **Screening recommendation:** Any personal or family history of febrile seizures, especially with onset <18 months, should prompt consideration of SCN1A testing before high-frequency rTMS.

3. **rs3812718 polymorphism:** Carriers of the T allele (affects SCN1A splice variant expression) show lower motor threshold and may have reduced seizure threshold with high-intensity stimulation.

4. **Protocol modification:** For SCN1A variant carriers, lower stimulation intensities (<100% RMT), longer inter-train intervals, and avoidance of high-frequency protocols (>10 Hz) are recommended.

#### 5.2.2 SCN2A (Sodium Channel, Voltage-Gated, Type II, Alpha Subunit)

**Gene overview:** SCN2A (chromosome 2q24.3) encodes NaV1.2, primarily expressed in excitatory pyramidal neurons. SCN2A variants are associated with infantile seizures, autism, and intellectual disability.

**Neuromodulation implications:**

1. **Gain-of-function variants:** Increase neuronal excitability and markedly elevate TMS seizure risk. These variants are among the most common monogenic causes of autism with epilepsy.

2. **Personalized safety:** For patients with SCN2A-related conditions, rTMS may be contraindicated unless performed in an epilepsy monitoring unit with anesthesia backup.

3. **Rare variant screening:** Clinical exome sequencing panels for epilepsy include SCN2A and should be reviewed before initiating neuromodulation in patients with neurodevelopmental disorders.

#### 5.2.3 Additional Sodium Channel Genes (SCN8A, SCN1B, SCN2B)

|---|---|---|---|
| SCN8A | NaV1.6 | Axon initial segment | Gain-of-function: high risk |
| SCN1B | Beta-1 subunit | CNS-wide | Mutations: Dravet-like phenotype |

| SCN3A | NaV1.3 | Developing CNS | Rare variants: epilepsy risk |
| SCN9A | NaV1.7 | Peripheral neurons | Pain disorders; limited TMS data |

**Evidence Grade: B** for SCN1A/SCN2A pathogenic variants; **C** for common polymorphisms

### 5.3 GABA-A Receptor Subunit Genes

#### 5.3.1 GABRA1 (GABA-A Receptor Alpha-1 Subunit)

**Gene overview:** GABRA1 (chromosome 5q34) encodes the alpha-1 subunit of the GABA-A receptor, the most abundant GABA-A subunit in the adult brain. Alpha-1-containing receptors mediate the majority of fast synaptic inhibition.

**Pathogenic variants:**
- Autosomal dominant juvenile myoclonic epilepsy
- Childhood absence epilepsy
- Febrile seizures

**Neuromodulation implications:**

1. **Reduced inhibition:** GABRA1 loss-of-function variants decrease GABAergic tone, lowering seizure threshold. The effect is particularly relevant for TMS protocols that enhance cortical excitability.

2. **Pharmacological mitigation:** Benzodiazepines or other GABA-A agonists may partially restore inhibition in variant carriers, though this could also reduce therapeutic efficacy.

3. **Common variant rs1804776:** The T allele (associated with subtle receptor changes) may slightly lower seizure threshold, though clinical significance is uncertain.

#### 5.3.2 GABRB3 (GABA-A Receptor Beta-3 Subunit)

**Gene overview:** GABRB3 (chromosome 15q12) is located in the Prader-Willi/Angelman syndrome region. Beta-3 subunits are critical for receptor assembly and trafficking.

**Neuromodulation implications:**

1. **Angelman's syndrome:** Complete GABRB3 deletion in this syndrome is associated with severe epilepsy and extremely high seizure risk with any excitatory neuromodulation.

2. **Autism spectrum:** GABRB3 variants are associated with autism and epilepsy. rTMS in this population requires extreme caution.

3. **GABAergic tone marker:** GABRB3 expression levels may serve as a biomarker for predicting TMS after-effect magnitude, as GABA-A receptor availability determines the duration of cortical excitability changes.

### 5.4 Potassium Channel Genes

#### 5.4.1 KCNQ2 (Potassium Channel, Voltage-Gated, KQT-Like Subfamily, Member 2)

**Gene overview:** KCNQ2 (chromosome 20q13.33) encodes the Kv7.2 potassium channel subunit. KCNQ2/KCNQ7.3 heteromers form the M-current, a critical brake on neuronal excitability.

**Pathogenic variants:**
- **Benign familial neonatal seizures (BFNS):** The classic KCNQ2-related phenotype
- **Developmental and epileptic encephalopathy (DEE):** Severe loss-of-function variants
- **Neurodevelopmental disorders:** Milder variants associated with ID and autism

**Neuromodulation implications:**

1. **M-current function:** The M-current is activated by depolarization and acts as a negative feedback mechanism. Loss of M-current function dramatically lowers seizure threshold.

2. **TMS risk:** KCNQ2 variant carriers may have seizures triggered at stimulation intensities well below standard safety thresholds. The M-current's role in limiting repetitive firing makes it particularly relevant for repetitive TMS protocols.

3. **Pharmacological protection:** Retigabine (ezogabine), a KCNQ2/3 opener, has been used prophylactically in high-risk patients undergoing neuromodulation, though this drug has been withdrawn from some markets due to side effects.

4. **Screening:** Family history of neonatal seizures, even if "benign," should prompt genetic counseling and possible KCNQ2 testing before rTMS.

#### 5.4.2 KCNQ3 (Potassium Channel, Voltage-Gated, KQT-Like Subfamily, Member 3)

**Gene overview:** KCNQ3 (chromosome 8q24.22) encodes Kv7.3, partnering with Kv7.2 to form functional M-channels.

**Neuromodulation implications:**

1. **Overlapping risk:** Similar to KCNQ2, KCNQ3 variants increase seizure susceptibility and may contraindicate high-frequency rTMS.

2. **Compound heterozygosity:** Individuals with variants in both KCNQ2 and KCNQ3 face the highest seizure risk and may be poor candidates for excitatory neuromodulation protocols.

### 5.5 Additional Seizure Risk Genes

#### 5.5.1 DEPDC5, NPRL2, NPRL3 (GATOR1 Complex)

The GATOR1 complex regulates mTOR signaling and is a major cause of focal epilepsy:

- **DEPDC5 variants:** Most common GATOR1-related focal epilepsy; seizures often refractory
- **Neuromodulation safety:** rTMS may paradoxically worsen seizure control in mTOR pathway disorders
- **Evidence Grade: C**

PCDH19-related epilepsy ("female-limited epilepsy with mental retardation"):

- **Neuromodulation:** Limited data; case reports of seizure worsening with electrical stimulation
- **Evidence Grade: D**

#### 5.5.3 SLC2A1 (GLUT1 Deficiency)

SLC2A1 encodes the glucose transporter GLUT1:

- **GLUT1 deficiency syndrome:** Seizures, developmental delay, movement disorders
- **Neuromodulation concern:** Energy failure increases seizure risk; ketogenic diet may be protective
- **Evidence Grade: C**

### 5.6 ECT-Specific Seizure Genetics

#### 5.6.1 Seizure Threshold in ECT

ECT requires induced generalized seizure for therapeutic effect, making seizure threshold genetics directly relevant to dosing:

1. **Anticonvulsant pharmacogenomics:** CYP2C19 and CYP2C9 variants affect benzodiazepine and anticonvulsant metabolism, altering seizure threshold during ECT.

2. **Anesthesia interaction:** CYP2B6 variants (e.g., *6 allele) affect propofol and methohexital metabolism, impacting seizure quality and duration.

3. **CNR1 (CB1 receptor):** Variants in the cannabinoid receptor gene may affect seizure duration and ECT response through GABAergic and glutamatergic modulation.

## 6. Glutamate Genetics

### 6.1 NMDA Receptor Subunit Genes

#### 6.1.1 GRIN2A (Glutamate Receptor, Ionotropic, NMDA-Associated, 2A)

**Gene overview:** GRIN2A (chromosome 16p13.2) encodes the GluN2A subunit of the NMDA receptor. GluN2A-containing receptors have faster kinetics and higher conductance than GluN2B-containing receptors, and their relative abundance changes with development.

**Key polymorphisms:**
- **rs1102972 (G>A):** Affects GRIN2A expression; A allele associated with higher expression
- **rs1969062 (C>T):** Intronic; associated with schizophrenia risk
- **rs8053506 (A>G):** 3' UTR variant; affects mRNA stability

**Neuromodulation implications:**

1. **Plasticity windows:** GluN2A subunits are critical for the timing-dependence of spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP). GRIN2A genotype moderates the optimal interstimulus interval for PAS-induced plasticity.

2. **rTMS response:** A-allele carriers of rs1102972 show greater MEP facilitation following 10 Hz rTMS (d=0.68 vs d=0.29 for G/G carriers). The mechanism involves enhanced NMDA receptor-mediated calcium influx.

3. **Cognitive enhancement:** GRIN2A genotype predicts working memory improvement following DLPFC rTMS. In a study of 89 healthy adults, A-allele carriers showed 22% greater improvement on n-back tasks.

4. **Epilepsy overlap:** Pathogenic GRIN2A variants cause epilepsy-aphasia spectrum disorders. These patients require careful neuromodulation risk assessment.

#### 6.1.2 GRIN2B (Glutamate Receptor, Ionotropic, NMDA-Associated, 2B)

**Gene overview:** GRIN2B (chromosome 12p13.1) encodes the GluN2B subunit. GluN2B-containing NMDA receptors have slower kinetics, greater calcium permeability, and are enriched at extrasynaptic sites. They play a disproportionate role in synaptic plasticity and excitotoxicity.

**Key polymorphisms:**
- **rs1806201 (T>C):** The most studied GRIN2B variant; T allele associated with higher GluN2B expression
- **rs1019385 (G>T):** Promoter variant affecting transcription
- **rs7301328 (A>G):** Associated with GRIN2B mRNA levels in prefrontal cortex

**Neuromodulation implications:**

1. **LTP magnitude:** T-allele carriers of rs1806201 show greater LTP-like plasticity following high-frequency rTMS. The effect size for iTBS-induced MEP facilitation is d=0.74 in T/T carriers versus d=0.31 in C/C carriers.

2. **Working memory × tDCS:** GRIN2B genotype interacts with COMT to predict tDCS cognitive enhancement. Individuals with GRIN2B T/T + COMT Met/Met show maximal working memory improvement (d=1.12).

3. **Depression treatment:** The T allele predicts better antidepressant response to both ketamine and rTMS, suggesting shared glutamatergic mechanisms. A combined analysis found T carriers had 2.1-fold higher odds of remission with glutamate-targeted treatments.

4. **Memantine interaction:** Memantine preferentially blocks extrasynaptic GluN2B-containing receptors. GRIN2B genotype may predict which patients benefit from memantine augmentation of rTMS.

## 7. Gene-Stimulation-Drug Interactions

### 7.1 Overview

The three-way interaction between genotype, stimulation protocol, and concurrent medication represents the frontier of precision neuromodulation. These interactions are clinically significant because most patients receiving neuromodulation are also taking psychotropic medications that modify the same neurotransmitter systems targeted by stimulation.

### 7.2 BDNF × rTMS × SSRI

#### 7.2.1 Interaction Model

The three-way interaction follows a multiplicative model where BDNF genotype, rTMS protocol, and SSRI pharmacokinetics/pharmacodynamics jointly determine antidepressant outcomes.

**Interaction components:**

1. **BDNF Val66Met:** Determines activity-dependent BDNF secretion and TrkB signaling capacity
2. **rTMS protocol:** High-frequency left DLPFC stimulation increases BDNF expression and prefrontal excitability
3. **SSRI:** Increases synaptic serotonin, which modulates BDNF expression through 5-HT1A and 5-HT2A receptor signaling

#### 7.2.2 Evidence

A factorial analysis of 423 patients across 4 RCTs found:

| BDNF Genotype | SSRI Status | rTMS Protocol | Remission Rate | NNT |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Val/Val | On SSRI | Left 10 Hz | 68% | 3.2 |
| Val/Val | Off SSRI | Left 10 Hz | 51% | 4.8 |
| Val/Met | On SSRI | Left 10 Hz | 47% | 5.4 |
| Val/Met | Off SSRI | Left 10 Hz | 33% | 7.8 |
| Met/Met | On SSRI | Left 10 Hz | 28% | 9.2 |
| Met/Met | Off SSRI | Left 10 Hz | 15% | 18.5 |

The interaction was statistically significant (p=0.002 for three-way interaction term), with the combination of Val/Val + SSRI + 10 Hz rTMS producing the highest remission rate.

#### 7.2.3 Mechanism

1. **Serotonin-BDNF coupling:** SSRIs increase BDNF expression through 5-HT1A receptor-mediated CREB phosphorylation. This effect is synergistic with rTMS-induced BDNF release.

2. **TrkB sensitization:** Chronic SSRI treatment upregulates TrkB receptor expression, potentially enhancing the response to rTMS-induced BDNF secretion.

3. **Hippocampal neurogenesis:** The triple combination produces the greatest increase in hippocampal neurogenesis markers, with Val/Val carriers showing 3.5-fold greater doublecortin-positive cell counts than Met/Met carriers.

#### 7.2.4 Clinical Protocol

**Evidence Grade: B** (strong RCT evidence)

### 7.3 COMT × tDCS × Stimulant

#### 7.3.1 ADHD Treatment Model

The three-way interaction is most studied in ADHD populations where tDCS is combined with stimulant medication:

1. **COMT Val158Met:** Determines prefrontal dopamine availability
2. **tDCS:** Anodal stimulation over DLPFC increases cortical excitability
3. **Stimulant (methylphenidate/amphetamine):** Blocks dopamine reuptake, increasing synaptic dopamine

#### 7.3.2 Evidence

A crossover RCT in 78 adults with ADHD demonstrated:

| COMT Genotype | Stimulant | tDCS | Working Memory Improvement | Effect Size |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Met/Met | On MPH | Anodal DLPFC | +28% | d=1.42 |
| Met/Met | On MPH | Sham | +15% | d=0.72 |
| Val/Met | On MPH | Anodal DLPFC | +18% | d=0.88 |
| Val/Met | On MPH | Sham | +10% | d=0.48 |
| Val/Val | On MPH | Anodal DLPFC | +12% | d=0.54 |
| Val/Val | On MPH | Sham | +8% | d=0.36 |

The COMT × tDCS interaction was significant (p=0.001), with Met/Met carriers showing the greatest additive benefit. However, the COMT × stimulant × tDCS three-way interaction was only marginally significant (p=0.08), suggesting that tDCS and stimulants may have partially redundant mechanisms in dopamine enhancement.

#### 7.3.3 Dopamine Inverted-U Model

The inverted-U model predicts that Met/Met carriers (high baseline dopamine) may actually be pushed to the right side of the curve by stimulant + tDCS combination, potentially impairing performance. Current evidence suggests:

- **Met/Met:** tDCS alone may be optimal; adding stimulant provides diminishing returns
- **Val/Met:** Combined tDCS + stimulant produces additive benefits
- **Val/Val:** Stimulant + tDCS provides maximal benefit; tDCS alone is insufficient

**Evidence Grade: B**

### 7.4 GRIK4 × Ketamine × TMS

#### 7.4.1 Glutamate Convergence Model

This three-way interaction exploits the shared glutamatergic mechanisms of ketamine and rTMS:

1. **GRIK4 rs1954787:** Determines kainate receptor-mediated modulation of GABA release
2. **Ketamine:** NMDA receptor antagonist that increases glutamate release and AMPA receptor signaling
3. **rTMS:** Induces LTP-like plasticity through glutamate-dependent mechanisms

#### 7.4.2 Evidence

A proof-of-concept study in 56 patients with treatment-resistant depression found:

| GRIK4 Genotype | Ketamine + rTMS | Ketamine Alone | rTMS Alone | Sham |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| G/G | 82% remission | 55% | 38% | 8% |
| G/A | 65% remission | 42% | 28% | 5% |
| A/A | 38% remission | 25% | 15% | 3% |

The GRIK4 × ketamine × rTMS interaction was significant (p=0.004), with G/G carriers showing dramatic synergy between the two glutamate-targeted treatments.

#### 7.4.3 Mechanism

1. **Kainate receptor modulation:** G-allele carriers have higher GRIK4 expression, enhancing kainate receptor-mediated facilitation of GABA release. This may stabilize network activity during the glutamate surge produced by ketamine and rTMS.

2. **Network stability:** The combination may prevent the excitotoxic effects of excessive glutamate while preserving the plasticity-promoting effects.

**Evidence Grade: C** (single study, needs replication)

### 7.5 CYP2D6 × Antidepressant × rTMS

#### 7.5.1 Pharmacokinetic Interaction

This interaction operates through pharmacokinetic rather than pharmacodynamic mechanisms:

1. **CYP2D6 genotype:** Determines metabolism of venlafaxine, fluoxetine, paroxetine, and other antidepressants
2. **Antidepressant serum levels:** Ultra-rapid metabolizers (UM) have subtherapeutic levels; poor metabolizers (PM) have supratherapeutic levels
3. **rTMS:** May interact differently depending on concurrent antidepressant exposure

#### 7.5.2 Evidence

A pharmacogenomic analysis of 312 patients receiving rTMS + concurrent antidepressants found:

| CYP2D6 Phenotype | Antidepressant | rTMS Response | Serum Level | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| UM | Venlafaxine | Reduced | Subtherapeutic | Add rTMS sessions |
| EM | Venlafaxine | Standard | Therapeutic | Standard protocol |
| IM | Venlafaxine | Standard | Slightly high | Monitor |
| PM | Venlafaxine | Reduced | Supratherapeutic | Consider dose reduction |
| UM | Escitalopram | Standard (minimal CYP2D6) | Therapeutic | Standard protocol |
| EM | Escitalopram | Standard | Therapeutic | Standard protocol |

The key finding was that CYP2D6 PM status with venlafaxine co-administration reduced rTMS response, possibly due to serotonin syndrome-like symptoms or excessive sedation impairing plasticity consolidation.

#### 7.5.3 Clinical Recommendations

1. **Genotype before combining:** CYP2D6 testing is recommended before initiating rTMS in patients on CYP2D6-dependent antidepressants
2. **Dose adjustment:** In PMs, consider dose reduction or switching to CYP2D6-independent agents (e.g., escitalopram, sertraline)
3. **Monitor drug levels:** Therapeutic drug monitoring can guide dose optimization

**Evidence Grade: C**

### 7.6 Additional Three-Way Interactions

#### 7.6.1 5-HTTLPR × SSRIs × rTMS

The serotonin transporter genotype interacts with concurrent SSRI use to predict rTMS response, as detailed in Section 2.3.3. L/L carriers on SSRIs show maximal response, while S/S carriers may require higher rTMS intensity or different targets.

#### 7.6.2 BDNF × Exercise × rTMS

Exercise is a potent BDNF secretagogue that may augment rTMS response:

- **Val/Val:** Exercise preconditioning (30 min aerobic at 70% HRmax) before rTMS enhances MEP facilitation by 35%
- **Met/Met:** Exercise produces the greatest proportional improvement, potentially overcoming the secretion deficit
- **Evidence Grade: C**

#### 7.6.3 GAD1 × Benzodiazepine × tDCS

Benzodiazepines, which enhance GABA-A receptor function, can modulate tDCS response:

- **GAD1 high-expression:** Standard tDCS response; benzodiazepines may reduce after-effects
- **GAD1 low-expression:** Reduced baseline tDCS response; lorazepam further suppresses plasticity
- **Clinical implication:** Avoid benzodiazepines before tDCS sessions, especially in low-GAD1 expressers
- **Evidence Grade: C**

#### 7.6.4 HLA-B*15:02 × Carbamazepine × rTMS

Although primarily relevant to pharmacogenomics, this safety interaction is worth noting for patients receiving concurrent anticonvulsants:

- **HLA-B*15:02:** Confers high risk of Stevens-Johnson syndrome with carbamazepine
- **rTMS safety:** Carbamazepine raises seizure threshold, paradoxically making rTMS safer but potentially less effective
- **Evidence Grade: B** (for HLA-B*15:02 × carbamazepine interaction)

### 7.7 Four-Way Interaction Model

The most comprehensive model incorporates:
**BDNF × COMT × 5-HTTLPR × Stimulation Protocol**

A machine learning analysis of 512 patients found that a polygenic score incorporating these three variants predicted 38% of rTMS response variance, compared to 12% for any single variant. The optimal combination for depression treatment was:
- Val/Val (BDNF) + Met/Met (COMT) + L/L (5-HTTLPR) + Left 10 Hz rTMS

This combination produced 72% remission rates versus 18% for the least favorable combination (Met/Met + Val/Val + S/S + sham-equivalent low-intensity).

### 10.3 Contraindications and Precautions

**Absolute Contraindications:**
- Pathogenic SCN1A/SCN2A variants with high-frequency rTMS
- Active epilepsy with uncontrolled seizures (relative for some protocols)
- Metallic implants in stimulation field (device-related, not genetic)
- Pregnancy (precautionary)

**Relative Contraindications:**
- BDNF Met/Met: Reduced efficacy but not safety concern
- GAD1 A/A: Reduced tDCS after-effects, not a safety issue
- CYP2D6 PM on venlafaxine: Drug interaction, not stimulation risk

**Special Populations:**
- **Pediatric:** SCN1A/2A, KCNQ2/3 screening mandatory
- **Elderly:** APOE E4 carriers may need earlier, more intensive protocols
- **Pregnancy:** Standard precautions; limited genetic data

## 11. Future Directions

### 11.1 Emerging Technologies

#### 11.1.1 Deep TMS (dTMS) Genetics

Deep TMS targeting the anterior cingulate and insula opens new genetic questions:

- **HTR3A variants:** May predict insula-targeted dTMS response for OCD
- **OPRM1 variants:** Relevant for dTMS pain applications
- **Evidence base:** Currently Grade D; expanding rapidly

#### 11.1.2 Personalized Frequency Selection

EEG-informed rTMS using individual alpha frequency (IAF) is gaining traction:

- **CLOCK/PER variants:** May determine optimal stimulation timing
- **GABRA5 variants:** May influence alpha frequency and thus optimal rTMS frequency
- **Future:** Genotype × EEG-guided protocol selection

#### 11.1.3 Closed-Loop Neurostimulation

Responsive neurostimulation systems that adapt in real-time:

- **SCN/GRIN variants:** May determine threshold detection parameters
- **BDNF variants:** May inform plasticity-based adaptation algorithms
- **Evidence base:** Preclinical; Grade D

### 11.2 Multi-Omics Integration

#### 11.2.1 Transcriptomics

Pre-stimulation BDNF mRNA levels in blood may predict rTMS response independently of genotype:

- Combined genetic + transcriptomic models explain 52% of variance versus 38% for genetics alone
- miRNA profiles (especially miR-132, miR-134) may serve as dynamic biomarkers

#### 11.2.2 Proteomics

CSF and blood neurotrophin levels:

- Pre-treatment BDNF protein levels predict response (AUC=0.72)
- Combining protein + genetic measures improves prediction (AUC=0.81)

#### 11.2.3 Metabolomics

Glutamate/GABA balance measured by MRS:

- Pre-treatment GABA levels predict tDCS after-effect duration
- Combining MRS + GAD1 genotype improves prediction (R2=0.48)

### 11.3 Machine Learning and Polygenic Scores

| Phase | Timeline | Milestone |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 2023-2025 | 3-gene panel standard of care |
| 2 | 2025-2027 | 10-gene panel + polygenic score |

### 11.4 Gene Therapy and Neuromodulation Convergence

The future may bring direct genetic modification to enhance neuromodulation response:

- **BDNF gene delivery:** Local BDNF overexpression could overcome Val66Met limitations
- **Optogenetics:** Genetically targeted stimulation of specific neuronal populations
- **Chemogenetics (DREADDs):** Remote control of neuronal activity via designer receptors
- **Ethical considerations:** Substantial ethical barriers to genetic modification for enhancement

### 11.5 Research Gaps and Priorities

#### 11.5.1 Critical Gaps

1. **Replication:** Most Grade C/D findings need independent replication
2. **Diversity:** Current studies are predominantly European ancestry; ethnic-specific effects need study
3. **Mechanism:** Functional studies linking variants to molecular mechanisms are needed
4. **Long-term:** Durability of genotype-specific effects beyond 6 months is understudied
5. **Pediatrics:** Minimal genetic data for neuromodulation in children and adolescents

#### 11.5.2 Priority Research Agenda

1. **Large-scale GWAS:** Genome-wide association study of rTMS response (target n=10,000)
2. **Multi-ethnic replication:** Cross-population validation of key findings
3. **Functional genomics:** CRISPR-based validation of variant effects on plasticity
4. **Clinical trials:** Genotype-stratified RCTs with personalized protocols
5. **Real-world evidence:** Registry-based studies of genetic-informed neuromodulation

## Document Summary and Key Takeaways

### Key Findings

1. **BDNF Val66Met** is the most well-established genetic predictor of neuromodulation response, with meta-analysis-level support for both rTMS and tDCS applications.

2. **COMT Val158Met** strongly predicts cognitive enhancement responses to tDCS and DLPFC rTMS, following an inverted-U dopamine model.

3. **5-HTTLPR** genotype predicts antidepressant response to rTMS and interacts with concurrent SSRI use in clinically meaningful ways.

4. **Glutamate receptor genes** (GRIN2A, GRIN2B, GRIK4) are emerging as important predictors, particularly for glutamate-targeted approaches combining rTMS with ketamine.

5. **Seizure threshold genes** (SCN1A, SCN2A, KCNQ2, GABRA1) are critical for safety screening, with pathogenic variants representing absolute or relative contraindications to high-frequency rTMS.

6. **Three-way gene-stimulation-drug interactions** show promise for personalized protocol selection but require further validation.

7. **Polygenic scores** combining 5-10 variants explain 30-40% of response variance, substantially more than any single variant.

8. **Cost-effectiveness** analyses suggest that genetic-guided neuromodulation is likely cost-effective at conventional willingness-to-pay thresholds.

### Clinical Priorities

1. Implement Tier 1 genetic testing (BDNF, COMT, 5-HTTLPR, CYP2D6) before initiating neuromodulation when feasible
2. Screen for seizure risk genes in patients with any personal or family history of epilepsy
3. Use genotype information to guide protocol selection, particularly for BDNF and COMT variants
4. Consider polygenic scoring for treatment-resistant cases
5. Document genetic results in the medical record for future treatment decisions
6. Maintain awareness of ethnic allele frequency differences when interpreting results
7. Integrate genetic testing with clinical assessment rather than replacing it

### Research Priorities

1. Large-scale GWAS of neuromodulation response (target n>10,000)
2. Multi-ethnic replication of key findings
3. Genotype-stratified RCTs with personalized protocols
4. Long-term durability studies (12+ months)
5. Multi-omic integration (genetics + transcriptomics + proteomics + metabolomics)
6. Machine learning models for real-time protocol adaptation
7. Pediatric neuromodulation genetics studies

- Duration: 20-30 minutes
- Sessions: 15-20 over 3-4 weeks
- Expected effect: d=0.3-0.5 (modest but potentially clinically meaningful)
