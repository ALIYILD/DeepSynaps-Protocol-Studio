# 1. DRUG-NUTRIENT DEPLETIONS

## 1.1 Statins --> CoQ10 Depletion

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Drug Class** | HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors (atorvastatin, simvastatin, rosuvastatin, pravastatin, lovastatin) |
| **Nutrient Depleted** | Coenzyme Q10 (ubiquinone) |
| **Mechanism** | Shared biosynthetic pathway: HMG-CoA reductase inhibition blocks both cholesterol and CoQ10 synthesis. CoQ10 is synthesized from mevalonate, the same pathway blocked by statins |
| **Magnitude** | 40-54% reduction in serum CoQ10 levels with chronic use |
| **Clinical Significance** | HIGH -- CoQ10 is critical for mitochondrial ATP production and muscle cell function |

### Clinical Manifestations
- Myalgias and myopathies (exacerbated by CoQ10 depletion)
- Exercise intolerance and fatigue
- Rhabdomyolysis (rare but severe)
- Potential cardiac effects (CoQ10 critical for cardiac energy metabolism)

### Monitoring Recommendations
| Action | Frequency |
|--------|-----------|
| Baseline CK level | Before statin initiation |
| CoQ10 level | Optional baseline (not routinely measured) |
| Clinical assessment for myalgias | Every visit |
| CK level | If muscle symptoms develop |

### Repletion Strategy
- **CoQ10 supplementation:** 100-200 mg/day (ubiquinol form preferred for absorption)
- **Timing:** Take CoQ10 with fatty meal for improved absorption
- **Evidence:** Supplementation can partially restore CoQ10 levels and may reduce myalgia severity
- **Interaction note:** CoQ10 does NOT interfere with statin cholesterol-lowering efficacy

### Psychiatric Considerations
- Many psychiatric patients receive statins for metabolic syndrome management
- Fatigue from CoQ10 depletion may be misattributed to depression or medication side effects
- Consider CoQ10 supplementation in patients on concurrent antipsychotics (which also increase metabolic risk) + statins

## 1.2 Metformin --> Vitamin B12 Depletion

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Drug Class** | Biguanide (oral hypoglycemic) |
| **Nutrient Depleted** | Vitamin B12 (cobalamin) |
| **Mechanism** | Multiple: (1) Altered intestinal motility affecting B12-intrinsic factor complex absorption; (2) Calcium-dependent ileal membrane disruption; (3) Bacterial overgrowth competing for B12; (4) Reduced intrinsic factor secretion |
| **Magnitude** | 10-30% of long-term users develop B12 deficiency; 5-10% develop clinically significant deficiency |
| **Clinical Significance** | HIGH -- B12 deficiency causes neuropsychiatric symptoms that can mimic or worsen primary psychiatric disorders |

### Clinical Manifestations
- **Hematologic:** Macrocytic anemia, megaloblastosis, pancytopenia
- **Neurologic:** Peripheral neuropathy, paresthesias, ataxia, posterior column dysfunction
- **Neuropsychiatric:** Cognitive impairment, depression, irritability, psychosis, dementia-like symptoms
- **Cardiovascular:** Elevated homocysteine (atherosclerosis risk)

### Monitoring Recommendations
| Action | Frequency |
|--------|-----------|
| Serum B12 | Baseline, then annually |
| MMA (methylmalonic acid) | If B12 borderline (200-400 pg/mL) |
| Homocysteine | If MMA unavailable |
| CBC with MCV | Annually |

### Repletion Strategy
- **Vitamin B12:** 1000 mcg/day oral OR 1000 mcg IM monthly if malabsorption present
- **Calcium supplementation:** 1200 mg/day elemental calcium may improve B12 absorption
- **Note:** Metformin-associated B12 deficiency responds to oral supplementation in most cases

### Psychiatric Considerations
- B12 deficiency can cause depression, cognitive decline, and psychotic symptoms -- critical differential diagnosis
- Mental status changes may be misattributed to psychiatric illness rather than medication-induced deficiency
- Metformin is increasingly used in psychiatry for antipsychotic-induced weight gain

## 1.3 Anticonvulsants --> Vitamin D, Folate, B12 Depletion

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Drugs Affected** | Carbamazepine, valproate, phenytoin, phenobarbital, primidone, oxcarbazepine, topiramate |
| **Nutrients Depleted** | Vitamin D, folate, vitamin B12, calcium, phosphorus, biotin |
| **Mechanism** | Mixed: hepatic enzyme induction (CYP450) accelerates vitamin D catabolism; interference with intestinal absorption; altered bone metabolism |
| **Clinical Significance** | VERY HIGH -- Anticonvulsants are widely used in psychiatry (bipolar disorder, mood stabilization, impulsivity); bone disease is a major complication |

### Nutrient-Specific Effects

| Nutrient | Mechanism | Clinical Risk |
|----------|-----------|---------------|
| **Vitamin D** | CYP induction increases 25(OH)D catabolism; reduced calcium absorption | Osteoporosis, osteomalacia, fractures |
| **Folate** | Interference with folate absorption and metabolism; enzyme induction | Megaloblastic anemia (especially valproate), neural tube defects in pregnancy, elevated homocysteine |
| **Vitamin B12** | Reduced absorption | Neuropathy, anemia, cognitive effects |
| **Calcium/Phosphorus** | Secondary to vitamin D depletion | Bone demineralization |
| **Biotin** | Competitive inhibition of biotin absorption | Hair loss, skin rash, neurological symptoms |

### Monitoring Recommendations (for chronic users)
| Action | Frequency |
|--------|-----------|
| 25(OH) vitamin D | Baseline, then annually |
| Serum calcium | Baseline, annually |
| Alkaline phosphatase | Baseline, annually |
| Bone density (DEXA) | Baseline if risk factors, then every 1-2 years |
| Serum folate | Baseline, annually |
| B12 level | Baseline, annually |
| CBC | Baseline, every 6 months |

### Repletion Strategy
- **Vitamin D:** 2000-4000 IU/day (higher doses may be needed; target 25(OH)D >30 ng/mL)
- **Folate:** 1-5 mg/day (especially important for women of childbearing age on valproate)
- **Calcium:** 1200 mg/day elemental calcium + vitamin D
- **Weight-bearing exercise:** Encourage as tolerated

### Psychiatric-Specific Notes
- Carbamazepine and valproate are first-line mood stabilizers in bipolar disorder
- Valproate specifically causes folate depletion and is HIGHLY teratogenic -- folate supplementation (4-5 mg/day) is mandatory in pregnancy
- Phenytoin and phenobarbital less commonly used in psychiatry but still relevant

## 1.4 Lithium --> Sodium, Iodine/Thyroid Interactions

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Drug Class** | Mood stabilizer (first-line for bipolar disorder) |
| **Nutrients Affected** | Sodium, iodine, thyroid hormones |
| **Mechanism** | Complex: competes with sodium for transport; inhibits thyroid hormone release; blocks iodine uptake and organification |
| **Clinical Significance** | VERY HIGH -- Lithium is a cornerstone psychiatric medication; sodium and thyroid dysregulation are major clinical concerns |

### Mechanism of Interactions

**Sodium Interaction:**
- Lithium is a monovalent cation that competes with sodium at nephron sites
- Sodium depletion (from diuretics, vomiting, diarrhea, low-salt diets) enhances lithium reabsorption --> lithium toxicity
- Sodium excess reduces lithium reabsorption --> subtherapeutic levels

**Iodine/Thyroid Interaction:**
- Lithium inhibits thyroglobulin proteolysis, blocking T3/T4 release
- Reduces peripheral conversion of T4 to T3
- Competes with iodine for thyroid uptake via sodium-iodide symporter
- May induce autoimmune thyroiditis
- Increases thyroid iodine retention
- Can cause goiter formation

### Clinical Manifestations
- **Lithium toxicity:** Tremor, ataxia, confusion, seizures, renal failure (aggravated by sodium depletion)
- **Hypothyroidism:** Fatigue, weight gain, depression, cognitive slowing (occurs in 10-20% of long-term users)
- **Hyperthyroidism:** Less common; may present as thyrotoxicosis
- **Goiter:** Enlarged thyroid gland

### Monitoring Recommendations
| Action | Frequency |
|--------|-----------|
| Serum lithium level | Weekly initially, then every 3-6 months (12h trough: 0.6-1.0 mEq/L maintenance) |
| Serum sodium | Baseline, every 3-6 months |
| TSH | Baseline, every 3-6 months |
| Free T4 | If TSH abnormal |
| Creatinine/eGFR | Baseline, every 3 months |
| Urinalysis | Baseline, annually |
| Pregnancy test | Before initiation, ongoing if applicable |

### Dietary Recommendations
- **Maintain consistent sodium intake** -- do NOT restrict or dramatically increase salt
- **Hydration:** Maintain adequate fluid intake (1.5-2 L/day minimum)
- **Avoid low-salt diets** while on lithium (unless medically necessary -- requires close monitoring)
- **Iodine:** Ensure adequate but not excessive iodine intake

### Drug Interaction Alerts
| Interacting Agent | Effect |
|-------------------|--------|
| Thiazide diuretics | Increase lithium levels 25-40% -- TOXICITY RISK |
| ACE inhibitors | Increase lithium levels -- TOXICITY RISK |
| NSAIDs | Increase lithium levels (except aspirin) -- TOXICITY RISK |
| Loop diuretics | Minimal effect on lithium |
| Osmotic diuretics | Decrease lithium levels |

## 1.5 Diuretics --> Potassium, Magnesium Depletion

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Drug Class** | Loop diuretics (furosemide, bumetanide), thiazides (HCTZ, chlorthalidone) |
| **Nutrients Depleted** | Potassium, magnesium, sodium, calcium (loops), thiamin |
| **Mechanism** | Increased renal excretion via inhibition of Na+/K+/2Cl- cotransport (loops) or Na+/Cl- cotransport (thiazides); increased flow rate reduces mineral reabsorption |
| **Clinical Significance** | HIGH -- Hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia are common and clinically significant; psychiatric patients may be at higher risk |

### Nutrient-Specific Effects

| Nutrient | Loop Diuretics | Thiazide Diuretics |
|----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Potassium** | Major depletion (+++) | Moderate depletion (++) |
| **Magnesium** | Major depletion (+++) | Moderate depletion (++) |
| **Sodium** | Variable | Variable |
| **Calcium** | Increased excretion | DECREASED excretion |
| **Thiamin (B1)** | Increased urinary loss | Minimal effect |
| **Zinc** | Increased urinary loss | Increased tissue depletion |

### Clinical Manifestations
- **Hypokalemia:** Arrhythmias, muscle weakness, fatigue, constipation, ECG changes (U waves, flattened T waves)
- **Hypomagnesemia:** Tremor, muscle cramps, tetany, arrhythmias, seizures, aggravates hypokalemia (magnesium required for K+ retention)
- **Hyponatremia:** Confusion, seizures, falls (especially in elderly)
- **Thiamin deficiency:** Wernicke encephalopathy, cognitive impairment (especially in alcohol use disorder)

### Monitoring Recommendations
| Action | Frequency |
|--------|-----------|
| Electrolytes (Na, K, Cl, CO2) | Baseline, 1-2 weeks after initiation, then every 3-6 months |
| Magnesium | Baseline, 1-2 weeks after initiation, then every 6-12 months |
| ECG | Baseline, if electrolyte abnormalities, cardiac symptoms |
| Thiamin level | If risk factors (alcohol use, malnutrition) |

### Repletion Strategy
- **Potassium:** Dietary (bananas, citrus, leafy greens) OR KCl 20-40 mEq/day if K+ <3.5 mEq/L
- **Magnesium:** Magnesium oxide 400 mg/day OR magnesium glycinate (better tolerated) if Mg <1.8 mg/dL
- **Thiamin:** 100 mg/day if deficiency suspected
- **Potassium-sparing agents:** Consider spironolactone or amiloride if chronic hypokalemia

### Psychiatric Considerations
- Many psychiatric patients receive diuretics for metabolic syndrome-associated hypertension
- Lithium + diuretic interaction is especially dangerous (see Section 1.4)
- Hypomagnesemia can cause agitation, tremor, and seizures -- may be misattributed to psychiatric illness
- Thiamin deficiency can cause Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome in patients with alcohol use disorder

## 1.6 PPIs --> B12, Magnesium, Calcium Depletion

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Drug Class** | Proton pump inhibitors (omeprazole, esomeprazole, pantoprazole, lansoprazole, rabeprazole) |
| **Nutrients Depleted** | Vitamin B12, magnesium, calcium, iron, zinc, beta-carotene |
| **Mechanism** | Acid suppression impairs nutrient absorption that requires gastric acid for solubilization and ionization |
| **Clinical Significance** | MODERATE-HIGH -- PPIs are widely used; long-term use (especially >1 year) carries significant nutritional risk |

### Nutrient-Specific Effects

| Nutrient | Mechanism | Risk Timeline |
|----------|-----------|---------------|
| **Vitamin B12** | Acid needed to release B12 from food protein; intrinsic factor function impaired | Months to years |
| **Magnesium** | Reduced intestinal absorption via transient receptor potential channels | Weeks to months |
| **Calcium** | Reduced absorption of calcium carbonate; may increase fracture risk | Months to years |
| **Iron** | Ferric iron requires acid for reduction to ferrous form | Months |
| **Zinc** | Reduced absorption | Months |

### Clinical Manifestations
- **B12 deficiency:** Anemia, neuropathy, cognitive impairment, depression
- **Hypomagnesemia:** Tremor, muscle cramps, arrhythmias, seizures
- **Calcium deficiency:** Bone loss, fractures (especially elderly)
- **Iron deficiency:** Anemia, fatigue, cognitive impairment

### Monitoring Recommendations
| Action | Frequency |
|--------|-----------|
| Serum B12 | Annually with chronic use |
| Magnesium | Baseline, 3 months, then every 6-12 months |
| Calcium | Annually |
| Consider DEXA | If >1 year use with other risk factors |

### Repletion Strategy
- **B12:** 1000 mcg/day oral (cyanocobalamin or methylcobalamin)
- **Magnesium:** 400 mg/day if deficient (magnesium glycinate preferred)
- **Calcium:** Calcium citrate (does not require acid; preferred over calcium carbonate)
- **Iron:** If iron deficiency confirmed

### Psychiatric Considerations
- PPIs are frequently co-prescribed for GI side effects of psychiatric medications (NSAIDs, SSRIs)
- Psychiatric patients have higher rates of GI complaints and GERD
- B12 deficiency can worsen depression and cognitive symptoms
- Hypomagnesemia can cause tremor and anxiety-like symptoms

## 1.7 Antipsychotics --> Weight Gain, Metabolic Syndrome

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Drug Class** | First-generation (FGAs) and second-generation (SGAs) antipsychotics |
| **Metabolic Effect** | Weight gain, dyslipidemia, insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes |
| **Risk by Agent** | Varies significantly by specific antipsychotic (see table below) |
| **Clinical Significance** | VERY HIGH -- Leading cause of morbidity and mortality in patients with serious mental illness; 10-20 year reduced life expectancy |

### Metabolic Risk by Antipsychotic

| Risk Category | Agents | Weight Gain | Diabetes | Dyslipidemia |
|--------------|--------|-------------|----------|--------------|
| **HIGHEST** | Clozapine, Olanzapine | ++++ | ++++ | ++++ |
| **HIGH** | Quetiapine, Risperidone, Paliperidone, Chlorpromazine | +++ | ++ | ++ |
| **MODERATE** | Asenapine, Iloperidone, Lurasidone | ++ | + | + |
| **LOW** | Aripiprazole, Ziprasidone, Haloperidol, Loxapine | + to ++ | + | + |
| **LOWEST** | Brexpiprazole, Cariprazine | + | + | + |

### Mechanism of Metabolic Effects
1. **H1 antagonism** --> increased appetite, weight gain
2. **5-HT2C antagonism** --> increased food intake, reduced satiety
3. **M3 antagonism** --> impaired insulin secretion
4. **D2 antagonism in hypothalamus** --> metabolic dysregulation
5. **Peripheral effects** --> direct effects on adipose tissue and liver

### Nutritional Impact
- Excessive weight gain (10-30 kg in first year with clozapine/olanzapine)
- Central obesity (metabolic syndrome component)
- Insulin resistance progressing to type 2 diabetes
- Dyslipidemia (elevated triglycerides, LDL; reduced HDL)
- Increased cardiovascular disease risk

### Monitoring Recommendations (ADA/APA Consensus Guidelines)

| Parameter | Baseline | 4 Weeks | 8 Weeks | 12 Weeks | Quarterly | Annually |
|-----------|----------|---------|---------|----------|-----------|----------|
| Weight/BMI | X | X | X | X | X | X |
| Waist circumference | X | | | | | X |
| Blood pressure | X | | | X | X | X |
| Fasting glucose or HbA1c | X | | | X | X | X |
| Fasting lipid panel | X | | | X | | X |

### Management Strategies

**Lifestyle Interventions (First Line):**
- Structured diet modification (Mediterranean, DASH, or low-carbohydrate)
- Exercise program (150 min/week moderate aerobic)
- Behavioral weight management
- Sleep hygiene optimization

**Pharmacologic Interventions:**
- **Metformin:** 500-2000 mg/day (best evidence; modest 3-5 kg weight loss)
- **Aripiprazole augmentation:** Switching from or adding to clozapine/olanzapine (modest benefit)
- **Topiramate:** 50-200 mg/day (weight loss but cognitive side effects)
- **GLP-1 agonists:** Emerging evidence for significant weight loss in psychiatric patients

### Psychiatric Considerations
- Metabolic monitoring compliance is poor in psychiatric populations (only 20-80% receive recommended monitoring)
- Weight gain is a major cause of medication non-adherence
- Cardiovascular disease is the #1 cause of death in patients with serious mental illness
- Metformin also depletes B12 (see Section 1.2) -- monitor if used for metabolic management

# 2. NUTRIENT EFFECTS ON DRUG METABOLISM

## 2.1 Grapefruit Juice / CYP3A4 Inhibition

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Source** | Grapefruit juice (Citrus paradisi), Seville orange juice, pomelos |
| **Active Compounds** | Furanocoumarins (bergamottin, 6',7'-dihydroxybergamottin), naringin, flavonoids |
| **Mechanism** | Irreversible inhibition of intestinal CYP3A4 (first-pass metabolism); inhibition of P-glycoprotein |
| **Duration** | Effect persists 24-72 hours after last consumption |
| **Clinical Significance** | VERY HIGH -- Can dramatically increase drug exposure |

### Affected Psychiatric Medications (CYP3A4 substrates)

| Drug | Effect of Grapefruit | Clinical Impact |
|------|---------------------|-----------------|
| **Buspirone** | 10-fold increase in AUC | Sedation, dizziness, serotonin syndrome risk |
| **Quetiapine** | Significant increase in levels | Excessive sedation, orthostatic hypotension |
| **Ziprasidone** | Variable (minor CYP3A4 contribution) | Lower risk but caution advised |
| **Alprazolam** | 1.5-fold increase in levels | Excessive sedation, respiratory depression |
| **Midazolam / Triazolam** | Marked increase in levels | Profound sedation, amnesia |
| **Diazepam** | Moderate increase | Excessive sedation |
| **Carbamazepine** | Minor effect (auto-inducer) | Less clinically relevant |
| **Trazodone** | Significant increase | Sedation, orthostasis |

### Patient Counseling
- **Avoid completely** while taking CYP3A4 substrates
- Small amounts (e.g., 200 mL) can produce significant effects
- Effect is NOT prevented by spacing timing -- CYP3A4 inhibition lasts 24-72 hours
- Orange juice (regular) does NOT have this effect (different furanocoumarin content)

## 2.2 St. John's Wort / CYP Induction

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Source** | Hypericum perforatum (herbal supplement for depression) |
| **Active Compounds** | Hyperforin (main), hypericin, pseudohypericin |
| **Mechanism** | Potent inducer of CYP3A4, CYP2C19, CYP2C9; induces P-glycoprotein; also has mild MAOI activity |
| **Onset** | Induction begins within 2-3 days; maximal effect 1-2 weeks |
| **Offset** | Effect persists 1-2 weeks after discontinuation |
| **Clinical Significance** | VERY HIGH -- Can cause therapeutic failure of many psychiatric medications |

### Affected Psychiatric Medications

| Drug Class | Effect | Clinical Impact |
|------------|--------|-----------------|
| **SSRIs (sertraline, citalopram, escitalopram)** | Reduced levels (CYP3A4, CYP2C19) | Therapeutic failure, relapse of depression |
| **Tricyclic antidepressants** | Reduced levels (CYP3A4, CYP2C19) | Therapeutic failure |
| **Antipsychotics (aripiprazole, quetiapine, risperidone)** | Reduced levels (CYP3A4) | Psychotic relapse, hospitalization |
| **Benzodiazepines (alprazolam, midazolam, diazepam)** | Reduced levels | Anxiety breakthrough, seizure risk |
| **Lamotrigine** | Reduced levels (UGT induction) | Seizure breakthrough, mood destabilization |
| **Anticonvulsants** | Complex interactions | Altered levels |

### Drug-Specific Interaction Table

| Psychiatric Medication | Pathway | Effect of SJW | Clinical Action |
|----------------------|---------|--------------|-----------------|
| Sertraline | CYP3A4 | Decreased AUC by ~50% | AVOID combination |
| Citalopram | CYP3A4, CYP2C19 | Decreased levels | AVOID combination |
| Aripiprazole | CYP3A4, CYP2D6 | Decreased AUC by 75% | AVOID combination |
| Quetiapine | CYP3A4 | Decreased levels by >80% | CONTRAINDICATED |
| Lamotrigine | UGT | Decreased levels by ~25% | Avoid or monitor levels |
| Methadone | CYP3A4 | Decreased levels | Opioid withdrawal risk |
| Alprazolam | CYP3A4 | Decreased AUC by 50% | AVOID combination |

### Additional Considerations
- **Serotonin syndrome risk:** SJW has mild SSRI activity; combining with SSRIs/SNRIs increases serotonin syndrome risk
- **Photosensitivity:** Hypericin causes phototoxic reactions
- **Pregnancy:** SJW is NOT safe in pregnancy
- **Quality control:** Supplement content varies widely (not FDA-regulated)

## 2.3 Vitamin K / Warfarin Interaction

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Drug** | Warfarin (vitamin K antagonist anticoagulant) |
| **Nutrient** | Vitamin K (phylloquinone K1, menaquinone K2) |
| **Mechanism** | Warfarin inhibits vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKORC1), preventing activation of clotting factors II, VII, IX, X. Dietary vitamin K antagonizes this effect |
| **Clinical Significance** | HIGH -- INR instability is common; consistency of vitamin K intake is key |

### Key Principles
- **Consistency is critical:** Maintain consistent daily vitamin K intake (approximately 90 mcg/day for women, 120 mcg/day for men)
- **High vitamin K foods:** Leafy greens (spinach, kale, collards), broccoli, Brussels sprouts, green tea
- **Low vitamin K foods:** Most fruits, grains, meats, dairy

### Psychiatric Relevance

| Scenario | Consideration |
|----------|--------------|
| Psychiatric patients on warfarin for PE/DVT/AFib | May have poor dietary consistency; monitor INR closely |
| Antipsychotic-induced metabolic syndrome | May change diet patterns affecting vitamin K intake |
| Alcohol use disorder | Alcohol affects warfarin metabolism; malnutrition alters vitamin K |
| Disorganized eating | Variable vitamin K intake causes INR fluctuations |

### Drug-Drug Interactions (Psychiatric Medications + Warfarin)

| Psychiatric Drug | Effect on Warfarin | Mechanism |
|-----------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Fluoxetine | INCREASES INR | CYP2C9 inhibition |
| Fluvoxamine | INCREASES INR | CYP1A2, CYP2C9, CYP3A4 inhibition |
| Sertraline | Mild increase in INR | Weak CYP2C9 inhibition |
| Carbamazepine | DECREASES INR | CYP induction |
| Phenytoin | DECREASES INR | CYP induction |
| Quetiapine | Variable | CYP3A4 involvement |
| Chlorpromazine | May increase INR | Unknown |

### Monitoring Recommendations
- INR: Weekly until stable, then every 2-4 weeks
- More frequent monitoring when diet changes, psychiatric medications change, or clinical status changes
- Pharmacogenomic testing (CYP2C9, VKORC1) for patients with difficult-to-manage INR

## 2.4 Omega-3 Fatty Acids / Anticoagulant Interactions

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Source** | Fish oil, EPA/DHA supplements, prescription icosapent ethyl (Vascepa), omega-3 acid ethyl esters (Lovaza) |
| **Mechanism** | EPA displaces arachidonic acid in platelet membranes, reducing thromboxane A2 formation; inhibits platelet aggregation; at high doses (>3 g/day), prolongs bleeding time |
| **Clinical Significance** | MODERATE -- Additive antiplatelet effect with anticoagulants |

### Interaction Summary

| Anticoagulant/Antiplatelet | Omega-3 Effect | Clinical Risk |
|---------------------------|----------------|---------------|
| **Warfarin** | May potentiate effect at >3 g/day | INR elevation; bleeding |
| **Aspirin** | Additive antiplatelet effect | Increased bleeding risk at >3 g/day |
| **Clopidogrel** | Additive antiplatelet effect | Minor increase in bleeding at >3 g/day |
| **Apixaban (Eliquis)** | No significant pharmacokinetic interaction | Low risk at <=3 g/day |
| **Rivaroxaban (Xarelto)** | No significant pharmacokinetic interaction | Low risk at <=3 g/day |
| **Dabigatran (Pradaxa)** | No significant pharmacokinetic interaction | Low risk at <=3 g/day |

### Evidence Summary
- Doses <=3 g/day EPA+DHA: "No significant risk for increased bleeding time beyond the normal range" (Albert et al., 2002, NEJM)
- Doses 4 g/day (REDUCE-IT trial): Minor bleeding 2.7% vs. 2.1% placebo (p=0.06); major bleeding not significantly increased
- Doses >3 g/day: May double bleeding time in patients on anticoagulants
- **ISMP Recommendation:** Omega-3 at doses <=3 g/day appears safe with DOACs; higher doses require monitoring

### Psychiatric Considerations
- Omega-3s (especially EPA) have evidence for adjunctive treatment in depression and bipolar disorder
- Many psychiatric patients are on anticoagulants (high rates of cardiovascular disease)
- Recommendation: Use <=3 g/day EPA+DHA when combined with anticoagulants; monitor for bleeding signs
- Discontinue 1 week before elective surgery

## 2.5 Caffeine / Medication Interactions

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Source** | Coffee, tea, energy drinks, caffeine supplements, chocolate |
| **Mechanism** | Caffeine is metabolized primarily by CYP1A2; inhibits CYP1A2 activity at high doses; competes for metabolism with other CYP1A2 substrates |
| **Clinical Significance** | MODERATE-HIGH -- Significant interaction with clozapine; other interactions worth noting |

### Critical Interaction: Caffeine + Clozapine

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Mechanism** | Caffeine is a CYP1A2 inhibitor; clozapine is primarily metabolized by CYP1A2 |
| **Effect** | Caffeine increases clozapine plasma levels |
| **Magnitude** | 600 mg/day caffeine increased clozapine level to 1796 ng/mL (case report of severe toxicity with multiorgan failure) |
| **Clinical Action** | Counsel patients to maintain consistent caffeine intake; monitor for toxicity if intake changes dramatically |

### Clozapine Toxicity Signs
- Severe sedation, confusion, delirium
- Sialorrhea (excessive drooling)
- Tachycardia, hypotension
- Hyperthermia
- Seizures
- Myocarditis
- Agranulocytosis (additive risk)

### Other Psychiatric Medication Interactions with Caffeine

| Psychiatric Drug | Interaction | Mechanism | Clinical Action |
|-----------------|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Lithium** | Caffeine may reduce lithium levels | Caffeine is a diuretic, increases lithium excretion | Maintain consistent caffeine intake |
| **Fluvoxamine** | Markedly increased caffeine half-life | CYP1A2 inhibition | Limit caffeine; may cause jitteriness, insomnia |
| **Olanzapine** | Caffeine may reduce olanzapine levels | CYP1A2 induction (smoking-like effect) | Clinical significance modest |
| **Clozapine** | Caffeine increases clozapine levels | CYP1A2 inhibition | MAINTAIN CONSISTENT INTAKE; monitor for toxicity |
| **Benzodiazepines** | Caffeine antagonizes sedative effect | Adenosine receptor antagonism | Reduced efficacy of anxiolytic |
| **SSRIs** | Variable; fluvoxamine most significant | CYP1A2 inhibition | Reduce caffeine with fluvoxamine |

### Caffeine Content Reference
| Beverage/Source | Caffeine Content (mg) |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| Brewed coffee (8 oz) | 95-200 |
| Espresso (1 oz) | 60-75 |
| Black tea (8 oz) | 25-50 |
| Green tea (8 oz) | 25-35 |
| Cola (12 oz) | 30-40 |
| Energy drink (8 oz) | 70-100 |
| Red Bull (8.4 oz) | 80 |
| 5-Hour Energy (2 oz) | 200 |
| Dark chocolate (1 oz) | 20 |

# 3. LAB MONITORING RECOMMENDATIONS

## 3.1 Baseline Labs Before Starting Psychiatric Medications

### General Pre-Treatment Laboratory Panel

## 3.3 Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM)

### TDM Guidelines Summary

| Drug | Therapeutic Range | Sampling Time | Indications for TDM |
|------|-------------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Lithium** | 0.6-1.0 mEq/L (maintenance); 0.8-1.2 mEq/L (acute mania) | 12 hours post-dose (trough) | Routine monitoring; toxicity suspicion; non-response; renal impairment; drug interactions |
| **Valproate** | 50-100 mcg/mL (mood); 50-125 mcg/mL (seizures) | Trough (immediately before next dose) | Non-response; toxicity symptoms; pregnancy planning; drug interactions; adherence check |
| **Carbamazepine** | 4-12 mcg/mL | Trough | Non-response; toxicity; auto-induction period; drug interactions; hyponatremia evaluation |
| **Clozapine** | 350-600 ng/mL (clinical response); >600 ng/mL (toxicity risk) | Trough | Subtherapeutic response; toxicity signs; smoking status change; drug interaction; adherence |
| **Haloperidol** | 5-15 ng/mL | 12-24h post-dose (varies) | EPS assessment; non-response; toxicity |
| **Aripiprazole** | 150-300 ng/mL (total: aripiprazole + active metabolite) | Trough | Non-response; CYP2D6 PM assessment |
| **Olanzapine** | 20-40 ng/mL | Trough | Non-response; smoking status change; drug interactions |
| **Lamotrigine** | 3-14 mcg/mL (established for seizures; mood data limited) | Trough | Limited role in psychiatry; consider in pregnancy or drug interactions |

### Practical TDM Notes
- **Sample timing is critical:** Always draw trough levels (immediately before next scheduled dose) for lithium, valproate, carbamazepine, clozapine
- **Document exact time:** Of last dose and blood draw
- **Consistent timing:** Draw at same interval from dose each time
- **Interpret in clinical context:** TDM guides but does not replace clinical judgment

## 3.5 Pre-TMS Safety Screening and Labs

### Pre-TMS Evaluation

| Assessment | Details | Priority |
|------------|---------|----------|
| **Medical history and physical examination** | Focus on neurologic, seizure history, implanted devices | REQUIRED |
| **TMS Adult Safety Screen (TASS)** | Standardized questionnaire for contraindications | REQUIRED |
| **Medication review** | Identify seizure-threshold-lowering medications | REQUIRED |

### Absolute Contraindications to TMS
1. Non-removable ferromagnetic metal in or near the head (excluding dental hardware)
2. Cochlear implants
3. Implanted cardiac defibrillators (ICD) within 30 cm of coil
4. Pacemakers within 30 cm of coil
5. Deep brain stimulation (DBS) devices
6. Medication pumps
7. Metal aneurysm clips or coils
8. Bullet fragments or shrapnel in skull

### Relative Contraindications
- History of epilepsy or seizures (first-degree family history)
- Traumatic, vascular, tumoral, or infectious brain lesions
- Cerebrovascular disease
- Pregnancy
- Unstable cardiac disease
- Medications that lower seizure threshold (see below)
- Sleep deprivation
- Alcohol/substance use or withdrawal
- Excessive caffeine use

### Seizure Threshold-Lowering Medications (Psychiatric)

| Drug | Risk Level | Recommendation |
|------|------------|----------------|
| **Bupropion** | HIGH | Consider dose reduction or switch |
| **Clozapine** | HIGH | Caution; use modified parameters |
| **Tricyclic antidepressants** | MODERATE-HIGH | Caution; reduce dose if possible |
| **Lithium** | MODERATE | Hold morning of treatment or reduce |
| **Antipsychotics** | MODERATE | Generally safe with standard parameters |
| **SSRIs/SNRIs** | LOW | Generally safe |
| **Benzodiazepines** | PROTECTIVE | May reduce TMS efficacy by raising threshold |

### Lab Testing for TMS
**No specific laboratory tests are routinely required** for pre-TMS evaluation unless clinically indicated by history:
- Pregnancy test (hCG) if pregnancy possible
- Urine drug screen if substance use concern

### TMS Safety Parameters
| Parameter | Guideline |
|-----------|-----------|

| **Total sessions** | Typically 36 treatments |

# 4. SPECIAL POPULATIONS

## 4.1 Pregnancy and Breastfeeding Considerations

### General Principles
- **Risk-benefit analysis:** Untreated psychiatric illness in pregnancy carries significant risks for mother and fetus
- **Medication risks must be weighed against risks of untreated illness**
- **Monotherapy at lowest effective dose preferred** over polypharmacy
- **First trimester is the highest risk period** for teratogenicity
- **Folic acid supplementation (4-5 mg/day) recommended** for all women of childbearing potential on anticonvulsants

### Medication-Specific Considerations

| Medication | Pregnancy Risk | Lactation Risk | Key Considerations |
|------------|---------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Lithium** | LOW-MODERATE (Ebstein anomaly risk: 0.1% vs. 0.005% baseline) | Compatible | Dose increases 25-50% in 3rd trimester; avoid toxicity at delivery; monitor levels every 1-2 weeks |
| **Valproate** | HIGH (neural tube defect 1-2%, neurodevelopmental delay) | Caution | CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy unless no alternative; mandatory folate 4-5 mg; check AFP/ultrasound |
| **Carbamazepine** | MODERATE (neural tube defect 0.5-1%) | Compatible | Folate 4-5 mg/day; vitamin K 20 mg/day last month to prevent neonatal hemorrhage; monitor levels |
| **Lamotrigine** | LOW-MODERATE | Compatible | Levels drop dramatically in pregnancy (increased glucuronidation); monitor levels monthly; dose may need doubling |
| **SSRIs (sertraline, fluoxetine)** | LOW (paroxetine: avoid; possible cardiac defect risk) | Generally compatible | Sertraline preferred; monitor for PPHN (persistent pulmonary hypertension); mild neonatal withdrawal possible |
| **Antipsychotics** | LOW-MODERATE | Generally compatible | Olanzapine most studied; metabolic monitoring critical; avoid high-dose antipsychotics near delivery |
| **Benzodiazepines** | MODERATE (cleft lip/palate risk in first trimester; floppy baby syndrome if near delivery) | Caution | Avoid first trimester; taper before delivery; use lowest effective dose |
| **ECT** | Generally safe | N/A | Treatment of choice for severe depression/psychosis in pregnancy when medications insufficient |

### Nutritional Considerations in Pregnancy

| Nutrient | Requirement | Rationale |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Folic acid** | 4-5 mg/day (if on anticonvulsants) or 0.4-0.8 mg/day (general) | Neural tube defect prevention |
| **Vitamin D** | 600-2000 IU/day | Bone health, immune function |
| **Iron** | 27 mg elemental/day | Increased demand; screen for anemia |
| **Calcium** | 1000-1300 mg/day | Fetal skeletal development |
| **DHA/Omega-3** | 200-300 mg DHA/day | Fetal brain development; may reduce preterm birth |
| **Iodine** | 220 mcg/day | Thyroid function; fetal neurodevelopment |
| **Choline** | 450 mg/day | Fetal brain development |

### Preconception Checklist
- [ ] Transition to pregnancy-compatible medication regimen (preferably before conception)
- [ ] Start folic acid 4-5 mg/day (if on anticonvulsants) or 0.4 mg/day (otherwise)
- [ ] Ensure vitamin D sufficiency (>30 ng/mL)
- [ ] Optimize thyroid function (TSH <2.5 mIU/L preconception)
- [ ] Baseline medication levels if indicated
- [ ] Genetic counseling if indicated
- [ ] Discussion of medication risks vs. untreated illness risks

## 4.2 Geriatric Polypharmacy Nutrition Risks

### Risk Factors in Elderly Psychiatric Patients
- Average elderly patient on 5+ medications (polypharmacy)
- Higher risk of drug-nutrient interactions due to:
  - Age-related changes in absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion
  - Decreased hepatic blood flow and CYP enzyme activity (30-40% reduction)
  - Reduced renal function (GFR declines ~1 mL/min/year after age 40)
  - Decreased albumin (affects drug binding)
  - Poor nutritional status (reduced intake, chronic disease, dentition)
  - Cognitive impairment affecting medication and diet management

### High-Risk Medication Combinations in Elderly

| Combination | Nutritional Risk | Clinical Concern |
|-------------|-----------------|------------------|
| PPI + Metformin + Diuretic | B12, magnesium, folate depletion | Severe multifactorial deficiency |
| Antipsychotic + Statin + PPI | CoQ10, metabolic syndrome, B12 | Cardiovascular risk + deficiency |
| Lithium + Thiazide diuretic | Sodium imbalance, lithium toxicity | Acute toxicity risk |
| Warfarin + PPI + Antidepressant | Vitamin K variability, B12, bleeding | INR instability + GI bleeding |
| Anticonvulsant + PPI | Vitamin D, B12, folate, magnesium | Multiple deficiencies |
| Anticholinergic + Antipsychotic + SSRI | Constipation, appetite changes, dry mouth | Poor oral intake, dehydration |

### Beers Criteria-Relevant Recommendations
- **Avoid:** Anticholinergics (nutrition risk: dry mouth, constipation, impaired swallowing)
- **Avoid:** Long-acting benzodiazepines (fall risk, confusion affecting nutrition)
- **Caution:** Antipsychotics (metabolic syndrome, EPS, increased mortality in dementia)
- **Caution:** NSAIDs (GI bleeding, renal effects on electrolytes)

### Geriatric-Specific Monitoring
| Parameter | Frequency |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Comprehensive medication review** | Every 6 months |
| **CBC, CMP, B12, folate, vitamin D** | Annually |
| **Albumin, prealbumin** | If malnutrition concern |
| **Weight** | Every visit |
| **Cognitive assessment (MMSE/MoCA)** | Annually |
| **Swallowing evaluation** | If choking, coughing with meals, unexplained weight loss |
| **Dentition assessment** | Annually |

### Nutritional Support Strategies
- **Medication reconciliation at every visit**
- **Simplify medication regimens** where possible (deprescribing)
- **Consistent timing** for medications affecting nutrient absorption
- **Nutrient-dense, easy-to-prepare foods**
- **Consider nutrition referral** for patients with unintentional weight loss
- **Hydration monitoring** (elderly at increased dehydration risk)

## 4.3 Pediatric Medication Considerations

### Developmental Considerations
- Children and adolescents metabolize medications differently than adults
- **Hepatic enzyme systems are not fully mature:** CYP activity may be higher (CYP3A4) or lower (CYP2C19) than adults depending on age
- **Weight-based dosing required** for most psychiatric medications
- **Nutrient requirements are higher** during periods of growth
- **Psychiatric medications may affect growth and development**

### Pediatric-Specific Risks

| Medication | Nutritional/Metabolic Risk | Monitoring |
|------------|---------------------------|------------|
| **Stimulants (methylphenidate, amphetamine)** | Appetite suppression, growth deceleration, weight loss, insomnia | Weight and height monthly during titration; plot on growth chart; drug holidays considered |
| **Antipsychotics** | Significant weight gain, metabolic syndrome (especially SGAs) | ADA metabolic monitoring; BMI percentile; quarterly for first year |
| **SSRIs** | Mild GI effects, rare hyponatremia | Monitor for SIADH symptoms |
| **Mood stabilizers** | Weight gain (valproate, lithium), polycystic ovary syndrome (valproate), thyroid effects (lithium) | Regular metabolic monitoring; TSH for lithium; testosterone/AMH for valproate in adolescent females |
| **Anticonvulsants** | Vitamin D depletion, folate antagonism, weight changes | Vitamin D, folate, B12 monitoring; DEXA if long-term |

### Growth and Development Monitoring
- **Weight and height:** Plot on standardized growth charts at every visit
- **BMI percentile:** Use age-appropriate percentiles (not adult cutoffs)
- **Growth velocity:** Calculate and compare to age norms
- **Pubertal staging:** Monitor if medications may affect pubertal timing

### Key Pediatric Nutrient Considerations
- **Iron:** Adolescent females at highest risk for deficiency; screen if fatigue, attention problems
- **Vitamin D:** Supplement if <20 ng/mL; crucial for growing bones
- **Folate:** Especially important if on anticonvulsants
- **Omega-3:** Evidence for ADHD; no major drug interactions at standard doses
- **Calcium:** Ensure adequate intake (1300 mg/day for adolescents) especially with anticonvulsants

### FDA Black Box Warnings (Pediatric)
- **Antidepressants:** Increased suicidal ideation in patients <24 years during first 2 months of treatment
- **Antipsychotics:** Increased mortality in elderly with dementia-related psychosis (applies to adolescents in some contexts)

# 5. TOP 10 MOST CLINICALLY SIGNIFICANT DRUG-NUTRIENT INTERACTIONS

## Ranked by Clinical Severity and Frequency

---

### 1. LITHIUM + SODIUM DEPLETION --> LITHIUM TOXICITY
**Severity: CRITICAL | Frequency: COMMON**

- **Mechanism:** Sodium depletion (from diuretics, vomiting, diarrhea, low-salt diets) causes compensatory lithium reabsorption in proximal tubule --> toxic lithium levels
- **Risk factors:** Thiazide diuretics (increase lithium 25-40%), ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs, dehydration, low-sodium diet
- **Clinical presentation:** Tremor, ataxia, confusion, seizures, coma, renal failure
- **Management:** Maintain consistent sodium intake; hold lithium if vomiting/diarrhea/dehydration; check lithium level within 3-5 days of starting interacting drugs
- **Prevention:** Patient education about maintaining consistent salt intake; avoiding NSAIDs; monitoring sodium levels regularly

---

### 2. ST. JOHN'S WORT + PSYCHIATRIC MEDICATIONS --> THERAPEUTIC FAILURE
**Severity: CRITICAL | Frequency: COMMON**

- **Mechanism:** Potent CYP3A4, CYP2C19, CYP2C9 and P-glycoprotein induction reduces drug levels by 50-80%
- **Affected drugs:** Sertraline (50% reduction), quetiapine (>80% reduction), aripiprazole (75% reduction), alprazolam (50% reduction), lamotrigine (25% reduction)
- **Clinical presentation:** Recurrence of depression, mania, psychosis, anxiety breakthrough; hospitalization
- **Management:** CONTRAINDICATED with most psychiatric medications; if patient using SJW, assist with supervised taper and transition to conventional antidepressants
- **Key point:** Patients often do not disclose herbal supplement use -- ASK SPECIFICALLY

---

### 3. VALPROATE + LOW FOLATE --> NEURAL TUBE DEFECTS (PREGNANCY)
**Severity: CRITICAL | Frequency: HIGH RISK WHEN PREGNANT**

- **Mechanism:** Valproate causes folate depletion and is a direct folate antagonist; folate is essential for neural tube closure in first trimester
- **Risk:** 1-2% risk of neural tube defects (20-40x baseline); 30-40% risk of neurodevelopmental disorders
- **Clinical presentation:** Spina bifida, anencephaly, cognitive impairment, autism spectrum features in offspring
- **Management:** If valproate absolutely necessary in pregnancy: folate 4-5 mg/day starting before conception; detailed fetal ultrasound; AFP screening; genetic counseling
- **Prevention:** CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy unless no alternatives exist; transition to safer mood stabilizer (lamotrigine, lithium) before conception

---

### 4. CLOZAPINE + CAFFEINE --> SEVERE TOXICITY
**Severity: HIGH | Frequency: MODERATE**

- **Mechanism:** Caffeine inhibits CYP1A2, the primary enzyme metabolizing clozapine; increases clozapine levels significantly
- **Case report:** Patient on stable clozapine 400 mg/day consumed 600 mg/day caffeine (energy drinks) --> clozapine level 1796 ng/mL --> multiorgan system failure
- **Clinical presentation:** Severe sedation, metabolic acidosis, respiratory failure, acute renal failure, seizures, coma
- **Management:** Counsel patients to maintain CONSISTENT caffeine intake; monitor for toxicity signs if intake changes
- **Smoking note:** Smoking induces CYP1A2 (lowers clozapine levels); smoking cessation can raise clozapine levels by 50-75% -- a double risk factor

---

### 5. THIAZIDE DIURETICS + LITHIUM --> LITHIUM TOXICITY
**Severity: HIGH | Frequency: COMMON**

- **Mechanism:** Thiazides cause sodium depletion --> enhanced proximal tubule lithium reabsorption --> 25-40% increase in lithium levels
- **Clinical presentation:** Lithium toxicity: tremor, confusion, ataxia, seizures, acute kidney injury, arrhythmias
- **Management:** If diuretic necessary, prefer loop diuretics (minimal lithium effect) or potassium-sparing agents; reduce lithium dose 25-50% when starting thiazide; check lithium within 3-5 days
- **Prevention:** Avoid thiazide diuretics in patients on lithium if possible; if unavoidable, monitor closely

---

### 6. GRAPEFRUIT JUICE + CYP3A4 SUBSTRATES --> DRUG TOXICITY
**Severity: HIGH | Frequency: COMMON**

- **Mechanism:** Furanocoumarins in grapefruit irreversibly inhibit intestinal CYP3A4, increasing bioavailability of affected drugs by 100-1000%
- **Psychiatric drugs affected:** Buspirone (10-fold increase), quetiapine, alprazolam, triazolam, midazolam
- **Clinical presentation:** Excessive sedation, respiratory depression, hypotension, serotonin syndrome
- **Management:** COMPLETE AVOIDANCE of grapefruit, Seville orange, and pomelo while on CYP3A4 substrates; effect persists 24-72 hours
- **Patient education:** Most patients are unaware of this interaction; specific counseling required

---

### 7. METFORMIN + VITAMIN B12 DEFICIENCY --> NEUROPSYCHIATRIC SYMPTOMS
**Severity: MODERATE-HIGH | Frequency: COMMON (10-30% long-term)**

- **Mechanism:** Metformin interferes with B12 absorption via multiple mechanisms (calcium-dependent ileal disruption, altered motility, bacterial overgrowth)
- **Clinical presentation:** Anemia, peripheral neuropathy, cognitive impairment, depression, dementia-like symptoms that can mimic or worsen psychiatric illness
- **Management:** Annual B12 monitoring; supplement 1000 mcg/day if deficient or borderline; consider calcium supplementation to improve absorption
- **Psychiatric relevance:** Metformin increasingly used for antipsychotic-induced metabolic syndrome; B12 deficiency can worsen depression and cognitive symptoms

---

### 8. ANTICONVULSANTS + VITAMIN D DEFICIENCY --> OSTEOPOROSIS/FRACTURES
**Severity: MODERATE-HIGH | Frequency: VERY COMMON (long-term use)**

- **Mechanism:** CYP450 enzyme induction accelerates catabolism of 25(OH) vitamin D; reduced calcium absorption; secondary hyperparathyroidism
- **Affected drugs:** Carbamazepine, valproate, phenytoin, phenobarbital, oxcarbazepine
- **Clinical presentation:** Osteoporosis, osteomalacia, pathological fractures (especially hip and spine); occurs after years of therapy
- **Management:** Baseline and annual 25(OH)D levels; replete to >30 ng/mL (2000-4000 IU/day typically needed); DEXA scan for long-term users; calcium 1200 mg/day
- **Psychiatric relevance:** Anticonvulsants are widely used as mood stabilizers; patients may remain on them for decades

---

### 9. ANTIPSYCHOTICS --> METABOLIC SYNDROME --> CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASE
**Severity: HIGH | Frequency: VERY COMMON**

- **Mechanism:** H1 antagonism (weight gain), 5-HT2C antagonism (increased appetite), M3 antagonism (impaired insulin secretion), peripheral effects on adipose and liver
- **Risk:** 10-30 kg weight gain in first year (clozapine, olanzapine); 2-3x increased risk of type 2 diabetes; 2-3x increased cardiovascular mortality
- **Clinical presentation:** Central obesity, hyperglycemia, dyslipidemia, hypertension; cardiovascular disease is the #1 cause of death in patients with serious mental illness (10-20 year reduced life expectancy)
- **Management:** Baseline and regular metabolic monitoring (ADA guidelines); metformin for weight/diabetes; statins for lipids; ACE inhibitors/ARBs for hypertension; lifestyle interventions
- **Prevention:** Choose lower-risk antipsychotics when clinically appropriate; metabolic monitoring is REQUIRED, not optional

---

### 10. PPIs + CHRONIC NUTRIENT DEPLETION (B12, MAGNESIUM, CALCIUM)
**Severity: MODERATE-HIGH | Frequency: COMMON (chronic use)**

- **Mechanism:** Gastric acid suppression impairs absorption of nutrients requiring acid for solubilization (B12, magnesium, calcium, iron, zinc)
- **Clinical presentation:** B12 deficiency (neuropathy, cognitive impairment, anemia), hypomagnesemia (tremor, arrhythmias, seizures -- mimics psychiatric symptoms), osteoporotic fractures
- **Management:** Annual B12 and magnesium monitoring; use calcium citrate (not carbonate) if supplementing; lowest effective PPI dose; consider H2-blocker alternative if appropriate
- **Psychiatric relevance:** PPIs are frequently co-prescribed for GI side effects of psychiatric medications; psychiatric patients have higher rates of GI complaints; B12 deficiency can worsen depression and cognitive function

| **Starting valproate** | Valproate level (if TDM needed), LFTs, CBC, pregnancy test | Baseline; LFTs/CBC q3-6 mo |
| **Starting carbamazepine** | Carbamazepine level, CBC, LFTs, Na, vitamin D, folate, B12, pregnancy test | Baseline; CBC q2 wks x 3 mo then q3 mo; Na q3-6 mo; vitamin D annually |
| **Starting clozapine** | ANC (mandatory REMS), CBC, CMP, fasting glucose, lipids, weight, ECG | ANC weekly x 6 mo, biweekly x 6 mo, then monthly; metabolic per ADA guidelines |
| **Starting antipsychotic (non-clozapine SGA)** | Weight, BMI, waist circumference, BP, fasting glucose/HbA1c, fasting lipids | Baseline, 4 wks, 8 wks, 12 wks, then quarterly for metabolic; weight every visit |
| **Starting SSRI** | Baseline CBC, CMP, Na (especially if >65) | Na at 2 weeks (elderly); otherwise per symptoms |
| **Pre-ECT** | CBC, CMP (K, Na), glucose, ECG, pregnancy test, cognitive assessment | Within 30 days |
| **Pre-TMS** | TASS questionnaire, medication review, device screening | Before treatment series |
| **Chronic anticonvulsant use** | 25(OH) vitamin D, Ca, P, ALP, folate, B12, DEXA | Vitamin D annually; DEXA every 1-2 years |
| **Chronic PPI use** | B12, magnesium, calcium | Annually |
| **Chronic diuretic use** | Electrolytes (Na, K, Cl, CO2), magnesium | 1-2 wks after start, then q3-6 mo |
| **Chronic statin use** | CK (if symptoms), consider CoQ10 level | CK if myalgias |
| **Pregnancy on psychiatric meds** | Medication levels, TSH, folate, vitamin D, CBC, CMP, fetal ultrasound | Per medication-specific protocol; lithium levels q1-2 wks |
| **Geriatric on psychotropics** | CBC, CMP, B12, folate, vitamin D, albumin, medication review | Every 6 months |
