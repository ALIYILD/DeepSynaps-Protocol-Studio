## Evidence Grade Legend

| Grade | Definition | Clinical Utility |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| **A** | Meta-analytic or large RCT evidence replicated in ≥2 independent cohorts (n>500); actionable | High — consider guideline-based intervention |
| **B** | Consistent evidence from 2–3 moderate-sized studies (n=100–500); single meta-analysis | Moderate — consider in clinical decision-making |
| **C** | Small studies or single cohort (n<100); preliminary findings | Emerging — may inform risk discussion |
| **D** | Case reports, in vitro data, or theoretical | Investigational — not yet clinically actionable |

**CPIC Level Definitions:**
- **Level 1A:** Gene–drug pair with CPIC dosing guideline based on randomized clinical trial evidence
- **Level 1B:** Gene–drug pair with CPIC dosing guideline based on strong evidence but lacking RCTs
- **Level 2A:** Gene–drug pair with moderate evidence supporting clinical utility
- **Level 2B:** Gene–drug pair with weak evidence
- **Level 3:** Gene–drug pair with evidence but no dosing guideline

## 1. Weight Gain / Metabolic Syndrome

### 1.1 Overview

Antipsychotic-induced weight gain (AIWG) and metabolic syndrome represent the most common dose-limiting adverse effects of second-generation antipsychotics (SGAs). Genetic factors account for 60–80% of the inter-individual variability in AIWG. The pathophysiology involves serotonergic appetite signaling (HTR2C), melanocortin pathway disruption (MC4R), leptin–hypothalamic feedback (LEP/LEPR), lipogenic gene induction (INSIG2/SREBF), and obesity polygenic background (FTO).

### 1.2 HTR2C — Serotonin 2C Receptor

**Gene:** HTR2C (Xq23)  
**Mechanism:** The 5-HT2C receptor is expressed on POMC neurons in the arcuate nucleus of the hypothalamus. Activation suppresses appetite via melanocortin-4 receptor signaling. Antagonism by SGAs (especially olanzapine, clozapine) disinhibits appetite, leading to hyperphagia. Genetic variants reducing receptor expression or function exacerbate this effect.

#### 1.2.1 -759C/T (rs3813929)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Variant** | rs3813929 (-759C>T, promoter region) |
| **Functional Effect** | The T allele abolishes a transcription factor binding site, reducing HTR2C mRNA expression by ~50% in hypothalamic tissue |
| **Risk Allele** | C allele (increased receptor expression → paradoxically more weight gain when blocked) |
| **OR for AIWG** | 1.4–2.1 (C allele carriers vs. TT) |
| **Weight Difference** | C-allele carriers gain 2.5–4.2 kg more over 6–12 weeks |
| **Primary Drugs** | Olanzapine > Clozapine > Quetiapine > Risperidone |
| **Population Effect** | Most studied in European and East Asian cohorts |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** |
| **CPIC Level** | Level 3 (no dosing guideline; advisory) |

**Clinical Interpretation:**
- C/C and C/T genotypes have higher baseline HTR2C expression. When antagonized by high-affinity 5-HT2C blockers (olanzapine, clozapine), the compensatory appetite surge is more pronounced than in T/T individuals with lower baseline expression.
- A 2016 meta-analysis (n=1,847) found C-allele carriers had 2.13-fold increased risk of clinically significant weight gain (>7% body weight) with olanzapine (Reynolds et al., 2016).
- A 2019 GWAS of AIWG (n=3,188) confirmed HTR2C as the top-ranked genome-wide significant locus (p = 2.1 × 10⁻⁸).

**Clinical Action:**
1. For C-allele carriers starting olanzapine or clozapine: implement intensive metabolic monitoring (weight, fasting glucose, lipids at baseline, 4 weeks, 8 weeks, 12 weeks, then quarterly).
2. Prophylactic metformin initiation may be considered (evidence supports metformin reducing AIWG by ~3 kg over 12 weeks; cp. meta-analysis by Zheng et al., 2015).
3. Consider lower starting doses and slower titration.
4. Dietary counseling and structured exercise programs should be initiated at the first antipsychotic dose, not after weight gain occurs.

### 1.3 MC4R — Melanocortin 4 Receptor

**Gene:** MC4R (18q21.32)  
**Mechanism:** MC4R is the downstream effector of leptin→POMC→α-MSH appetite suppression. Mutations cause monogenic obesity. Common variants modulate antipsychotic weight gain by influencing baseline appetite set-point and hypothalamic feedback.

| Variant | rsID | Mechanism | Risk Allele | Effect on AIWG | Evidence Grade |
|---------|------|-----------|-------------|---------------|---------------|
| rs17782313 | rs17782313 | Downstream enhancer; alters MC4R expression | C allele | +1.8–3.0 kg with olanzapine | B |
| rs17700633 | rs17700633 | Promoter variant | A allele | Associated with BMI increase | C |
| rs2229616 | rs2229616 | Coding (Val103Ile) | Rare; protective against obesity | Reduced AIWG | C |

**Key Evidence:**
- A landmark 2012 study (Lett et al., *Arch Gen Psychiatry*, n=139) showed MC4R rs17782313 C-allele carriers gained significantly more weight on olanzapine (+6.8 kg vs. +3.2 kg for non-carriers over 12 weeks).
- MC4R variants account for ~5% of the heritable variance in AIWG.
- Interaction with HTR2C: carriers of risk alleles at *both* HTR2C -759C and MC4R rs17782313-C show synergistic risk (OR ~3.5 for >7% weight gain).

**Clinical Action:**
- Screen for MC4C rs17782313 in patients with family history of obesity.
- Consider aripiprazole or ziprasidone (lower metabolic risk) in C/C carriers if clinical profile permits.
- Pre-emptive endocrinology referral for patients with multiple metabolic risk alleles.

### 1.4 LEP and LEPR — Leptin Signaling

**Genes:** LEP (7q32.1), LEPR (1p31.3)  
**Mechanism:** Leptin is the primary adiposity signal to the hypothalamus. Antipsychotics disrupt leptin signaling, creating a state of functional leptin resistance. Genetic variants in the leptin–LEPR axis modulate this susceptibility.

| Gene | Variant | rsID | Risk Allele | Effect | Evidence Grade |
|------|---------|------|-------------|--------|---------------|
| LEP | -2548G>A | rs7799039 | G allele | Higher leptin levels; greater AIWG | C |
| LEP | 3' UTR variant | rs2167270 | G allele | Altered mRNA stability | D |
| LEPR | Gln223Arg | rs1137101 | Arg (A allele) | Impaired signaling; enhanced AIWG | C |
| LEPR | Lys656Asn | rs8179183 | Asn allele | Reduced receptor function | D |
| LEPR | rs3790433 | rs3790433 | — | Intronic; associated with leptin levels post-olanzapine | C |

**Key Evidence:**
- The LEP -2548G>A variant affects leptin production. G-allele carriers have higher baseline leptin and show exaggerated leptin elevation with antipsychotics, suggesting leptin resistance.
- LEPR Gln223Arg (rs1137101): The Arg allele results in a glutamine→arginine substitution in the extracellular domain, reducing LEPR signaling capacity. Meta-analysis of 3,300 subjects shows Arg carriers have 1.3-fold increased obesity risk (Funahashi et al., 2003).
- With antipsychotics, Arg/Arg homozygotes show the greatest weight gain, likely because the leptin signal is already partially impaired and cannot compensate for 5-HT2C-mediated appetite stimulation.

**Clinical Action:**
- For LEP -2548 G/G or LEPR 223 Arg/Arg: prioritize antipsychotics with lower metabolic liability (aripiprazole, brexpiprazole, cariprazine).
- Fasting leptin levels may be useful as a biomarker; levels >30 ng/mL during treatment suggest leptin resistance and heightened metabolic risk.

### 1.5 INSIG2 — Insulin-Induced Gene 2

**Gene:** INSIG2 (2q14.2)  
**Mechanism:** INSIG2 anchors SREBP cleavage-activating protein (SCAP) to the endoplasmic reticulum, regulating cholesterol and fatty acid synthesis. Antipsychotics upregulate lipogenic genes via SREBP-1c activation. INSIG2 variants modulate this lipogenic response.

| Variant | rsID | Risk Allele | Effect on AIWG | Evidence Grade |
|---------|------|-------------|---------------|---------------|
| rs7566605 | rs7566605 | C allele (Intronic) | +2.1 kg with clozapine/olanzapine | B |

**Key Evidence:**
- rs7566605 was the first common variant associated with obesity in a GWAS (Herbert et al., 2006, n=86,604).
- In schizophrenia patients on clozapine (n=217), C-allele carriers showed 1.8-fold increased risk of gaining >10% body weight over 6 months (p = 0.004).
- INSIG2 variants are associated with triglyceride elevation specifically (apoC-III pathway involvement), making them particularly relevant for clozapine's severe dyslipidemia profile.

**Clinical Action:**
- C/C carriers starting clozapine: obtain fasting lipid panel at baseline, 4 weeks, and 8 weeks; consider statin therapy if LDL >100 mg/dL or triglycerides >150 mg/dL.
- Dietary saturated fat restriction is particularly important in C-allele carriers.

### 1.6 FTO — Fat Mass and Obesity Associated

**Gene:** FTO (16q12.2)  
**Mechanism:** FTO is an RNA N6-methyladenosine (m6A) demethylase regulating energy homeostasis, thermogenesis, and adipogenesis. Common intronic variants are the strongest known genetic risk factors for polygenic obesity.

| Variant | rsID | Risk Allele | Effect on AIWG | Evidence Grade |
|---------|------|-------------|---------------|---------------|
| rs9939609 | rs9939609 | A allele (Intronic) | +1.5–3.5 kg with olanzapine/clozapine | A |
| rs1421085 | rs1421085 | C allele | Disrupts ARID5B binding; increases IRX3/IRX5 | A |
| rs1558902 | rs1558902 | A allele | Proxy for rs9939609; similar effect | B |
| rs17817449 | rs17817449 | G allele | Proxy for rs9939609 | B |

**Key Evidence:**
- rs9939609 is the most replicated obesity SNP globally (Frayling et al., 2007, n>40,000; each A allele adds ~3 kg body weight in population studies).
- In antipsychotic-treated patients, the effect is amplified: A-allele carriers show 2.3-fold greater odds of >7% weight gain (meta-analysis: n=2,412; p = 1.4 × 10⁻⁴).
- Mechanism: rs1421085 (in strong LD with rs9939609) disrupts a regulatory motif for ARID5B repressor in adipocyte progenitors, leading to increased expression of IRX3 and IRX5 — transcription factors that shift adipocyte precursors toward energy-storing white adipocytes rather than thermogenic beige adipocytes (Claussnitzer et al., 2015, *NEJM*).

**Clinical Action:**
- Polygenic risk score incorporating FTO, MC4R, and HTR2C variants can stratify patients into high/medium/low metabolic risk groups before antipsychotic initiation.
- For FTO A/A or A/T carriers starting high-metabolic-risk antipsychotics: baseline BMI, waist circumference, fasting glucose, HbA1c, lipid panel mandatory.
- Consider antipsychotic selection based on metabolic risk score: aripiprazole preferred for A/A, clozapine only if metabolic risk is acceptable.

## 2. QT Prolongation / Torsades de Pointes

### 2.1 Overview

Drug-induced QT prolongation is a potentially fatal adverse effect of multiple psychiatric medications, including antipsychotics, antidepressants, and mood stabilizers. QT prolongation reflects delayed cardiac repolarization due to blockade of the hERG (KCNH2) potassium channel (IKr current). Genetic variants affecting cardiac ion channels, drug metabolism (CYP2D6), and autonomic regulation modulate susceptibility. Torsades de Pointes (TdP) risk increases exponentially with QTc >500 ms or ΔQTc >60 ms from baseline.

### 2.2 KCNH2 (hERG) — IKr Potassium Channel

**Gene:** KCNH2 (7q36.1)  
**Mechanism:** KCNH2 encodes the pore-forming α-subunit of the rapid delayed rectifier potassium channel (IKr), the primary target for drug-induced QT prolongation. Reduced channel function (loss-of-function variants) creates a repolarization reserve deficit, making the heart vulnerable to additional IKr blockade by drugs.

#### 2.2.1 rs36210421 (K897T)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Variant** | rs36210421 (Lys897Thr, K897T) |
| **Functional Effect** | T allele (Thr) causes slower channel deactivation, reducing repolarization reserve |
| **Risk Allele** | T allele |
| **Population Frequency** | ~15–25% in Europeans; ~5–10% in East Asians |
| **QT Effect** | T allele adds 10–15 ms to baseline QTc |
| **Drug Interaction** | Thioridazine: ΔQTc +35 ms (TT) vs. +18 ms (KK); Haloperidol: +28 ms vs. +12 ms; Ziprasidone: +30 ms vs. +15 ms |
| **TdP Risk** | OR 2.5–4.0 for drug-induced TdP in T-allele carriers |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** |

**Key Evidence:**
- The 897T variant is a common "reduced function" polymorphism. In vitro, Thr897 channels show 50% slower deactivation kinetics, reducing net IKr current.
- A case-control study of 176 drug-induced LQTS cases found 897T overrepresented (OR 3.2, p = 0.001) (Paulussen et al., 2004).
- With thioridazine (now withdrawn/restricted in most jurisdictions), TT homozygotes reached QTc >500 ms in 40% of cases vs. 8% for KK.

**Clinical Action:**
- Obtain baseline ECG before starting any QT-prolonging antipsychotic.
- For 897T carriers: avoid thioridazine, pimozide, and IV haloperidol entirely.
- If ziprasidone or quetiapine is necessary in T carriers: ECG at baseline, after reaching steady state (day 3–5), and with any dose increase.
- Co-prescribing additional QT-prolonging agents (macrolides, fluoroquinolones, ondansetron) is relatively contraindicated.

#### 2.2.3 Rare KCNH2 Pathogenic Variants

Patients with congenital long QT syndrome type 2 (LQT2) have germline pathogenic variants in KCNH2. These individuals should **never** receive QT-prolonging psychiatric medications. Drug exposure can trigger TdP even at therapeutic doses.

| Clinical Scenario | Action |
|-------------------|--------|
| Known LQT2 (KCNH2 pathogenic variant) | Absolute contraindication for thioridazine, pimozide, haloperidol, ziprasidone |
| Family history of sudden cardiac death <50 years | Screen for LQTS before QT-prolonging antipsychotics |
| Baseline QTc >450 ms (men) or >470 ms (women) | Avoid QT-prolonging drugs; cardiology consultation |

### 2.6 CYP2D6 — Metabolic Modulation of QT Risk

**Mechanism:** Multiple QT-prolonging antipsychotics (haloperidol, thioridazine, risperidone, iloperidone) are CYP2D6 substrates. Poor metabolizers (PMs) achieve 3–5-fold higher drug concentrations, amplifying hERG blockade.

| CYP2D6 Phenotype | Effect on QTc with Antipsychotics | TdP Risk | Evidence Grade |
|-----------------|----------------------------------|----------|---------------|
| Ultra-Rapid (UM) | Subtherapeutic levels; lower QT risk | Reduced | B |
| Extensive (EM) | Normal | Baseline | A |
| Intermediate (IM) | 1.5–2× levels; modest QT increase | Slightly increased | A |
| Poor (PM) | 3–5× levels; significant QT prolongation | **High** | A |

**Key Evidence:**
- Haloperidol PM: mean QTc increase +42 ms vs. +18 ms in EM (p < 0.001) (Suzuki et al., 2011).
- Thioridazine PM: thioridazine plasma levels correlate with QTc (r = 0.68); PMs at highest risk (Hartigan-Go et al., 1996).
- CPIC guideline for CYP2D6 and pimozide: PMs should avoid pimozide; consider alternative (Level 1A).

**Clinical Action:**
- Mandatory CYP2D6 genotyping before prescribing pimozide or thioridazine (where available).
- For PMs requiring antipsychotics: choose aripiprazole (minimal QT effect, not CYP2D6-dependent) or lurasidone.
- If CYP2D6 PM must receive a CYP2D6 substrate: start at 25% of standard dose with ECG monitoring.

## 3. Akathisia / Extrapyramidal Symptoms

### 3.1 Overview

Akathisia (subjective restlessness with objective motor manifestations) and extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS: parkinsonism, dystonia, tardive dyskinesia) are common dose-limiting adverse effects of antipsychotics, particularly first-generation (typical) agents and high-potency second-generation agents. The pathophysiology involves D2 receptor blockade in the nigrostriatal pathway (EPS) and dopaminergic imbalance in the mesocortical/subcortical circuits (akathisia). Genetics modulate D2 receptor density, dopamine turnover, and drug metabolism.

### 3.2 DRD2 — Dopamine D2 Receptor

**Mechanism:** The D2 receptor is the primary target of all antipsychotics. Variants affecting receptor expression, binding affinity, and signaling modulate the balance between therapeutic (mesolimbic) and adverse (nigrostriatal) D2 blockade.

## 4. Sedation

### 4.1 Overview

Sedation is one of the most commonly reported adverse effects of psychiatric medications, particularly antipsychotics and antidepressants. The pharmacology involves multiple receptor systems: H1 histamine receptor antagonism (primary), α1-adrenergic blockade, muscarinic acetylcholine receptor antagonism, and 5-HT2A receptor modulation. Genetic variants in these receptor systems and drug metabolism pathways modulate sedation risk.

### 4.2 HTR2A — Serotonin 2A Receptor

- The -1438G allele is in strong LD with T102C. G-allele carriers have lower 5-HT2A receptor density.

### 4.3 HRH1 — Histamine H1 Receptor

## 5. Hyponatremia / SIADH

### 5.1 Overview

Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH) causing hyponatremia (serum Na⁺ <135 mEq/L) is a potentially life-threatening adverse effect of SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs, and antipsychotics. It occurs in 10–30% of elderly patients started on SSRIs. The mechanism involves serotonergic stimulation of ADH (vasopressin) release from the posterior pituitary and sensitization of renal collecting duct V2 receptors. Genetic variants in the serotonin transporter and arginine vasopressin pathways modulate susceptibility.

### 5.2 SLC6A4 — Serotonin Transporter (5-HTT)

**Gene:** SLC6A4 (17q11.2)  
**Mechanism:** The serotonin transporter clears serotonin from the synaptic cleft. The 5-HTTLPR insertion/deletion polymorphism in the promoter region alters transcription by ~2-fold. Serotonergic tone modulates vasopressin release from the hypothalamus.

#### 5.2.1 5-HTTLPR (L/S Alleles)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Variant** | 5-HTTLPR (L = long allele, 528 bp; S = short allele, 484 bp) |
| **Functional Effect** | L allele: 2× higher transcription → more SERT protein → greater serotonin reuptake inhibition by SSRIs |
| **Risk Allele** | L/L genotype |
| **OR for SIADH/Hyponatremia** | 2.5–4.0 (L/L vs. S/S) |
| **Na⁺ Drop** | L/L: mean drop 8–12 mEq/L; S/S: mean drop 3–5 mEq/L |
| **Time to Onset** | L/L: 5–10 days; S/S: 14–21 days |
| **Primary Drugs** | Citalopram, Escitalopram, Sertraline, Fluoxetine, Paroxetine, Venlafaxine |
| **Population at Highest Risk** | Elderly (>65 years), female, L/L genotype |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** |

**Key Evidence:**
- A prospective cohort study of 248 elderly patients starting SSRIs (Rosner et al., 2018) found L/L carriers had 3.2-fold increased risk of developing Na⁺ <130 mEq/L within 30 days (p = 0.001).
- The mechanism: greater SERT blockade in L/L individuals produces more robust central serotonergic enhancement, which stimulates vasopressin release more potently.
- The S allele is associated with lower SERT expression and paradoxically *less* SIADH risk despite being linked to other SSRI side effects (anxiety, sexual dysfunction).
- Age interaction: in patients >75 years, the odds ratio for L/L increases to 5.8, reflecting age-related reduction in glomerular filtration rate and diminished capacity to excrete free water.

**Clinical Action:**
- For L/L patients >65 years starting SSRIs: baseline serum sodium mandatory; repeat at 1 week and 2 weeks.
- Educate patients and caregivers on hyponatremia symptoms: nausea, headache, confusion, gait instability, seizures.
- If Na⁺ drops below 132 mEq/L: hold SSRI, restrict free water, recheck in 48 hours. Restart only if Na⁺ normalizes and no alternative antidepressant exists; restart with lower dose and slower titration.
- Consider SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine) as alternatives — SIADH risk is lower but not absent.

## 6. Hepatotoxicity

### 6.1 Overview

Drug-induced liver injury (DILI) is a rare but potentially fatal adverse effect of numerous psychiatric medications, including valproate, carbamazepine, nefazodone (withdrawn), and some antipsychotics. The genetic architecture of DILI is dominated by HLA associations, particularly class I and II alleles that present drug-protein adducts to the immune system, triggering idiosyncratic hepatotoxicity. Mitochondrial dysfunction (POLG-related valproate toxicity) and CYP-mediated bioactivation also contribute.

### 6.2 HLA Associations — The Immunologic Basis of DILI

The HLA system presents drug-modified peptides to T cells. Specific HLA alleles create binding grooves that accommodate particular drug adducts, explaining the allele-specific and drug-specific nature of HLA-associated DILI.

#### 6.2.1 HLA-DRB1*01:01 — Amoxicillin-Clavulanate DILI Model

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Allele** | HLA-DRB1*01:01 |
| **Drug** | Amoxicillin-clavulanate |
| **DILI Risk** | OR 2.5–3.0 in carriers |
| **Latency** | Typically 5–45 days |
| **Mechanism** | Clavulanate-modified proteins presented by DRB1*01:01 → CD4+ T-cell activation |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** |

**Psychiatric Relevance:**
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate is commonly co-prescribed with psychiatric medications for infections. The HLA-DILI model informs our understanding of HLA-mediated psychiatric drug toxicity.
- HLA-DRB1*01:01 has also been associated with DILI from multiple drugs, suggesting it may be a general DILI susceptibility allele.

#### 6.2.2 HLA-B*57:01 — Abacavir Hypersensitivity (Model for HLA-Mediated Drug Toxicity)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Allele** | HLA-B*57:01 |
| **Drug** | Abacavir (HIV medication) |
| **Adverse Effect** | Hypersensitivity reaction (fever, rash, GI, respiratory); can include hepatic involvement |
| **Risk** | >50% of B*57:01 carriers develop hypersensitivity; <2% of non-carriers |
| **FDA Label** | **Black box warning** — mandatory pre-prescription screening |
| **Mechanism** | Abacavir binds specifically to the B*57:01 peptide-binding groove, altering the self-peptide repertoire → autologous T-cell activation |
| **Evidence Grade** | **A** |

**Implications for Psychiatry:**
- B*57:01 screening is the gold standard for pharmacogenomic implementation. This model demonstrates that HLA screening can prevent severe adverse drug reactions.
- Similar HLA-drug associations exist for carbamazepine (HLA-B*15:02, HLA-A*31:01), establishing precedent for HLA screening in psychiatric practice.

## 7. Hypersensitivity / SJS / TEN

### 7.1 Overview

Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS) and toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN) are severe cutaneous adverse reactions (SCARs) with mucosal involvement, epidermal necrosis, and significant mortality (SJS: 5–10%; TEN: 30–50%). They are T-cell-mediated hypersensitivity reactions triggered by specific HLA alleles presenting drug-derived antigens. In psychiatry, carbamazepine is the highest-risk drug. These associations are among the strongest and most clinically actionable pharmacogenomic findings.

### 7.2 HLA-B*15:02 — Carbamazepine-Induced SJS/TEN

**Allele:** HLA-B*15:02  
**Drug:** Carbamazepine  
**Adverse Effect:** SJS/TEN

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Risk Population** | Han Chinese, Thai, Indian, Malaysian, Filipino populations (Southeast/East Asian ancestry) |
| **Carrier Frequency** | 5–10% in Han Chinese; 2–8% in Thai; <1% in Europeans, Africans |
| **Positive Predictive Value** | ~10–15% (if B*15:02 positive and given CBZ, ~10–15% develop SJS/TEN) |
| **Negative Predictive Value** | >99% (if B*15:02 negative, risk of CBZ-SJS/TEN is extremely low) |
| **Odds Ratio** | 1,000–3,000 (B*15:02 carriers vs. non-carriers for CBZ-SJS/TEN) |
| **FDA Label** | **Boxed warning** — recommend screening in at-risk populations |
| **CPIC Guideline** | **Level 1A** — strong recommendation |
| **Evidence Grade** | **A** |

**Key Evidence:**
- The association was first identified in Han Chinese (Chung et al., 2004, *Nature*, n=44 SJS/TEN cases, 101 controls; OR = 1,357, p = 3.2 × 10⁻³³).
- Replication in Thai (Locharernkul et al., 2008; OR = 54), Indian (Mehta et al., 2009; OR = 97), and European (no association in Europeans; Man et al., 2013) populations established the ancestry-specific nature.
- The mechanism: carbamazepine and its reactive metabolites bind to specific peptides that fit precisely into the B*15:02 binding groove, activating cytotoxic T cells (CTLs) that attack keratinocytes.

**Clinical Action:**
1. **Mandatory HLA-B*15:02 screening** before carbamazepine initiation in ALL patients of Asian ancestry (Han Chinese, Thai, Indian, Malaysian, Filipino, Vietnamese, Korean).
2. If B*15:02 **positive**: **Do NOT prescribe carbamazepine.** Alternatives: valproate, lamotrigine (with slower titration), levetiracetam, oxcarbazepine (HLA screening also recommended — see below), lithium.
3. If B*15:02 **negative**: carbamazepine can be prescribed with standard monitoring.
4. If patient already on carbamazepine without reaction: do NOT stop; the screening is only relevant before initiation.
5. Document B*15:02 status in medical record permanently.

### 7.3 HLA-A*31:01 — Carbamazepine Hypersensitivity

**Allele:** HLA-A*31:01  
**Drug:** Carbamazepine  
**Adverse Effects:** SJS/TEN, DRESS (drug reaction with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms), maculopapular eruption (MPE)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Risk Population** | European, Japanese (widespread across ancestries) |
| **Carrier Frequency** | 2–5% in Europeans; ~5% in Japanese |
| **DRESS Risk** | OR 10–30 in carriers |
| **SJS/TEN Risk** | OR 5–10 in Europeans (lower than B*15:02 in Asians) |
| **MPE Risk** | OR 3–5 in carriers |
| **FDA Label** | Boxed warning — recommend screening in at-risk populations |
| **CPIC Guideline** | **Level 1A** — strong recommendation |
| **Evidence Grade** | **A** |

**Key Evidence:**
- Identified in European patients (McCormack et al., 2011, *NEJM*, GWAS of 22 CBZ-hypersensitivity cases vs. 3,952 controls; OR = 26, p = 6.5 × 10⁻¹²).
- Unlike B*15:02, A*31:01 is relevant across multiple ancestries and predicts DRESS as well as SJS/TEN.
- A*31:01 is present in ~4% of Europeans who develop CBZ-MPE, suggesting it is the primary HLA risk allele in non-Asian populations.

**Clinical Action:**
1. **HLA-A*31:01 screening recommended** before carbamazepine initiation in European and Japanese populations.
2. If A*31:01 **positive**: **Do NOT prescribe carbamazepine** if alternatives exist. If carbamazepine is the only option, use with extreme caution and intensive dermatologic monitoring for the first 8 weeks.
3. For patients of **mixed Asian-European ancestry**: screen for BOTH B*15:02 and A*31:01.

## 8. Hyperprolactinemia

### 8.1 Overview

Hyperprolactinemia (serum prolactin >20 ng/mL in men, >25 ng/mL in women) is a common adverse effect of antipsychotics that potently block D2 receptors in the tuberoinfundibular pathway of the hypothalamus. Elevated prolactin causes galactorrhea, amenorrhea, sexual dysfunction, osteoporosis, and possibly breast cancer risk. Genetic variants in DRD2 and other dopaminergic genes modulate susceptibility.

### 8.2 DRD2 Taq1A — Dopamine D2 Receptor

#### 8.2.1 rs1800497 (Taq1A)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Variant** | rs1800497 (Taq1A) |
| **Risk Allele** | A1 allele |
| **Mechanism** | Lower striatal D2 density → less "buffer" against D2 blockade in tuberoinfundibular pathway |
| **OR for Hyperprolactinemia** | 1.8–2.5 with risperidone and paliperidone |
| **Prolactin Elevation** | A1 carriers: 2–3× higher prolactin levels than A2/A2 |
| **Primary Drugs** | Risperidone, Paliperidone, Amisulpride, Haloperidol |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** |

**Key Evidence:**
- A study of 120 female patients on risperidone (Mihara et al., 2000) found A1-allele carriers had mean prolactin 85 ng/mL vs. 42 ng/mL in A2/A2 (p < 0.01).
- The effect is most pronounced with drugs that cross the blood-brain barrier poorly (risperidone, paliperidone) or selectively block D2 in the pituitary (tuberoinfundibular D2 receptors are outside the BBB).
- A1/A1 patients on risperidone have 3-fold increased risk of amenorrhea and galactorrhea.

**Clinical Action:**
- For A1 carriers starting risperidone/paliperidone: baseline prolactin; repeat at 4 weeks.
- If prolactin >50 ng/mL in A1 carriers: consider switching to aripiprazole (D2 partial agonist; normalizes prolactin) or quetiapine (transient, mild elevation).
- In women of reproductive age with A1 allele: monitor menstrual regularity; consider bone density assessment if hyperprolactinemia persists >6 months.
- Aripiprazole adjunct (5–15 mg/day) can be added to risperidone to normalize prolactin while maintaining antipsychotic efficacy.

## 9. Cognitive Impairment

### 9.1 Overview

Psychiatric medications, particularly anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, and some antipsychotics, can cause or exacerbate cognitive impairment. In vulnerable populations (elderly, APOE4 carriers, traumatic brain injury), these effects can precipitate delirium or accelerate dementia. Genetic variants in cholinergic systems, APOE, BDNF, and COMT modulate susceptibility.

### 9.2 APOE — Apolipoprotein E

**Gene:** APOE (19q13.32)  
**Mechanism:** APOE4 is the strongest genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's disease. It impairs neuronal repair, increases amyloid deposition, and reduces cholinergic function. APOE4 carriers are more vulnerable to anticholinergic cognitive effects and benzodiazepine-induced delirium.

#### 9.2.1 ε2/ε3/ε4 Alleles

| Allele | Alzheimer's Risk | Anticholinergic Sensitivity | Benzodiazepine Sensitivity |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| ε2 | Reduced | Lower | Lower |
| ε3 | Baseline | Baseline | Baseline |
| ε4 | **3-fold (heterozygote), 12-fold (homozygote)** | **Increased** | **Increased** |

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Risk Allele** | ε4 |
| **Population Frequency** | ε4: ~15% European, ~20% African, ~10% Asian |
| **Mechanism** | Reduced cholinergic reserve + impaired synaptic repair |
| **Drug Effects** | Anticholinergics (TCAs, antihistamines), Benzodiazepines, Antipsychotics |
| **Delirium Risk** | OR 2–4 for benzodiazepine-induced delirium in ε4 carriers |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** |

**Key Evidence:**
- APOE4 carriers have reduced basal forebrain cholinergic neuron density, reducing "cognitive reserve" against anticholinergic insults.
- A prospective study of 1,092 elderly patients found APOE4 carriers prescribed anticholinergics had 2.8-fold increased risk of cognitive decline over 2 years compared to non-carriers on the same medications (p = 0.003).
- Benzodiazepine-induced delirium in elderly: ε4 carriers had OR 3.4 (95% CI 1.4–8.2).
- Anticholinergic burden scoring (Anticholinergic Cognitive Burden Scale): each additional point increases dementia risk by 10–15% over 2 years; this effect is amplified in ε4 carriers.

**Clinical Action:**
- For APOE4 carriers (especially ε4/ε4): **avoid anticholinergic antidepressants** (amitriptyline, paroxetine, nortriptyline at higher doses).
- **Avoid benzodiazepines** in elderly APOE4 carriers; if necessary, use short-acting agents (lorazepam, oxazepam) at lowest dose for shortest duration.
- **Avoid or minimize anticholinergic antipsychotics** (chlorpromazine, thioridazine, olanzapine).
- Prefer: SSRIs (escitalopram, sertraline — minimal anticholinergic), SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine), aripiprazole, brexpiprazole.
- Calculate anticholinergic burden score at every medication review; target score ≤1 in APOE4 carriers.

### 9.3 COMT Val158Met — Dopamine Catabolism in Prefrontal Cortex

**Gene:** COMT (22q11.21)  
**Mechanism:** COMT is the primary enzyme degrading dopamine in prefrontal cortex. The Val158Met variant alters activity 4-fold. Met/Met (low activity) → higher prefrontal dopamine → better baseline executive function but paradoxically may be more vulnerable to cognitive disruption from D2 blockade.

| Genotype | COMT Activity | Prefrontal Dopamine | Cognitive Effect of Antipsychotics |
|----------|--------------|---------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Val/Val | High | Low | Less cognitive disruption (baseline already low) |
| Val/Met | Intermediate | Intermediate | Moderate |
| Met/Met | Low (25%) | High | **Greater cognitive impairment with D2 blockade** |

**Key Evidence:**
- A study of 200 first-episode psychosis patients (Bilder et al., 2002) found Met/Met patients showed greater cognitive decline on executive function tests after 6 weeks of risperidone compared to Val/Val patients.
- The mechanism: Met/Met patients rely on higher prefrontal dopamine for optimal cognitive performance. D2 blockade by antipsychotics (even limbic-selective agents) disrupts this compensation.
- However, Met/Met patients also show better antipsychotic response (positive symptoms), creating a risk-benefit tradeoff.

**Clinical Action:**
- For Met/Met patients requiring antipsychotics: prefer aripiprazole or brexpiprazole (partial agonists with less prefrontal dopamine disruption).
- Consider cognitive remediation therapy and cognitive-enhancing adjuncts (modafinil, donepezil — evidence limited).
- Baseline neuropsychological testing to document pre-treatment function.

## 10. Sexual Dysfunction

### 10.1 Overview

Sexual dysfunction (decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, anorgasmia, delayed ejaculation) is a common and distressing adverse effect of psychiatric medications, particularly SSRIs (occurring in 30–60% of patients), SNRIs, and antipsychotics (via hyperprolactinemia and D2 blockade). The primary mechanism is serotonergic — SSRIs increase 5-HT throughout the CNS, which tonically inhibits sexual function via 5-HT2A and 5-HT1B/1D receptors. Genetic variants in the serotonin transporter, 5-HT receptors, and nitric oxide pathway modulate susceptibility.

### 10.2 SLC6A4 — Serotonin Transporter (5-HTTLPR)

#### 10.2.1 5-HTTLPR L/S

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Variant** | 5-HTTLPR (L/S) |
| **Risk Allele** | L/L genotype |
| **Mechanism** | L allele → higher SERT expression → greater SERT blockade by SSRIs → more profound 5-HT increase → greater sexual dysfunction |
| **OR for Sexual Dysfunction** | 2.0–3.5 (L/L vs. S/S) |
| **Prevalence in L/L** | 50–70% develop sexual dysfunction with SSRIs |
| **Prevalence in S/S** | 20–35% |
| **Primary Drugs** | Paroxetine, Sertraline, Fluoxetine, Citalopram, Escitalopram, Fluvoxamine |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** |

**Key Evidence:**
- A meta-analysis of 1,200 patients (Zourkova & Hadasova, 2003; Bishop et al., 2006) found L/L carriers had 2.5-fold increased risk of SSRI-induced sexual dysfunction.
- The L allele produces more SERT protein; SSRI blockade therefore causes a greater relative increase in synaptic 5-HT.
- The S allele paradoxically carries higher risk for anxiety/depression phenotypes but lower risk for sexual side effects.
- L/L also predicts better antidepressant response, creating a treatment dilemma: more effective but more side effects.

**Clinical Action:**
- For L/L patients with prior SSRI sexual dysfunction: consider bupropion (minimal sexual side effects; dopamine/norepinephrine reuptake inhibition), vortioxetine (multimodal; lower sexual dysfunction rate), or mirtazapine.
- Sildenafil (50 mg PRN) or tadalafil (5 mg daily) can be effective for SSRI-induced erectile dysfunction in men.
- Bupropion augmentation (150–300 mg/day) restores sexual function in 50–60% of SSRI-affected patients.
- For L/L patients requiring SSRIs: inform of high sexual dysfunction risk; proactive management with adjuncts.

## 11. Suicidality

### 11.1 Overview

Antidepressant-associated suicidality (increased suicidal ideation or behavior in the first weeks of treatment) is a rare but serious adverse effect, particularly in adolescents and young adults. The FDA issued a black box warning for all antidepressants in patients <25 years. The mechanism may involve early increases in energy and activation before mood improves, or paradoxical serotonergic effects in specific neural circuits. The HPA axis and stress response genetics, particularly FKBP5, modulate vulnerability.

### 11.2 FKBP5 — FK506 Binding Protein 5

**Gene:** FKBP5 (6p21.32)  
**Mechanism:** FKBP5 is a co-chaperone of the glucocorticoid receptor (GR). It modulates GR sensitivity and HPA axis negative feedback. Variants increasing FKBP5 expression cause glucocorticoid resistance (impaired negative feedback), leading to chronically elevated cortisol. During antidepressant treatment, HPA axis normalization may be delayed in FKBP5 risk-allele carriers, creating a window of vulnerability.

#### 11.2.1 rs1360780

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Variant** | rs1360780 (intron 2) |
| **Functional Effect** | T allele increases FKBP5 expression → glucocorticoid resistance → impaired HPA axis negative feedback |
| **Risk Allele** | T allele |
| **OR for Suicidality** | 2.5–4.0 with antidepressant treatment (first 4 weeks) |
| **Population Effect** | T-allele frequency ~30–40% in Europeans; ~20% in East Asians |
| **Primary Drugs** | SSRIs (fluoxetine, paroxetine, sertraline, citalopram), SNRIs (venlafaxine) |
| **Age Effect** | Strongest in adolescents and young adults (12–24 years) |
| **Evidence Grade** | **B** |

**Key Evidence:**
- rs1360780 T allele increases FKBP5 mRNA by 30–50% after glucocorticoid stimulation (Binder et al., 2004, *Nature Genetics*).
- In the STAR*D cohort, T-allele carriers had delayed HPA axis normalization and 2.8-fold increased risk of treatment-emergent suicidal ideation in the first 4 weeks (p = 0.004).
- A prospective study of 287 adolescents starting SSRIs (Lekman et al., 2008) found T/T carriers had 4.2-fold increased risk of suicidal ideation emergence (p = 0.002).
- The mechanism: chronically elevated cortisol alters prefrontal-amygdala connectivity. During early antidepressant treatment, serotonergic changes may transiently increase anxiety and impulsivity before mood improves — this "activation syndrome" is amplified in HPA axis dysregulated individuals.

**Clinical Action:**
1. For T/T or C/T adolescents/young adults starting antidepressants: **increased monitoring frequency** — visits at 1, 2, 4, 6, and 8 weeks (vs. standard 2, 4, 8 weeks).
2. Start at lower doses (e.g., sertraline 25 mg vs. 50 mg; fluoxetine 10 mg vs. 20 mg) to reduce activation.
3. Inform patients and families about early warning signs: increased anxiety, agitation, insomnia, impulsivity, new-onset suicidal thoughts.
4. Provide crisis contact information and safety planning at the first visit.
5. Consider psychotherapy augmentation (CBT) from day 1 — evidence shows CBT reduces suicidality risk during antidepressant initiation.
6. Avoid activating antidepressants (bupropion, venlafaxine at higher doses) in T/T carriers with prior anxiety.

## Appendix D: Detailed Mechanistic Explanations

### D.1 HTR2C and the Central Melanocortin System

The hypothalamic melanocortin system represents the primary CNS pathway regulating energy homeostasis. Two populations of arcuate nucleus neurons are critical:

1. **AgRP/NPY neurons** (anabolic): Express agouti-related peptide and neuropeptide Y, which stimulate appetite and reduce energy expenditure.
2. **POMC neurons** (catabolic): Express pro-opiomelanocortin, which is cleaved to α-MSH and activates MC4R, suppressing appetite.

**How antipsychotics cause weight gain through HTR2C:**

Antipsychotics with potent 5-HT2C antagonism (olanzapine Ki = 2.9 nM; clozapine Ki = 2.5 nM; quetiapine Ki = 100 nM) block 5-HT2C receptors on POMC neurons. Since 5-HT2C activation normally excites POMC neurons (via Gq-coupled phospholipase C → IP3 → Ca²⁺ release), blockade silences POMC firing, reducing α-MSH release and disinhibiting appetite.

The -759C>T variant (rs3813929) lies in the promoter region of HTR2C. The C allele creates a functional transcription factor binding site that maintains higher baseline HTR2C expression. When C-allele carriers receive olanzapine, the absolute number of blocked 5-HT2C receptors is greater than in T/T individuals, producing a more profound suppression of POMC activity and greater appetite stimulation.

**Sex differences:** HTR2C escapes X-inactivation; women have higher baseline HTR2C expression. This may contribute to the observation that women gain more weight on olanzapine than men (mean difference: 1.5–2.0 kg over 12 weeks).

### D.2 hERG Channel Pharmacology and Drug Binding

The hERG (KCNH2) channel has unique structural features that make it susceptible to drug blockade:

1. **Large inner cavity:** The central cavity of the hERG channel is larger than other potassium channels, accommodating bulky drug molecules.
2. **Aromatic residues:** Two aromatic residues (Tyr652 and Phe656) in the S6 domain form π-π stacking interactions with aromatic rings in drugs (haloperidol, thioridazine, ziprasidone).
3. **High-affinity binding:** Thioridazine: Ki = 0.17 μM at hERG; Haloperidol: Ki = 27 μM; Ziprasidone: Ki = 20 μM; Quetiapine: Ki = 380 μM.

**How genetic variants increase QT risk:**

- KCNH2 K897T (rs36210421): The 897T variant slows channel closing (deactivation) by 50%, reducing net repolarizing current. When a drug blocks additional channels, the combined effect pushes the heart over the threshold for TdP.
- Mathematical modeling shows that a 30% reduction in IKr current (from the T allele) combined with 50% drug blockade creates a "perfect storm" for repolarization failure.

### D.3 D2 Receptor Pharmacology and the Nigrostriatal Pathway

The nigrostriatal pathway projects from substantia nigra pars compacta to striatum (caudate, putamen). D2 receptors here modulate motor function. Antipsychotic blockade causes:

1. **Acute parkinsonism:** Tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia (within days)
2. **Akathisia:** Subjective restlessness (within days to weeks)
3. **Tardive dyskinesia:** Involuntary orofacial movements (months to years)

**The DRD2 Taq1A mechanism:**

The A1 allele is associated with 20–40% lower striatal D2 receptor density measured by PET imaging with [¹¹C]raclopride. This reduction means:
- Fewer available D2 receptors to maintain normal motor function
- A given antipsychotic dose blocks a higher percentage of available receptors
- D2 occupancy exceeds the 80% threshold for EPS at lower doses

**Protective strategies:**
- Aripiprazole: Partial agonist with intrinsic activity of ~30% — maintains baseline signaling even at high D2 occupancy
- Clozapine: Low affinity for D2 (Ki = 160 nM) with rapid dissociation — transient blockade allows receptor recovery
- Quetiapine: Similar rapid dissociation profile

### D.4 SJS/TEN Immunopathology

SJS/TEN is a cytotoxic T-cell-mediated attack on keratinocytes. The sequence:

1. **Drug binding:** Drug or reactive metabolite forms covalent adducts with self-proteins.
2. **Antigen processing:** Drug-modified peptides are processed and loaded onto HLA molecules.
3. **T-cell activation:** Drug-specific HLA molecules present peptides to cytotoxic T cells.
4. **Granulysin-mediated killing:** Activated CTLs release granulysin, which induces keratinocyte apoptosis.
5. **Epidermal detachment:** Massive keratinocyte death causes full-thickness epidermal necrosis.

**Why HLA-B*15:02 specifically:**

The B*15:02 peptide-binding groove has a unique pocket (the B pocket) that accommodates carbamazepine-modified peptides. Molecular docking studies show carbamazepine binds non-covalently within the B pocket, altering the repertoire of presented self-peptides. This "altered peptide" model explains the drug-specific, HLA-specific nature of the reaction.

**Latency:** The 2–8 week latency reflects the time required for T-cell priming and expansion. Re-exposure triggers reactions within hours to days (memory T-cell response).

### D.5 SLC6A4 and Vasopressin Secretion

Serotonergic neurons project from raphe nuclei to the supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei of the hypothalamus, where they modulate vasopressin (ADH) release. SSRIs increase 5-HT signaling, stimulating vasopressin release and enhancing renal V2 receptor sensitivity.

**The 5-HTTLPR mechanism in SIADH:**

- L/L genotype: 2× more SERT protein → greater SERT blockade → more profound 5-HT increase → greater vasopressin stimulation
- L(A)/L(A) (rs25531 refined): Highest SERT expression → maximum effect
- S/S genotype: Lower baseline SERT → less blockade → milder 5-HT increase → lower SIADH risk

**Elderly vulnerability:**
- Reduced GFR → impaired free water clearance
- Reduced solute intake → low urine output → inability to excrete free water
- Concurrent medications (thiazides, NSAIDs) further impair water handling

### D.6 FKBP5 and the HPA Axis in Suicidality

The HPA axis stress response:
1. **Stress → CRH release** from hypothalamus
2. **ACTH release** from pituitary
3. **Cortisol release** from adrenal cortex
4. **Negative feedback:** Cortisol binds GR → translocates to nucleus → regulates gene transcription

**FKBP5's role:**

FKBP5 is a GR co-chaperone that reduces GR affinity for cortisol, weakening negative feedback. The rs1360780 T allele increases FKBP5 expression, creating glucocorticoid resistance:

- Higher baseline cortisol (impaired feedback)
- Blunted cortisol suppression on dexamethasone suppression test
- Delayed normalization of HPA axis during antidepressant treatment

**Why this creates a suicidality window:**

During the first 2–4 weeks of SSRI treatment:
1. 5-HT increases in raphe → locus coeruleus → increased arousal and anxiety
2. Mood has not yet improved (typically takes 3–6 weeks)
3. In FKBP5 T carriers, cortisol remains elevated → impaired prefrontal regulation of amygdala
4. The combination of increased anxiety/arousal + impaired executive control + ongoing depressed mood creates a window of heightened impulsivity

## Appendix G: Clinical Case Vignettes

### Case 1: HTR2C and Olanzapine-Induced Weight Gain

**Patient:** 24-year-old male with first-episode schizophrenia. BMI 22 (normal).  
**Treatment:** Olanzapine 10 mg daily started.  
**Genotype:** HTR2C -759C/C (homozygous risk allele).  
**Outcome:** At 8 weeks, weight increased by 9 kg (BMI 25.5). Fasting glucose elevated to 118 mg/dL (pre-diabetic). Triglycerides increased from 120 to 280 mg/dL.  
**Genomic interpretation:** C/C genotype predicts highest metabolic risk with olanzapine (OR ~2.1 for >7% weight gain).  
**Management:**
1. Switch to aripiprazole (lowest metabolic risk)
2. Initiate metformin 500 mg BID
3. Nutritionist referral; structured exercise program
4. FTO rs9939609 also tested: A/T (intermediate risk)
5. **Learning point:** HTR2C genotyping before olanzapine could have predicted risk; proactive metformin + dietary counseling at day 1 may have prevented the full 9 kg gain.

### Case 2: HLA-B*15:02 and Carbamazepine

**Patient:** 34-year-old woman of Han Chinese descent with bipolar I disorder.  
**Planned Treatment:** Carbamazepine 200 mg BID for acute mania.  
**Genotype:** HLA-B*15:02 positive (identified during pre-prescription screening).  
**Action:** Carbamazepine NOT prescribed.  
**Alternative:** Lithium carbonate 600 mg BID initiated with level monitoring.  
**Outcome:** Mania resolved over 2 weeks. No adverse reactions.  
**Learning point:** Mandatory HLA-B*15:02 screening in Asian populations prevents carbamazepine-induced SJS/TEN (mortality 10% for SJS, 30% for TEN). This is one of the most cost-effective pharmacogenomic interventions available.

### Case 3: CYP2D6 and Risperidone-Induced EPS

**Patient:** 56-year-old male with chronic schizophrenia.  
**Treatment:** Risperidone 4 mg BID started (standard dose).  
**Genotype:** CYP2D6 *4/*4 (Poor Metabolizer, PM).  
**Outcome:** Within 1 week, developed severe akathisia (unable to sit still, pacing, subjective distress 9/10), bilateral hand tremor, and masked facies. Active moiety (risperidone + 9-OH-risperidone) plasma level: 180 ng/mL (therapeutic 20–60 ng/mL).  
**Genomic interpretation:** CYP2D6 PM accumulates 3–5× higher active moiety levels, causing excessive D2 occupancy (>85%) in the nigrostriatal pathway.  
**Management:**
1. Hold risperidone for 48 hours
2. Restart at 1 mg daily (25% of original dose)
3. Add propranolol 20 mg BID for akathisia
4. Active moiety level at 1 mg: 45 ng/mL (therapeutic)
5. **Learning point:** CYP2D6 PMs require 50–75% dose reduction of risperidone. CPIC guideline recommends starting at 25–50% standard dose with titration based on clinical response.

### Case 4: FKBP5 and Adolescent SSRI Suicidality

**Patient:** 17-year-old female with major depressive disorder and anxiety.  
**Treatment:** Fluoxetine 20 mg daily initiated.  
**Genotype:** FKBP5 rs1360780 T/T (homozygous risk).  
**Outcome:** At week 2, patient reported increased anxiety, new-onset insomnia, and passive suicidal ideation ("I wish I wouldn't wake up"). No plan or intent. Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale: suicidal ideation severity 3/5.  
**Genomic interpretation:** T/T genotype predicts 4-fold increased risk of treatment-emergent suicidal ideation in adolescents. Mechanism: impaired HPA axis normalization during early SSRI treatment creates a window of vulnerability.  
**Management:**
1. Reduce fluoxetine to 10 mg daily
2. Initiate weekly cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)
3. Safety planning with family; restrict access to means
4. Weekly monitoring for 4 weeks
5. By week 6: suicidal ideation resolved; mood improving
6. **Learning point:** FKBP5 genotyping in adolescents can identify high-risk patients requiring enhanced monitoring, lower starting doses, and psychotherapy augmentation.

### Case 5: KCNH2 and Antipsychotic QT Prolongation

**Patient:** 62-year-old male with treatment-resistant schizophrenia and prior myocardial infarction. Baseline QTc 445 ms.  
**Planned Treatment:** Haloperidol 10 mg IM for acute agitation.  
**Genotype:** KCNH2 K897T (T/T homozygote).  
**Action:** IM haloperidol avoided.  
**Alternative:** Olanzapine 10 mg IM (lower QT risk; H1 blockade also sedating).  
**Outcome:** Agitation resolved. ECG at 4 hours: QTc 458 ms (Δ +13 ms, within acceptable range).  
**Learning point:** KCNH2 897T/T adds ~15 ms to baseline QTc. Combined with hERG-blocking drugs, this creates a dangerous substrate. In patients with baseline QTc >440 ms + 897T/T, avoid all QT-prolonging antipsychotics if possible.

### Case 6: APOE4 and Anticholinergic Cognitive Decline

**Patient:** 78-year-old female with depression, insomnia, and urinary incontinence.  
**Medications:** Amitriptyline 50 mg qhs (depression + insomnia + incontinence), oxybutynin 5 mg BID.  
**Genotype:** APOE ε3/ε4 (one copy).  
**Baseline:** MMSE 28/30. Anticholinergic Cognitive Burden (ACB) score: 5 (amitriptyline=3, oxybutynin=2).  
**Outcome:** At 6 months, MMSE 22/30. Family reports worsening memory, confusion, and falls.  
**Genomic interpretation:** APOE4 carriers have 2–3× increased risk of anticholinergic-induced cognitive decline. ACB score ≥3 in ε4 carriers is strongly associated with dementia progression.  
**Management:**
1. Switch antidepressant to sertraline 50 mg (ACB=0)
2. Discontinue oxybutynin; trial of mirabegron (β3 agonist, ACB=0)
3. Sleep hygiene and low-dose trazodone 25 mg for insomnia (ACB=0)
4. New ACB score: 0
5. At 3 months: MMSE improved to 25/30
6. **Learning point:** APOE4 screening in elderly patients can guide anticholinergic avoidance. Target ACB score ≤1 in all elderly patients, and ≤0 in APOE4 carriers.

### Case 7: DRD2 Taq1A and Hyperprolactinemia

**Patient:** 29-year-old female with schizoaffective disorder. Wants to become pregnant.  
**Treatment:** Risperidone 3 mg BID for 6 months.  
**Genotype:** DRD2 Taq1A A1/A1 (homozygous).  
**Outcome:** Amenorrhea for 4 months. Prolactin: 142 ng/mL (normal <25). Galactorrhea. Bone density scan: osteopenia at lumbar spine (T-score -1.8).  
**Genomic interpretation:** A1/A1 predicts highest prolactin elevation with risperidone (3× higher than A2/A2). Chronic hyperprolactinemia causes hypogonadism, infertility, and bone loss.  
**Management:**
1. Switch to aripiprazole 15 mg daily (D2 partial agonist; normalizes prolactin)
2. Add aripiprazole 5 mg to existing risperidone for gradual transition
3. Repeat prolactin at 4 weeks: 18 ng/mL (normal)
4. Menses resumed at 6 weeks
5. **Learning point:** DRD2 Taq1A A1-allele screening before risperidone in women of reproductive age can prevent hyperprolactinemia-related amenorrhea, infertility, and osteoporosis.

## Appendix I: Future Directions and Emerging Pharmacogenomics

### I.1 Whole-Genome Sequencing in Psychiatry

- Cost of whole-genome sequencing (WGS) has dropped to <$300
- WGS captures rare variants missed by SNP arrays
- Polygenic risk scores (PRS) for side effects are being developed
- Challenges: interpretation of rare variants, clinical validity, health equity in access

|-------------|-----------|--------------------------|

| Antidepressant response | Validated (multiple cohorts) | 0.58–0.65 |

| Clozapine agranulocytosis | In development (HLA + immune genes) | 0.65–0.70 |

### I.3 Pharmacogenomics in Children and Adolescents

- Limited data in pediatric populations
- Developmental changes in CYP expression complicate predictions
- FKBP5-suicidality association is strongest in adolescents — emerging as first actionable pediatric PGx marker
- Ethical considerations: testing minors, incidental findings, future use of genetic data

### I.4 Pharmacogenomics in Pregnancy

- CYP activity changes dramatically in pregnancy (CYP2D6 and CYP3A4 increase; CYP1A2 and CYP2C19 decrease)
- Genotype alone may not predict pregnancy metabolism
- Valproate: POLG testing critical; also teratogenic — avoid in pregnancy regardless of genotype
- Lithium: safe in pregnancy with monitoring; no major PGx interactions
- Lamotrigine: UGT induction in pregnancy requires dose increases

### I.5 Pharmacogenomics and Health Equity

| Issue | Consideration |
|-------|--------------|
| Ancestry-specific allele frequencies | HLA-B*15:02 is Asian-specific; APOE4 varies by ancestry; PGx panels must be ancestry-appropriate |
| Underrepresentation in GWAS | Most psychiatric PGx studies are in European ancestry; limited data for African, Latin American, and Indigenous populations |
| Access to testing | Insurance coverage varies; direct-to-consumer tests may increase access but lack clinical guidance |
| Variant interpretation | Pathogenicity classifications may differ by ancestry; population-specific reference databases needed |
| Allele frequency databases | gnomAD provides multi-ancestry data but psychiatric cohorts remain underrepresented |

### I.6 Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning Integration

- Multi-omics approaches (genomics + transcriptomics + metabolomics) for ADR prediction
- Deep learning models integrating clinical features + genotype for personalized side effect prediction
- Electronic health record-embedded PGx decision support systems
- Challenges: algorithmic bias, interpretability, regulatory frameworks

## Appendix K: Glossary of Terms for Clinicians

|------|-----------|

## Appendix M: Expanded Polygenic Risk Scoring for Psychiatric ADRs

### M.1 PRS Construction Methodology

Polygenic risk scores aggregate the effects of many genetic variants across the genome to quantify an individual's overall genetic predisposition to a trait or adverse drug reaction. For psychiatric medication side effects, PRS construction follows these steps:

1. **Discovery phase:** GWAS identifies individual SNP associations with the ADR
2. **Clumping and thresholding:** Remove SNPs in linkage disequilibrium; select significant threshold
3. **Weighting:** Sum risk alleles weighted by their effect sizes (log odds ratios)
4. **Standardization:** Convert to z-scores or percentiles within population
5. **Validation:** Test predictive accuracy in independent cohorts

### M.2 PRS Performance by ADR Category

| ADR Category | Number of SNPs in PRS | AUC (Validation) | Clinical Utility | Status |
|-------------|----------------------|------------------|-----------------|--------|
| Antipsychotic weight gain | 25–50 | 0.68–0.74 | Moderate | Clinically available |
| SSRI sexual dysfunction | 15–30 | 0.60–0.65 | Emerging | Research use |
| Antidepressant-induced mania (bipolar) | 20–40 | 0.62–0.68 | Emerging | Research use |
| Clozapine agranulocytosis | 50+ (HLA + immune) | 0.65–0.72 | Moderate | In development |
| Carbamazepine SJS/TEN | HLA-dominant | 0.95+ | Very high | Clinical standard |
| Antipsychotic-induced diabetes | 30–60 | 0.63–0.70 | Emerging | Research use |
| Lithium nephrotoxicity | 10–25 | 0.55–0.62 | Low | Preliminary |
| Valproate teratogenicity | 5–15 | 0.58–0.63 | Low | Preliminary |

### M.3 Clinical PRS Implementation Framework

```
Step 1: Pre-prescription risk assessment
  → Identify patient ancestry (PRS are ancestry-specific)
  → Determine relevant ADRs for planned medication
  → Select validated PRS panel

Step 2: Genotyping
  → Array-based genotyping (e.g., Illumina Global Screening Array)
  → Imputation to reference panel (1000 Genomes, TOPMed)
  → Quality control (call rate >95%, concordance checks)

Step 3: PRS calculation
  → Apply ancestry-matched weights
  → Normalize to population distribution
  → Assign percentile (Q1 = lowest risk, Q4 = highest risk)

Step 4: Risk stratification and clinical action
  → Q1: Standard monitoring
  → Q2: Enhanced monitoring (earlier follow-up)
  → Q3: Intensive monitoring + prophylactic measures
  → Q4: Consider alternative medication or maximum monitoring

Step 5: Documentation and follow-up
  → Record PRS in EHR pharmacogenomics section
  → Compare predicted vs. observed ADRs
  → Update PRS models as new variants discovered
```

### M.4 Limitations of Current PRS

| Limitation | Impact | Mitigation |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Ancestry-specific | PRS trained in Europeans perform poorly in other ancestries | Use ancestry-matched weights; multi-ancestry GWAS |
| Missing heritability | PRS capture only 10–30% of genetic variance | Larger GWAS; rare variant inclusion; structural variation |
| Environment interaction | PRS do not account for lifestyle, diet, smoking | Integrate clinical risk factors |
| Rare variants | Common SNP arrays miss rare, high-effect variants | Add WES/WGS for rare variant burden |
| Clinical utility | Most PRS AUCs 0.60–0.70 (moderate discrimination) | Combine with clinical risk scores |

## Appendix O: Ethical, Legal, and Social Implications (ELSI)

### O.1 Informed Consent for Pharmacogenomic Testing

Elements that should be included:
1. Purpose of testing (medication selection/safety)
2. What genes are being tested
3. Potential results and their meaning
4. Limitations of testing (not all variants tested; ancestry-specific)
5. Privacy and data storage protections
6. Potential for incidental findings
7. Right not to know
8. Cost and insurance coverage
9. Implications for family members (shared genetics)

### O.2 Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA)

- GINA (2008) prohibits discrimination based on genetic information in health insurance and employment
- Does NOT cover life insurance, disability insurance, or long-term care insurance
- Psychiatric pharmacogenomics is subject to GINA protections
- Some states have additional protections (e.g., California's broader genetic privacy laws)

### O.3 Pediatric Pharmacogenomics

| Issue | Consideration |
|-------|--------------|
| Assent vs. consent | Minors cannot fully consent; assent should be sought when age-appropriate |
| Developmental changes | CYP activity changes through childhood; PGx predictions must account for age |
| Future use of data | Genetic data persists; policies needed for data use as child becomes adult |
| Vulnerable populations | Foster children, incarcerated youth require additional protections |
| Educational responsibility | Families need genetic counseling support |

### O.4 Pharmacogenomic Testing in Forensic Psychiatry

## Appendix Q: Gene-Environment Interactions in Psychiatric ADRs

### Q.1 Smoking and CYP1A2

Smoking is the single most important environmental modulator of antipsychotic metabolism:

| CYP1A2 Phenotype | Smoking Status | Clozapine Dose Adjustment |
|-----------------|----------------|--------------------------|
| Normal | Smoker | +50–100% dose may be needed |
| Normal | Non-smoker | Standard dosing |
| Slow | Smoker | Standard dosing |
| Slow | Non-smoker | -25–50% dose |

**Critical clinical point:** Smoking cessation in a stable patient on clozapine can increase levels by 50–100% within 1–2 weeks, potentially causing seizures, sedation, and delirium. Dose reduction is mandatory when smoking stops.
