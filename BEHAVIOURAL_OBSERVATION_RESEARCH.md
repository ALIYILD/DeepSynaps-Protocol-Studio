## 1. Executive Summary

This report provides an evidence-based review of behavioural observation frameworks suitable for clinical video assessment across multiple domains: autism spectrum disorder (ASD), developmental disorders, social communication deficits, psychomotor slowing, and nonverbal communication impairment. All frameworks described have established psychometric properties and are supported by peer-reviewed research. This document explicitly excludes pseudoscientific approaches such as micro-expression analysis and unvalidated emotion-detection claims.

The reviewed instruments represent the current gold standard in clinical behavioural observation, with documented reliability, validity, and clinical utility. Where video-based applications exist, these are noted with appropriate caveats regarding interpretation and clinical supervision.

**Key findings:**
- The ADOS-2 remains the gold standard observational assessment for ASD, with validated video-based adaptations
- The BOSCC provides a validated framework for measuring social communication change over time
- Joint attention behaviours, coded via the ESCS or JA Protocol, represent well-established observable biomarkers
- Psychomotor retardation can be assessed through validated clinical scales (SRRS, CORE) and objective actigraphy
- Gesture analysis requires structured coding schemes with demonstrated inter-rater reliability
- All video-based behavioural observation requires clinician oversight; no automated system replaces clinical judgment

## 2. Autism Behavioural Observation Frameworks

### 2.1 ADOS-2 (Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule, 2nd Edition)

**Authors:** Lord et al. (2012)
**Domain:** Standardised observational assessment for ASD
**Age Range:** Module 1 (non-verbal/limited speech) through Module 4 (fluent adolescents/adults)

#### Description and Structure

The ADOS-2 is a semi-structured, standardised assessment of communication, social interaction, play, and imaginative use of materials. It involves a series of planned "presses" (structured activities designed to elicit social communicative behaviours) administered by a trained examiner. The assessment is video-recorded to enable reliability checking and secondary coding.

The ADOS-2 algorithm yields scores across two domains:
- **Social Affect (SA):** Social communication behaviours including quality of eye contact, facial expressions, gestures, shared enjoyment, and conversation
- **Restricted and Repetitive Behaviours (RRB):** Stereotyped behaviours, compulsions, circumscribed interests, and sensory sensitivities

#### Video Recording Protocol

Clinical guidelines specify:
- Digital video camera with external directional microphone placed approximately 0.5 metres from the participant
- Camera positioned slightly outside the participant's direct line of sight to minimise distraction
- Recording at minimum 30 frames per second with adequate resolution for facial expression and gesture coding
- Two-examiner model: one administering, one observing and keeping records
- Consensus discussion between examiners to reach final ratings

#### Psychometric Properties

- **Inter-rater reliability:** ICC values for SA and Total scores demonstrate high reliability (ICC > 0.85) when coders are research-reliable trained
- **Internal consistency:** SA items show high internal coherence; RRB items are adequate but more variable
- **Validity:** Calibrated scores correlate with independent clinical DSM-5 diagnostic formulations; SA and RRB domains show independent distribution consistent with DSM-5 two-domain model

#### Video-Based Applications

Research has demonstrated that ADOS-2 assessments can be coded reliably from video recordings. Studies at multiple sites (e.g., Caltech, West Virginia University) have used video recordings of ADOS-2 Module 4 administrations (720x480 to 1920x1080 resolution) for:
- Automated gaze pattern analysis via computer vision algorithms
- Machine learning classification of ASD-related behaviours
- Inter-rater reliability studies with blinded secondary coders

**Clinical note:** Video-based ADOS-2 scoring should only be performed by ADOS-2 research-reliable clinicians. Automated behavioural analysis from video represents a research tool, not a diagnostic instrument.

#### Modified ADOS-2 for Special Populations

Research demonstrates feasibility of modifying the ADOS-2 for populations with visual impairment (VI), with exclusion of vision-dependent items and retention of social communication and RRB items that do not require visual assessment. Modified versions require independent psychometric validation before clinical use.

### 2.2 ADI-R (Autism Diagnostic Interview-Revised)

**Authors:** Lord, Rutter & Le Couteur
**Domain:** Standardised parent/caregiver interview for ASD diagnostic history
**Age Range:** Individuals with mental age from 18 months through adulthood

#### Description and Structure

The ADI-R is a comprehensive, semi-structured diagnostic interview conducted with a parent or caregiver. It covers three domains corresponding to DSM-5 ASD criteria:
- **Domain A:** Qualitative abnormalities in reciprocal social interaction
- **Domain B:** Qualitative abnormalities in communication
- **Domain C:** Restricted, repetitive, and stereotyped patterns of behaviour

The interview requires 1.5-2.5 hours to administer and is typically video-recorded for reliability monitoring.

#### Psychometric Properties

- **Sensitivity:** 99.27% (98.42-99.73%) for ASD vs. non-ASD
- **Specificity:** 84.75% (81.08-87.96%) for ASD vs. non-ASD; 99.55% for ASD vs. typically developing
- **Positive Predictive Value:** 92.33% (90.63-93.75%)
- **Cohen's Kappa:** 0.868 (p < 0.001) agreement with clinical best-estimate diagnosis
- **Inter-rater reliability:** 43.4% of video-recorded sessions independently double-rated; 10% reviewed in consensus meetings

#### Video Administration

Video recording of ADI-R sessions enables:
- Inter-rater reliability monitoring
- Consensus coding for ambiguous responses
- Training and calibration of new examiners
- Longitudinal comparison of parent report changes

**Clinical note:** The ADI-R relies on caregiver report and cannot be scored from direct observation alone. Video recording captures the interview process but the scoring is based on reported developmental history, not real-time behaviour.

### 2.3 CARS-2 (Childhood Autism Rating Scale, 2nd Edition)

**Authors:** Schopler, Van Bourgondien, Wellman & Love (2010)
**Domain:** Behavioural rating scale for ASD identification and severity
**Age Range:** 2 years through adulthood

#### Description and Structure

The CARS-2 comprises two forms:
- **CARS2-ST (Standard Form):** For individuals aged 2+ years below average IQ or below age 6
- **CARS2-HF (High Functioning):** For individuals aged 6+ years with IQ above 80

The 15-item scale is rated by a clinician based on direct observation and parent interview. Each item is rated 1 (normal for age) to 4 (severely abnormal), yielding total scores from 15-60.

**CARS2-ST cut-off scores:**
- < 30: Non-autism
- 30-36.5: Mild to moderate autism
- >= 37: Severe autism

**CARS2-HF cut-off scores:**
- <= 27.5: Non-autism
- 28-33.5: Moderate autism
- >= 34: Severe autism

#### Psychometric Properties

- **Internal consistency:** Cronbach's alpha = 0.932 (ST), 0.907 (HF)
- **Test-retest reliability:** r = 0.994 (ST), r = 0.996 (HF) at 3-week interval
- **Inter-rater reliability:** Pearson's r = 0.991 (ST), r = 0.901 (HF); ICC = 0.98 (ST), 0.95 (HF)
- **Factor structure:** CARS2-ST shows one dominant factor (social interaction/communication); CARS2-HF shows three-factor structure

#### Video-Based Scoring

The CARS-2 can be scored from video recordings when both observation of the individual and parent interview data are available. Studies in cross-cultural contexts (Iran, Korea) have validated video-assisted CARS-2 administration with equivalent psychometric properties.

**CARS-2 Items (Observable from Video):**
1. Relating to people
2. Imitation
3. Emotional response
4. Body use
5. Object use
6. Adaptation to change
7. Visual response
8. Listening response
9. Taste, smell, and touch response
10. Fear or nervousness
11. Verbal communication
12. Nonverbal communication
13. Activity level
14. Level and consistency of intellectual response
15. General impressions

**Clinical note:** The CARS-2 was developed as a clinical observation tool, and many items (imitation, body use, nonverbal communication, activity level) are directly observable from video recordings. However, comprehensive scoring requires parent-report data for items such as fear/nervousness and adaptation to change.

### 2.4 BOSCC (Brief Observation of Social Communication Change)

**Authors:** Grzadzinski et al. (2016); multiple versions for different language levels
**Domain:** Treatment response measure for social communication change in ASD
**Age Range:** Multiple versions (Minimally Verbal, Phrase Speech/Young Fluent, BOSCC-F1/F2 for older fluent individuals)

#### Description and Structure

The BOSCC is a 12-16 minute video-recorded play interaction between an individual and a play partner. It uses standardised toys designed to offer opportunities for active participation at various play levels. The interacting partner (caregiver, clinician, or research assistant) must be consistent across all timepoints for a given participant.

**Versions:**
- **BOSCC-MV:** Minimally verbal individuals
- **BOSCC-PSYF:** Phrase speech/young fluent (ages ~3-8)
- **BOSCC-F1:** Fluent ages 6-11
- **BOSCC-F2:** Fluent ages 11+

**Scoring:** 15-17 items rated on a 6-point scale (0 = atypical behaviour not present; 5 = atypical behaviour present and significantly impairs functioning). Videos are split into two segments (5-8 minutes each), each watched and coded twice.

**Domains:**
- Items 1-8: Social Communication (SC)
- Items 9-12: Restricted and Repetitive Behaviours (RRB)
- Items 13-15: Mood/disposition indicators (not included in scoring)

#### Psychometric Properties

- **Inter-rater reliability:** Established through blinded coding by trained raters
- **Sensitivity to change:** Demonstrated across multiple intervention studies
- **Cross-context validity:** Comparable scores across home and school contexts
- **Comparison to ADOS:** BOSCC-coded ADOS segments show comparable results to standalone BOSCC administrations
- **Online coding reliability:** Video-based coding from digitised recordings maintains psychometric properties

#### Video Coding Procedures

The BOSCC was designed for video-based coding:
1. Videos are split into two equal segments
2. Each segment is watched and coded twice by blinded raters
3. Coders are blind to treatment condition, timepoint, and participant characteristics
4. Final scores are averaged across segments and coders
5. Primary raters achieve reliability through consensus training with gold-standard coders

**Key advantages of video-based BOSCC:**
- Eliminates real-time scoring demands
- Enables blinding to condition and timepoint
- Allows multiple independent coders for reliability
- Facilitates consensus coding for complex items
- Permits archival analysis for longitudinal studies

### 2.5 BOSA (Brief Observation of Symptoms of Autism)

**Authors:** Lord et al. (2020)
**Domain:** Standardised naturalistic social context for ASD symptom observation
**Age Range:** All ages, with versions for different developmental levels

#### Description and Structure

The BOSA provides a 12-14 minute standardised naturalistic social context between a participant and a familiar adult. It uses the same scoring procedures as the ADOS-2, with activities designed to elicit social communication and restricted/repetitive behaviours.

**Scoring approach:**
- ADOS-2 codes are transferred to a BOSA algorithm specific to the ADOS-2 module scored
- Codes are recoded into binary scores (presence/absence) based on item distributions
- Domain scores for Social Communication (SC) and RRB, plus combined total
- Clinical cut-offs: score of 5 (Module 3), score of 3 (Module 4)

#### Video-Based Administration

Research demonstrates that the BOSA can be:
- Administered and video-recorded in any setting (clinic, home, school)
- Coded reliably from video by ADOS-2-trained clinicians
- Double-coded with blinded independent raters
- Used when full ADOS-2 administration is not feasible

#### BOSA vs. ADOS-2

| Feature | ADOS-2 | BOSA |
|---------|--------|------|
| Administration | Trained examiner | Familiar adult (caregiver) |
| Duration | 40-60 minutes | 12-14 minutes |
| Examiner training | Research-reliable required | Minimal instruction for partner |
| Setting | Clinic/research | Any consistent setting |
| Scoring | Direct or video | Video-based |
| Materials | Standardised kit | Flexible with recommendations |

### 2.6 ESCS (Early Social Communication Scales)

**Authors:** Mundy et al. (2003); Seibert, Hogan & Mundy (1982)
**Domain:** Structured observation of nonverbal communication skills
**Age Range:** 8-30 months (typical development); older with developmental delays

#### Description and Structure

The ESCS is a 15-25 minute videotaped structured observation measure administered by a trained examiner. It includes 25 semi-structured eliciting situations designed to encourage interaction between tester and child.

**Coding domains:**
- **Initiating Joint Attention (IJA):** Behaviences that direct another's attention to an object/event for sharing interest
- **Responding to Joint Attention (RJA):** Following another's gaze or point to share attention
- **Initiating Behavioural Requests (IBR):** Behaviour used to request objects or assistance
- **Responding to Behavioural Requests:** Compliance with requests
- **Social Interaction Behaviours:** Reciprocal social engagement

**Specific behaviours coded from video:**
- IJA Alternate: Alternating gaze between object and examiner
- IJA Point: Pointing to direct attention
- IJA Show: Holding up object to share attention
- RJA Left/Right: Following gaze/point to left or right
- RJA Behind: Following gaze/point to behind
- IBR Appeal: Requesting through vocalisation with eye contact
- IBR Point: Pointing to request
- IBR Give: Giving object to request help

#### Video Coding Procedures

Coding is conducted from videotape using:
- ProcoderDV software for frame-level event coding
- MOOSES (Multiple Option Observation System for Experimental Studies) for data tabulation
- Functional coding: classify behaviour function, determine initiation vs. response, identify specific code
- Frame-level coding for temporal analysis of gaze patterns

**Inter-rater reliability (from validation data):**
- Initiating Joint Attention: r = 0.84
- IJA High level: r = 0.89
- Responding to Joint Attention: r = 0.86
- Initiating Behavioural Requests: r = 0.78
- Initiating Social Interaction: r = 0.91

#### Temporal-Contextual Analysis

High-density video coding studies using frame-level analysis (56 ASD, 58 TD children) have demonstrated:
- Atypical temporal patterns of gaze to examiner's eyes during toy spectacle tasks
- Reduced eye contact contingent on examiner gaze direction
- Altered object-directed attention timing relative to social gaze

### 2.7 SORF (Systematic Observation of Red Flags)

**Authors:** Dow et al. (2016, 2020)
**Domain:** Level 2 ASD screener for toddlers in naturalistic settings
**Age Range:** 18-24 months

#### Description and Structure

The SORF is a practical observational screening tool designed for implementation by non-experts using video-recorded samples of parent-child interactions in the home. It codes 22 red flags (RF) associated with DSM-5 diagnostic criteria on a 4-point scale.

**Optimal 6-item composite (best psychometric performance):**
1. Poor eye gaze directed to faces
2. Limited showing and pointing
3. Limited coordination of nonverbal communication
4. Less interest in people than objects
5. Repetitive use of objects
6. Excessive interest in particular objects, actions, or activities

#### Psychometric Properties

- **Sensitivity:** 94.29% for ASD identification
- **Specificity:** 100% (in validation sample)
- **Positive Predictive Value:** 100%
- **Inter-rater agreement:** 94.87% correlation between two independent clinician raters
- **Kappa:** 0.803 (substantial agreement with final clinical diagnosis)

#### Video-Based Administration

The SORF was specifically designed for video-based coding:
- Parent records brief naturalistic interaction videos at home
- Trained undergraduate research assistants (blind to diagnosis) complete coding
- 4-point scale: 0 = not observed, 1 = slight, 2 = clear symptom, 3 = pronounced symptom
- Scoring includes total score, domain scores (social communication, RRB), and red flag count

## 3. Developmental Observation Frameworks

### 3.1 Bayley Scales of Infant and Toddler Development (BSID)

**Authors:** Bayley (current version: Bayley-4)
**Domain:** Comprehensive developmental assessment
**Age Range:** 16 days to 42 months (Bayley-III)

#### Video-Observable Domains

The BSID assesses five domains, three of which are partially observable from video:

**Motor Skills (Video-Observable):**
- Fine motor: Grasping, manipulative skills, hand-eye coordination
- Gross motor: Sitting, walking, running, jumping, head control
- Administration time for motor subtest only: 10-30 minutes depending on age

**Language (Partially Video-Observable):**
- Receptive language: Following directions, pointing to named objects
- Expressive language: Vocabulary, word combinations

**Cognitive (Partially Video-Observable):**
- Object permanence, means-end understanding, problem-solving with objects

**Administration Protocol:**
- Conducted in specific sequential order
- Requires trained administrator
- Caregiver present but instructed to encourage without interfering
- Testing environment minimised for distractions
- Accurate documentation of behaviours essential

**Video Considerations:**
Video recording of BSID motor subtests enables:
- Frame-by-frame analysis of movement quality
- Review of borderline items
- Inter-rater reliability checking for qualitative motor observations
- Longitudinal comparison of motor skill acquisition

### 3.2 Denver Developmental Screening Test (DDST-II)

**Authors:** Frankenburg & Dodds
**Domain:** Developmental screening across four domains
**Age Range:** 0-6 years

#### Four Developmental Domains

1. **Personal-Social:** Interaction with people, self-care skills, emotional responses (smiling, playing games, independence)
2. **Fine Motor-Adaptive:** Hand-eye coordination, grasping, manipulative skills (reaching, drawing, copying shapes)
3. **Language:** Receptive and expressive communication (hearing, understanding, word use)
4. **Gross Motor:** Large muscle development (sitting, walking, running, jumping)

#### Administration

- Materials needed: Toys, blocks, balls, paper, pencils
- Quiet, comfortable environment required
- Parent/caregiver provides input on home skills
- 15-20 minute administration
- Age-specific item selection (not all items tested)
- Items administered flexibly to maintain cooperation

#### Video Application

Video recording of DDST administration enables:
- Precise observation of milestone achievement
- Frame-level analysis of motor quality
- Identification of subtle qualitative differences in performance
- Review by specialists not present during assessment
- Archival record for longitudinal comparison

### 3.3 Primitive Reflex Assessment

**Domain:** Neurological assessment of infant motor patterns
**Age Range:** Newborn through 6 months (typical); any age for pathological persistence

#### Key Primitive Reflexes Observable from Video

**Oral Reflexes:**
- **Sucking reflex:** Coordination of breathing and swallowing when oral region stimulated
- **Rooting reflex:** Mouth turning toward stimulus on cheek
- **Snout reflex:** Lip puckering after pressure on upper lip

**Postural Reflexes:**
- **Asymmetric tonic neck reflex (ATNR):** "Fencing" posture when head rotated; arm extends on face side, flexes on opposite side
- **Moro reflex:** Startle response to sudden movement - arms abduct and extend, then flex, with cry
- **Palmar grasp:** Hand closing around object placed in palm

**Significance for Video Observation:**
- Absence or abnormality of sucking reflex suggests basal ganglia/brainstem dysfunction
- Weak Moro reflex in preterm infants correlates with delayed motor development
- Persistence of primitive reflexes past 4-6 months predicts cerebral palsy
- Presence of 5+ abnormal reflexes correlates with CP or mental delays

#### Video Recording Protocol

- Supine positioning with neutral head position
- Quiet, warm environment
- Minimal clothing for full body observation
- Camera positioned for full body view
- Consistent lighting for movement clarity

Frame-by-frame analysis enables:

- Observation of symmetry in bilateral movements
- Documentation of compensatory movement patterns
- Quantification of movement frequency and duration

## 4. Social Communication Observation

### 4.1 Joint Attention Markers

**Definition:** Joint attention refers to the sharing of attentional focus with others on objects or events. It encompasses two primary forms observable from video:

**Initiating Joint Attention (IJA):**
- Pointing to direct another's attention to an object/event
- Showing/holding up objects to share interest
- Alternating gaze between object and social partner
- Using vocalisation + gesture to direct attention

**Responding to Joint Attention (RJA):**
- Following a point or gaze direction to a target
- Shifting attention in response to another's attention-directing behaviour
- Coordinated attention between object and social partner

#### Evidence Base

Joint attention has been identified as a pivotal developmental skill and a key early intervention target for ASD. Meta-analytic evidence demonstrates that:
- Joint attention development is delayed in children with ASD
- IJA behaviours show stronger ASD discrimination than RJA
- Joint attention skills at 12-18 months predict later language outcomes
- Joint attention deficits persist across the lifespan in ASD

#### Video Coding Protocols

**ESCS-Based Coding:**
- Frequency counts of IJA and RJA behaviours
- Duration of attention shifts
- Quality ratings (low vs. high level behaviours)
- Inter-rater reliability: r = 0.84-0.91

**JA Protocol (Live-Coded Alternative):**
- 16-item live-coded measure of RJA and IJA
- Designed for clinical settings without video recording
- Requires >= 80% agreement on sequential reliability tapes
- Valid for use with older children who have developmental delays

**High-Density Video Coding:**
- Frame-level coding (30 fps) of gaze transitions
- Temporal analysis of eye-to-object and eye-to-face shifts
- Contextual factors: examiner gaze, toy activation, toy possession

### 4.2 Eye Contact Patterns (Evidence-Based)

**Important note:** This section summarises only evidence-based findings about eye contact from peer-reviewed research. Claims about emotion detection, micro-expressions, or lie detection are excluded as pseudoscientific.

#### Research Findings on Eye Contact in ASD

**Laboratory studies (eye-tracking):**
- Individuals with ASD show reduced gaze to faces compared to controls
- When looking at faces, ASD individuals spend less time on eye region
- Mouth-directed gaze may compensate for reduced eye contact
- Atypical gaze patterns are heterogeneous across but reliable within individuals

**Naturalistic interaction studies (video coding):**
- Reduced eye contact during ADOS-2 Module 4 administration correlates with ADOS social affect scores
- Individuals with ASD do not follow gaze after eye contact as much as typical controls
- Direct gaze does not facilitate action generation in ASD as it does in typical individuals
- Social functions of gaze (e.g., regulating turn-taking) appear partially preserved

**Developmental findings:**
- High-risk infants (siblings of children with ASD) show atypical brain responses to eye contact by 6 months
- Amount of gaze to mouth (not eyes) at 6 months correlates with later verbal development
- Eye contact patterns show developmental changes that differ between ASD and typical trajectories

#### Clinically Observable Eye Contact Features (from Video)

The ADOS-2 "Unusual Eye Contact" item (B1) codes:
- **Score 0:** Appropriate eye contact that is clear, flexible, and socially modulated
- **Score 2:** Inadequately modulated eye contact used to start, end, or regulate social interaction
- Quality dimensions: flexibility, social modulation, contextual appropriateness

#### Video Analysis Considerations

- Eye contact coding requires clear view of both child's and examiner's upper face
- 30 fps minimum recording rate for reliable detection of brief eye contact episodes
- Context matters: eye contact during object manipulation vs. social interaction are coded differently
- Fatigue effects must be considered during extended assessments

**Clinical caveat:** Eye contact patterns alone are not diagnostic of ASD. Cultural norms, anxiety, language processing demands, and other conditions affect eye contact behaviour.

### 4.3 Reciprocal Interaction Patterns

#### Observable Reciprocal Behaviours

**Social engagement (ADOS-2 scored):**
- Quality of social overtures
- Amount of social initiation
- Shared enjoyment in interaction
- Quality of social response

**Conversation (for verbal individuals):**
- Integration of verbal and nonverbal communication
- Reporting of events
- Descriptive, conventional, informational gestures
- Emotional gestures

**Play behaviours:**
- Functional play with objects
- Pretend/creative play
- Turn-taking in play
- Offering/sharing objects

#### Reciprocal Interaction Deficits in ASD

Research demonstrates that reciprocal interaction deficits in ASD are characterised by:
- Reduced frequency of social initiations (frequency-based measures)
- Qualitative differences in social overtures (less coordinated, less socially modulated)
- Reduced shared enjoyment during interaction
- Less integration of verbal and nonverbal communication channels
- Atypical response patterns to social bids from others

### 4.4 Turn-Taking Behaviours

#### Definition and Components

Turn-taking is a fundamental social communication skill involving the coordinated exchange of communicative acts between interaction partners. Observable components include:

**Verbal turn-taking:**
- Appropriate latency between turns (neither too short nor too long)
- Topic maintenance across turns
- Contingent responses to prior speaker's contribution
- Repair behaviours when communication breaks down

**Nonverbal turn-taking:**
- Passing objects in reciprocal play
- Coordinated action sequences (e.g., rolling ball back and forth)
- Gesture-speech coordination during conversation
- Gaze patterns signalling turn transitions

#### Assessment via Video

Turn-taking is assessed in the ADOS-2 (turn-taking tasks with ball and car) and ESCS (turn-taking task). Video coding captures:
- Frequency of reciprocal exchanges
- Quality of contingent responses
- Initiation of turn-taking bids
- Maintenance of reciprocal interaction over multiple exchanges
- Use of gaze and gesture to regulate turn transitions

## 5. Psychomotor Slowing and Fatigue Indicators

### 5.1 Psychomotor Retardation in Depression

**Definition:** Psychomotor retardation (PMR) refers to a clinically observable slowing of physical movement and reduction in cognitive processing speed. It is considered one of the most reliable observable signs of major depressive disorder, particularly melancholic subtypes.

**Prevalence:** Present in approximately 50-75% of patients with MDD; more common in older adults and those with melancholic depression.

#### Observable Signs (Video-Detectable)

**Motor manifestations:**
- Slowed gait (reduced walking speed)
- Reduced stride length
- Increased double limb support time during walking
- Diminished spontaneous gestures
- Reduced facial expressiveness (flat affect)
- Prolonged movement initiation latency
- Decreased overall activity level
- Altered temporal segmentation of movements
- Increased brief repetitive body touching
- Continuous hand-to-head/hand-to-hand touching

**Speech manifestations:**
- Long pauses before answering questions
- Monotonous tone of voice
- Decreased speech volume (soft-spokenness)
- Slowed articulation
- Reduced verbal output (poverty of speech)

**Cognitive/behavioural manifestations:**
- Delayed response to questions
- Difficulty initiating activities
- Extended processing time for simple decisions
- Reduced environmental engagement

#### Clinical Assessment Scales

**Salpetriere Retardation Rating Scale (SRRS):**
- 15 items rated 0-4 (0 = normal, 4 = severe)
- Total score range: 0-60
- Three factors: motor retardation (items 1-5), cognitive retardation (items 7-8), subjective retardation (items 9-13)
- Good convergent validity with HDRS (r = 0.58) and MADRS (r = 0.68)
- Correlates with speech pause time, activity level, and reaction time

**CORE Index:**
- 18 items rated 0-3 (0 = absent, 3 = severe)
- Total score range: 0-54
- Cut-off: 21 for melancholic depression classification
- Three factors: retardation (52% variance), agitation (15% variance), non-interactiveness (5% variance)
- Can be scored in stuporous or mute patients (does not require verbal responses)
- Good structural validity and internal consistency

**Motor Agitation and Retardation Scale (MARS):**
- 19 observable behaviours across five domains: trunk, limbs, eyes, face, voice
- Each item rated 0-4
- Offers rapid clinical assessment
- Does not include non-interactiveness or cognitive slowing items

---

### 5.2 Bradyphrenia Indicators

**Definition:** Bradyphrenia is the cognitive equivalent of bradykinesia - slowness of thinking and information processing. It is a characteristic feature of Parkinson's disease and other frontostriatal disorders.

#### Observable Indicators from Video

**Cognitive processing signs:**
- Delayed comprehension of verbal instructions
- Slowed mental operation performance
- Difficulty with tasks requiring rapid information processing
- Word-finding difficulties
- Inability to follow group conversations
- Difficulty multi-tasking
- Reduced ability to concentrate

**Associated behavioural features:**
- Difficulty starting activities or tasks (appears as low motivation/energy)
- Fatigue from increased cognitive effort
- Disengagement from complex social situations
- Cognitive fatigue requiring extended processing time

#### Research Evidence

Studies using mental-operation tasks (independent of motor response) demonstrate:
- Cognitive impairment in PD becomes evident at higher processing speed demands
- Verbal processing more affected than spatial processing
- Cognitive slowing and motor slowing are significantly correlated (r = 0.42-0.43)
- Slowing is not restricted to motor domain but extends to cognitive mental operations
- Baseline performance at slow speeds is often normal; deficits emerge under speed demands

**Neurobiological basis:** Dysfunction in the striatum and medial premotor cortex, which are involved in both motor control and internally cued cognitive operations.

---

### 5.3 Movement Reduction and Fatigue

#### Observable Movement Parameters

Research using wearable monitoring systems and video analysis has identified measurable movement reductions in depression:

**Hand movements:**
- Reduced speed of hand movements
- Reduced quantity of hand movements
- Positive correlation between depression severity and movement quantity reduction

**Gait parameters:**
- Reduced cadence (steps per minute)
- Longer double limb support time
- Longer swing time
- Reduced gait velocity
- Reduced stride length

**Postural/activity patterns:**
- More time spent in lying position
- Less time in active positions (walking, standing)
- Less time outdoors
- Reduced overall activity levels in late morning, early afternoon, early evening
- Disorganisation of circadian motor activity patterns

#### Assessment Approaches

**Actigraphy:**
- Wearable devices (worn like wristwatches) detect movement along 1-3 axes
- Measures physical activity unobtrusively in everyday life
- Meta-analytic evidence confirms reduced activity in depression
- Correlates with clinical ratings of retardation (r = 0.717) and depression severity (r = 0.66)

**Handwriting-based measures:**
- Digital tablet recording of handwriting movements
- Measures: velocity, velocity scaling, initiation time, movement time
- Excellent internal consistency for velocity measures (>= 0.90)
- Velocity scaling between small and large movements detects motor slowing

**Kinematic analysis of drawing tasks:**
- Computerised recording of geometric figure copying
- Motor slowing affects both motor and cognitive processes
- Melancholic patients more severely affected than non-melancholic
- Internally cued movements more affected than externally cued reactions

**Eye movement assessment:**
- Normal reflexive saccades in depression
- Normal latencies and velocities for voluntary saccades but increased durations
- Reduced accuracy for memory-guided saccades
- Melancholic patients show greater intrasubject variability of latencies and lower peak velocities

---

### 5.4 Facial Movement and Expression Changes

#### Hypomimia (Reduced Facial Expressiveness)

Hypomimia refers to diminished facial expressiveness and is observed in:
- Parkinson's disease (mask-like facies)
- Major depressive disorder (flat affect)
- Negative symptoms of schizophrenia

**Observable features from video:**
- Reduced frequency of spontaneous facial expressions
- Diminished range of expression (reduced amplitude)
- Prolonged expression latency
- Reduced facial mobility during conversation
- Monotonous expression across different emotional contexts

**Electromyographic findings:**
- Depressed subjects show significantly reduced electromyographic patterns for happiness generation
- Retardation is detectable even when trained clinicians cannot identify obvious clinical signs
- This suggests subclinical motor slowing detectable with instrumentation

## 6. Nonverbal Communication Observation

### 6.1 Gesture Production and Comprehension

#### Gesture Definition for Clinical Observation

For research and clinical purposes, gesture is defined as a symbolic communicative hand movement that does not involve direct manipulation of objects. Exceptions include "hold-up" gestures where the child shows an object to share attention.

#### Gesture Types Observable from Video

**Deictic gestures:**
- Pointing (to direct attention or request)
- Showing/holding up objects
- Reaching

**Representational gestures:**
- Iconic gestures (depicting action or object)
- Symbolic gestures (conventionalised gestures like waving goodbye)
- Pantomimed actions (tool use gestures without the tool)

**Interactive gestures:**
- Turn-regulating gestures
- Emphatic gestures
- Interactive gestures maintaining dialogue

#### Gesture Coding Scheme (Research Protocol)

Validated video coding protocols include:
1. **Gesture identification:** Distinguish gestures from object manipulation
2. **Gesture type coding:** Classify by form and informational relation to speech
3. **Gesture + speech analysis:** Code the relationship between gesture and accompanying speech
4. **Quality ratings:** Rate accuracy, comprehensibility, and communicative effectiveness

**Inter-rater reliability (published):**
- Gesture identification: 79-98% agreement
- Gesture type coding: 99% agreement
- Gesture + speech coding: 76-98% agreement

#### Gesture Findings in ASD

Research on gesture in ASD demonstrates:
- Reduced frequency of spontaneous deictic gestures (pointing, showing)
- Qualitative differences in representational gesture quality
- Reduced integration of gesture with speech
- Difficulty with pantomimed action tasks (charades-type activities)
- More variable performance across gesture types

**Video analysis parameters:**
- Total gesture frequency
- Gesture type distribution
- Gesture-speech integration quality
- Gesture comprehensibility ratings
- Use of body as object vs. miming tool use
- Use of environmental context in gesture

#### Gesture Comprehension Assessment

Comprehension is assessed by:
- Presenting gestures on video (without speech)
- Coding response accuracy
- Measuring response latency
- Assessing generalisation across gesture types

---

### 6.2 Facial Expression Range (Hypomimia)

#### Clinically Supported Observations

**Important limitation:** The assessment of facial expression range from video is a descriptive, observational activity. Claims about specific "emotion detection" or "internal state inference" from facial expressions alone are not scientifically supported. The following describes only observable, quantifiable movement parameters.

#### Observable Facial Movement Parameters

**Frequency measures:**
- Number of spontaneous facial movements per unit time
- Number of distinct facial expressions observed
- Frequency of expression changes during interaction

**Amplitude measures:**
- Range of facial muscle movement
- Symmetry of facial movement
- Degree of eyebrow, eye, mouth, and forehead involvement

**Temporal measures:**
- Expression onset latency
- Expression duration
- Time between expressions
- Appropriateness of expression timing in conversation

**Clinical contexts where reduced facial expression is observed:**
- Parkinson's disease (hypomimia/mask-like facies)
- Major depression (flat affect)
- Negative symptoms of schizophrenia (blunted affect)
- Autism spectrum disorder (reduced facial expression variety)

#### Assessment Tools

The ADOS-2 codes "Facial Expressions Directed to Others" (A7), rating:
- **Score 0:** Wide range of facial expressions used flexibly
- **Score 1:** Somewhat limited range or unusual expressions
- **Score 2:** Very limited range or flat affect

The CORE Index includes facial items assessing:
- Facial immobility
- Reduced animation during interaction
- Poverty of facial expression

#### Measurement Approaches

**Clinical observation:**
- Trained rater assessment during structured interaction
- Qualitative descriptors of expressiveness range
- Comparison to age and context norms

**Instrumental measurement (research):**
- Facial electromyography (EMG) for objective muscle activation measurement
- Computer vision analysis of facial landmark movement
- Frame-by-frame coding of facial action units

**Clinical caveat:** Reduced facial expression can reflect neurological conditions, psychiatric states, medication effects, cultural display rules, or individual temperament. No inference about internal emotional state is warranted from facial expression alone.

---

### 6.3 Body Language in Clinical Conditions

#### Observable Body Language Parameters

**Postural features:**
- Torso positioning (forward lean, upright, slumped)
- Shoulder position (raised, relaxed, forward)
- Overall body tension/relaxation
- Head position and orientation

**Movement quality:**
- Movement initiation speed
- Movement amplitude
- Movement fluidity vs. jerkiness
- Gesture frequency and range
- Self-touching behaviours

**Interactive body language:**
- Body orientation toward/away from interaction partner
- Proximity regulation
- Mirroring/synchronisation with partner
- Postural shifts during interaction

#### Clinical Observations by Condition

**Depression:**
- Slumped posture (reduced spinal extension)
- Reduced spontaneous movement
- Slowed postural shifts
- Decreased gesture production
- Increased self-touching behaviours
- Reduced body orientation toward interaction partner

**Anxiety disorders:**
- Increased muscle tension
- Restless movements (fidgeting, shifting)
- Hypervigilant postural orientation
- Increased self-soothing behaviours

**Parkinson's disease:**
- Forward-flexed posture
- Reduced movement amplitude (hypokinesia)
- Bradykinesia (slowed movement)
- Loss of associated movements (arm swing when walking)
- Micrographia (small handwriting)

**Schizophrenia (negative symptoms):**
- Reduced spontaneous movement
- Decreased gesture production
- Postural rigidity
- Reduced body orientation toward others
- Poverty of expressive movement

#### Video Analysis Framework

Body language analysis from video requires:
- Full-body camera view (not just head/shoulders)
- Sufficient lighting for movement observation
- Contextual information about task demands
- Comparison to age-matched norms
- Attention to medication effects and physical conditions

**Clinical caveat:** Body language interpretation is highly context-dependent. Any clinical interpretation must consider the individual's baseline, cultural norms, physical condition, and situational demands.

## 7. Video Recording Standards for Clinical Observation

### Technical Requirements

**Camera specifications:**
- Minimum resolution: 720 x 480 (SD); 1920 x 1080 (HD) preferred
- Frame rate: 30 fps minimum; 60 fps for fine motor analysis
- Data rate: 9.1 Mbps minimum
- External directional microphone recommended

**Camera placement:**
- 0.5-1 metre from participant (for autism assessments)
- Slightly outside participant's direct line of sight
- Full-body view for movement analysis
- Side lighting to avoid shadows
- Stable tripod mounting (no handheld recording)

**Environmental controls:**
- Quiet, private room
- Minimal distractions and background activity
- Consistent, adequate lighting
- Comfortable temperature
- Appropriate furniture for assessment tasks

**Recording protocol:**
- Written consent obtained before recording
- Recording restricted to assessment purposes only
- Secure storage with access controls
- Backup procedures documented
- Retention policy compliant with institutional requirements

### Quality Standards

**Video quality checklist:**
- [ ] Participant fully visible throughout assessment
- [ ] Audio quality sufficient for speech analysis
- [ ] No glare or backlighting obscuring facial features
- [ ] Camera stable with no movement/shaking
- [ ] Full interaction visible (both participant and examiner)
- [ ] Sufficient duration for all planned coding
- [ ] Timestamp or counter visible
- [ ] No pausing or editing during recording

## 8. Recommendations for Protocol Implementation

### Tier 1: Core Autism Observation (Essential)

1. **Primary instrument:** ADOS-2 administration with video recording
   - Research-reliable examiner required
   - Standardised toy kit and written script
   - Consensus coding between two examiners
   - Video available for reliability checking

2. **Screening supplement:** SORF for naturalistic observation
   - Parent-recorded home videos
   - Trained rater coding (non-experts feasible)
   - 6-item optimal composite for efficiency

3. **Joint attention assessment:** ESCS video coding
   - 15-25 minute structured interaction
   - Frequency-based coding of IJA, RJA, IBR
   - Frame-level analysis for research applications

### Tier 2: Developmental Observation (When Indicated)

1. **Motor milestone analysis:** Video-based movement assessment
   - Full-body camera view
   - Standardised positioning and environment
   - Frame-by-frame analysis for quality assessment

2. **Primitive reflex assessment:** Video documentation
   - Supine positioning protocol
   - Clear view of all limbs and trunk
   - Standardised elicitation procedures

### Tier 3: Social Communication Detail (When Indicated)

1. **BOSCC for change measurement:**
   - Consistent administrator across timepoints
   - Blinded video coding for treatment studies
   - Multiple versions for different language levels

2. **Gesture analysis:**
   - Structured elicitation tasks
   - Standardised coding scheme with reliability checking
   - Speech-gesture relationship analysis

### Tier 4: Psychomotor Assessment (When Indicated)

1. **Psychomotor retardation observation:**
   - CORE Index or SRRS clinical rating
   - Video-based movement analysis
   - Actigraphy for objective measurement

2. **Hypomimia assessment:**
   - ADOS-2 facial expression coding
   - Descriptive observation of expression range
   - Contextual interpretation (no emotion inference)

### General Principles

1. **All video observation requires clinician oversight** - no automated system replaces clinical judgment
2. **Inter-rater reliability must be established** before clinical use of any coding system
3. **Cultural and contextual factors** must be considered in all behavioural interpretation
4. **Individual baseline patterns** are essential for meaningful change assessment
5. **Multi-informant, multi-method approaches** provide the most valid assessment
6. **Privacy and consent** requirements must be met for all video recording
7. **No claims about internal emotional states** should be made from behavioural observation alone
