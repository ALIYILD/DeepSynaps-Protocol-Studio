## 1. Executive Summary

This evidence matrix documents 23 hormone and endocrine biomarkers with relevance to neurological and psychiatric conditions. The biomarkers span thyroid function (n=5), HPA axis (n=4), sex hormones (n=7), reproductive hormones (n=3), metabolic hormones (n=3), and chronobiological hormones (n=1). Evidence strength varies from Grade A (well-established) to Grade D (emerging/preliminary).

### Key Findings
- **Strongest evidence** exists for cortisol (HPA axis dysfunction in depression, PTSD, cognitive decline), TSH (thyroid dysfunction and mood/cognition), and insulin resistance ("Type 3 Diabetes" hypothesis in Alzheimer's disease)
- **Moderate evidence** supports testosterone (cognitive aging in men), estradiol (timing hypothesis for neuroprotection in women), DHEA-S (depression and aging), and vitamin D (neuroprotection)
- **Emerging evidence** includes melatonin (circadian disruption and depression), prolactin (antipsychotic monitoring), and thyroid antibodies (Hashimoto encephalopathy)
- All biomarkers exhibit significant diurnal, cyclical, or age-related variation that must be accounted for in clinical interpretation

### 4.1 TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Thyroid Stimulating Hormone / Thyrotropin |
| **Synonyms** | Thyrotropin, TSH |
| **Reference Range** | Adult: 0.4-4.0 mIU/L (some labs: 0.5-5.0); Optimal: 0.45-2.5; Age >60: upper limit may extend to 4.5-6.0 |
| **Units** | mIU/L |
| **Specimen** | Serum, fasting preferred |

**Evidence Summary:**
TSH is the most sensitive screening marker for thyroid dysfunction. Both overt and subclinical thyroid dysfunction are associated with depression, anxiety, and cognitive impairment. Subclinical hypothyroidism (TSH 4.5-10 with normal T4) has been linked to depression, fatigue, and cognitive slowing. The upper limit of TSH reference range increases progressively with age, especially after age 60 in males and 50 in females (to ~6-7 mIU/L in those over 70).

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Major depressive disorder (hypothyroidism mimics depression)
- Subclinical hypothyroidism and mood disorders
- Cognitive impairment (thyroid hormone essential for brain development and function)
- Hashimoto encephalopathy (TSH may be normal despite antibodies)

**Evidence Strength:** A - Multiple guidelines (ATA, AACE); established clinical practice

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Minimal diurnal variation; TSH has a nocturnal peak. Not cycle-dependent but affected by age and pregnancy status.

**Key Confounders:** Age (TSH rises with age), pregnancy (trimester-specific ranges), biotin supplementation (causes falsely low TSH), medications (glucocorticoids, dopamine agonists suppress TSH; lithium, amiodarone elevate TSH), non-thyroidal illness ("euthyroid sick syndrome"), time of day (prefer morning testing).

**Clinical Wording:** "TSH screening is recommended in all patients presenting with new-onset depression, cognitive complaints, or treatment-resistant psychiatric symptoms. Subclinical thyroid dysfunction may contribute to mood and cognitive symptoms even when values are within broad reference ranges. Interpretation should account for age-specific ranges, especially in adults over 60."

**Key References:**
- PMC12205146: Reference intervals for thyroid parameters in adults and older individuals
- PMC712 nutritional: Thyroid function and depression guidelines

### 4.2 Free T4 (Free Thyroxine)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Free Thyroxine |
| **Synonyms** | Free T4, FT4, Unbound T4 |
| **Reference Range** | Adult: 0.8-1.8 ng/dL (0.75-1.09 ng/dL per 2024 Thai study; manufacturer: 0.70-1.48) |
| **Units** | ng/dL or pmol/L (x 12.87) |
| **Specimen** | Serum |

**Evidence Summary:**
Free T4 is the biologically active, unbound fraction of thyroxine. It is the primary measure of thyroid gland output. Low free T4 (with elevated TSH) confirms clinical hypothyroidism, which is strongly associated with depression, cognitive slowing, fatigue, and memory impairment. Even within the reference range, lower quartile free T4 has been associated with worse cognitive performance in older adults.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Clinical hypothyroidism (depression, cognitive impairment)
- Subclinical hypothyroidism (normal T4, elevated TSH)
- Hashimoto encephalopathy (T4 may be normal or low-normal; 42% euthyroid at diagnosis)
- Congenital hypothyroidism (severe intellectual disability if untreated)

**Evidence Strength:** A - Established clinical biomarker

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Modest diurnal variation; generally stable throughout menstrual cycle.

**Key Confounders:** Pregnancy (increases TBG, but free T4 should remain stable in early pregnancy), severe illness (free T4 may be low in euthyroid sick syndrome), medications (heparin falsely elevates free T4), assay methodology differences.

**Clinical Wording:** "Free T4 should be measured alongside TSH when thyroid dysfunction is suspected. Low free T4 with elevated TSH confirms clinical hypothyroidism, a reversible cause of depression and cognitive impairment. Borderline values warrant monitoring and may warrant intervention in symptomatic patients."

### 4.3 Free T3 (Free Triiodothyronine)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Free Triiodothyronine |
| **Synonyms** | Free T3, FT3 |
| **Reference Range** | Adult: 2.1-3.3 pg/mL (2.15-3.36 adult, 1.99-2.94 older per 2024 study; manufacturer: 1.58-3.91) |
| **Units** | pg/mL or pmol/L |
| **Specimen** | Serum |

**Evidence Summary:**
T3 is the metabolically active thyroid hormone, with approximately 80% produced by peripheral conversion (deiodination) of T4. Free T3 is lower in older adults and in women compared to men. Low T3 syndrome ("euthyroid sick syndrome") is common in chronic illness, critical illness, and psychiatric conditions. Some evidence suggests that T3 supplementation may augment antidepressant treatment in refractory depression, though evidence is mixed.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Euthyroid sick syndrome (non-thyroidal illness)
- Refractory depression (T3 augmentation)
- Cognitive impairment (older adults have lower FT3)
- Wilson's Temperature Syndrome (controversial)

**Evidence Strength:** B - Clinical use established; brain-related evidence emerging

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Diurnal rhythm with peak in morning; lower in evening.

**Key Confounders:** Age (FT3 declines with age), severe illness (markedly reduced in euthyroid sick syndrome), medications (beta-blockers, amiodarone inhibit T4-to-T3 conversion), fasting, selenium deficiency (impairs deiodinase enzymes), gender (women may have lower FT3).

**Clinical Wording:** "Free T3 provides additional information about thyroid hormone bioavailability. Low T3 in the setting of normal TSH and T4 may indicate non-thyroidal illness or impaired peripheral conversion. In patients with refractory depression on levothyroxine, assessing free T3 may guide consideration of combination T4/T3 therapy under specialist supervision."

### 4.4 Anti-TPO (Anti-Thyroid Peroxidase Antibodies)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Anti-Thyroid Peroxidase Antibodies |
| **Synonyms** | TPOAb, Anti-TPO, Thyroid Peroxidase Antibody |
| **Reference Range** | <35 IU/mL (<5.61 manufacturer; <2.28 per 2024 Thai study) |
| **Units** | IU/mL |
| **Specimen** | Serum |

**Evidence Summary:**
Anti-TPO antibodies are the primary marker of autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto's thyroiditis). Elevated levels are found in 95-100% of Hashimoto encephalopathy cases, even when thyroid function tests are normal. Prevalence increases with age and is higher in women. Anti-TPO positivity is associated with increased risk of depression independent of thyroid function status, possibly through shared autoimmune mechanisms.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Hashimoto encephalopathy (SREAT) - elevated in 95-100% of cases
- Autoimmune thyroiditis
- Depression (association independent of thyroid function)
- Subclinical hypothyroidism (progression risk marker)

**Evidence Strength:** B - Strong diagnostic marker; emerging brain-related evidence

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** None significant; relatively stable over time.

**Key Confounders:** Prevalence increases with age and is higher in women; 10-15% of general population has elevated titers without thyroid disease; titre levels do not always correlate with disease severity.

**Clinical Wording:** "Anti-TPO antibodies should be measured in patients with unexplained cognitive decline, treatment-resistant depression, or suspected Hashimoto encephalopathy. Significantly elevated titers (>10x upper limit) in the context of encephalopathic symptoms strongly support a diagnosis of Hashimoto encephalopathy, even when thyroid function is normal."

### 4.5 Anti-Tg (Anti-Thyroglobulin Antibodies)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Anti-Thyroglobulin Antibodies |
| **Synonyms** | TgAb, Anti-Tg, Thyroglobulin Antibody |
| **Reference Range** | <4 IU/mL (<4.11 manufacturer; <3.13 per 2024 Thai study) |
| **Units** | IU/mL |
| **Specimen** | Serum |

**Evidence Summary:**
Anti-Tg antibodies, along with Anti-TPO, are markers of autoimmune thyroid disease. They are present in approximately 60-80% of Hashimoto's thyroiditis and are elevated in Hashimoto encephalopathy. When both Anti-TPO and Anti-Tg are elevated with neurological symptoms, the diagnostic confidence for Hashimoto encephalopathy increases.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Hashimoto encephalopathy (elevated alongside Anti-TPO)
- Autoimmune thyroiditis
- Thyroid cancer monitoring

**Evidence Strength:** B - Diagnostic marker

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** None significant.

**Key Confounders:** Present in 5-10% of general population; more prevalent in women and older adults; titre levels may fluctuate.

**Clinical Wording:** "Anti-Tg measurement complements Anti-TPO testing in the workup of autoimmune thyroid disease and Hashimoto encephalopathy. Combined elevation with compatible neurological symptoms supports immunotherapy trial."

### 4.6 Cortisol - Morning Serum

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Morning Serum Cortisol |
| **Synonyms** | Hydrocortisone, Compound F |
| **Reference Range** | 6-8 AM: 10-20 mcg/dL (some labs: 5-25 mcg/dL) |
| **Units** | mcg/dL or nmol/L (x 27.6) |
| **Specimen** | Serum, morning (6-8 AM) |

**Evidence Summary:**
Morning cortisol is the cornerstone of HPA axis assessment. Elevated morning cortisol is well-documented in major depressive disorder (MDD), particularly melancholic depression. Chronic cortisol elevation leads to hippocampal atrophy (via glucocorticoid receptor-mediated neurotoxicity), prefrontal cortex alterations, impaired memory consolidation, and reduced neurogenesis. The cortisol-depression relationship is bidirectional: elevated cortisol causes depression-like changes, and depression dysregulates cortisol secretion.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Major depressive disorder (elevated basal cortisol)
- PTSD (altered circadian pattern, often lower basal but exaggerated response)
- Cushing's syndrome (severe cognitive impairment, depression)
- Alzheimer's disease (HPA axis dysregulation)
- Burnout/chronic stress syndrome

**Evidence Strength:** A - Extensive evidence; established clinical practice

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Strong diurnal rhythm - peaks at 6-8 AM, nadirs at midnight. Testing must be time-standardized.

**Key Confounders:** Time of day (critical - must be morning specimen), physical stress (acute illness, surgery, pain), psychological stress, CBG (cortisol-binding globulin) levels (elevated in pregnancy, oral contraceptives; decreased in liver disease), exercise before testing, alcohol, sleep deprivation, dexamethasone suppression testing.

**Clinical Wording:** "Morning serum cortisol is essential in the evaluation of depression with suspected HPA axis involvement, chronic stress, and cognitive impairment. Elevated levels support HPA axis hyperactivity, a known contributor to depression and hippocampal volume loss. Morning levels >20 mcg/dL warrant further evaluation for Cushing's syndrome."

### 4.7 Cortisol - Evening Serum

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Evening Serum Cortisol |
| **Synonyms** | 4 PM Cortisol, Late Afternoon Cortisol |
| **Reference Range** | 4 PM: 3-10 mcg/dL (approximately 50% of morning value) |
| **Units** | mcg/dL |
| **Specimen** | Serum, late afternoon |

**Evidence Summary:**
Evening cortisol assessment is valuable for evaluating diurnal slope. A flattened diurnal slope (higher evening cortisol relative to morning) is characteristic of chronic stress, burnout, PTSD, and depression. Failure to suppress adequately in the evening reflects impaired glucocorticoid receptor feedback.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Major depression (flattened diurnal rhythm)
- PTSD (attenuated diurnal variation)
- Burnout syndrome
- Insomnia (elevated evening cortisol)

**Evidence Strength:** A - Well-established pattern

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Should be ~50% of morning value; flat slope indicates HPA axis dysfunction.

**Key Confounders:** Same as morning cortisol; food intake, recent exercise.

**Clinical Wording:** "Evening cortisol, when measured alongside morning cortisol, provides information about diurnal HPA axis regulation. An inadequate evening decline suggests impaired negative feedback and is associated with chronic stress, depression, and sleep disturbance."

### 4.8 Salivary Cortisol (Cortisol Awakening Response - CAR)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Salivary Cortisol Awakening Response |
| **Synonyms** | CAR, Salivary Cortisol Slope |
| **Reference Range** | Morning saliva: 10.2-27.3 ng/mL; Night: 2.2-4.1 ng/mL; Peak: 15-30 min post-waking |
| **Units** | ng/mL or nmol/L |
| **Specimen** | Saliva (typically 4-5 samples across the day) |

**Evidence Summary:**
The cortisol awakening response (CAR) measures the cortisol rise in the first 30-45 minutes after waking. An enhanced CAR is associated with acute stress and anticipation. A blunted CAR is associated with chronic stress, burnout, PTSD, depression, and fatigue. Salivary cortisol reflects free (unbound) cortisol and is particularly useful for assessing diurnal patterns without the confounding effects of CBG variations.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Major depression (blunted or exaggerated CAR depending on subtype)
- PTSD (blunted CAR)
- Burnout/chronic fatigue
- Chronic work stress

**Evidence Strength:** B - Growing evidence base; research and specialty use

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** The CAR itself is the key diurnal measure; sampling must begin immediately upon waking.

**Key Confounders:** Sleep quality and timing, awakening method (alarm vs natural), position upon waking (stay in bed vs get up immediately), oral contraceptives (increase CBG, making serum less reliable than saliva), smoking, caffeine consumption, nasal/oral inflammation.

**Clinical Wording:** "Salivary cortisol profiling, including the cortisol awakening response, offers a non-invasive method for assessing HPA axis dynamics. A blunted awakening response is associated with chronic stress states and burnout, while an exaggerated response may indicate acute stress or anticipatory anxiety. Serial monitoring can track treatment response."

### 4.9 DHEA-S (Dehydroepiandrosterone Sulfate)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Dehydroepiandrosterone Sulfate |
| **Synonyms** | DHEA-S, DHEAS, Prasterone sulfate |
| **Reference Range** | M (18-29): 280-640 mcg/dL; M (40-49): 90-450; M (60-69): 42-290. F (18-29): 65-380; F (40-49): 32-240; F (60-69): 13-130 |
| **Units** | mcg/dL or umol/L |
| **Specimen** | Serum |

**Evidence Summary:**
DHEA-S is the most abundant circulating steroid hormone and a precursor to sex steroids. It is also a neurosteroid with direct effects on GABA-A and NMDA receptors. DHEA-S peaks in the mid-20s and declines predictably with age (~1-2% per year). Higher DHEA-S levels are associated with lower depression risk and better cognitive function in older adults. The ELSA study found an inverse association between DHEA-S and depressive symptoms, and low baseline DHEA-S predicted incident depression at 4-year follow-up.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Depression (inverse association; low levels predict onset)
- Cognitive aging (higher levels associated with better cognition)
- Chronic stress (cortisol:DHEA-S ratio marker)
- Adrenal insufficiency
- Autoimmune conditions

**Evidence Strength:** B - Strong observational data; limited RCT evidence for supplementation

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Minimal diurnal variation (unlike cortisol); relatively stable throughout the day.

**Key Confounders:** Age (strong age-related decline), sex (men have 2-3x higher levels), chronic illness, medications (corticosteroids suppress DHEA-S), smoking, body composition, time of day (minimal but measurable variation).

**Clinical Wording:** "DHEA-S provides information about adrenal androgen reserve and serves as a marker of biological aging. Low levels, particularly when disproportionate to age, are associated with increased depression risk and cognitive decline. The cortisol:DHEA-S ratio may offer additional insight into HPA axis balance and stress resilience."

**Key References:**
- PMC4712651: DHEA-S and depression in the English Longitudinal Study of Aging
- Elpers & Steptoe (2020): DHEAS and cognitive function in 5,061 older adults

### 4.10 Testosterone (Total, Male)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Total Testosterone |
| **Synonyms** | T, Total T, TT |
| **Reference Range** | Adult male: 300-1000 ng/dL (10.4-34.7 nmol/L); Young men (19-39): higher range expected |
| **Units** | ng/dL or nmol/L (x 0.0347) |
| **Specimen** | Serum, morning (7-10 AM) |

**Evidence Summary:**
Testosterone declines with male aging ("andropause" or "late-onset hypogonadism"). Multiple prospective cohort studies associate lower testosterone with higher dementia and Alzheimer's disease risk. The hazard ratio for dementia was 0.74 per 10 nmol/mol increase in testosterone:SHBG ratio in one 19-year follow-up study. However, RCTs have shown mixed results - while some small trials showed cognitive improvements, larger trials (TEAAM, T Trials) did not demonstrate cognitive benefits of testosterone therapy. Current consensus: lower testosterone is a biomarker, not yet a proven therapeutic target for cognitive decline.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Cognitive decline and dementia (inverse association)
- Depression in aging men (some evidence for antidepressant effect of TRT)
- Alzheimer's disease (lower levels associated with higher risk)
- Androgen deprivation therapy-related cognitive effects

**Evidence Strength:** B - Strong observational data; inconsistent intervention evidence

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Strong diurnal variation (peak 6-8 AM, nadir evening); requires morning testing.

**Key Confounders:** Age (declines ~1% per year after 30), SHBG (increases with age, reducing free testosterone), obesity (reduces total T via aromatase), acute illness, medications (opioids, glucocorticoids, 5-ARIs), alcohol, assay methodology (LC-MS/MS preferred over immunoassay).

**Clinical Wording:** "Testosterone measurement is indicated in men with depression, cognitive complaints, fatigue, and sexual dysfunction. While lower testosterone is associated with increased dementia risk in observational studies, evidence that testosterone replacement improves cognition remains inconsistent. Low testosterone should be confirmed with repeat morning testing and free testosterone assessment before considering therapy."

**Key References:**
- PMC9789006: Testosterone, cognitive decline and dementia in ageing men
- PMC12962056: Psychiatric and cognitive effects of testosterone therapy in adult men

### 4.11 Testosterone (Total, Female)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Total Testosterone |
| **Synonyms** | Female Total T |
| **Reference Range** | Adult female: 15-70 ng/dL (0.5-2.4 nmol/L) |
| **Units** | ng/dL |
| **Specimen** | Serum |

**Evidence Summary:**
Women produce testosterone in the ovaries and adrenal glands. Low testosterone in women (particularly postmenopausal) is associated with decreased libido, fatigue, and potentially cognitive effects. However, the clinical utility of measuring total testosterone in women is debated, as most circulating testosterone in women is bound to SHBG, and free testosterone may be more informative. Evidence for testosterone therapy in women is limited and primarily addresses sexual function.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Female sexual dysfunction
- Postmenopausal symptoms
- Hypoactive sexual desire disorder

**Evidence Strength:** C - Limited female-specific cognitive data

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Modest diurnal variation; mid-cycle elevation during ovulation.

**Key Confounders:** SHBG levels (highly variable with OCP use, pregnancy), menstrual cycle phase, adrenal vs ovarian sources.

**Clinical Wording:** "Testosterone assessment in women may be considered in specific contexts, particularly premenopausal women with low libido or postmenopausal women with persistent symptoms. Interpretation requires accounting for SHBG status and menstrual cycle phase. Clinical utility for cognitive symptoms in women remains investigational."

### 4.12 Free Testosterone

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Free Testosterone |
| **Synonyms** | FT, Bioavailable Testosterone, cFT (calculated) |
| **Reference Range** | M (19-39 yr): 120-368 pg/mL (415-1274 pmol/L); M (all adult): 66-309 pg/mL (229-1072 pmol/L) |
| **Units** | pg/mL or pmol/L |
| **Specimen** | Serum |

**Evidence Summary:**
Free testosterone represents the unbound, biologically active fraction (~2% of total). It is calculated from total testosterone and SHBG or measured directly by equilibrium dialysis. Free testosterone declines more steeply than total testosterone with aging because SHBG increases with age. Lower free testosterone has been more consistently associated with cognitive decline than total testosterone. In UK Biobank data, lower calculated free testosterone was associated with higher dementia incidence.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Cognitive decline and dementia
- Depression
- Type 2 diabetes risk (T4DM trial showed reduction)
- Androgen deficiency symptoms

**Evidence Strength:** B - Stronger association with outcomes than total testosterone

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Follows same diurnal pattern as total testosterone.

**Key Confounders:** SHBG (key determinant of free fraction), albumin, obesity.

**Clinical Wording:** "Free testosterone provides a more accurate assessment of androgen bioavailability than total testosterone alone, particularly when SHBG levels are altered. It is the preferred measure when evaluating men with suspected androgen deficiency, especially in older adults and those with conditions affecting SHBG."

### 4.13 Estradiol (E2, Premenopausal Female)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | 17-beta Estradiol |
| **Synonyms** | E2, Estradiol-17beta |
| **Reference Range** | Follicular phase: 30-100 pg/mL; Mid-cycle peak: 200-400 pg/mL; Luteal phase: 50-150 pg/mL |
| **Units** | pg/mL or pmol/L (x 3.67) |
| **Specimen** | Serum |

**Evidence Summary:**
Estradiol is the primary estrogen in premenopausal women and has well-documented neuroprotective effects. It enhances synaptic plasticity, promotes dendritic spine density in the hippocampus, increases cerebral blood flow, and modulates neurotransmitter systems (serotonin, dopamine, acetylcholine). Estrogen receptors (ER-alpha and ER-beta) are widely distributed in the brain. The timing hypothesis suggests that estrogen's neuroprotective effects are greatest when initiated near menopause rather than later.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Menstrual cycle-related mood disorders (PMDD)
- Perimenopausal cognitive changes (verbal memory, executive function)
- Neuroprotection (preclinical evidence strong)
- Depression (estrogen withdrawal associated with mood changes)

**Evidence Strength:** B - Strong preclinical evidence; mixed clinical trial results

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Strong menstrual cycle variation; testing should specify cycle phase.

**Key Confounders:** Menstrual cycle phase (critical for interpretation), pregnancy, medications (OCPs, aromatase inhibitors), BMI (adipose tissue produces estrone), time of day.

**Clinical Wording:** "Estradiol levels in premenopausal women should be interpreted within the context of menstrual cycle phase. Cyclical estradiol fluctuations are associated with mood and cognitive changes in susceptible women. The timing of any hormonal intervention relative to menopause onset appears critical for neuroprotective outcomes."

### 4.14 Estradiol (E2, Postmenopausal Female)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | 17-beta Estradiol |
| **Synonyms** | Postmenopausal E2 |
| **Reference Range** | <30 pg/mL (<110 pmol/L), typically <20 pg/mL |
| **Units** | pg/mL |
| **Specimen** | Serum |

**Evidence Summary:**
Postmenopausal estradiol decline is associated with increased dementia risk. Women have a significantly higher lifetime risk of Alzheimer's disease than men. The WHIMS trial showed increased dementia risk with conjugated equine estrogens started in women >65 years, while KEEPS and ELITE trials (starting HT earlier) found neutral cognitive effects. The timing hypothesis - that HT started within the critical window of perimenopause/early postmenopause may be neuroprotective while delayed initiation is not - is supported by multiple studies. Up to 60% of midlife women report cognitive difficulties during perimenopause.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Alzheimer's disease risk (higher in postmenopausal women)
- Cognitive decline after menopause
- Menopause-related cognitive impairment (MCI)
- Vascular cognitive impairment

**Evidence Strength:** B - Strong observational data; timing hypothesis well-supported

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Minimal diurnal/cyclical variation postmenopause; levels are consistently low.

**Key Confounders:** Body composition (BMI affects estrone from adipose tissue), APOE genotype (modifies risk), vascular health, timing of menopause.

**Clinical Wording:** "Postmenopausal estradiol levels are characteristically low. The relationship between estrogen and cognitive health is complex: observational studies suggest neuroprotective effects of endogenous estrogen, but clinical trial results depend critically on timing of intervention initiation. Assessment of APOE status and vascular risk factors helps stratify individual risk. Hormone therapy decisions should be individualized."

**Key References:**
- PMC12256231: Estrogen, menopause, and Alzheimer's disease
- PMC8394691: Menopause and cognitive impairment: narrative review
- PMC10842974: Cognitive problems in perimenopause

### 4.17 Prolactin

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Prolactin |
| **Synonyms** | PRL, Lactogenic Hormone, Luteotropic Hormone |
| **Reference Range** | M: <20 ng/mL; F (non-pregnant): <25 ng/mL; Pregnant: 80-400 ng/mL |
| **Units** | ng/mL or mIU/L |
| **Specimen** | Serum, fasting, morning |

**Evidence Summary:**
Prolactin is primarily known for lactation but has CNS effects. Hyperprolactinemia is a common adverse effect of antipsychotic medications (particularly dopamine D2 receptor antagonists like risperidone, paliperidone, amisulpride). Prevalence ranges from 42-93% in women and 18-72% in men on antipsychotics. Chronic hyperprolactinemia causes hypogonadism, osteoporosis, sexual dysfunction, and may contribute to cognitive effects. Monitoring is essential for patients on long-term antipsychotic therapy.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Antipsychotic-induced side effects (monitoring)
- Prolactinoma (pituitary tumor)
- Hypogonadism-related symptoms
- Postpartum mood disorders
- Osteoporosis risk (chronic elevation)

**Evidence Strength:** A - Well-established; routine clinical monitoring

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Diurnal rhythm - highest during sleep, lowest in late afternoon; pulsatile secretion; increases with stress, exercise, and meals.

**Key Confounders:** Time of day (morning preferred), recent eating, stress, exercise, medications (antipsychotics, metoclopramide, verapamil, estrogen), pregnancy, lactation, nipple stimulation, chest wall injury, seizures (prolactin rises after seizure), renal failure (reduced clearance), hypothyroidism (TRH stimulates prolactin).

**Clinical Wording:** "Prolactin monitoring is essential for all patients on antipsychotic therapy, particularly risperidone, paliperidone, and amisulpride, which elevate prolactin in the majority of patients. Levels >100 ng/mL on antipsychotics warrant endocrinology referral and pituitary imaging. Hyperprolactinemia can cause significant physical and psychological morbidity including sexual dysfunction, osteoporosis, and mood changes."

### 4.18 LH (Luteinizing Hormone)

**Evidence Summary:**
LH rises dramatically during the menopausal transition. Higher LH levels, along with FSH, have been associated with worse verbal learning and memory performance in peri- and postmenopausal women. The ALSPAC study found that higher LH levels were associated with worse verbal episodic memory, independent of age. LH changes parallel the cognitive changes observed during perimenopause.

**Clinical Wording:** "LH measurement is valuable for staging the menopausal transition and evaluating gonadal function. Elevated LH during perimenopause is associated with cognitive changes, particularly affecting verbal memory. LH should be interpreted alongside FSH and estradiol for comprehensive assessment."

### 4.19 FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Follicle Stimulating Hormone |
| **Synonyms** | FSH, Follitropin |
| **Reference Range** | M: 1.5-12.4 IU/L; F (follicular): 3.5-12.5 IU/L; F (mid-cycle): 4.7-21.5 IU/L; F (postmenopausal): 25.8-134.8 IU/L |
| **Units** | IU/L |
| **Specimen** | Serum |

**Evidence Summary:**
FSH is the primary marker of ovarian reserve and menopausal status. It rises during the menopausal transition and is the strongest hormonal predictor of menopause stage. Higher FSH levels have been associated with worse cognitive performance in midlife women. The SWAN study and other longitudinal studies have found that cognitive performance changes correlate with FSH fluctuations during perimenopause. - Perimenopausal cognitive changes
- Menopause staging
- Premature ovarian insufficiency
- Hypogonadism evaluation

**Evidence Strength:** B - Established for menopause; emerging cognitive associations

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Menstrual cycle dependent; rises postmenopause.

**Key Confounders:** Menstrual cycle phase, menopausal status, hormonal contraceptives, GnRH analogs.

**Clinical Wording:** "FSH is the primary marker for assessing menopausal status. Rising FSH during perimenopause marks the transition associated with cognitive changes in many women. FSH >25 IU/L with low estradiol confirms postmenopausal status and may help contextualize cognitive complaints in midlife women."

### 4.20 Melatonin

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Melatonin (N-acetyl-5-methoxytryptamine) |
| **Synonyms** | N-acetyl-5-methoxytryptamine, Pineal Hormone |
| **Reference Range** | 3 AM: M 18.3-134 pg/mL, F 19.0-197 pg/mL; 8 AM: M 5.6-61.8 pg/mL, F 3.3-93.2 pg/mL |
| **Units** | pg/mL |
| **Specimen** | Serum or saliva (timing critical) |

**Evidence Summary:**
Melatonin is a chronobiotic hormone produced by the pineal gland during darkness. It regulates circadian rhythms and has antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and neuroprotective properties. Decreased melatonin levels are reported in major depressive disorder. Melatonin receptors (MT1 and MT2) are present in the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex. Agomelatine (MT1/MT2 agonist and 5-HT2C antagonist) is an approved antidepressant. Melatonin improves glymphatic function and AQP4 polarization in depression models. Circadian disruption is a risk factor for depression, neurodegeneration, and cognitive impairment.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Major depressive disorder (decreased melatonin)
- Insomnia and circadian rhythm disorders
- Jet lag, shift work disorder
- Alzheimer's disease (reduced melatonin production)
- Parkinson's disease (circadian disruption)
- Neuroprotection (antioxidant, anti-inflammatory)

**Evidence Strength:** B - Strong preclinical evidence; growing clinical evidence

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Extreme diurnal variation - peaks at 2-4 AM, virtually undetectable during daylight; light suppresses melatonin within minutes.

**Key Confounders:** Light exposure (even dim light suppresses melatonin), age (production declines with age), medications (beta-blockers suppress melatonin, fluvoxamine increases it), blue light exposure, shift work, seasonal variation, blindness (free-running rhythms in some).

**Clinical Wording:** "Melatonin assessment is valuable in patients with sleep disorders, depression, and suspected circadian rhythm disruption. Exogenous melatonin has evidence for improving sleep onset, jet lag, and depressive symptoms in some populations. The decline in melatonin production with aging may contribute to sleep disturbance and cognitive vulnerability in older adults. Light hygiene should be addressed alongside melatonin therapy."

**Key References:**

### 4.21 Insulin (Fasting)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Fasting Insulin |
| **Synonyms** | Serum Insulin, Immunoreactive Insulin |
| **Reference Range** | 2.6-24.9 uIU/mL (18.0-173 pmol/L); Optimal: <10 uIU/mL |
| **Units** | uIU/mL or pmol/L |
| **Specimen** | Serum, fasting (8-12 hours) |

**Evidence Summary:**
Insulin is central to the "Type 3 Diabetes" hypothesis of Alzheimer's disease. Brain insulin resistance is an early and progressive abnormality in AD, with insulin and IGF-1 receptor expression declining with Braak stage severity. Peripheral hyperinsulinemia (compensating for insulin resistance) saturates insulin-degrading enzyme (IDE), which also degrades amyloid-beta, leading to A-beta accumulation. Fasting insulin levels, alongside HOMA-IR, provide a practical measure of insulin resistance associated with cognitive decline. Intranasal insulin has shown promise in early AD clinical trials.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Alzheimer's disease ("Type 3 Diabetes" hypothesis)
- Mild cognitive impairment
- Type 2 diabetes and cognitive decline
- Vascular dementia (shared metabolic risk)
- Depression (insulin resistance associated)

**Evidence Strength:** A - Extensive evidence linking insulin resistance to cognitive decline

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Basal secretion with postprandial rises; fasting measurement standardizes assessment.

**Key Confounders:** Fasting duration, recent carbohydrate intake, medications (metformin, insulin, sulfonylureas), BMI, physical activity, time of day, renal function, assay methodology.

**Clinical Wording:** "Fasting insulin, alongside glucose and HOMA-IR calculation, is recommended in the evaluation of cognitive decline, particularly in patients with metabolic risk factors. Insulin resistance is increasingly recognized as a modifiable risk factor for Alzheimer's disease. Interventions targeting insulin sensitivity (exercise, metformin, intranasal insulin trials) show promise for cognitive protection."

**Key References:**
- PMC2769828: Alzheimer's Disease Is Type 3 Diabetes - Evidence Reviewed
- PMC7246646: Type 3 Diabetes and Its Role Implications in Alzheimer's Disease

### 4.22 C-Peptide

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Connecting Peptide |
| **Synonyms** | C-Peptide, Proinsulin C-Peptide |
| **Reference Range** | 0.8-3.85 ng/mL (0.27-1.27 nmol/L); Fasting: 0.78-1.89 ng/mL |
| **Units** | ng/mL or nmol/L |
| **Specimen** | Serum or urine, fasting |

**Evidence Summary:**
C-peptide is co-secreted with insulin in equimolar amounts but has a longer half-life, making it a more stable marker of endogenous insulin secretion. Unlike insulin, C-peptide is not extracted by the liver and is not affected by exogenous insulin administration. It helps distinguish between type 1 diabetes (low/absent C-peptide) and type 2 diabetes (normal or elevated C-peptide). In the context of brain health, elevated fasting C-peptide indicates hyperinsulinemia and insulin resistance, both associated with cognitive decline and AD risk.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Insulin resistance and metabolic syndrome
- Type 2 diabetes and cognitive decline
- Hypoglycemia evaluation
- Beta cell function assessment

**Evidence Strength:** B - Established metabolic marker; indirect brain health relevance

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Follows insulin secretion patterns; fasting measurement preferred.

**Key Confounders:** Renal function (C-peptide cleared by kidneys), exogenous insulin (does not contain C-peptide), time of day, meals.

**Clinical Wording:** "C-peptide provides a stable measure of endogenous insulin secretion and is useful for assessing insulin resistance and beta cell function. Elevated fasting C-peptide in the context of cognitive evaluation indicates hyperinsulinemia, a marker of metabolic risk for cognitive decline. It is particularly useful for distinguishing type 1 from type 2 diabetes when brain health comorbidities are present."

### 4.23 SHBG (Sex Hormone Binding Globulin)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Sex Hormone Binding Globulin |
| **Synonyms** | SHBG, Sex Steroid Binding Globulin, Testosterone-Estradiol Binding Globulin |
| **Reference Range** | M: 10-57 nmol/L; F: 18-144 nmol/L (age and cycle dependent) |
| **Units** | nmol/L |
| **Specimen** | Serum |

**Evidence Summary:**
SHBG binds testosterone and estradiol in circulation, regulating their bioavailability. Elevated SHBG reduces free hormone levels and has been independently associated with increased dementia risk in observational studies. Higher SHBG was associated with increased risk of cognitive decline (executive function and motor speed) in the MrOS study. SHBG increases with age (contributing to "free testosterone decline" in aging men) and is inversely associated with insulin sensitivity.

**Conditions with Evidence:**
- Cognitive decline and dementia (elevated SHBG associated with risk)
- Hypogonadism assessment
- Insulin resistance
- Liver disease (SHBG produced by liver)
- Hyperthyroidism (increases SHBG)

**Evidence Strength:** C - Emerging as independent risk marker

**Diurnal/Cyclical Variation:** Modest diurnal variation; relatively stable.

**Key Confounders:** Age (increases with age), liver function (produced by liver), thyroid status (hyperthyroidism increases, hypothyroidism decreases), medications (estrogens increase, androgens decrease), obesity (decreases SHBG), insulin resistance (decreases SHBG).

**Clinical Wording:** "SHBG measurement is essential for calculating free testosterone and interpreting total sex hormone levels. Elevated SHBG independently associates with increased dementia risk and should be considered when evaluating cognitive complaints, particularly in older adults. It also helps contextualize total testosterone levels - high SHBG may mask functionally significant androgen deficiency."

## 5. Clinical Guidance & Caveats

### 5.1 General Principles for Hormone Testing in Neuropsychiatric Context

1. **Always interpret hormones within clinical context** - Reference ranges are population statistics; individual optimal ranges may differ.
2. **Account for diurnal/cyclical variation** - Single measurements may mislead; timed sampling or repeated testing improves accuracy.
3. **Consider age-specific ranges** - Many reference ranges change significantly with aging (TSH rises, testosterone and DHEA-S fall).
4. **Evaluate patterns, not isolated values** - The ratio of hormones (cortisol:DHEA-S, testosterone:SHBG) often provides more clinical insight than individual values.
5. **Screen for confounding conditions** - Always consider non-endocrine causes of cognitive and psychiatric symptoms.
6. **Avoid over-interpretation** - Hormone abnormalities may be markers rather than causes of brain conditions.

### 5.2 Safe Clinical Wording for Patient Communication

- **Thyroid:** "Your thyroid function tests help us understand whether thyroid imbalance may be contributing to your symptoms. Both overactive and underactive thyroid can affect mood, energy, and thinking."
- **Cortisol:** "Cortisol is your body's stress hormone. We can measure it to understand how your stress response system is functioning. Chronic stress can affect memory, mood, and sleep."
- **Testosterone:** "Testosterone levels naturally decline with age. While low testosterone is associated with cognitive changes, the evidence that replacing it improves brain function is mixed. We'll discuss whether testing is appropriate for you."
- **Estrogen:** "The relationship between estrogen and brain health is complex and depends on many factors including timing, genetics, and overall health. We'll consider your individual risk profile."
- **Insulin:** "Insulin resistance affects not just your body but potentially your brain as well. The good news is that it's modifiable through diet, exercise, and sometimes medication."

### 5.3 Pre-Analytical Considerations

| Factor | Recommendation |
|--------|---------------|
| **Time of day** | Morning (7-10 AM) for cortisol, testosterone; specify for all others |
| **Fasting status** | Fasting 8-12 hours for insulin, C-peptide, lipid panel |
| **Menstrual cycle** | Specify cycle day for all reproductive hormones in cycling women |
| **Medications** | Document all medications; note potential drug-lab interactions |
| **Supplements** | Biotin interferes with many immunoassays; discontinue 48-72h prior |
| **Acute illness** | Defer non-urgent testing until recovery (non-thyroidal illness effects) |
| **Exercise** | Avoid strenuous exercise 24 hours before testing |
| **Alcohol** | Avoid alcohol 24 hours before testing |

### 5.4 Evidence Limitations and Future Directions

1. **Observational vs. Interventional:** Most brain-hormone associations are observational; RCT evidence for hormonal interventions improving cognition remains limited.
2. **Sex Differences:** Many studies have been underpowered for sex-specific analyses; results may not generalize across sexes.
3. **Timing Hypothesis:** The critical window concept for estrogen (and potentially testosterone) requires further validation.
4. **Individual Variation:** Genetic factors (APOE, VDR, AR polymorphisms) modify hormone-brain relationships and are rarely accounted for.
5. **Assay Standardization:** Methodology differences limit comparability across studies and laboratories.
