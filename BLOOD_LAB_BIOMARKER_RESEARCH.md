## 1. CBC Panel

### 1.1 Hemoglobin (Hgb, Hb)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Hemoglobin |
| **Abbreviations** | Hgb, Hb |
| **Reference Range** | Male: 13.8-17.2 g/dL; Female: 12.1-15.1 g/dL |
| **Conditions** | Anemia-related cognitive fatigue, depression, impaired concentration, restless legs syndrome (when iron-deficiency anemia present) |
| **Evidence** | B (RCT for iron supplementation in cognitive outcomes); C (observational for anemia-depression link) |
| **Clinical Status** | Routine lab |
| **Key Confounders** | Iron deficiency, B12/folate deficiency, chronic disease, altitude, smoking, pregnancy, hydration status |
| **Clinical Wording** | "Hemoglobin reflects oxygen-carrying capacity. Low levels may contribute to fatigue, reduced concentration, and depressive symptoms. When anemia is identified, further investigation into iron, B12, and folate status is recommended." |
| **Key References** | Tseng et al. (2018) Sci Rep meta-analysis; NHS/NIH Anemia guidelines; Cleveland Clinic guidelines |

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Mean Corpuscular Volume, Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin |
| **Abbreviations** | MCV, MCH |
| **Reference Range** | MCV: 80-100 fL; MCH: 27-34 pg/cell |
| **Conditions** | Macrocytosis (high MCV) associated with B12/folate deficiency-related neurological symptoms; Microcytosis with iron deficiency and cognitive impairment |

| **Key Confounders** | Alcohol use, liver disease, hypothyroidism, thalassemia, reticulocytosis, medications (e.g., methotrexate, phenytoin) |
| **Clinical Wording** | "MCV and MCH provide clues about red blood cell size and hemoglobin content. Elevated MCV may suggest B12 or folate deficiency, which can impact neurological function. Low MCV may indicate iron deficiency affecting cognitive performance." |
| **Key References** | LabCorp reference ranges; Cleveland Clinic nutritional deficiency neuropathy guidelines |

## 2. Iron Studies Panel

### 2.1 Ferritin

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Ferritin |
| **Abbreviations** | Ferritin, SF |
| **Reference Range** | Male: 30-400 ng/mL; Female: 15-150 ng/mL (age-dependent; varies by lab) |
| **Conditions** | Restless Legs Syndrome (RLS), ADHD (children), depression, fatigue, cognitive impairment, iron deficiency anemia |
| **Evidence** | A (Meta-analysis: Tseng et al. 2018 - significantly lower ferritin in ADHD children, Hedges' g = -0.246, p=0.013); B (RCTs: Trenkwalder et al. IV iron for RLS); C (multiple observational studies) |
| **Clinical Status** | Routine lab |
| **Key Confounders** | Acute phase reactant (elevated in inflammation/infection), liver disease, obesity, age, sex, dietary iron intake, supplementation status, time of day |
| **Clinical Wording** | "Ferritin is the primary marker of iron stores. Low ferritin has been associated with restless legs syndrome and attention difficulties in children. Levels should be interpreted alongside clinical symptoms and other iron parameters. Ferritin is also an acute phase reactant and may be elevated during inflammation." |
| **Key References** | Tseng et al. (2018) Sci Rep meta-analysis; Connor et al. (2017) PMC RLS iron review; Konofal et al. (2004) Arch Pediatr Adolesc Med; Earley et al. (2000) Neurology CSF ferritin study |

---

### 2.2 Serum Iron

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Serum Iron |
| **Abbreviations** | SI, Fe |
| **Reference Range** | 60-170 mcg/dL (male); 54-140 mcg/dL (female) (varies by lab) |
| **Conditions** | Iron deficiency anemia-related cognitive impairment, depression, RLS, ADHD (supporting evidence) |
| **Evidence** | C (observational); D (supporting) |
| **Clinical Status** | Routine lab |
| **Key Confounders** | Diurnal variation (highest in morning), recent iron intake, inflammation, liver disease, hemolysis, fasting status, oral contraceptives |
| **Clinical Wording** | "Serum iron reflects circulating iron levels and fluctuates throughout the day. It is typically assessed alongside ferritin and transferrin saturation for a comprehensive evaluation of iron status in relation to fatigue and neurological symptoms." |
| **Key References** | Wang et al. (2018) iron meta-analysis; WHO iron deficiency guidelines |

### 2.3 TIBC/Transferrin Saturation

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Total Iron Binding Capacity / Transferrin Saturation |
| **Abbreviations** | TIBC, TSAT, TS |
| **Reference Range** | TIBC: 240-450 mcg/dL; Transferrin Saturation: 20-50% |
| **Conditions** | Iron deficiency anemia, RLS, Parkinson's disease (discriminant analysis), restless legs syndrome |
| **Evidence** | C (observational: evidence-based discriminant analysis for Parkinson's - UIBC emerged as highest discriminating variable, sensitivity 98.73%); B (RCT for IV iron in RLS) |
| **Clinical Status** | Routine lab |
| **Key Confounders** | Oral contraceptives (increase TIBC), pregnancy, inflammation, liver disease, malnutrition, diurnal variation |
| **Clinical Wording** | "TIBC and transferrin saturation help characterize iron status. Low transferrin saturation suggests iron deficiency, which has been linked to restless legs syndrome and may contribute to cognitive symptoms. These markers are assessed alongside ferritin for comprehensive iron evaluation." |
| **Key References** | PMC8232484 (Evidence-Based Discriminant Analysis for PD); Trenkwalder et al. IV iron RCTs for RLS; Earley et al. CSF iron studies |

## 3. Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin) |
| **Abbreviations** | B12, Cbl, Cobalamin |
| **Reference Range** | 200-900 pg/mL (148-664 pmol/L) - cutoff varies by lab; some use >400 pg/mL as optimal |
| **Conditions** | Peripheral neuropathy, subacute combined degeneration, depression, cognitive impairment/dementia, memory loss, gait disorders, carpal tunnel syndrome, fatigue, anxiety, psychosis (rare), obsessive-compulsive disorder (via homocysteine elevation) |
| **Evidence** | A (Systematic review: neuropathy associated with lowered B12, pooled estimate 1.51 [1.23-1.84]); B (RCT: Kuzminski et al. 1998 oral vs. parenteral B12 equivalence; folate+B12 supplementation improved MMSE in cognitive impairment); C (multiple large observational studies); D (extensive case series) |
| **Clinical Status** | Routine lab |
| **Key Confounders** | Metformin use, PPI use, vegan/vegetarian diet, GI surgery (gastric bypass), pernicious anemia, age >60, nitrous oxide exposure, malabsorption disorders; methylmalonic acid (MMA) and homocysteine may be elevated when B12 is in low-normal range |
| **Clinical Wording** | "Vitamin B12 is essential for nerve function and neurotransmitter synthesis. Deficiency can present with neurological symptoms including numbness, memory difficulties, and mood changes. Early detection and treatment may lead to improvement or stabilization of symptoms. B12 levels in the low-normal range may still warrant attention when neurological symptoms are present, and additional markers such as methylmalonic acid may provide further clarity." |
| **Key References** | Medscape B12 Neurological Diseases (2025); Cureus systematic review (2025); Dekker et al. (2024) Food Nutr Bull case study; NHS B12 deficiency complications; Cleveland Clinic B12 deficiency guide; MedlinePlus B12 deficiency |

## 4. Folate (Serum)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Folate (Folic Acid) |
| **Abbreviations** | Folate, Folic Acid, FA |
| **Reference Range** | >3.0-4.0 ng/mL (>6.8-9.0 nmol/L) - varies by lab; optimal >4.0 ng/mL |
| **Conditions** | Depression, cognitive decline/dementia, OCD (mediates cognitive function), elevated homocysteine-related neurotoxicity, neural tube defects (pregnancy) |
| **Evidence** | B (RCT: folate supplementation 5mg/day improved MMSE from 20.1 to 22.2, p<0.001, effect size 0.59 in 28-63 days); A (meta-analysis: ~20% reduction in dementia risk with folic acid + B12 therapy per Ward et al.); C (strong observational: serum folate inversely related to dementia risk, OR 3.68 for folate <3.0 ng/mL) |
| **Clinical Status** | Routine lab |
| **Key Confounders** | B12 status (must be checked before folate supplementation to avoid masking B12 deficiency), alcohol use, pregnancy, malabsorption, medications (methotrexate, phenytoin, sulfasalazine), dietary intake |
| **Clinical Wording** | "Folate plays a critical role in brain function through its involvement in neurotransmitter synthesis and homocysteine metabolism. Low folate levels have been associated with depressive symptoms and cognitive changes. Folate supplementation should only be initiated after confirming B12 status to avoid masking a B12 deficiency." |
| **Key References** | PMC7602498 (folate supplementation and cognition); PMC3766893 (Homocysteine/folate and Korean dementia cohort); Nature (2025) OCD homocysteine/folate study; Douaud et al. PNAS 2013 (B-vitamins and gray matter atrophy) |

## 5. Vitamin D (25-OH Vitamin D)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | 25-Hydroxyvitamin D |
| **Abbreviations** | 25(OH)D, Vitamin D, VitD |
| **Reference Range** | Sufficient: >30 ng/mL (75 nmol/L); Insufficient: 20-30 ng/mL; Deficient: <20 ng/mL (<50 nmol/L) - (Endocrine Society/NIH) |
| **Conditions** | Depression, seasonal affective disorder, cognitive decline/dementia risk, schizophrenia (supporting evidence), chronic pain, fatigue |
| **Evidence** | A (Multiple meta-analyses: Mikola et al. 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis of RCTs for depression; Anglin et al. 2013 systematic review; Vellekkatt et al. 2019 meta-analysis); B (VITAL trial: no overall effect but benefit in those with low baseline D; subgroup Mendelian randomization suggests benefit in low-D individuals); A (Mendelian randomization: suggestive association with depression in low 25(OH)D stratum) |
| **Clinical Status** | Routine lab |
| **Key Confounders** | Sun exposure, season, latitude, skin pigmentation, BMI (adipose sequestration), age, malabsorption, liver/kidney disease, supplementation use; reverse causation (depressed individuals may spend less time outdoors) |
| **Clinical Wording** | "Vitamin D plays a role in neuroprotection and mood regulation through effects on serotonin synthesis and neurotrophic factors. Research suggests that supplementation may benefit individuals with documented deficiency, particularly in relation to depressive symptoms. Optimal levels may also support cognitive health." |
| **Key References** | Mikola et al. (2023) Crit Rev Food Sci Nutr meta-analysis; Anglin et al. (2013) Br J Psychiatry systematic review; Okereke et al. (2020) JAMA (VITAL trial); PMC10097210 (Vitamin D in Depression 2022 comprehensive review); Musazadeh et al. (2023) Pharmacol Res umbrella meta-analysis |

## 6. Magnesium (Serum)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Magnesium |
| **Abbreviations** | Mg, Serum Mg |
| **Reference Range** | 1.7-2.2 mg/dL (0.70-0.95 mmol/L) - varies by lab |
| **Conditions** | Depression, anxiety, migraine, neuropathic pain, insomnia, chronic fatigue, cognitive impairment, ADHD (supporting evidence), seizures (severe deficiency) |
| **Evidence** | A (Meta-analysis: dietary magnesium intake inversely associated with depression risk, pooled RR=0.81, dose-response at 320 mg/day); B (RCT: magnesium equivalent to imipramine for depression in elderly with diabetes; 2008 RCT); C (multiple observational studies: lower serum magnesium in anxiety/depression); D (extensive mechanism literature on NMDA receptor) |
| **Clinical Status** | Routine lab |
| **Key Confounders** | Only ~1% of body magnesium is in serum; normal serum Mg may not reflect intracellular/tissue status; renal function, alcohol use, diuretics, PPIs, malabsorption, stress, diabetes; red blood cell magnesium may be more representative |
| **Clinical Wording** | "Magnesium plays a fundamental role in nervous system function, including modulation of the NMDA receptor and neurotransmitter regulation. Low levels have been associated with anxiety, depressive symptoms, and migraine. It is important to note that serum magnesium may not fully reflect tissue stores, and normal levels do not exclude functional deficiency. Dietary intake assessment may provide additional information." |
| **Key References** | PMC6024559 (Magnesium in Neurological Disorders 2018); NCBI Magnesium in Neuroses (NBK507254); Huang et al. dose-response meta-analysis (depression); Tarleton et al. (2015) magnesium and depression RCTs; MDPI Nutrients 2025 comprehensive review |

## 7. Zinc (Serum)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Zinc |
| **Abbreviations** | Zn, Serum Zn |
| **Reference Range** | 70-120 mcg/dL (11-23 umol/L) - varies by age and lab |
| **Conditions** | Depression (robust evidence), ADHD, cognitive decline, anxiety, anorexia nervosa, psychosis (supporting), impaired taste/smell |
| **Evidence** | A (Meta-analysis: Swardfager et al. 2013 - zinc concentrations approximately -1.85 umol/L lower in depressed subjects, p<0.00001; 17 studies, n=1643 depressed, n=804 controls; greater severity = greater zinc deficiency); B (RCTs: 25mg zinc + antidepressants effective in women for major depression); C (observational for ADHD) |
| **Clinical Status** | Routine lab |
| **Key Confounders** | Acute phase reactant (decreased during infection/inflammation), albumin levels, dietary intake (vegetarian/vegan diets lower), PPI use, alcohol use, pregnancy, time of day, fasting status; copper levels should be assessed alongside zinc |
| **Clinical Wording** | "Zinc is an essential cofactor for neurotransmitter synthesis and immune function. Lower zinc levels have been associated with depression severity. Zinc supplementation may enhance antidepressant medication response in some individuals. Assessment of zinc status may be particularly relevant for individuals with depression who have risk factors for deficiency." |
| **Key References** | Swardfager et al. (2013) Biol Psychiatry meta-analysis; Cambridge Core systematic review (zinc in depressed pregnant/non-pregnant women); Psychiatry Redefined (zinc augmentation for antidepressants); Lai et al. (2012) zinc and depression meta-analysis |

## 8. Liver Function Panel

|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Alanine Aminotransferase |
| **Abbreviations** | ALT, SGPT, ALAT |
| **Reference Range** | 7-56 U/L (male), slightly lower for females - varies by lab |
| **Conditions** | Hepatic encephalopathy (indirect marker via liver synthetic function), medication-induced liver injury (psychiatric drug monitoring), NAFLD-associated cognitive decline |
| **Evidence** | C (observational for NAFLD-cognition); D (clinical practice guidelines for medication monitoring) |

| **Key References** | PMC5490964 (Hepatic Encephalopathy in Liver Cirrhosis); standard hepatology references |

## 9. Kidney Function Panel

|-----------|---------|

| **Key References** | PMC2593146 (Kidney Function and Cognitive Impairment in US Adults - REGARDS); PMC12914485 (CKD Severity and Cognitive Impairment - CRIC); PMC12827087 (BUN-albumin ratio and cognition in PD) |

### 9.2 eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate |
| **Abbreviations** | eGFR, GFR |
| **Reference Range** | Normal: >=90 mL/min/1.73m2; Stage 1: >=90 with proteinuria; Stage 2: 60-89; Stage 3a: 45-59; Stage 3b: 30-44; Stage 4: 15-29; Stage 5: <15 |
| **Conditions** | Uremic encephalopathy (eGFR <15), CKD-associated cognitive impairment (CKD-CI), vascular cognitive impairment, depression in CKD, Parkinson's disease cognitive decline |
| **Evidence** | C (REGARDS study: CKD associated with 23% increased prevalence of cognitive impairment, OR 1.23 [1.06-1.43]; each 10 mL/min decrease below 60 = 11% increased impairment); C (CRIC study: lower eGFR associated with attention/processing speed impairment); C (PPMI PD study: eGFR associated with MoCA scores) |
| **Clinical Status** | Routine lab (calculated from creatinine) |
| **Key Confounders** | Age, race/ethnicity, muscle mass, nutritional status, hydration; eGFRcr vs eGFRcys (cystatin C-based may be more sensitive for cognitive impairment assessment); does not capture microvascular injury alone |
| **Clinical Wording** | "eGFR estimates kidney filtration capacity. Reduced eGFR has been associated with cognitive changes, particularly affecting attention and processing speed. The combination of eGFR with urine protein assessment provides more comprehensive risk stratification for cognitive health. Many medications require dose adjustment based on eGFR." |
| **Key References** | PMC2593146 (REGARDS); PMC12914485 (CRIC cognitive impairment); ScienceDirect 2025 (renal function and PD cognition); Nature 2024 (CHARLS longitudinal CKD-CI evidence); StatPearls Uremic Encephalopathy |

### 9.3 BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Blood Urea Nitrogen |
| **Abbreviations** | BUN, Urea |
| **Reference Range** | 6-24 mg/dL (2.1-8.5 mmol/L) - varies by lab; BUN:Creatinine ratio: 10:1 to 20:1 |

| **Key References** | PMC11264492 (BUN/Cr ratio and cognition/depression - CHARLS); PMC12827087 (BUN-albumin ratio and PD cognition); StatPearls Uremic Encephalopathy; Front Neurol 2025 (BUN-albumin ratio PD) |

## 10. Glucose/Insulin Panel

### 10.1 HbA1c (Glycated Hemoglobin)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Glycated Hemoglobin A1c |
| **Abbreviations** | HbA1c, A1c, GHb |
| **Reference Range** | Normal: <5.7%; Prediabetes: 5.7-6.4%; Diabetes: >=6.5% |
| **Conditions** | Diabetes-associated cognitive decline/dementia, memory decline (even in non-diabetics), depression, vascular cognitive impairment |
| **Evidence** | A (Meta-analysis: diabetes associated with accelerated cognitive decline); B (HRS cohort n=8,888: each 1% increase in HbA1c = 0.05 SD decrease in memory per decade; higher HbA1c predicted memory decline even in non-diabetics below diabetes threshold); A (2024 JAMA: HbA1c time-in-range associated with dementia risk in older adults with diabetes); C (ARIC study: glycated hemoglobin and cognitive decline) |
| **Clinical Status** | Routine lab |
| **Key Confounders** | Hemoglobin variants (sickle cell, thalassemia), anemia, recent blood transfusion, pregnancy, renal failure, alcoholism, aspirin/ vitamin C/E use, hemolysis; race/ethnic differences in glycation rates |
| **Clinical Wording** | "HbA1c reflects average blood glucose over approximately three months. Elevated levels have been associated with accelerated memory decline, even in individuals without diabetes. Maintaining HbA1c within target range supports both metabolic and cognitive health. Results should be interpreted alongside current glucose levels." |

## Important Caveats for Clinical Practice

1. **Correlation vs. Causation:** Many biomarker-disease associations are observational. Abnormal levels may be consequences rather than causes of neurological/psychiatric conditions.

2. **Reverse Causation:** Depression may lead to poor nutrition (low zinc, B12, D), reduced outdoor activity (low vitamin D), or medication effects (elevated liver enzymes).

3. **Confounding by Indication:** Multiple comorbidities often cluster together (CKD, diabetes, depression, cognitive impairment).

4. **Reference Range Limitations:** Being within the reference range does not guarantee optimal function for neurological health. Individual variation matters.

5. **Supplementation Caution:** Not all deficiencies respond equally to supplementation. B12 repletion may not reverse established neurological damage. Folate supplementation without B12 assessment may mask deficiency.

6. **Medication Interactions:** Many psychiatric and neurological medications affect liver function, kidney function, and metabolic parameters. Regular monitoring is essential.
