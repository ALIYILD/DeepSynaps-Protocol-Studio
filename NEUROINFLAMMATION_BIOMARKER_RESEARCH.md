## 1. Executive Summary

This evidence matrix synthesizes current literature on 20 neuroinflammation biomarkers measurable in blood (serum/plasma) and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). These biomarkers reflect distinct pathophysiological processes: axonal injury, astrogliosis, demyelination, microglial activation, blood-brain barrier disruption, and complement-mediated inflammation. Evidence was sourced from PubMed/PMC, Europe PMC, NIH reports, and peer-reviewed journals (2017-2025).

### Key Findings

| Domain | Leading Biomarkers | Evidence Level |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| Axonal injury | NfL (serum/CSF) | A - Strong |
| Astrogliosis | GFAP (serum), YKL-40 (CSF) | A - Strong / B - Moderate |
| AD-specific pathology | p-Tau217, p-Tau181, A-beta42/40 | A - Strong |
| Demyelination | MBP (CSF), Oligoclonal Bands | B - Moderate / A - Strong |
| Microglial activation | sTREM2 (CSF), TSPO PET | B - Moderate |
| BBB integrity | Albumin Quotient, S100B | B - Moderate |
| Cytokine inflammation | IL-6, TNF-alpha, IL-10 | C - Emerging |
| Complement | C3, C4 | C - Emerging |
| Infectious/inflammatory | CXCL13 (CSF) | A - Strong (Lyme) |

### 3.1 Neurofilament Light Chain (NfL)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Neurofilament Light Chain |
| **Abbreviation** | NfL, NF-L, NEFL |
| **Alternative Names** | Neurofilament light polypeptide, 68 kDa neurofilament protein |
| **Sample Type** | Serum, Plasma (EDTA/heparin), CSF |
| **Normal Reference Range (Serum)** | Age-dependent: ~4 pg/mL (age 30) to ~19 pg/mL (age 80). Z-score >2.0 considered elevated. Per Benkert et al. (largest cohort, n=5390): 8.5 pg/mL at age 20; 23.8 pg/mL at age 60 (97.7th percentile). Reference interval in healthy Thai adults: 2.3-15.9 pg/mL overall; 20-29y: 1.7-8.7; 60-69y: 4.2-18.2. |
| **Normal Reference Range (CSF)** | Age-dependent: ~212 pg/mL (age 30) to ~1,243 pg/mL (age 80) |
| **Conditions** | **MS:** Strong correlate of disease activity, future disability, brain atrophy. Higher in RRMS than PPMS. Decreases with effective DMT (natalizumab, ocrelizumab). More reliable than MRI T2 lesion load for predicting progression. **TBI:** Elevated acutely; correlates with injury severity and outcome. **AD:** Elevated but non-specific; reflects neurodegeneration rather than AD-specific pathology. Also elevated in FTD, DLB. **ALS:** Elevated; correlates with disease progression and survival. **Stroke:** Elevated acutely. |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A - Strong** |
| **Clinical Status** | FDA-adjunct (not approved as standalone diagnostic). CE-marked assays available (Simoa, Lumipulse). Clinical adjunct for disease monitoring in MS and neurodegeneration. |
| **Key Confounders** | **Age** (strong - increases ~2x per decade after 50); **BMI** (weak inverse); **Kidney function** (reduced clearance elevates levels); **Blood volume/hemodilution**; **Recent strenuous exercise**; **Cardiovascular disease**; **Diabetes**. |
| **Safe Clinical Wording** | "Elevated NfL levels indicate ongoing neuroaxonal injury. NfL is a non-specific marker of neuronal damage and is not diagnostic for any single condition. Levels should be interpreted in the clinical context, considering age-adjusted reference ranges. Serial monitoring may help assess treatment response in MS." |
| **Key Assay Platforms** | Simoa HD-X (Quanterix), Lumipulse G (Fujirebio), ELISA (UmanDiagnostics), Ella (BioTechne) |

**Key References:**
- Benkert et al. (2020) - GAMLSS reference values, n=5390. PMC12062521.
- Cai L, Huang J (2018) - Meta-analysis NfL in MS. Neuropsychiatr Dis Treat 14:2241.
- Barro C et al. (2022) - Serum GFAP and NfL differentiate progression in progressive MS. Neurol Neuroimmunol Neuroinflamm 10:e200052.
- Khalil M et al. (2018, 2020) - Neurofilament light chain reference values and clinical utility.

### 3.2 Glial Fibrillary Acidic Protein (GFAP)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Glial Fibrillary Acidic Protein |
| **Abbreviation** | GFAP |
| **Alternative Names** | Astrocytic marker, intermediate filament protein |
| **Sample Type** | Serum, CSF |
| **Normal Reference Range (Serum)** | Median ~30 pg/mL in healthy controls (Lumipulse assay). Reference interval varies by assay. Healthy controls typically <40-70 pg/mL. NMOSD cut-off: >90 pg/mL (75th percentile of healthy controls predicts attack risk). |
| **Normal Reference Range (CSF)** | Very low/undetectable in healthy individuals |
| **Conditions** | **TBI:** Strong diagnostic and prognostic marker. Peaks at ~20h, declines over 72h. Serum GFAP on admission AUC 0.76-0.85 for predicting 6-month outcome. Cut-off ~1.5-1.7 ng/mL for severe TBI. **AD:** Elevated; correlates with A-beta deposition. GFAP >31.4 pg/mL showed 90.4% sensitivity, 82.1% specificity for AD diagnosis in one study. AUC 0.928. Distinguishes AD from MCI. **Stroke:** AIS patients median 132.9 vs 30.0 pg/mL in controls. AUC 0.88. Cut-off 71 pg/mL (74% sensitivity, 92% specificity). Correlates with NIHSS (rho=0.37-0.40). **MS:** Moderate elevations; serum GFAP and NfL together differentiate progressive MS. **NMOSD:** Predicts clinical relapses; elevated >90 pg/mL at baseline = 3-fold higher risk of attack. |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A - Strong** |
| **Clinical Status** | CE-marked assays available (Lumipulse G GFAP). FDA 510(k) clearance for TBI triage (Banyan BTI). Clinical adjunct for astrocytic injury assessment. |
| **Key Confounders** | **Age** (increases with age); **Peripheral trauma** (may cause false elevation from extracerebral sources); **Time since injury** (rapid clearance); **Assay platform** (values not interchangeable between platforms). |
| **Safe Clinical Wording** | "GFAP is a marker of astrocytic injury and activation. Elevated serum GFAP suggests the presence of brain tissue injury, including conditions such as traumatic brain injury, stroke, or neurodegenerative disease. GFAP should be interpreted alongside clinical presentation and imaging. It is not specific for any single neurological diagnosis." |
| **Key Assay Platforms** | Lumipulse G GFAP (Fujirebio), Simoa GFAP Discovery assay (Quanterix), ELISA (BioVendor) |

**Key References:**
- Papa L et al. (2016) - GFAP outperforms S100B in predicting CT lesions after TBI. J Neurotrauma.
- Zetterberg H et al. (2019, 2023) - GFAP as astrocytic biomarker in AD.
- Abdelhak A et al. (2022) - GFAP as a diagnostic biomarker. Front Neurol.

### 3.3 S100 Calcium-Binding Protein B (S100B)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | S100 Calcium-Binding Protein B |
| **Abbreviation** | S100B |
| **Alternative Names** | S-100B, S100 protein beta-beta homodimer |
| **Sample Type** | Serum, CSF, Capillary blood, Urine |
| **Normal Reference Range (Serum)** | <0.1 ug/L (100 ng/L) in adults. Healthy control serum: ~0.328 +/- 0.101 pg/mL. Values vary by laboratory. Pediatric values are age-dependent and generally higher due to active CNS development. |
| **Normal Reference Range (CSF)** | ~0.075 +/- 0.003 ng/mL in healthy controls |
| **Conditions** | **TBI:** Primary indication. Scandinavian guidelines use S100B <0.1 ug/L at <6h post-injury to safely rule out CT-visible intracranial lesions in mild TBI (saves ~30% of CT scans). Meta-analysis suggests higher cutoffs (0.16-0.20 ug/L) improve specificity to ~51%. Serum levels 2.16-14.0 ug/L predict unfavorable outcome in moderate-severe TBI. **Stroke:** Elevated in acute stroke. **Melanoma:** Used as tumor marker for monitoring. |
| **Evidence Strength** | **B - Moderate to Strong (TBI-specific)** |
| **Clinical Status** | Incorporated into Scandinavian CT guidelines for mild TBI (SNC 2013, updated). CE-marked assays available. Clinical adjunct for TBI triage. NOT FDA-approved as standalone diagnostic. |
| **Key Confounders** | **Age** (pediatric values much higher); **Skin pigmentation** (melanin-related false elevations); **Multiple trauma** (fractures elevate S100B); **Time since injury** (short half-life, ~30-60 min); **Extracerebral sources** (adipose tissue, bone marrow, skin); **Intra- and inter-assay variability**. |
| **Safe Clinical Wording** | "S100B is a calcium-binding protein elevated after brain injury. In the context of mild traumatic brain injury within 6 hours of trauma, a normal S100B level can support clinical decision-making regarding the need for CT imaging. However, S100B is not specific for brain injury and may be elevated from extracerebral sources. It should always be used in conjunction with clinical examination." |
| **Key Assay Platforms** | Elecsys S100 (Roche), LIAISON S100 (DiaSorin), ELISA platforms |

**Key References:**
- Unden J et al. (2013, 2019) - Scandinavian S100B guidelines for mTBI.
- Thelin EP et al. (2017) - A review of the clinical utility of S100B. PMC5241347.
- Biberthaler P et al. (2001, 2006) - S100B in TBI outcome prediction.

### 3.4 Total Tau (t-Tau)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Total Tau Protein |
| **Abbreviation** | t-Tau |
| **Alternative Names** | T-tau, tau protein, microtubule-associated protein tau |
| **Sample Type** | CSF (primary), Plasma (limited utility) |
| **Normal Reference Range (CSF)** | Age-dependent: increases with age. Generally <300-500 pg/mL in younger adults; higher in elderly. |
| **Normal Reference Range (Plasma)** | Emerging assays; limited clinical utility currently |
| **Conditions** | **AD:** Elevated CSF t-tau is a core AT(N) framework biomarker. Correlates with neurofibrillary tangle burden and neuronal loss. **FTD:** May also be elevated but typically less than AD. **CJD:** Markedly elevated - among the highest t-tau levels seen neurologically. **TBI:** Acutely elevated. **Stroke:** Elevated acutely. |
| **Evidence Strength** | **B - Moderate (AD); A - Strong (CJD)** |
| **Clinical Status** | Research/clinical adjunct. Core CSF biomarker in AT(N) framework. Not FDA-approved as blood test. |
| **Key Confounders** | **Age** (strong increase with age); **Renal function** (affects clearance); **Hemolysis** (affects CSF measurements); **Time of day** (circadian variation reported); **Sample handling** (tau is sticky, adsorbs to surfaces). |
| **Safe Clinical Wording** | "Total tau in CSF reflects the intensity of neuronal/synaptic injury. Elevated levels are seen in several neurodegenerative conditions, particularly Alzheimer's disease and Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease. CSF t-tau should be interpreted as part of a comprehensive biomarker panel rather than in isolation." |

**Key References:**
- Jack CR Jr et al. (2018) - AT(N) research framework. Alzheimers Dement.
- Zetterberg H, Blennow K (2018, 2023) - CSF biomarkers in neurodegeneration.

### 3.5 Phosphorylated Tau (p-Tau181, p-Tau217, p-Tau231)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Phosphorylated Tau at epitopes 181, 217, or 231 |
| **Abbreviation** | p-Tau181, p-Tau217, p-Tau231 |
| **Alternative Names** | Phospho-tau, p-tau species |
| **Sample Type** | Plasma (primary), CSF |
| **Normal Reference Range (Plasma p-Tau217)** | Assay-dependent. ALZpath Simoa: Cut-off ~0.50 pg/mL for A-beta positivity. 3-range classification: low (<0.50), intermediate (0.50-1.50), high (>1.50). |
| **Normal Reference Range (Plasma p-Tau181)** | Assay-dependent. Simoa: ~2.36 pg/mL cut-off for A-beta positivity. |
| **Normal Reference Range (Plasma p-Tau231)** | Assay-dependent. Earlier marker of tau pathology than p-Tau217/181. |
| **Conditions** | **AD:** All three p-tau species are elevated in AD, but p-Tau217 currently shows the strongest diagnostic performance. Plasma p-Tau217 AUC 0.92-0.98 for predicting amyloid/tau PET positivity. Non-inferior to CSF p-Tau217. Superior to p-Tau181 and p-Tau231 for identifying amyloid status. p-Tau217 better distinguishes AD from non-AD dementias (90% accuracy vs 78% for p-Tau181). p-Tau231 is an early marker of tau phosphorylation, useful for identifying tau-PET positivity. **Non-AD dementias:** Lower levels typically seen in FTD, PSP, CBS, DLB. |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A - Strong (p-Tau217); B - Moderate (p-Tau181/231)** |
| **Clinical Status** | **p-Tau217:** FDA-approved (2024) for AD risk assessment. Commercially available (ALZpath Simoa, C2N Diagnostics PrecivityAD2). **p-Tau181/231:** Research/clinical adjunct use. |
| **Key Confounders** | **Age** (increases after ~65-70; rate 12x faster in amyloid-positive); **BMI** (inverse relationship - lower BMI associated with higher p-Tau217); **Sex** (mixed results; males may show higher levels in CU individuals); **APOE4 status** (carriers show higher levels); **Renal function**; **Assay platform** (values NOT interchangeable). |
| **Safe Clinical Wording** | "Plasma p-Tau217 is a biomarker that reflects abnormal tau phosphorylation associated with Alzheimer's disease pathology. Elevated levels suggest the presence of amyloid and tau pathology in the brain. A p-Tau217 test can support, but not replace, clinical evaluation for Alzheimer's disease. Results should be interpreted by a dementia specialist, considering age, kidney function, and overall clinical picture. A normal result does not rule out all causes of cognitive impairment." |
| **Key Assay Platforms** | ALZpath p-Tau217 Simoa (Quanterix), Lumipulse p-Tau217 (Fujirebio), C2N PrecivityAD2 (mass spectrometry), MSD S-PLEX |

**Key References:**
- Ashton NJ et al. (2024) - Diagnostic accuracy of plasma p-Tau217. JAMA Neurol 81:255-263.
- Janelidze S et al. (2023) - Head-to-head comparison of 10 plasma p-tau assays. Brain 146:1592.
- NIHR Evidence (2026) - Blood test predicts start of Alzheimer's symptoms. PMID: 41714746.
- Petersen KK et al. (2026) - Plasma p-tau217 clocks. Nat Med.
- Schindler SE et al. (2024) - Highly accurate blood test for AD. Nat Med 30:1085-1095.

### 3.6 Beta-Amyloid 42/40 Ratio (Plasma)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Beta-Amyloid Peptide 42 to 40 Ratio |
| **Abbreviation** | A-beta42/40, Abeta42/40 |
| **Alternative Names** | Plasma amyloid ratio, A-beta ratio |
| **Sample Type** | Plasma, CSF |
| **Normal Reference Range (Plasma)** | LC-MS/MS: >0.170 considered negative/low risk for amyloid pathology. 0.150-0.169 indeterminate. <0.150 positive/high risk. Simoa-based: ~0.045-0.047 threshold for amyloid positivity. Community-dwelling older adults median: 0.113 (IQR 0.103-0.123). |
| **Normal Reference Range (CSF)** | >0.05 generally considered normal; varies by assay |
| **Conditions** | **AD:** Decreased plasma A-beta42/40 ratio is the earliest detectable blood-based change in AD, preceding cognitive symptoms by years. Reflects sequestration of A-beta42 into brain plaques. Ratio <0.107 associated with cognitive decline. **Preclinical AD:** May identify individuals at risk years before symptoms. |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A - Strong** |
| **Clinical Status** | Clinical adjunct. FDA breakthrough device designation for some assays. Available through PrecivityAD (C2N Diagnostics), Simoa platforms. Not yet standard of care as standalone. |
| **Key Confounders** | **Age** (ratio decreases with age); **APOE4** (carriers have lower ratios); **Assay platform** (mass spectrometry vs immunoassay values not interchangeable); **Pre-analytical handling** (platelet contamination affects plasma A-beta levels); **Anticoagulant** (EDTA vs heparin affects values); **Time of collection** (circadian variation). |
| **Safe Clinical Wording** | "The plasma A-beta42/40 ratio is a screening biomarker for amyloid pathology associated with Alzheimer's disease. A low ratio suggests accumulation of amyloid plaques in the brain. This test can help identify individuals who may benefit from further evaluation, but it does not diagnose Alzheimer's disease. Confirmatory testing with amyloid PET or CSF analysis may be recommended for intermediate or positive results." |

**Key References:**
- Janelidze S et al. (2021, 2023) - Plasma Abeta42/40 head-to-head comparisons. JAMA Neurol.
- Schindler SE et al. (2019) - High-precision plasma Abeta42/40 predicts amyloidosis. Neurology.
- Nakamura A et al. (2018) - High performance plasma amyloid biomarkers. Nature 554:249.
- Leuzy A et al. (2023) - Threshold definition for plasma Abeta42/40. PMC10816992.

### 3.7 Myelin Basic Protein (MBP)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Myelin Basic Protein |
| **Abbreviation** | MBP |
| **Alternative Names** | Basic A1 protein, encephalitogenic protein |
| **Sample Type** | CSF (primary; serum lacks clinical utility) |
| **Normal Reference Range (CSF)** | 0.0 - 5.5 ng/mL (ARUP reference laboratory). Undetectable or very low in healthy individuals. |
| **Conditions** | **MS:** Elevated during acute relapses (within 7 days of symptom onset). NOT specific for MS - elevated in any demyelinating condition. **ADEM:** Elevated. **Encephalitis:** May be elevated. **Acute cerebral infarction:** Elevated. **CNS trauma:** Elevated. **Spinal cord demyelination:** Elevated. |
| **Evidence Strength** | **C - Limited** |
| **Clinical Status** | Research-only. NOT included in 2024 McDonald criteria for MS diagnosis. Superseded by OCB and kappa-FLC index. |
| **Key Confounders** | **Hemolysis** (causes false elevation); **Timing of LP** (must be within 7 days of acute demyelination for optimal sensitivity); **Blood contamination** of CSF. |
| **Safe Clinical Wording** | "MBP in CSF indicates active myelin breakdown. Elevated levels suggest recent demyelinating injury but are not specific for multiple sclerosis. MBP testing has been largely replaced by oligoclonal band and kappa free light chain analysis in modern MS diagnostic workup." |

### 3.8 YKL-40 (CHI3L1)

### 3.9 Complement C3

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Complement Component 3 |
| **Abbreviation** | C3 |
| **Alternative Names** | Third component of complement, C3a, C3b |
| **Sample Type** | Serum, CSF, Plasma |
| **Normal Reference Range (Serum)** | 0.9 - 1.8 g/L (adults). Reference ranges vary by laboratory. |
| **Normal Reference Range (CSF)** | Low levels normally; increases with BBB disruption and intrathecal synthesis |
| **Conditions** | **MS:** Elevated plasma C3 in progressive MS (PPMS, SPMS). CSF C3 correlates with EDSS disability score and MRI lesion count (>9 cerebral lesions). Highest levels in PPMS. **NMOSD:** Elevated plasma C3. **Stroke:** Elevated; part of acute inflammatory response. **AD:** Some evidence of complement dysregulation. |
| **Evidence Strength** | **C - Emerging** |
| **Clinical Status** | Research-only. Standard clinical chemistry assay available but not validated for neurological diagnostic use. |
| **Key Confounders** | **Acute phase response** (C3 is an acute phase reactant); **Autoimmune disease** (SLE, RA elevate C3 consumption); **Infection** (activates complement); **Liver disease** (C3 synthesized in liver); **Age** (slight decrease with age). |
| **Safe Clinical Wording** | "Complement C3 is part of the innate immune system. Altered levels have been observed in some neurological conditions, particularly multiple sclerosis, but C3 is not currently recommended as a standalone diagnostic biomarker for neurological disease. Elevated C3 may reflect systemic inflammation rather than CNS-specific pathology." |

| **Abbreviation** | C4 |
| **Alternative Names** | Fourth component of complement, C4a, C4b, C4d |

### 3.11 Soluble TREM2 (sTREM2)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Soluble Triggering Receptor Expressed on Myeloid Cells 2 |
| **Abbreviation** | sTREM2 |
| **Alternative Names** | TREM2 ectodomain, soluble TREM2 fragment |
| **Sample Type** | CSF (primary), Plasma |
| **Normal Reference Range (CSF)** | Assay-dependent. sTREM2 increases with age. |
| **Conditions** | **AD:** Elevated CSF sTREM2 peaks at early symptomatic phase (MCI stage). Positively correlated with CSF t-tau and p-tau. Reflects microglial activation around amyloid plaques. Higher CSF sTREM2 associated with slower amyloid accumulation (suggesting protective microglial response). Associated with tau-dependent pathway. **FTD:** Elevated (especially GRN carriers). **PD:** Some elevation reported. **MS:** Variable findings. |
| **Evidence Strength** | **B - Moderate** |
| **Clinical Status** | Research-only. No commercially validated clinical assay. Core biomarker in research studies of microglial biology. |
| **Key Confounders** | **Age** (strongly correlated); **TREM2 genotype** (R47H carriers have different baseline); **CSF collection methods**; **Assay platform** (only research-grade ELISAs available). |
| **Safe Clinical Wording** | "Soluble TREM2 in CSF reflects microglial activation, which is part of the brain's innate immune response. Elevated levels have been observed in early-stage Alzheimer's disease and some other neurodegenerative conditions. sTREM2 is a research biomarker and is not yet available for routine clinical use." |

### 3.17 CXCL13

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | C-X-C Motif Chemokine Ligand 13 |
| **Abbreviation** | CXCL13, BLC, BCA-1 |
| **Alternative Names** | B-lymphocyte chemoattractant, B cell-attracting chemokine 1 |
| **Sample Type** | CSF (primary) |
| **Normal Reference Range (CSF)** | <35 pg/mL (undetectable to very low). Cut-off for neuroborreliosis: 271 pg/mL (Euroimmun ELISA). Median in controls: 0 pg/mL (IQR 0-8). Median in neuroborreliosis: 2,384 pg/mL (IQR 795-9,010). |

### 3.18 Oligoclonal Bands (OCB)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Oligoclonal Immunoglobulin G Bands |
| **Abbreviation** | OCB, OCBs |
| **Alternative Names** | CSF-restricted oligoclonal bands, intrathecal IgG synthesis markers |
| **Sample Type** | CSF (requires paired serum sample) |
| **Normal Reference** | Type 1 pattern: no bands in CSF or serum. Type 2/3 patterns (>2 bands in CSF only) indicate intrathecal IgG synthesis and are abnormal. |
| **Conditions** | **MS:** Present in >90% of clinically definite MS cases. Type 2/3 pattern is highly characteristic. Hallmark feature incorporated into McDonald diagnostic criteria. **NMOSD:** Present in ~25% or less. **MOGAD:** Present in ~10%. **CNS infections:** May be present. **CNS autoimmune conditions:** Variable. **CNS lymphoma:** May show restricted bands. |
| **Evidence Strength** | **A - Strong** |
| **Clinical Status** | **Clinical standard** for MS diagnosis (2024 McDonald criteria). Widely available. Requires specialized CSF laboratory with isoelectric focusing and immunoblotting expertise. |
| **Key Confounders** | **Contamination** from serum (requires proper collection technique); **Timing** (may be negative very early in disease); **Technical quality** (requires experienced laboratory); **Blood in CSF** (can obscure interpretation). |
| **Safe Clinical Wording** | "Oligoclonal bands in CSF indicate intrathecal immunoglobulin synthesis - that is, antibody production within the central nervous system. The presence of oligoclonal bands that are unique to CSF (not found in the blood) is a characteristic finding in multiple sclerosis and is incorporated into the diagnostic criteria. However, oligoclonal bands can also be seen in other CNS inflammatory conditions, including infections and autoimmune disorders. Results should be interpreted by a neurologist in the context of clinical presentation and MRI findings." |

### 3.19 IgG Index

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | Immunoglobulin G Index |
| **Abbreviation** | IgG Index |
| **Alternative Names** | CSF IgG synthesis rate, Reibergram IgG quotient |
| **Sample Type** | CSF and Serum (paired samples required) |
| **Normal Reference Range** | <0.7 (most laboratories). Age-dependent; values >0.7 suggest intrathecal IgG synthesis. |
| **Conditions** | **MS:** Elevated IgG index is supportive of MS diagnosis (McDonald criteria). Reflects chronic intrathecal immunoglobulin synthesis. **Other CNS inflammatory conditions:** May be elevated in neuroborreliosis, neurosyphilis, CNS lupus. **CNS infections:** Variable elevation. |

| **Safe Clinical Wording** | "The IgG index is a calculation that assesses whether immunoglobulin G is being produced within the central nervous system. The IgG index is interpreted alongside oligoclonal band testing and clinical findings." |

### 3.20 Albumin Quotient (QAlb)

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Full Name** | CSF/Serum Albumin Quotient |
| **Abbreviation** | QAlb, Alb Quotient, Albumin Ratio |
| **Alternative Names** | Blood-CSF barrier function index, Albumin quotient |
| **Sample Type** | CSF and Serum (paired samples required) |
| **Normal Reference Range** | Age-dependent. Formula: URL = age/25 + 8. Examples: Age 18-70: median 4.1-6.1, URL (95th percentile) 8.7-11.0. Age 15-40: <6.5. Age 40-60: <8.0. Age >60: <9.0. QAlb >100 = severe BBB dysfunction. |
| **Conditions** | **MS:** Mild-moderate elevation possible. Not a primary MS biomarker but useful for BBB assessment. **Neuroborreliosis:** Elevated. **CNS infections:** Elevated. **GBS/AIDP:** Elevated. **Stroke:** May be elevated acutely. **Any condition with BBB disruption.** |

| **Clinical Status** | Clinical adjunct. Part of routine CSF analysis. Automated nephelometric assays widely available. |
| **Key Confounders** | **Age** (strong increase with age - must use age-corrected reference); **Blood contamination** of CSF; **Collection technique** (traumatic LP); **Fever** (can transiently affect BBB); **Systemic hypoalbuminemia** (affects calculation). |
| **Safe Clinical Wording** | "The albumin quotient assesses the integrity of the blood-brain barrier. An elevated quotient suggests increased permeability of the barrier that normally protects the brain. This can occur in various conditions, including infection, inflammation, and vascular injury. The albumin quotient should be interpreted with age-adjusted reference ranges and in the clinical context." |

## 4. Combination Panels

### 4.1 NfL + GFAP Combination (Alzheimer's and Neurodegeneration)

| Aspect | Evidence |
|--------|----------|
| **Rationale** | NfL reflects neuroaxonal injury; GFAP reflects astrocytic activation. Combined, they capture complementary pathophysiological processes. |
| **AD Diagnosis** | Combined GFAP + NfL AUC 0.931 (vs 0.928 GFAP alone, vs lower for NfL alone). GFAP >31.4 pg/mL + NfL >40.09 pg/mL: 78.8% sensitivity, 92.3% specificity. GFAP distinguishes AD from MCI; NfL does not. |
| **AD-Tau Pathway** | p-Tau217 + GFAP + NfL panel: p-Tau217 identifies A-beta/tau stages; GFAP promotes tau progression; NfL captures non-specific neuronal injury. |
| **MS Monitoring** | Both markers decrease with effective DMT. NfL more sensitive for axonal damage; GFAP tracks astrocytic response. |
| **Clinical Status** | Emerging clinical adjunct. Available on some platforms as combined panels. |

### 4.2 AT(N) Framework

| Biomarker Category | Blood Equivalent | CSF Equivalent |
|-------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| A - Amyloid | A-beta42/40 ratio | A-beta42/40, A-beta42 |
| T - Tau | p-Tau217, p-Tau181 | p-Tau, t-Tau |
| N - Neurodegeneration | NfL | NfL, t-Tau |
| Additional: Glial | GFAP | YKL-40, GFAP |

### 4.3 Recommended Biomarker Panels by Clinical Scenario

| Clinical Scenario | Primary Panel | Confirmatory Testing |
|-------------------|--------------|---------------------|
| AD diagnostic workup | Plasma p-Tau217 + A-beta42/40 | Amyloid PET or CSF AT(N) |
| MS diagnosis | Serum NfL + CSF OCB/IgG Index + QAlb | MRI, clinical criteria |
| MS treatment monitoring | Serum NfL (serial) | MRI, clinical assessment |
| TBI triage (mild) | S100B (<6h) + clinical rules | CT if positive |

| Stroke (diagnostic) | Serum GFAP | Neuroimaging |
| Neuroborreliosis | CSF CXCL13 + QAlb + CSF pleocytosis | Borrelia antibody index |
| ALS monitoring | Serum NfL | EMG, clinical assessment |
| Parkinson's differential | Serum NfL + DaTscan | Clinical evaluation |
| Suspected CJD | CSF t-Tau (markedly elevated) + RT-QuIC | MRI, EEG |

## 6. Confounders and Pre-Analytical Considerations

### 6.1 Universal Confounders (All Biomarkers)

| Confounder | Impact | Mitigation |
|------------|--------|------------|
| Age | Strong effect on NfL, GFAP, p-Tau, QAlb | Use age-adjusted reference ranges/Z-scores |
| BMI | Inverse with NfL and p-Tau217 | Consider in interpretation; not all labs adjust |
| Sex | Moderate effect on some markers | Use sex-specific reference where available |
| Renal function | Reduces clearance of NfL, other proteins | Check eGFR; elevated NfL in CKD |
| Time of day | Affects some cytokines | Standardize collection time |
| Exercise | Acutely elevates NfL, cytokines | Fasting sample; avoid post-exercise collection |
| Hemolysis | Interferes with many assays | Proper collection; inspect sample |
| Storage/handling | Affects labile markers (cytokines) | Freeze promptly; minimize freeze-thaw |

### 6.2 Pre-Analytical Requirements

| Biomarker | Tube Type | Processing | Stability |
|-----------|-----------|------------|-----------|
| NfL (serum) | SST/gold top | Centrifuge within 2h | -80C >1 year |
| NfL (plasma) | EDTA | Centrifuge within 1h | -80C >1 year |
| GFAP | Serum | Centrifuge within 1h | -80C stable |
| p-Tau217 | EDTA plasma | Centrifuge within 1h; avoid freeze-thaw | -80C >1 year |
| A-beta42/40 | EDTA + protease inhibitor | Centrifuge; careful handling | -80C; sensitive to handling |
| S100B | Serum | Centrifuge promptly | -80C stable |
| CSF biomarkers | Sterile tube for CSF | Process immediately | -80C preferred |

## 7. Evidence Strength Classification

| Grade | Description | Criteria |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **A - Strong** | Established evidence; consistent findings across multiple large studies; clinical utility demonstrated | Meta-analyses, randomized trials, large cohort studies (>500 participants); validated cutoffs |
| **B - Moderate** | Good evidence; consistent findings but limited validation or smaller studies | Cohort studies, case-control studies; some clinical validation |
| **C - Emerging** | Preliminary evidence; inconsistent findings; primarily research use | Pilot studies, single-center studies; no validated clinical cutoffs |
| **D - Limited** | Minimal evidence; conflicting data; hypothesis-generating only | Small studies, preclinical data |

### Evidence Strength by Biomarker

| Biomarker | Grade | Basis |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| NfL | A | Multiple meta-analyses; >100 studies; validated platforms; clinical use in MS monitoring |
| GFAP | A | Strong TBI and AD evidence; FDA 510(k) for TBI; CE-marked |
