## 1. ABC Recording

### Framework Overview

ABC Recording (Antecedent-Behaviour-Consequence) is a foundational structured observation method used to track and understand patterns of challenging behaviour. It is a core component of person-centred care and applied behaviour analysis (ABA). The framework treats behaviour as a form of communication, helping clinicians identify triggers and environmental factors that maintain behavioural episodes.

The ABC approach involves recording three sequential elements for each behavioural event:
- **Antecedent (A):** Events, conditions, or triggers that occurred immediately before the behaviour
- **Behaviour (B):** An objective, descriptive account of the behaviour itself (without value judgements)
- **Consequence (C):** What occurred immediately after the behaviour, including environmental and caregiver responses

### Evidence Strength: **B**

ABC recording has been validated across numerous clinical and educational settings. Multiple systematic reviews and clinical practice guidelines support its use in functional behaviour assessment (FBA) procedures. The framework is particularly well-validated for use with individuals with intellectual disabilities, autism spectrum disorder (ASD), dementia, and learning disabilities. Studies have shown that consistent ABC recording significantly improves the accuracy of functional hypothesis generation when compared to informal observation alone. The method is endorsed by the British Psychological Society (BPS), the Behavior Analyst Certification Board (BACB), and NICE guidelines for behaviour management.

### Clinical-Use Status

**Status:** Widely adopted in clinical practice  
**Settings:** Care homes, schools, home care, community mental health, ABA clinics, paediatric psychology  
**Target Populations:** Autism, ADHD, intellectual disability, dementia, learning disabilities, challenging behaviours across the lifespan  
**Practitioner Types:** BCBAs, clinical psychologists, registered behaviour technicians, care providers, educators, occupational therapists

ABC recording is one of the most widely used structured observation tools in applied behaviour analysis and developmental services. It is considered a prerequisite skill for Board Certified Behaviour Analysts (BCBAs) and is routinely required in educational settings for students with behavioural support plans.

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Description | Recording Method |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Antecedent triggers | Time, location, people present, activities, environmental conditions (noise, temperature, lighting), recent meals, medication status, routine disruptions | Narrative with structured prompts |
| Behaviour topography | Objective description of observable behaviours (e.g., "threw pencils across room, shouted, banged head against wall four times") | Descriptive narrative avoiding pejorative labels |
| Consequence events | Caregiver responses, peer reactions, environmental changes, redirection outcomes, de-escalation strategies | Sequential narrative recording |
| Temporal patterns | Time of day, day of week, duration of episodes, frequency trends | Timestamp logging |
| Setting events | Broader contextual factors (sleep quality, health status, medication changes, environmental stressors) | Background contextual notes |

### Safe Clinical Wording

- Use objective, descriptive language without value judgements (e.g., avoid terms like "tantrum," "meltdown," "non-compliant")
- Record observable behaviours only: "John threw pencils across the room" rather than "John was being difficult"
- Frame behaviour as communication: "The behaviour may indicate distress or unmet need"
- Avoid diagnostic or causal language when describing patterns: note correlation, not causation
- Maintain person-first language unless the individual prefers identity-first language
- Document consequences as environmental responses, not punishments
- Include protective factors and strengths alongside challenging behaviours

### Integration with Digital Phenotyping

ABC recording maps directly to multiple digital phenotyping streams:

- **Smartphone sensors:** GPS can identify location-based triggers; accelerometer data can capture activity level changes preceding behavioural episodes
- **Environmental sensors:** Ambient noise, light, and temperature sensors can automatically log setting conditions
- **Wearable devices:** Heart rate variability (HRV) and electrodermal activity (EDA) may detect physiological arousal preceding antecedent events
- **Electronic health records (EHR):** Integration with medication logs, sleep data, and appointment schedules can identify temporal setting events
- **Machine learning applications:** Pattern recognition across ABC datasets can predict high-risk contexts and generate pre-emptive alerts for caregivers
- **Voice and video analysis:** Automated transcription and emotion detection from incident recordings can supplement written ABC narratives

## 2. Functional Behaviour Assessment (FBA)

### Framework Overview

Functional Behaviour Assessment (FBA) is a comprehensive, systematic process for identifying the purpose or function of challenging behaviour. It goes beyond simple behaviour tracking to develop hypotheses about why behaviours occur, based on understanding the relationship between behaviour and environmental variables. FBA integrates indirect assessments, descriptive assessments, and functional analysis to build a complete picture of behavioural function.

The FBA process follows a structured sequence:
1. **Indirect Assessment:** Information gathering through interviews, questionnaires, and record reviews (e.g., Functional Assessment Interview - FAI, Questions About Behavioural Function - QABF, Functional Analysis Screening Tool - FAST, Motivation Assessment Scale - MAS)
2. **Descriptive Assessment:** Direct observation using ABC recording, scatterplots, and structured observation protocols in natural settings
3. **Functional Analysis (if needed):** Experimental manipulation of antecedent and consequent conditions to confirm behavioural function
4. **Hypothesis Development:** Integration of data to generate testable hypotheses about the maintaining variables
5. **Behaviour Intervention Plan (BIP):** Development of function-based intervention strategies

The four primary hypothesised functions of behaviour are:
- **Attention:** Behaviour maintained by social interaction or acknowledgement
- **Escape/Avoidance:** Behaviour maintained by removal of demands or aversive stimuli
- **Access to tangibles:** Behaviour maintained by obtaining preferred items or activities
- **Automatic/Sensory:** Behaviour maintained by internal sensory consequences

### Evidence Strength: **A**

FBA has one of the strongest evidence bases in behavioural science. Meta-analyses and systematic reviews consistently demonstrate that function-based interventions derived from FBA are significantly more effective than non-function-based approaches. The framework is mandated by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) in the United States for students with disabilities whose behaviour impedes learning. Multiple randomised controlled trials have validated the effectiveness of FBA-based interventions across clinical, educational, and residential settings. The approach is endorsed by the BACB, the American Psychological Association (APA), and numerous international regulatory bodies.

### Clinical-Use Status

**Status:** Gold-standard assessment method; legally mandated in educational settings (US)  
**Settings:** Schools, ABA clinics, residential facilities, community mental health, developmental services, forensic settings  
**Target Populations:** Autism, intellectual disability, ADHD, emotional and behavioural disorders, challenging behaviours across the lifespan  
**Practitioner Types:** BCBAs, school psychologists, clinical psychologists, behaviour analysts, special educators

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Description | Assessment Tool |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Behaviour topography | Detailed, operationalised definition of target behaviours | Operational definitions, behavioural interview |
| Functional hypotheses | Identified antecedent and consequent maintaining variables | FAI, QABF, FAST, MAS, direct observation |
| Setting events | Contextual factors influencing behaviour (sleep, health, medication, routine changes) | Ecological assessment, record review |
| Baseline frequency/duration | Rate of behaviour before intervention | Frequency counts, duration recording, latency recording |
| Replacement behaviours | Alternative, functionally equivalent behaviours to teach | Skills assessment, preference assessment |
| Intervention fidelity | Adherence to the Behaviour Intervention Plan | Fidelity checklists, coaching protocols |

### Safe Clinical Wording

- Frame assessment as "understanding the purpose the behaviour serves for the individual"
- Use "hypothesised function" rather than definitive causal statements
- Describe behaviours as "occurring at higher/lower rates under specific conditions" rather than as inherent traits
- Emphasise that behaviour is context-dependent and malleable
- Refer to "maintaining variables" rather than "causes" to acknowledge the probabilistic nature of behaviour-environment relationships
- Highlight that assessment aims to inform supportive interventions, not to restrict the individual
- Use function-neutral language when discussing preliminary hypotheses

### Integration with Digital Phenotyping

Digital phenotyping significantly enhances FBA methodology:

- **Passive smartphone sensing:** Continuous GPS tracking can identify geospatial triggers; accelerometer data captures activity changes; microphone analysis detects vocal patterns associated with distress
- **Wearable physiological monitoring:** HRV, EDA (galvanic skin response), and skin temperature sensors provide objective measures of physiological arousal preceding behavioural episodes
- **Environmental sensors:** Automated logging of ambient conditions (noise, light, crowding) reduces reliance on caregiver recall for setting events
- **Data visualisation platforms:** Real-time dashboards display behavioural frequency, antecedent patterns, and contextual variables, enabling dynamic hypothesis refinement
- **Predictive analytics:** Machine learning models trained on multimodal FBA data can generate early warning alerts when individuals enter high-risk contexts
- **Telehealth integration:** Remote observation via video platforms enables FBA in home settings without in-person clinician presence

## 3. Behavioural Activation Monitoring

### Framework Overview

Behavioural Activation (BA) is a structured, evidence-based psychological treatment for depression that focuses on increasing engagement with rewarding, meaningful activities and reducing patterns of avoidance and withdrawal. BA monitoring involves systematic tracking of daily activities, mood ratings, and activation levels to inform therapeutic decision-making. The core principle is that depressed mood is maintained by reduced contact with environmental reinforcers and increased behavioural avoidance, creating a negative feedback loop that can be interrupted through structured activity planning.

The BA monitoring process typically involves:
1. **Activity Monitoring:** Hour-by-hour tracking of daily activities alongside mood ratings (0-10 scale)
2. **Activity Scheduling:** Planned engagement with mastery and pleasure activities
3. **Values Assessment:** Identification of personally meaningful life domains
4. **Avoidance Detection:** Recognition of subtle avoidance patterns that maintain low mood
5. **Reward Tracking:** Monitoring the relationship between activity completion and mood improvement

### Evidence Strength: **A**

BA has been evaluated in multiple large-scale randomised controlled trials and is recognised as a first-line treatment for depression by NICE, the American Psychological Association, and the World Health Organization. A landmark trial by Dimidjian et al. (2006) demonstrated that BA was as effective as antidepressant medication and cognitive therapy for severe depression. Meta-analyses confirm large effect sizes for depression symptom reduction. Recent research using ecological momentary assessment (EMA) and passive smartphone sensing has validated the mechanistic assumption that increased activation in daily life leads to symptom change.

### Clinical-Use Status

**Status:** First-line treatment; widely implemented in clinical services  
**Settings:** Community mental health centres, primary care, IAPT services, outpatient psychology, telehealth  
**Target Populations:** Major depressive disorder, dysthymia, persistent depressive disorder, comorbid depression with chronic illness  
**Practitioner Types:** Clinical psychologists, psychological wellbeing practitioners, counsellors, CBT therapists, mental health nurses

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Description | Measurement Tool |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Activity level | Hour-by-hour recording of activities across waking hours | Activity monitoring worksheet, smartphone apps |
| Mood ratings | Subjective mood ratings on 0-10 scale for each time period | Self-report Likert ratings, EMA prompts |
| Activation score | Standardised measure of activation and avoidance behaviours | Behavioural Activation for Depression Scale - Short Form (BADS-SF) |
| Reward value | Subjective rating of pleasure and mastery experienced during activities | Reward Probability Index (RPI), activity ratings |
| Avoidance patterns | Identification of avoided activities, procrastination, social withdrawal | Activity log analysis, therapist interview |
| Environmental reward | Objective measures of reward exposure in the environment | RPI, social interaction frequency, step count |

### Safe Clinical Wording

- Describe BA as "helping individuals reconnect with meaningful activities and environmental rewards"
- Frame avoidance as a natural response to low mood that inadvertently maintains symptoms, not as a personal failure
- Use "reduced engagement" rather than "laziness" or "lack of motivation"
- Emphasise the behavioural model: "low mood leads to withdrawal, which reduces access to positive experiences, which maintains low mood"
- Refer to "patterns of activation and avoidance" rather than character-based descriptions
- Highlight that tracking is collaborative and serves the individual's goals
- Use "activity that aligns with your values" rather than imposing externally defined goals

### Integration with Digital Phenotyping

BA monitoring has strong natural alignment with digital phenotyping approaches:

- **Passive smartphone sensing:** Step count, GPS-derived mobility metrics (locations visited, time at home, travel patterns), accelerometer-based activity levels, and social interaction frequency (call/SMS logs) serve as objective proxies for activation
- **GPS and mobility analysis:** Time spent outside the home and number of unique locations visited correlate with self-reported activation and depression severity; reduced mobility predicts symptom worsening
- **Large language models (LLMs):** GPT-based analysis of daily text entries (e.g., from EMA free-text responses) can derive activation ratings with substantial inter-rater agreement (weighted Cohen's kappa = 0.77) compared to human raters
- **Real-time feedback:** Digital platforms can provide automated feedback on activity patterns and mood trends, supporting between-session therapeutic work
- **Personalised baselines:** Within-person changes in mobility and activity patterns are more predictive of symptom change than between-person comparisons, supporting individualised monitoring
- **Predictive modelling:** Integration of multiple digital phenotyping streams can forecast mood deterioration and prompt proactive intervention

## 4. CBT-I Sleep Diary

### Framework Overview

The Consensus Sleep Diary (CSD) is the standardised, expert-consensus instrument for subjective sleep assessment in the context of cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia (CBT-I). It was developed by a panel of insomnia experts led by Carney et al. (2012) through a systematic process involving expert review, focus groups with individuals with insomnia, and cognitive interviewing. The CSD captures the core sleep metrics necessary for CBT-I treatment planning, sleep restriction therapy titration, and outcome monitoring.

The Core Consensus Sleep Diary captures the following items each morning:
1. Time got into bed
2. Time tried to go to sleep
3. Sleep onset latency (time to fall asleep)
4. Number of awakenings (excluding final awakening)
5. Total duration of awakenings
6. Time of final awakening
7. Time got out of bed for the day
8. Sleep quality rating (Very Poor / Poor / Fair / Good / Very Good)
9. Optional comments

From these items, key sleep parameters are calculated:
- **Total Sleep Time (TST):** Total time actually asleep
- **Time in Bed (TIB):** Duration from getting into bed to getting out of bed
- **Sleep Efficiency (SE):** Percentage of time in bed that is spent sleeping (TST/TIB x 100)
- **Wake After Sleep Onset (WASO):** Total time awake after initially falling asleep

### Evidence Strength: **A**

The Consensus Sleep Diary was developed through a rigorous expert consensus process involving 25 insomnia researchers and subsequent focus groups with good sleepers and individuals with insomnia and sleep apnea. It is endorsed as the "gold standard" for subjective sleep assessment in insomnia research and clinical practice. The CSD is used as the primary outcome measure in virtually all CBT-I clinical trials. A Swedish validation study using cognitive interviewing confirmed adequate comprehension and response processes, though noted challenges with retrieval accuracy when diaries are not completed immediately upon waking. Digital adaptations of the CSD have demonstrated feasibility and engagement, with median completion rates of 81 entries over 12 weeks in clinical samples.

### Clinical-Use Status

**Status:** Gold-standard subjective sleep assessment; universally adopted in CBT-I  
**Settings:** Sleep clinics, primary care, CBT-I programmes, IAPT services, telehealth sleep programmes  
**Target Populations:** Insomnia disorder, comorbid insomnia with depression/anxiety, circadian rhythm disorders  
**Practitioner Types:** Sleep specialists, clinical psychologists, CBT-I therapists, primary care physicians

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Description | Clinical Significance |
|--------|-------------|----------------------|
| Sleep Onset Latency (SOL) | Time from attempting sleep to sleep onset | Indicator of sleep initiation difficulty; primary target for stimulus control |
| Wake After Sleep Onset (WASO) | Total duration of nocturnal awakenings | Measure of sleep maintenance difficulty |
| Total Sleep Time (TST) | Total minutes of actual sleep | Primary outcome measure for treatment effectiveness |
| Time in Bed (TIB) | Total duration from bed entry to rising | Used to calculate sleep restriction therapy prescription |
| Sleep Efficiency (SE) | TST/TIB x 100 | Primary metric for sleep restriction therapy titration; target >85% |
| Sleep Quality Rating | Subjective global appraisal of sleep | Captures subjective experience not reflected in objective metrics |
| Sleep Window | Prescribed bed and rise times | Therapeutic prescription based on calculated sleep need |

### Safe Clinical Wording

- Refer to "sleep difficulties" or "insomnia symptoms" rather than "poor sleep hygiene" (which may imply blame)
- Frame the diary as "a tool to understand your individual sleep pattern" rather than a test
- Use "sleep efficiency" as a neutral, objective metric rather than a judgment
- Describe sleep restriction therapy as "consolidating your sleep" rather than "depriving you of sleep"
- Acknowledge variability: "sleep patterns naturally vary from night to night"
- Emphasise that subjective sleep perception may differ from objective measures, and both are valid
- Avoid alarmist language about sleep duration; focus on sleep quality and daytime functioning

### Integration with Digital Phenotyping

Digital phenotyping transforms CBT-I sleep monitoring:

- **Digital sleep diaries:** Electronic CSD implementations with automated graphical feedback and visualisation improve engagement and reduce retrospective reporting bias; median completion of 81 entries over 12 weeks has been demonstrated
- **Wearable sleep trackers:** Actigraphy and consumer wearables (e.g., smartwatches, fitness bands) provide objective estimates of sleep onset, awakenings, and total sleep time, complementing subjective diary data
- **Smartphone-based sleep detection:** Accelerometer and screen-time data can estimate sleep-wake patterns with moderate accuracy
- **Polysomnography (PSG) integration:** Objective PSG data validates and contextualises subjective diary entries
- **Automated feedback systems:** Digital platforms can calculate sleep efficiency, generate sleep restriction prescriptions, and provide standardised feedback without requiring clinician manual calculation
- **Circadian phenotyping:** Integration of sleep diary data with light exposure sensors, body temperature monitoring, and melatonin sampling enables comprehensive circadian assessment
- **Predictive analytics:** Machine learning models combining sleep diary data with daytime activity, mood ratings, and physiological signals can predict sleep quality and identify intervention opportunities

## 5. DBT Skills Diary Card

### Framework Overview

The DBT Skills Diary Card is a structured daily self-monitoring tool that is a core component of Dialectical Behaviour Therapy (DBT), originally developed by Marsha M. Linehan for the treatment of borderline personality disorder (BPD) and chronic suicidality. The diary card tracks emotions, urges, target behaviours, and skills usage to inform treatment planning and monitor progress. It serves as the agenda-setting tool for individual therapy sessions and provides data for identifying patterns, triggers, and treatment targets.

The standard diary card captures:
- **Emotions:** Daily intensity ratings (typically 0-5) for emotions including sadness, anger, anxiety, guilt, shame, love, disgust, and emptiness
- **Urges:** Intensity ratings for urges to engage in target behaviours (e.g., self-harm, substance use, suicidal ideation)
- **Target Behaviours:** Frequency or occurrence of specific target behaviours including suicidal acts, non-suicidal self-injury, substance use, therapy-interfering behaviours, and quality-of-life-interfering behaviours
- **Skills Used:** Checklist of DBT skills practised each day across core modules (Mindfulness, Distress Tolerance, Emotion Regulation, Interpersonal Effectiveness)
- **Optional items:** Medication adherence, sleep quality, eating patterns, physical pain

### Evidence Strength: **A**

DBT, including the diary card as an integral component, has been evaluated in multiple RCTs and meta-analyses. A 2014 meta-analysis demonstrated that DBT significantly reduced suicidal behaviour, self-harm, and depression in individuals with BPD. The diary card methodology is derived from cognitive-behavioural self-monitoring, which has extensive empirical support as a mechanism of change. Studies of DBT for substance use disorders and eating disorders have also demonstrated efficacy, with diary card self-monitoring contributing to improved emotion regulation and reduced target behaviours. Research on digital DBT adaptations shows maintained efficacy with electronic diary cards.

### Clinical-Use Status

**Status:** Core component of standard DBT; required element of adherent treatment delivery  
**Settings:** DBT programmes, community mental health, inpatient units, forensic services, eating disorder clinics  
**Target Populations:** Borderline personality disorder, chronic suicidality, self-harm, substance use disorders, eating disorders, treatment-resistant depression  
**Practitioner Types:** DBT-trained psychologists, psychiatrists, mental health nurses, clinical social workers

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Description | Scale |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| Emotion intensity | Daily ratings of core emotion intensity | 0-5 Likert scale per emotion |
| Urge intensity | Strength of urges to engage in target behaviours | 0-5 Likert scale |
| Target behaviour frequency | Count of specific target behaviours per day | Frequency count or yes/no |
| Skills utilisation | Number and type of DBT skills practised | Checklist across four skill modules |
| Therapy-interfering behaviours | Behaviours that interfere with therapy progress (e.g., missed sessions, incomplete homework) | Frequency or severity rating |
| Quality-of-life behaviours | Behaviours affecting relationships, work, or daily functioning | Context-specific ratings |
| Missing links analysis | Identification of which skills could have been used but weren't | Structured clinical review |

### Safe Clinical Wording

- Frame the diary card as "a tool to understand what helps you cope and what gets in the way"
- Use "target behaviours" rather than "problem behaviours" to maintain a non-judgmental stance
- Describe urges as "natural signals" that provide information, not as dangerous or shameful
- Emphasise that tracking is collaborative and serves the individual's goals
- Use "skills practice" rather than "homework" to reduce performance pressure
- Frame therapy-interfering behaviours as expected and grist for the mill, not as failures
- Highlight that every data point provides useful information for treatment refinement

### Integration with Digital Phenotyping

Digital phenotyping offers significant opportunities to enhance DBT diary cards:

- **Electronic diary card platforms:** Digital implementations enable real-time data entry, automated pattern visualisation, and secure data sharing with therapists between sessions
- **Passive emotion detection:** Voice analysis from smartphone microphones can detect emotional arousal and valence changes, supplementing self-reported emotion ratings
- **Behavioural proxies:** GPS data can identify isolation patterns; call/text frequency can index social connection; screen time and app usage may reflect avoidance or emotion regulation strategies
- **Physiological monitoring:** Wearable devices capturing HRV and EDA can provide objective markers of emotional dysregulation episodes
- **Crisis prediction:** Integration of diary card data with passive sensing may enable early detection of escalating suicidal ideation or self-harm urges
- **Skills use verification:** Accelerometer data can confirm engagement in behavioural activation or exercise-based distress tolerance skills
- **Missing links analysis:** Automated comparison of emotion-urge-behaviour chains with skills databases can generate personalised suggestions for alternative coping strategies

## 6. Exposure Hierarchy Tracking

### Framework Overview

Exposure hierarchy tracking is a structured, central component of exposure-based therapies, particularly Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) for obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) and systematic desensitisation for anxiety disorders. The hierarchy serves as a treatment roadmap, listing feared situations, thoughts, or stimuli ranked from least to most distressing. Clients systematically work through the hierarchy, beginning with moderately challenging items and progressively confronting more difficult exposures.

The standard hierarchy development process involves:
1. **Stimulus Inventory:** Identification of all feared situations, objects, thoughts, images, and bodily sensations
2. **SUDS Rating:** Assignment of Subjective Units of Distress Scale (SUDS) ratings (0-100) for each item, reflecting the estimated distress if the feared situation were confronted without engaging in safety behaviours or compulsions
3. **Hierarchy Construction:** Organisation of items from lowest to highest SUDS rating
4. **Response Prevention Specification:** Identification of the compulsions, safety behaviours, or avoidance strategies to be resisted for each item
5. **Step Selection:** Choosing a starting point (typically SUDS 40-50) that is challenging but manageable
6. **Progress Tracking:** Recording of SUDS ratings before, during, and after each exposure; tracking of completion, habituation, and readiness to advance

### Evidence Strength: **A**

Exposure-based therapy, including ERP for OCD, is supported by one of the strongest evidence bases in psychological treatment. Multiple meta-analyses and systematic reviews confirm that ERP is the first-line psychological treatment for OCD, with large effect sizes for symptom reduction. The American Psychiatric Association identifies ERP as one of the most effective therapies for OCD, providing long-term symptom relief. The use of hierarchies to structure graduated exposure is a core element of all evidence-based exposure protocols. NICE guidelines recommend ERP as the treatment of choice for OCD. The hierarchical approach has been extended to panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, PTSD, and specific phobias with strong empirical support.

### Clinical-Use Status

**Status:** Core component of ERP and exposure therapy; required for evidence-based practice  
**Settings:** OCD speciality clinics, anxiety disorder clinics, CBT services, trauma services, telehealth  
**Target Populations:** OCD, panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, specific phobias, PTSD, agoraphobia, health anxiety  
**Practitioner Types:** CBT therapists, clinical psychologists, ERP-trained therapists, psychiatric nurses

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Description | Scale/Method |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| SUDS rating (pre-exposure) | Estimated distress before beginning exposure | 0-100 Subjective Units of Distress Scale |
| Peak SUDS during exposure | Maximum distress experienced during the exposure task | 0-100, recorded in real-time |
| SUDS after exposure | Distress level following completion of exposure | 0-100, typically after 10-20 minutes |
| Compulsion/ritual urge intensity | Strength of urge to engage in safety behaviour | 0-100 or 0-5 scale |
| Response prevention success | Whether the individual resisted compulsions/safety behaviours | Binary (yes/no) or percentage |
| Exposure completion | Whether the exposure task was completed as planned | Binary with partial completion option |
| Habituation within session | Reduction in SUDS from peak to post-exposure | Calculated difference score |
| Habituation between sessions | Reduction in peak SUDS for the same item across repeated sessions | Trajectory analysis |
| Hierarchy advancement | Movement to higher items on the hierarchy | Number of items completed, highest SUDS confronted |

### Safe Clinical Wording

- Describe the hierarchy as "a personalised plan for building confidence at your own pace"
- Frame exposure as "practising being with uncomfortable feelings" rather than "facing your fears"
- Use "safety behaviours" or "compulsions" rather than "bad habits" to maintain neutrality
- Emphasise that anxiety is expected and temporary during exposure: "the feeling will pass"
- Describe response prevention as "choosing a different response" rather than "stopping yourself"
- Use "learning" and "building tolerance" rather than "desensitisation" to avoid mechanistic connotations
- Frame setbacks as "information for adjusting the plan" rather than failures

### Integration with Digital Phenotyping

Digital phenotyping enhances exposure hierarchy tracking through:

- **Real-time SUDS capture:** Smartphone apps enable momentary SUDS ratings during in-vivo exposures, providing fine-grained habituation curves
- **GPS-triggered exposures:** Location-based reminders and SUDS prompts when clients enter feared contexts (e.g., public spaces for social anxiety)
- **Physiological monitoring:** Wearable HRV and EDA sensors provide objective arousal indices that complement subjective SUDS ratings
- **Exposure logging:** Automated recording of exposure completion, duration, and context, reducing reliance on retrospective recall
- **Virtual reality (VR) integration:** VR platforms deliver standardised in-silico exposure stimuli with automated SUDS capture and progress tracking
- **Between-session monitoring:** Digital platforms detect avoidance patterns (reduced mobility, altered routines) that may indicate hierarchy circumvention
- **Predictive modelling:** Machine learning models combining SUDS trajectories, physiological data, and behavioural patterns can predict readiness for hierarchy advancement
- **Gamification:** Digital progress tracking with visual progress indicators enhances motivation and engagement

## 7. Panic Diary

### Framework Overview

The Panic Diary is a structured self-monitoring tool used in cognitive-behavioural therapy for panic disorder (CT-Panic). It captures the key elements of panic attacks including frequency, situational triggers, physical sensations, catastrophic cognitions, and safety behaviours. The diary serves both assessment and intervention functions, promoting awareness of panic-maintenance cycles and providing data for cognitive restructuring and interoceptive exposure planning.

The standard Panic Diary captures:
1. **Panic Attack Frequency:** Number and timing of panic attacks
2. **Triggering Situation:** Context in which the attack occurred
3. **Physical Sensations:** Specific bodily symptoms experienced (palpitations, chest pain, dizziness, breathlessness, etc.)
4. **Catastrophic Thoughts:** feared catastrophic interpretations of bodily sensations ("I'm having a heart attack," "I'm going crazy")
5. **Anxiety Level:** Peak anxiety rating during the episode
6. **Safety Behaviours:** Actions taken to manage or escape the panic (avoidance, reassurance-seeking, carrying safety items, controlled breathing)
7. **Outcome:** What happened after the panic episode

### Evidence Strength: **A**

Cognitive therapy for panic disorder, including panic diary self-monitoring, is recommended by NICE as a first-line treatment. Panic diaries are based on the well-established panic disorder model (Clark, 1986), which posits that catastrophic misinterpretation of bodily sensations maintains panic. Multiple RCTs have demonstrated the efficacy of CT-Panic, with panic diaries serving as both a therapeutic tool and an outcome measure. The panic diary methodology is embedded in the Oxford Cognitive Therapy for Panic Disorder manual, one of the most widely disseminated treatment protocols. Research demonstrates that self-monitoring alone can produce therapeutic benefit through increased awareness and decatastrophisation.

### Clinical-Use Status

**Status:** Core component of CT-Panic; standard clinical practice  
**Settings:** CBT services, IAPT programmes, primary care mental health, anxiety clinics, telehealth  
**Target Populations:** Panic disorder, panic attacks with agoraphobia, subclinical panic symptoms  
**Prctitioner Types:** CBT therapists, clinical psychologists, psychological wellbeing practitioners, psychiatric nurses

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Description | Scale/Method |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Panic attack frequency | Number of panic attacks per day/week | Frequency count |
| Peak anxiety level | Maximum anxiety during the episode | 0-100 SUDS or 0-10 Likert |
| Physical sensations | Specific bodily symptoms experienced | Checklist (e.g., palpitations, dizziness, breathlessness, chest pain, sweating, trembling) |
| Catastrophic cognitions | Feared catastrophic interpretations | Open-text or structured list |
| Trigger identification | Contextual triggers for the panic episode | Situational description |
| Safety behaviours | Actions taken to cope with or escape panic | Checklist (avoidance, reassurance, safety signals, controlled breathing, escape) |
| Belief ratings | Strength of belief in catastrophic thought | 0-100% belief rating |
| Alternative thought | Rational response to catastrophic thought | Open-text with belief rating |

### Safe Clinical Wording

- Describe panic attacks as "intense waves of anxiety that feel uncomfortable but are not dangerous"
- Use "bodily sensations" rather than "symptoms" to reduce medical anxiety
- Frame catastrophic thoughts as "anxiety speaking" rather than accurate predictions
- Emphasise the temporary nature of panic: "panic attacks peak and then subside on their own"
- Describe safety behaviours as "things that help in the moment but maintain anxiety long-term"
- Avoid medicalising language that could reinforce health anxiety
- Use "building confidence in your body's responses" rather than "getting used to symptoms"

### Integration with Digital Phenotyping

Digital phenotyping offers powerful augmentation of panic diary monitoring:

- **Real-time panic capture:** Smartphone apps enable immediate recording of panic episodes, reducing recall bias and capturing SUDS ratings at peak anxiety
- **Physiological validation:** Wearable heart rate monitors can objectively confirm physiological arousal during self-reported panic episodes, distinguishing panic from anticipatory anxiety
- **Interoceptive detection:** Accelerometer and gyroscope data can detect hyperventilation patterns, movement changes, and postural shifts associated with panic episodes
- **Location-based triggers:** GPS data can identify geospatial patterns in panic occurrence, supporting situational exposure planning
- **Activity context:** Automatic activity recognition can identify whether panic occurred during specific activities (driving, social situations, exercise)
- **Cognitive phenotyping:** Voice analysis during panic episodes may capture changes in speech rate, pitch, and coherence
- **Predictive alerts:** Models combining physiological, behavioural, and contextual data may generate early warnings for high-risk panic periods
- **Interoceptive exposure support:** Biofeedback from wearable devices can guide and titrate interoceptive exposure exercises (e.g., heart rate biofeedback during exercise-induced panic cues)

## 8. Social Skills Assessment

### Framework Overview

Social skills assessment encompasses a range of structured tools designed to evaluate an individual's social competencies, including the ability to initiate and maintain interactions, interpret social cues, demonstrate empathy, cooperate with others, and respond appropriately in social situations. Social skills are learned behaviours that facilitate effective social interactions and are amenable to systematic assessment and training.

Major social skills assessment frameworks include:

**Social Skills Improvement System - Rating Scales (SSIS-RS):**  
A norm-referenced, multi-informant (parent, teacher, student) behaviour rating system assessing social skills, problem behaviours, and academic competence. The SSIS-RS Social Skills scale comprises seven subscales: Communication, Cooperation, Assertion, Responsibility, Empathy, Engagement, and Self-Control. Items are rated on a 4-point frequency scale (Never, Seldom, Often, Almost Always) and yield standard scores (M = 100, SD = 15).

**Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule (ADOS):**  
A semi-structured, standardised observational assessment of social communication and interaction, considered a gold standard tool for autism assessment. It involves structured activities that elicit social communicative behaviours.

**Social Responsiveness Scale (SRS):**  
A quantitative measure of social impairments associated with ASD, capturing social awareness, social cognition, social communication, social motivation, and autistic mannerisms.

**Social Communication Questionnaire (SCQ):**  
A screening tool assessing social communication skills and repetitive behaviours, used in autism diagnostic evaluations.

### Evidence Strength: **A**

The SSIS-RS is the most widely used social skills assessment tool, cited in over 400 research works, with translated versions available in numerous languages. It was standardised on a nationally representative sample of over 4,700 children and demonstrates strong reliability (internal consistency alpha = 0.96-0.97 for Social Skills scales; test-retest = 0.84) and validity evidence. The ADOS is recognised as a gold standard diagnostic tool for ASD with extensive psychometric validation. The Social Responsiveness Scale and Social Communication Questionnaire have been validated in large clinical and community samples. Recent longitudinal research applying the Measurement Model of Derivatives (MMOD) has examined the dynamic developmental properties of the SSIS-RS, supporting its utility for tracking social skill growth over time.

### Clinical-Use Status

**Status:** Gold-standard assessment; routinely used in educational and clinical settings  
**Settings:** Schools, autism assessment centres, paediatric psychology, developmental clinics, educational psychology  
**Target Populations:** Autism spectrum disorder, ADHD, social anxiety, intellectual disability, social skills deficits, developmental delays  
**Practitioner Types:** Clinical psychologists, educational psychologists, school psychologists, developmental paediatricians, speech and language therapists

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Description | Assessment Tool |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Communication | Appropriate verbal and nonverbal communication skills | SSIS-RS Communication subscale |
| Cooperation | Helping, sharing, and complying with others | SSIS-RS Cooperation subscale |
| Assertion | Initiating and responding to social interactions | SSIS-RS Assertion subscale |
| Empathy | Showing concern and consideration for others | SSIS-RS Empathy subscale |
| Self-Control | Appropriate conflict response and behaviour management | SSIS-RS Self-Control subscale |
| Social awareness | Ability to pick up on social cues | SRS Social Awareness subscale |
| Social motivation | Drive to engage in social interaction | SRS Social Motivation subscale |
| Joint attention | Shared focus on objects or events | ADOS social communication items |
| Reciprocal interaction | Quality of back-and-forth social exchange | ADOS, direct observation |
| Problem behaviours | Behaviours that interfere with social functioning | SSIS-RS Problem Behaviours scale |

### Safe Clinical Wording

- Use "social differences" or "social support needs" rather than "social deficits" where possible
- Frame assessment as "understanding social strengths and areas for development"
- Describe social skills as "learned behaviours that can be developed with practice"
- Use "neurodivergent social styles" alongside "social skills" to validate different ways of interacting
- Emphasise strengths-based assessment: identifying areas of competence alongside areas of need
- Avoid comparing individuals to normative standards in ways that could be shaming
- Highlight that social skills assessment informs intervention planning, not diagnosis alone

### Integration with Digital Phenotyping

Digital phenotyping provides novel opportunities for social skills assessment:

- **Social interaction quantification:** Call logs, text message frequency, and social media activity provide objective indices of social engagement
- **Speech pattern analysis:** Voice recordings from natural interactions can be analysed for turn-taking, prosody, and conversational reciprocity
- **GPS-derived social geolocation:** Patterns of visiting social venues and duration of social outings index community social participation
- **Facial expression analysis:** Video-based analysis during structured social tasks can capture nonverbal communication skills
- **Wearable sociometric badges:** Devices that capture physical proximity, face-to-face interaction frequency, and speech patterns in group settings
- **Virtual social environments:** Standardised virtual social scenarios with automated behavioural coding provide controlled assessment contexts
- **Natural language processing:** Analysis of written communication (text messages, emails) for pragmatic language skills, emotional content, and social appropriateness
- **Longitudinal tracking:** Continuous passive sensing enables within-person tracking of social engagement patterns over time

## 9. Parent-Child Interaction Coding

### Framework Overview

Parent-Child Interaction Coding systems are structured observational frameworks used to assess the quality of interactions between caregivers and children. These systems provide objective, standardised measurement of parenting behaviours, child compliance, and dyadic synchrony. They are used for assessment, treatment planning, and outcome measurement in parent-training interventions.

The Dyadic Parent-Child Interaction Coding System (DPICS) is the most widely used and validated system. It was originally developed by Robinson and Eyberg (1981) and is the assessment backbone of Parent-Child Interaction Therapy (PCIT). The DPICS codes parent and child verbalisations during structured 5-minute play sessions.

Key DPICS coding categories include:

**Parent Behaviour Codes:**
- **Labeled Praise:** Specific positive statements about the child's behaviour ("Thank you for sharing so nicely")
- **Unlabeled Praise:** General positive statements ("Good job," "Well done")
- **Reflection:** Repeating or paraphrasing the child's verbalisations
- **Behavioural Description:** Neutral descriptions of the child's ongoing behaviour
- **Questions:** Information-seeking verbalisations
- **Commands:** Direct ("Please put that away") and indirect ("Let's clean up now") instructions
- **Negative Talk:** Critical, corrective, or sarcastic statements

**Child Behaviour Codes:**
- **Compliance:** Following caregiver instructions
- **Noncompliance:** Refusing or ignoring instructions
- **Physical Proximity:** Seeking or maintaining closeness to caregiver

### Evidence Strength: **A**

PCIT and the DPICS have been evaluated in numerous RCTs and are recognised as evidence-based treatments for young children with disruptive behaviour disorders. The DPICS has demonstrated strong inter-rater reliability and validity, with coded parent behaviours predicting treatment outcomes. Meta-analyses confirm that PCIT produces significant improvements in child behaviour and parenting practices. The system has been validated across diverse populations and cultural groups. Research demonstrates that live parent coaching using DPICS-based feedback produces superior outcomes compared to delayed feedback. The Therapist-Parent Interaction Coding System (developed in 2014) extends the framework to evaluate therapist coaching quality.

### Clinical-Use Status

**Status:** Gold-standard observational system; core component of PCIT  
**Settings:** Child mental health clinics, community mental health, early intervention, child welfare, telehealth  
**Target Populations:** Children ages 2-7 with disruptive behaviour, oppositional defiant disorder, conduct problems, ADHD, child maltreatment  
**Practitioner Types:** PCIT-trained therapists, clinical psychologists, child mental health professionals

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Description | Assessment Method |
|--------|-------------|------------------|
| Parent skill frequency | Rate of labelled praise, reflections, behavioural descriptions, questions per minute | DPICS coding of 5-minute play session |
| Command ratio | Proportion of direct vs. indirect commands and their effectiveness | DPICS command coding |
| Child compliance rate | Percentage of commands followed | DPICS child behaviour coding |
| Negative talk frequency | Rate of critical or corrective statements | DPICS parent behaviour coding |
| ECBI score | Eyberg Child Behaviour Inventory frequency and intensity scores | Parent-report questionnaire |
| CDI mastery criteria | Achievement of skill benchmarks for Child-Directed Interaction phase | DPICS observation + therapist checklist |
| PDI mastery criteria | Achievement of skill benchmarks for Parent-Directed Interaction phase | DPICS observation + therapist checklist |
| Dyadic synchrony | Quality of reciprocal interaction between parent and child | Global rating scales, microanalytic coding |

### Safe Clinical Wording

- Frame assessment as "understanding the unique strengths and patterns in your parent-child interactions"
- Use "skills coaching" rather than "parenting training" to reduce stigma
- Describe parent behaviours as "interaction patterns" rather than "good" or "bad" parenting
- Emphasise that all parents can benefit from learning new interaction strategies
- Use "child behaviour support needs" rather than "behaviour problems" where possible
- Frame the DPICS as a "snapshot of interaction patterns" rather than a comprehensive assessment of parenting quality
- Highlight the collaborative nature of the work: "we are partners in supporting your child"

### Integration with Digital Phenotyping

Digital phenotyping transforms parent-child interaction assessment:

- **Automated video coding:** Machine learning models can automatically classify parent verbalisations (praise, commands, reflections) and child behaviours (compliance, noncompliance) from video recordings, reducing coding time
- **Acoustic analysis:** Voice analysis can quantify prosodic features (tone, warmth, pace) of parent speech and detect changes associated with coaching
- **Wearable proximity sensors:** Bluetooth or RFID devices can track physical proximity patterns between parent and child during natural interactions
- **Smartphone-based home observation:** Families can record structured play sessions at home using smartphones, enabling assessment outside clinic settings
- **Real-time coaching feedback:** Earpiece coaching systems can be enhanced with real-time coding alerts to prompt skill use during sessions
- **Longitudinal monitoring:** Repeated digital video captures enable tracking of interaction quality changes across treatment
- **Telehealth adaptation:** Video-based DPICS assessment via telehealth platforms maintains fidelity while increasing accessibility
- **Multimodal integration:** Combining video, audio, and physiological data (e.g., parent and child heart rate synchrony) provides comprehensive dyadic assessment

## 10. Relaxation & Biofeedback Tracking

### Framework Overview

Relaxation and biofeedback tracking involves the use of physiological monitoring to measure and train self-regulation of bodily processes associated with stress, anxiety, and arousal. Biofeedback provides real-time information about physiological states, enabling individuals to learn to modulate their responses through conscious control. Relaxation training encompasses diaphragmatic breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, guided imagery, and mindfulness-based techniques.

Types of biofeedback used in clinical settings:
- **Heart Rate Variability (HRV) Biofeedback:** Training to increase coherence between heart rate and breathing through paced breathing at the resonant frequency (~6 breaths/minute)
- **Electromyography (EMG) Biofeedback:** Monitoring of muscle tension using surface electrodes, used for tension-type headache, chronic pain, and stress-related disorders
- **Galvanic Skin Response (GSR) / Electrodermal Activity (EDA):** Measurement of skin conductance changes reflecting sympathetic nervous system arousal
- **Electroencephalography (EEG) Biofeedback (Neurofeedback):** Measurement of brainwave patterns to train specific EEG frequencies
- **Temperature Biofeedback:** Monitoring of peripheral skin temperature, which decreases under stress due to peripheral vasoconstriction
- **Respiratory Biofeedback:** Monitoring of breathing rate, depth, and patterns

### Evidence Strength: **A** (for HRV biofeedback); **B** (for other modalities)

HRV biofeedback has been evaluated in multiple meta-analyses. Goessl et al. conducted a random-effects meta-analysis demonstrating significant effects of HRV biofeedback on anxiety and stress symptoms. A recent meta-analysis (2021) on HRV biofeedback for depressive symptoms found significant effects with moderate effect sizes. Lehrer et al.'s systematic review confirmed significant effects of HRV biofeedback across a wide range of psychological conditions. EMG biofeedback has strong evidence for tension-type headache. EEG biofeedback (neurofeedback) shows promise for ADHD. The overall biofeedback literature supports its efficacy as an adjunctive treatment for anxiety, depression, PTSD, and stress-related conditions.

### Clinical-Use Status

**Status:** Well-established clinical intervention; increasingly available through consumer devices  
**Settings:** Pain clinics, anxiety clinics, PTSD programmes, neurology services, physical therapy, sports psychology, telehealth  
**Target Populations:** Anxiety disorders, depression, chronic pain, tension headaches, PTSD, ADHD, stress management, athletic performance  
**Practitioner Types:** Clinical psychologists, biofeedback-certified therapists, physical therapists, occupational therapists, psychiatrists

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Description | Measurement Method |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| HRV (SDNN) | Standard deviation of NN intervals; overall HRV index | ECG or PPG sensor |
| HRV (RMSSD) | Root mean square of successive differences; parasympathetic index | ECG or PPG sensor |
| HRV (HF power) | High-frequency power; primarily respiratory sinus arrhythmia | Spectral analysis of HRV |
| LF/HF ratio | Low-frequency to high-frequency ratio; sympathovagal balance | Spectral analysis of HRV |
| Resonant frequency | Individual breathing rate that maximises HRV amplitude | Systematic titration protocol |
| EMG muscle tension | Surface electromyography amplitude | Surface EMG electrodes |
| EDA/GSR | Electrodermal activity; skin conductance level and responses | Fingertip or wrist electrodes |
| Skin temperature | Peripheral skin temperature | Thermistor sensor |
| Respiration rate | Breaths per minute | Respiratory belt or accelerometer |
| Biofeedback accuracy | Proportion of successful self-modulation attempts | Session data analysis |
| Pre-post arousal change | Reduction in physiological arousal indices during/after session | Within-session comparison |

### Safe Clinical Wording

- Describe biofeedback as "a way to listen to your body's signals and learn to work with them"
- Frame the approach as "building awareness and control over automatic responses"
- Use "arousal" or "activation" rather than "stress response" in technical contexts
- Emphasise that biofeedback is a learning process that requires practice, not a passive treatment
- Describe physiological signals as "information" rather than "problems" to be fixed
- Highlight that biofeedback builds on natural self-regulation capacities
- Use "training" rather than "treatment" to emphasise the active learning component

### Integration with Digital Phenotyping

Digital phenotyping and biofeedback are deeply interconnected:

- **Consumer wearable HRV:** Smartwatches and fitness trackers now provide continuous HRV monitoring, enabling biofeedback training and relaxation practice outside clinical settings
- **Smartphone-based biofeedback apps:** Apps that use PPG sensors (camera-based heart rate) or connected wearable devices provide portable biofeedback training
- **Real-time arousal monitoring:** Wearable EDA and HRV sensors can detect physiological arousal episodes in daily life, prompting relaxation interventions at opportune moments
- **Sleep-integrated biofeedback:** HRV and respiratory biofeedback during sleep onset can support insomnia treatment
- **Stress pattern detection:** Machine learning models applied to continuous HRV, EDA, and accelerometer data can identify individual stress signatures and trigger pre-emptive relaxation prompts
- **VR-enhanced biofeedback:** Virtual reality environments combined with physiological monitoring create immersive relaxation and biofeedback experiences
- **Longitudinal self-regulation tracking:** Continuous passive sensing enables tracking of baseline HRV trends, providing feedback on cumulative stress and recovery patterns
- **Integration with CBT protocols:** Biofeedback data can be integrated into CBT for anxiety and panic to provide objective evidence of arousal reduction during therapy

## 11. Cross-Cutting Integration: Digital Phenotyping

### Overview

Digital phenotyping refers to the moment-by-moment quantification of individual-level human behaviour using data from personal digital devices (smartphones, wearables). It encompasses behavioural phenotyping (activity, mobility, sleep, communication), physiological phenotyping (HRV, EDA, EEG), psychological phenotyping (mood, cognition, stress), and social phenotyping (interaction patterns, language use).

### Classification of Digital Phenotyping for Behavioural Observation

| Data Source | Phenotype Category | Clinical Applications |
|-------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Smartphone accelerometer | Physical activity, step count, sedentary behaviour | Behavioural activation, exercise monitoring, depression screening |
| Smartphone GPS | Mobility patterns, locations visited, home time | Social anxiety, depression, agoraphobia, activation assessment |
| Smartphone microphone | Speech patterns, vocal features, conversation frequency | Depression, psychosis, social skills, mania |
| Smartphone screen/app usage | Screen time, app usage patterns, digital behaviour | Depression, ADHD, anxiety, behavioural activation |
| Wearable HRV | Heart rate variability, autonomic regulation | Stress, anxiety, depression, biofeedback training |
| Wearable EDA/ GSR | Electrodermal activity, sympathetic arousal | Panic, anxiety, stress reactivity, FBA |
| Wearable EMG | Muscle tension, movement patterns | Relaxation training, pain, motor symptoms |
| Wearable EEG | Brainwave patterns | Neurofeedback, ADHD, sleep staging |
| Wearable temperature | Skin temperature, peripheral vasoconstriction | Stress, anxiety, circadian rhythms |
| Text/voice analysis | Language sentiment, word choice, semantic coherence | Depression, mania, psychosis risk |
| Video analysis | Facial expressions, body posture, movement | Autism, depression, social skills |

### Clinical Benefits of Digital Phenotyping

- **Predictive capability:** Early identification of symptom deterioration before clinical presentation
- **Preventive intervention:** Timely prompts and just-in-time adaptive interventions
- **Personalised treatment:** Individual-level pattern identification supporting personalised care
- **Participatory care:** Patient engagement through self-tracking and shared decision-making
- **Objective measurement:** Reduction in recall bias and subjective reporting limitations
- **Ecological validity:** Assessment in natural environments rather than artificial clinic settings
- **Continuous monitoring:** Detection of within-person fluctuations invisible to episodic assessment

### Ethical and Regulatory Considerations

- Informed consent for passive data collection requires special attention for vulnerable populations
- Data security and privacy protection are paramount given the sensitive nature of behavioural and physiological data
- Regulatory frameworks for digital phenotyping in clinical practice are still evolving
- Risk of re-identification from anonymised multimodal datasets
- Equity concerns: access to smartphones and wearables may not be universal
- Clinician training required for interpretation of digital phenotyping data
- Standardisation of data collection, processing, and analysis protocols is needed

## 13. Clinical Implementation Recommendations

### Tier 1: Ready for Immediate Digital Integration
1. **CBT-I Sleep Diary** - Digital CSD implementations with automated calculation and visualisation
2. **Behavioural Activation Monitoring** - Smartphone-based activity tracking with mood ratings
3. **Relaxation/Biofeedback Tracking** - Consumer wearable HRV biofeedback applications
4. **Exposure Hierarchy Tracking** - App-based SUDS capture with progress visualisation

### Tier 2: Requires Technical Development
5. **Panic Diary** - Integration of real-time physiological validation with self-report
6. **DBT Skills Diary Card** - Multimodal emotion detection combined with skills use tracking
7. **ABC Recording** - Environmental sensor integration with structured incident logging
8. **Functional Behaviour Assessment** - Predictive analytics for high-risk context detection

### Tier 3: Emerging/Research Stage
9. **Social Skills Assessment** - Automated video and speech analysis for naturalistic assessment
10. **Parent-Child Interaction Coding** - AI-assisted video coding for home-based observation

### General Implementation Principles

- **Start with validated tools:** Use established, psychometrically validated measures before developing novel digital variants
- **Maintain clinical fidelity:** Digital adaptations should preserve the therapeutic mechanisms of the original tools
- **Multi-method approach:** Combine digital phenotyping with self-report and clinical observation; no single data stream is sufficient
- **Person-centred implementation:** Tailor monitoring intensity and methods to individual preferences, capacity, and clinical needs
- **Data governance:** Establish clear protocols for data security, consent, sharing, and retention
- **Clinician training:** Ensure practitioners understand both the clinical framework and the digital tools being used
- **Iterative refinement:** Use implementation data to continuously improve digital integration
- **Equity considerations:** Address access barriers and ensure digital phenotyping does not exclude underserved populations
